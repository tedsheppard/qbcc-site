1. **Match the format model exactly.** Read the model below and replicate
   the structure: breadcrumb h4, h2 title, opening paragraph, statute
   excerpt, prose body with topical h3 sub-headings, optional case
   extract block, "What this means on a project" bullet list,
   footnotes ordered list at the bottom.

2. **No headings labelling cases.** Do NOT use sub-headings like
   "Key authority", "Leading authority", "Key case" or "Important
   decision". Discuss the case naturally in prose under a topical
   sub-heading instead.

3. **Section reference style.** Always use `s 80` with a space, not
   `s80`. Apply this to every section reference in your prose, your
   footnotes, and your statute-excerpt summary text. The ONE exception
   is the verbatim statute body itself, where the gazetted heading
   number stays as printed (e.g. `<strong>61 Application of chapter</strong>`).

4. **Plain prose default.** Do NOT use `<div class="analysis">`. The
   only special blocks you may use are:
   - `<details class="statute-excerpt" open>` for verbatim statute
   - `<div class="case-excerpt">` for a single judicial extract (use
     this at most ONCE per page, only where the extract earns its
     place — i.e. it's the leading proposition or contains words the
     courts repeatedly quote)
   - `<ul>` / `<ol>` for lists
   - `<ol class="footnotes">` for the footnotes block at the bottom

5. **Verbatim source.** Statutory text, case names, citations, judicial
   block quotes and adjudication reference numbers must be reproduced
   word-for-word from the source. Curly→straight quote conversion is
   fine; everything else is not. Do NOT silently "correct" what looks
   like a typo or wrong cross-reference in the source — reproduce as
   written.

6. **No hallucination.** If a case, section or proposition is not in
   the source bundled below, do not include it.

7. **Audience and register.** Construction project manager / project
   director. Plain English, active voice, "you" framing where natural.
   Avoid "pursuant to", "notwithstanding", "inter alia", "it is
   submitted", "the authorities establish".

8. **Length.** Aim for 400–800 words of prose plus a tight footnote
   block. Don't pad. Don't truncate either if the source has substance.

9. **Case selection.** Pick at most TWO cases to discuss in-text. The
   rest go in the footnotes with citation, judge in parentheses, and a
   one-line gloss of what the case is for. The two in-text cases should
   be either:
   - the leading appellate authority (HCA, QCA, recent NSWCA), or
   - a case whose facts directly illustrate the point.
   Use AT MOST ONE `<div class="case-excerpt">` block on the page,
   reserved for a passage the courts treat as the canonical formulation.

10. **HTML entities.** Use `&mdash;`, `&ndash;`, `&hellip;`,
    `&ldquo;`/`&rdquo;`, `&amp;` rather than literal characters where
    appropriate.

11. **Output ONE `<section id="SLUG">…</section>` block.** No `<html>`,
    `<head>`, `<body>`, `<style>`, `<script>` wrappers. No markdown
    fences. No preamble or explanation outside the HTML.

## Format model — replicate this structure exactly

```html
        <section id="pc-construction-contract">
          <h4 class="breadcrumb-heading">Requirements of a payment claim</h4>
          <h2>A construction contract must exist</h2>

          <p>The BIF Act payment regime only switches on if there is a &ldquo;construction contract&rdquo; between you and the other side. Without one, no payment claim can be made and any adjudication that follows has no foundation.</p>

          <details class="statute-excerpt" open>
            <summary>Section 64 BIF Act &mdash; &ldquo;construction contract&rdquo;</summary>
            <div class="statute-body">
              <p><em>construction contract</em> means a contract, agreement or other arrangement under which 1 party undertakes to carry out construction work for, or to supply related goods and services to, another party.</p>
            </div>
          </details>

          <h3>What counts</h3>

          <p>The definition does three things at once. It captures any contract or agreement; it extends to an &ldquo;other arrangement&rdquo; beyond a contract; and it removes any requirement that the agreement be in writing. An oral agreement is enough, and so is a partly-oral / partly-written exchange &mdash; for example, a series of emails confirming scope and price, or a sequence of agreed meeting minutes.<sup><a href="#fn-pc-cc-1">1</a></sup></p>

          <p>The work being undertaken must be construction work, or the supply of related goods and services in connection with construction work. Those concepts are defined separately at s 65 and s 66 and are dealt with on the next two pages.</p>

          <h3>The &ldquo;other arrangement&rdquo; limb</h3>

          <p>The &ldquo;other arrangement&rdquo; words exist to catch dealings that don&rsquo;t quite fit the legal definition of a contract &mdash; for instance, a head contractor&rsquo;s direction to a subcontractor to perform variation work where the parties are operating informally &mdash; without letting in every situation where someone has done work for someone else. Drawing the line has produced the most case law under this part of the Act.</p>

          <p>The current position is that an &ldquo;other arrangement&rdquo; must give rise to either:</p>

          <ul>
            <li>a legally binding obligation (not necessarily a contract &mdash; an estoppel or other source of legal compulsion can suffice, provided some legal consequence flows from it); or</li>
            <li>a &ldquo;concluded state of affairs&rdquo; involving reciprocity &mdash; that is, both parties have accepted mutual rights and obligations relating to payment or price for the works.</li>
          </ul>

          <p>The most recent appellate guidance is <em>Bettar Holdings Pty Ltd trading as Hunt Collaborative v RWC Brookvale Investment Pty Ltd as trustee for Brookvale Development Trust</em> [2025] NSWCA 242. The pleading in <em>Bettar</em> alleged only a common law contract or, in the alternative, an estoppel preventing denial of a contract. The NSW Court of Appeal held that wasn&rsquo;t enough &mdash; what was missing was a separate allegation that the claimant had &ldquo;undertaken&rdquo; to perform work on some basis other than a common law contract. McHugh JA (Bell CJ and Kirk JA agreeing) put the test for an &ldquo;other arrangement&rdquo; in these terms:</p>

          <div class="case-excerpt">
            <p><span class="para-number">[139]</span> a legally binding obligation&hellip; or a concluded state of affairs&hellip; involving some element of reciprocity or acceptance of mutual rights and obligations relating to payment or price for the works.</p>
          </div>

          <p>His Honour expressly declined to resolve the earlier first-instance conflict between <em>Lendlease Engineering Pty Ltd v Timecon Pty Ltd</em> [2019] NSWSC 685 (Ball J &mdash; required a legally binding obligation) and <em>Crown Green Square Pty Ltd v Transport for NSW</em> [2018] NSWSC 1080 (McDougall J &mdash; adopted a less stringent reciprocity-based test),<sup><a href="#fn-pc-cc-2">2</a></sup> because the pleading in <em>Bettar</em> failed under either test. So which test applies in close cases remains open at appellate level.</p>

          <p>The practical consequence is that a bare quantum-meruit claim &mdash; where work has been done but there is no agreement at all about price or payment terms &mdash; is unlikely to qualify. The &ldquo;arrangement&rdquo; requires reciprocal payment obligations, not merely the fact that work was performed.</p>

          <h3>What this means on a project</h3>

          <ul>
            <li>If you have a signed contract, a construction contract exists. Move on to the next element.</li>
            <li>If your engagement is by exchange of emails, purchase orders or agreed meeting minutes, that&rsquo;s still capable of being a construction contract &mdash; provided there is offer, acceptance and an intention to be bound. Document what was agreed and when; you may need to prove it later.</li>
            <li>For variation or out-of-scope work without a written variation order, capture the instruction and your response in writing as soon as possible. Most &ldquo;other arrangement&rdquo; disputes turn on whether there is evidence of agreement on price or rate.</li>
            <li>A bare quantum-meruit case &mdash; just a claim for the value of work done, with no agreement &mdash; will probably fall outside the Act. You&rsquo;d need to show reciprocal payment obligations, not just the fact of work done.</li>
            <li>If you anticipate a challenge to the existence of a contract, plead the arrangement carefully: the material facts of the agreement, the undertaking to carry out work, and the payment obligation. After <em>Bettar</em> the appellate courts are scrutinising those pleadings closely.</li>
          </ul>

          <ol class="footnotes">
            <li id="fn-pc-cc-1">That a written instrument is not required follows from the words &ldquo;contract, agreement or other arrangement&rdquo; in s 64: see <em>Okaroo Pty Ltd v Vos Construction &amp; Joinery Pty Ltd</em> [2005] NSWSC 45 (Nicholas J); <em>Machkevitch v Andrew Building Constructions Pty Ltd</em> [2012] NSWSC 546 (McDougall J).</li>
            <li id="fn-pc-cc-2"><em>Lendlease Engineering Pty Ltd v Timecon Pty Ltd</em> [2019] NSWSC 685, [29]&ndash;[33] (Ball J); <em>Crown Green Square Pty Ltd v Transport for NSW</em> [2018] NSWSC 1080 (McDougall J). For Queensland, <em>Capricorn Quarries Pty Ltd v Devcon Pty Ltd</em> [2010] QSC 28 (Daubney J) is the leading reading of the contract limb of the BIF Act definition.</li>
          </ol>

        </section>

```

## Verbatim BIF Act statute

### `statute/chapter_3/section_088.txt`
```
# Source: BIF Act 2017 — Chapter 3
# Section 88 — Adjudicator’s decision
(1) An adjudicator is to decide—
(a) the amount of the progress payment, if any, to be paid by
the respondent to the claimant (the adjudicated
amount); and
(b) the date on which any amount became or becomes
payable; and
(c) the rate of interest payable on any amount.
(2) In deciding an adjudication application, the adjudicator is to
consider the following matters only—
(a) the provisions of this chapter and, to the extent they are
relevant, the provisions of the Queensland Building and
Construction Commission Act 1991, part 4A;
(b) the provisions of the relevant construction contract;
(c) the payment claim to which the application relates,
together with all submissions, including relevant
documents, that have been properly made by the
claimant in support of the claim;
(d) the payment schedule, if any, to which the application
relates, together with all submissions, including relevant
documents, that have been properly made by the
respondent in support of the schedule;
(e) the results of any inspection carried out by the
adjudicator of any matter to which the claim relates.
(3) However, the adjudicator must not consider any of the
following—
(a) an adjudication response, to which the adjudication
application relates, that was not given to the adjudicator
within the time required under section83;

(b) a reason included in an adjudication response to the
adjudication application, if the reason is prohibited from
being included in the response under section82.
(4) Also, the adjudicator may disregard an adjudication
application or adjudication response to the extent that the
submissions or accompanying documents contravene any
limitations relating to submissions or accompanying
documents prescribed by regulation.
(5) The adjudicator’s decision must—
(a) be in writing; and
(b) include the reasons for the decision, unless the claimant
and the respondent have both asked the adjudicator not
to include the reasons in the decision.
(6) The adjudicator must give the registrar—
(a) a copy of the decision; and
(b) notice of all fees and expenses paid, and to be paid, to
the adjudicator for the decision.
Maximum penalty—40 penalty units.
(7) The adjudicator must give the registrar the information
mentioned in subsection(6) at the same time the adjudicator
gives a copy of the decision to the claimant and the
respondent.

```

Due to the high number of variation claims, the adjudicator formed a view that it was not possible to properly examine each separate claim in the adjudication. Rather, the adjudicator took a section of claims (12), including the largest claims in the adjudication, and carried out an assessment of these claims. Of the 12 claims assessed, the adjudicator valued 6 of the claims. It was alleged on behalf of the respondent to this adjudication (inter alia) that the adjudicator had failed to make a bona fide attempt or an attempt in good faith to discharge his obligations under section 26(2) of BCIPA (equivalent to section 88(2) the Act).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Skoien AJ held that there was nothing improper or illegitimate in the methodology adopted by the adjudicator. Skoien AJ said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] He did nothing underhand. His methodology was able to be understood after careful reading and what he actually did accorded with his methodology. The only questionable thing is the status in which he left many of the claims in payment claim 17, which directly led to the O’Sullivan application. On that point, however, it should immediately be noted that he did not cause them to be lost forever. The contractual rights between ODG and Hitachi would ultimately determine if they were valid and recoverable. All that may have happened to them is to make their recovery under a future progress claim debatable.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] If I can see nothing in principle illegitimate in his methodology, does the Payments Act or legal authority reveal illegality? Section s 26 says nothing specifically on the point. It does not in terms require the adjudicator to consider, assess and value each separate claim under pain of having the adjudication annulled. Given the time constraints and the size of many adjudications, that would be an unlikely intention of the legislature and, one would expect, would have been carefully spelled out if intended.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] One of the major intentions of the legislature in passing the Payments Act was the quick assessment of progress claims to keep up the vital cash flow to sub- contractors. Does s 26(2) require that every separate claim in a voluminous progress claim must be considered in full detail before the adjudicator’s decision can be handed down? It does not say so in terms. Any number of possible factual situations can be advanced to argue against such an inflexible construction of s 26. Would failure to deal specifically with a handful of claims worth a few hundred dollars nullify a decision on a payment claim made up of many hundred claimed variations, worth many millions of dollars all of the other claims having been carefully considered and individually valued? I hardly think so.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] No amount to be paid
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Alucity Architectural Produce Supply Pty Ltd v Australian Solutions Centre; Alucity Architectural Produce Supply Pty Ltd v Paul J Hick [2016] NSWSC 608, an adjudicator determined that the adjudicated amount was $0.00 because the payment claim was invalid. On whether the adjudicator made a determination under section 22 of the NSW Act, Hammerschlag J held:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] A determination that no amount is to be paid because the claim is invalid is no less a determination than one which determines that no money is payable for some other reason. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Parrwood Pty Ltd v Trinity Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd [2020] NSWCA 172, the New South Wales Court of Appeal upheld the primary judge’s decision that the adjudication decision was null and void for jurisdictional error, as the adjudicator failed to discharge their statutory duty to properly determine the amount of the progress payment to be paid to the claimant. In that case, the adjudicator stated in their reasons that they “decline[d] [from] determining” any entitlement arising from the payment claim since a finding would be “premature” in the circumstances. The adjudicator determined there was “No amount” instead of stating the adjudication amount was “nil” or “$0”. The Court of Appeal concluded the adjudicator failed to do the “very thing” they were required to do under the Act and thus failed to discharge their duty under section 22(1)(a) of the SOP Act (NSW) (equivalent to s 88(1)(a) of BIFSOPA). Moreover, the Court of Appeal confirmed that a party is entitled to treat an adjudication decision as void and seek a fresh adjudication rather than await a Court’s decision that an initial adjudication determination is void, so as not to deny a claimant from “exercising its statutory rights”.

[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Forte Sydney Carlingford Development Pty Ltd v Forte Sydney Carlingford Pty Ltd [2024] FCAFC 9, the Full Court of the Federal Court dismissed an application for leave to appeal from orders made restraining the applicant from referring a payment claim and adjudication application to an adjduicator. In this case, Forte Sydney raised five proposed grounds of appeal. In summary, the Forty Sydney contended that the primary judge erred in concluding that:
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] the adjduicator’s determination was not vitiated by jurisdictional error;
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] s 7(3)(c)(iii) of the NSW Act operated to deny jurisdiction to determine the payment claim;
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] there was a right to withdraw the adjudication application under s 26(1) of the NSW Act because the adjudicator had concluded he lacked jurisdiction and thus had failed to decide the application;
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] it was an abuse of process for it to withdraw and re-submit its adjudication application under s 26 of the NSW Act; and 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] the balance of convenience supported an interlocutory injunction.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In relation to grounds 1-3, Markovic and Helley JJ (Derrington J agreeing) determined that the true question was whether the adjduicator’s determination was one for the purposes of s 22 of the NSW Act (equivalent to s 88 of BIFSOPA). However, their Honours found that this is not a question their Honours need to resolve on the application because the determination has not been the subject of any finding to that effect. Their Honous otherwise observed the power to set aside a determination pursuant to section 32A of the NSW Act and relevant authories including Ceerose at [132]-[135] and Seymour Whyte at [175]-[176].
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In relation to ground 4, their Honours emphysised that the primary judge’s finding was that it was an abuse of process of the procedures of the NSW Act for Forte Sydney to re-agitate a claim which has already been decided, and not, as Forte Sydney contended, that it was an abuse for it to withdraw and re-submit an application.Therefore, it was held that the primary judge did not fail to have regard to relevant matters because the primary judge was aware of the NSW Act’s policy and considered whether the adjdudicator asked the wrong question, as alleged, finding that he did not.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In relation to ground 5, Forte Sydney made seven contentions to support the ground that the primary judge erred in considering the balance of convenience, thereby not justifying the grant of the interlocutory injunction. Their Honours disagreed with all seven contentions raised by Forte Sydney.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Binah Constructions Pty Ltd v PTMG Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 872, a Respondent to an adjudication sought to quash an adjudication determination on the basis that the adjudicator awarded more in the determination than that which had been claimed.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The relevant Payment Claim claimed an amount of $15,465.07, and the Payment Schedule certified Nil. The Adjudication application set out that the scheduled amount was $27,465.07, and the total amount owing to the claimant was instead $43,059.59. The logic behind the scheduled amount of $27,465.07, and not $0.00, was because the Respondent failed to respond to the previous payment claim in the amount of $27,465.07. As such, by default, the Respondent had certified $27,465.07 in respect of the previous payment claim. The Adjudicator was satisfied that $27,465.07 was not in dispute, and therefore determined it was payable.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Ball J held that this was not a case of the Claimant being paid more than they claimed. Although the Payment Claim only claimed an amount of $15,465.07, the $27,465.07 was included in the sum of the amounts that had been previously certified by the Respondent. Ball J confirmed it was open to the Claimant to serve a payment claim that included all previous progress claims and to apply for adjudication of its whole claim. Therefore, it was open to the adjudicator to determine that the $27,465.07 was not in dispute and was payable under the Contract. As such, this approach did not constitute jurisdictional error and did not lead to a substantial practical injustice. The application was dismissed.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Chevron Park Pty Ltd v Groupline Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2024] QSC 202, Chevron applied for judicial review of an adjudication determination, arguing that the adjudicator failed to consider payments preceding the date of the payment claim and payments made post-dating the payment claim. Treston J dismissed Chevron’s application on the basis that the adjudicator cannot consider the subsequent payments as per the terms of section 88(3) of the Act as the adjudication response provided by the respondent must not include any new reasons for withholding payment that were not included in the payment schedule.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.2    Limits on amounts awarded
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Creative Building Services Pty Ltd v TIO Air Conditioning Pty Ltd [2016] ACTSC 367 an adjudicator awarded the claimant an amount greater than the amount claimed in the payment claim. On whether the adjudicator exceeded the jurisdiction he was given as a consequence of the claimant’s claim and thus jurisdictionally erred, Mossop AsJ held:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …in my view, having regard to the statutory provisions earlier referred to, the capacity to determine the amount of a progress payment must be confined, at the upper end, by the claimed amount.  That is because the statutory process of a payment claim, payment schedule, adjudication application and adjudication response are all based upon the identification by a claimant of the claimed amount and a response by the respondent to that claim.  The statutory process is clearly one which permits the parties by an adversarial process to define the scope of their dispute.  That is consistent with the contractual processes which the Act mirrors and partially adopts.  It is also consistent with the autonomy that parties have to determine the claims which they wish to make and pursue under the Act.  It would be inconsistent with those statutory processes if, notwithstanding that a claimant had only sought a particular amount, the adjudicator awarded a greater amount. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] His Honour went on to conclude that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] While it might be possible to characterise the existence of a payment claim for a claimed amount at least as great as the amount awarded as a jurisdictional fact necessary for the making of an award, I consider that it is more appropriate to characterise what occurred in the present case as a jurisdictional error. The jurisdictional error involved the adjudicator exceeding the jurisdiction which he was given as a consequence of TIO’s payment claim.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The amount claimed in the payment claim and the amount certified in the payment schedule creates a ‘floor and ceiling’ for the potential amount determined by an adjudicator. An adjudicator will fall into jurisdictional error if the determined amount is more than the claimed amount, or less than the amount certified in the schedule. This was the case in Maxcon Constructions Pty Ltd v Vadasz (No 2) [2017] SASCFC 2 at [228] where Blue J held in the context of the SA Act:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In the present case, the adjudicator was required by section 22 of the Act to determine Mr Vadasz’s application for adjudication of his payment claim. Mr Vadasz in his payment claim and his adjudication application claimed payment of $204,864.55. Maxcon in its payment schedule and in its submissions to the adjudicator accepted liability to pay $141,163.55. The adjudicator had no jurisdiction in the circumstances to determine the amount of the progress payment at less than $141,163.55 or more than $204,864.55. Because of the manner in which the parties framed the issues in the payment claim, payment schedule, adjudication application and Maxcon’s submissions in response, the adjudicator was required to determine two discrete issues, namely the retention sum claim and the administration charges setoff.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.3    Operation of section 88(2)
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Section 88(2) of the Act has a dual role, it limits what an adjudicator may consider, ‘but mandates the consideration of those which are listed’: see Ware Building Pty Ltd v Centre Projects Pty Ltd & Anor (No 1) [2011] QSC 424, 6 (Fryberg J) in the context of section 26(2) BCIPA.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Livingstone Shire Council v EarthTEC Pty Ltd and Ors [2018] QSC 271, Lyons SJA declared an adjudication decision void of the basis that, when calculating the adjudicated amount, the adjudicator refused to consider submissions made regarding amounts paid into court by Livingstone Shire Council in satisfaction of notices of charge issued pursuant to the Subcontractors’ Charges Act 1974 (Qld). Her Honour stated that the language of section 26(2) BCIPA (equivalent to s 88(2) of the Act) prevents an adjudicator from considering a matter not identified in the subsection; but does not exclude from consideration something which comes within one of those matters. The language of the subsection does not preclude from consideration a statutory provision raised in a payment schedule or a properly made submission. Lyons SJA held that, because of the broad scope of matters capable of being raised in an adjudication response, section 26(2)(d) BCIPA would not preclude an adjudicator from considering such a submission.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In 1155 Nepean Highway Pty Ltd v Promax Buildings Pty Ltd [2020] VSCA 253, the Victorian Court of Appeal held that it is permissible for an adjudicator to infer, based on the failure of a respondent to provide a payment schedule, that the respondent was not in a position to contest the claim. Further, the Court found that, if a respondent does not provide a payment schedule, an adjudication may proceed without hearing from a respondent and the adjudicator may draw an inference from such failure (even if it is later established that there is a satisfactory explanation for the respondent’s failure).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Insite Construction Services Pty Ltd v Daniels Civil Pty Ltd [2023] QSC 33 concerned an application to set aside an adjudication decision and the court’s consideration of whether an adjudicator acted without jurisdiction by accepting the claimant’s payment claim and value, uncontested. The question before the court was - where “Nil” is scheduled, but no reasons for withholding payment are included, what steps is the adjudicator required to take to determine the amount of progress payment the claimant is entitled to. In dismissing the application, Crowley J of the Supreme Court held:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] If a payment schedule schedules “Nil”, the reasons for withholding payment must be included under s.69 of BIFSOPA [20].
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] a payment schedule which fails to include reasons for withholding payment or proposing to pay less than the amount claimed will be an invalid payment schedule under s.69 of BIFSOPA (see [108] of the decision).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Failure to contest a claimant’s valuation or amount worked can be interpreted as an implicit admittance of the valuation of work put forth and work completed within the payment claim, subject only to particular reasons set out in the relevant payment schedule (see [105-109] of the decision).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Given that the valuation ascribed to the work (and the amount of work performed) by the claimant was uncontested by the respondent, the Adjudicator was entitled to “decide the adjudicated amount by adopting the unchallenged (and implicitly accepted) valuation” (see [105] of the decision).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Under s.88 of BIFSOPA, the obligation on an Adjudicator is to ‘decide’ and ‘consider’. There is no obligation upon an Adjudicator to ‘inquire’ or undertake their own valuation exercise (see [107] of the decision).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Kangaroo Point Developments MP Property Pty Ltd v RHG Construction Fitout and Maintenance Pty Ltd [2021] QSC 30, Dalton J declared an adjudication determination void on the basis that: 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] the adjudicator failed to determine whether the document that the claimant nominated as the payment schedule in the adjudication application was, in fact, a payment schedule within the meaning of section 69 of the BIF act; and
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] the adjudicator based his determination on a document that did not constitute a payment schedule and failed to consider the actual payment schedule submitted by the respondent.

In Kuatro Build Pty Ltd v Elite Formwork Group Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 372, Hmelnitsky J accepted that Kuatro submitted a valid adjudication response under s 20 of the SOP Act (equivalent to section 88(2) BIFSOPA) on 13 January 2025. The response included submissions and supporting documents, such as ‘Attachment 4 BACK CHARGE – Formwork.’ Although Kuatro later alleged that the adjudicator failed to consider these documents, the Court found that the materials were referenced in the adjudication response and acknowledged in the adjudicator’s reasons, confirming compliance with s 20 and undermining the procedural fairness claim.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.4    Section 88(2) – ‘consider’ 
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] ‘To consider’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The obligation on an adjudicator ‘to consider’ the matters under section 88(2) of the Act, requires ‘an active process of intellectual engagement’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In the context of the NSW Act, in Timwin Construction v Façade Innovations [2005] NSWSC 548, McDougall J said (at [39]):
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …A requirement to consider, or take into consideration, is equivalent to a requirement to have regard to something: see Zhang v Canterbury City Council (2001) 51 NSWLR 589 at 602 (Spigelman CJ, with whom Meagher and Beazley JJA agreed).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Laing O’Rourke Australia Construction v H & M Engineering & Construction [2010] NSWSC 818, McDougall J said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] As a matter of plain English, the obligation to “consider” something requires that it be given attention, or looked at on its merits (see, for example, the Australian Oxford Dictionary, Second Edition, 2004). Thus, in Tickner v Chapman (1995) 57 FCR 451, Black CJ, speaking of a statutory obligation on a minister of the Crown to consider representations made to him, said at 464 that “the consideration of a representation involves an active intellectual process directed at that representation”. In the same case, Burchett J said at 476 that the obligation required “the Minister… to apply his own mind to the issues raised by [the representations]”, which involved obtaining “an understanding of the facts and circumstances set out in them, and of the contentions they urged based on those facts and circumstances”. Kiefel J said at 495 that the obligation “requires that the Minister have regard to what is said in the representations, to bring his mind to bear upon the facts stated in them and the arguments or opinions put forward and to appreciate who is making them”.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my view, the obligation to consider various matters imposed by s 22(2) of the Act should be read in the same way: namely, as requiring an active process of intellectual engagement. It may be thought that this imposes a substantial burden on adjudicators. That may be so; but there are at least two reasons why, even if that is correct, it does not justify reading down the statutory obligation to “consider”. The first is that adjudicators are not forced to accept nomination. They may decline nomination; or they may accept only on condition that they are given some longer period of time than ten working days to produce their determination. The second reason is that the outcome of the adjudicator’s consideration may have very significant consequences…Having regard both to the limited ability for adjudicators’ determinations to be reviewed and to the nature of the estoppels that they create, the parties to an adjudication are entitled to have the adjudicator’s consideration, in the sense that I have explained, of the case that each of them brings.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292, [40] (Peter Lyons J).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Refer below in relation to the commentary on a failure to consider a submission ‘properly made’ or a failure to consider a matter prescribed under section 88(2) of the Act.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] A failure to ‘consider’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292, on whether a failure ‘to consider’ a submission properly made would invalidate an adjudication decision, Peter Lyons J held that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …Notwithstanding the limited time specified in the BCIP Act for an adjudicator to determine an adjudication application, it seems to me that the failure to consider submissions which are of such significance constitutes a failure by the second respondent to comply with an essential requirement of the BCIP Act for a valid decision.  On that basis, I consider that the adjudication decision fails to comply with one of the “basic and essential requirements” of the BCIP Act, and for that reason is not a valid adjudication decision.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In TWT Property Group Pty Ltd v Cenric Group Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 72, Stevenson J expressed in obiter that, in circumstances where an adjudicator reached a conclusion that a fact was “common ground” between the parties, but had only reached this conclusion because of a refusal to consider submissions which were properly made  (contrary to section 22(2)(d) of the NSW Act (equivalent to section 88(2)(c) of the Act)), the Adjudicator’s error would amount to a material breach of natural justice and render the adjudication determination void.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Martinus Rail Pty Ltd v Qube RE Services (No.2) Pty Ltd [2025] NSWCA 49, Payne JA held that jurisdictional error under s 32A of the NSW Act is confined to circumstances where an adjudicator steps outside the statutory limits of their power (equivalent to section 101(4) BIFSOPA). The Court of Appeal rejected Qube’s argument that errors in reasoning or factual misunderstanding could constitute jurisdictional error, applying the High Court’s approach in Hossain v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection (2018) 264 CLR 123 and Minister for Immigration and Citizenship v Li (2013) 249 CLR 332. Payne JA emphasised that the SOP Act operates on a “pay now, fight later” basis, and adjudications are intended to be “determined informally, summarily and quickly”. His Honour cautioned against excessive judicial interference, concluding that “the Court will not readily find jurisdictional error” in an adjudicator determination made within the statutory framework.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In BuiltCom Construction Pty Ltd v VSD Investments Pty Ltd atf the VSD Investments Trust; VSD Investments Pty Ltd atf The VSD Investments Trust v Builtcom Construction Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 250, Peden J noted that s 32A (equivalent to section 101(4) BIFSOPA) allows a court to sever part of an adjudication determination affected by jurisdictional error and uphold the unaffected part. However, as no jurisdictional error was found, it was unnecessary to exercise this power. Even if jurisdictional error had been established, Peden J indicated that the Court would not have remitted the matter to the adjudicator, citing Kirk v Industrial Court of NSW (2010) 239 CLR 531 and ProBuild Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd v Shade Systems Pty Ltd (2018) 264 CLR 1. Her Honour emphasised that a more appropriate remedy existed in the form of final relief under the construction contract.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 16, Wilson J held that the adjudicator’s decision was affected by jurisdictional error because:
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] procedural fairness was denied because Iris’s rights were determined on the basis of clause 5.2 when neither party made submissions in repected of this clause; and 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] the adjudicator failed to consider Iris’s submission about the effect of clause 5.7(a) of the Contract.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In this case, the adjdudicator dertmined that Descon was entitled to the release of the retention and dismissed Iris’ submission under clause 5.7(a) of the Contract on the basis that Iris had not referred to adjudicator to “any claim it made against Descon prior to the termination of the Contract pursuant to clause 5.2 of the Contract or another provision. The Respondent has simply stated that it has a claim against the Claimant without identifying the basis for the claim, the contractual provision on which is relies to withhold the retention from the Payment Claim etc”. The Court found at [27] that that the adjudicator “relied upon clause 5.2 in coming to her decision which was a clause of the contract not relied upon by either party in their submissions to her”. Further, it was held at [63] that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] even if the applicant was not denied natural justice, in my view the adjudicator did not “consider” the applicant’s submissions as to why clause 5.7 (a) of the contract entitled it to withhold the retention, as mandated by section 88 (2) (d) of the Payment Act.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.5    Section 88(2)(b) – ‘the provisions of the relevant construction contract’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Theiss Pty Ltd & John Holland Pty Ltd v Civil Works Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 187; [2011] 2 Qd R 276, Daubney J held that an adjudicator did not commit jurisdictional error in not considering clauses that were raised in the adjudication response, but not in the payment schedule.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In 1155 Nepean Highway Pty Ltd v Promax Buildings Pty Ltd [2020] VSCA 253, the Victorian Court of Appeal held that, despite certain documents forming part of a ‘construction contract’, an adjudicator will not commit jurisdictional error if an adjudicator does not consider that material. In this case, an adjudicator made their determination based on photographs and did not consider, nor did the claimant provide, drawings which formed part of the contract. The respondent, who was prohibited from lodging an adjudication response, argued that the failure by the adjudicator to consider those drawings, meant that the adjudicator failed to consider the “provisions of the construction contract” (i.e. the drawings), as required under s.23(2)(b) of the Vic SOP Act (equivalent of s.88(2)(b)BIF Act), and this failure amounted to a jurisdiction error. In rejecting this argument, the Court of Appeal found that ‘contract documents’ which did not constitute part of the construction contract but were incorporated by reference, are not ‘provisions of the construction contract’ that an adjudicator must consider under s 23(2)(b) of the Vic SOP Act (equivalent of s.88(2)(b)BIF Act).  
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Justar Property Group Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 231, Mossop J found that the adjudicator committed jurisdictional error by concluding that there was a “construction contract” between Justar and Chase. In this case, a contract did exist, but with a different company, Maxon Group Pty Ltd which the respondent (Justar) was associated with under the ‘Maxon Group’. His Honour applied the decision in Steel Contracts Pty Ltd v Simon[2014] ACTSC 146, proceeding on the basis that both the existence of a construction contract and the identity of the parties are matters that go to the jurisdiction of the adjudicator. The Court concluded (at 51) that an adjudicator that correctly determines that a “construction contract” exists but incorrectly identifies the parties to that contract, the adjudicator will have fallen into jurisdictional error.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Ratcliffe v Horizon Glass & Aluminium Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 196, Rees J set aside an adjudication determination on the basis that there was no “construction contract” between the claimant and the respondent. In coming to that decision, Rees J found that the adjudicator had incorrectly concluded that there was a construction contract between the claimant and the individual director of the principal company (whom the payment claim was addressed to). The correct respondent, with whom the contract was with, was the principal company and not the individual director. In arriving at that decision, Rees J explained that a construction contract between the Claimant and Respondent is an “essential pre-requisite” to the exercise of an adjudicator’s powers and functions. It was held that there was no such contract with the director because, on the balance of probabilities, the parties had intended that the principal company, and not the individual director, enter the construction contract with the claimant. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 92, Justice Martin held that while an adjudicator may consider the provisions of a construction contract pursuant to section 88(3) of the BIF Act, the adjudicator must not consider a reason for withholding payment that relies on a particular provision of the construction contract if that reason is a “new reason” that was not included in the payment schedule.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.6    Section 88(2)(c), (d) – ‘all submissions…properly made’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Pursuant to section 88(2) of the Act, an adjudicator is to consider “all submissions, including relevant documents, that have been properly made” by the claimant in support of the payment claim (section 88(2)(c)) and by the respondent in support of the payment schedule (section 88(2)(d) of the Act).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Under the Act, section 88(2)(c) and (d) are expressed in terms of reasons ‘properly made’. In the context of the NSW Act (and any case law arising under the NSW Act), the equivalent provision under section 22(2) of the NSW Act is expressed in terms of reasons ‘duly made’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Section 26(2) ‘limits the matters which an adjudicator might consider’: J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Cada Formwork Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 63, [59] (Peter Lyons J). 
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] ‘Properly made’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] As to what is a ‘properly made’ submission, in J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Cada Formwork Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 63, Peter Lyons J considered that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It seems to me that, for the purposes of s 26(2) of the BCIP Act, a properly made submission is one made in a manner provided for in that Act. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similarly, as to what are reasons ‘properly made’ (or ‘duly made’), in John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads & Traffic Authority of New South Wales & Ors [2007] NSWCA 19, Hodgson JA (Beazley JA agreeing) said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The legislature plainly entrusts to the adjudicator the role of determining whether submissions are or are not duly made, and thus of determining whether a submission contained in an adjudication response is one that should not be there because of the effect of s.20(2B).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Hodgson JA has also made similar comments in relation to an adjudicator’s power to determine whether submissions made by a claimant are ‘properly made’ (or ‘duly made’).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In State Water Corporation v Civil Team Engineering Pty Ltd [2013] NSWSC 1879, upon a review of the authorities, Sackar J said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] To summarise, the general position is that it is for the adjudicator to determine whether submissions made by a respondent were "duly made" and in compliance with s 20(2B). However, that does not necessarily place the adjudicator's decision beyond review. If the adjudicator formed his or her opinion by taking into account irrelevant considerations, or by misconstruing the terms of the Act, or the adjudicator's opinion simply cannot be described as reasonable, or is without foundation, that may provide a basis for the intervention of the court.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 92, Justice Martin declared that an adjudicator had fallen into jurisdictional error because an inference could be drawn that the adjudicator had not given regard to all relevant documents properly submitted by the claimant as required by section 88(2)(c) of the BIF Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text]  In that case, the claimant submitted a statement in support of its payment claim that directly contradicted the respondent’s position. The statement provided evidence that the respondent had removed work from the claimant’s agreed scope of works. The adjudicator stated in his reasons, that he had considered “the payment claim to which the application relates, together with all submissions, including relevant documentation, that has been properly made by the Claimant in support of the claim”.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Martin J held that where a document is crucial to the claimant’s case and directly contradicts the case advanced by the respondent, an inference can be drawn that an adjudicator did not have regard to a properly submitted document if the adjudicator fails to expressly refer to that document. His Honour held that an adjudicator cannot discharge its obligation set out in section 88(2)(c) of the BIF Act to consider all properly submitted documents by merely stating that it had regard to all relevant documentation.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Canterbury Bankstown Council v General Works and Construction Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 310, Nixon J dismissed an application to set aside an adjudication determinaton because no jurisdicational error was established. In this case, Nixon J found that s 22(2) of the Building and Construction Industry Security of Payment Act 1999 (NSW) (the Act) (Queensland equivalent BIFSOPA s 88(2)) implicitly confers on the adjudicator the power to form an opinion as to the scope of a payment claim. The adjudicator was entitled to rely on his own experice and expertise, and an error of law is not jurisdictional and could not form a bsis for quashing a determination. As s 22(2) of the Act requires the adjudicator to consider submissions “that have been duly made by the claimant in support of the claim”, whether or not a submission was “duly” made is a matter for the adjudicator to determine. Nixon J relevantly cited Ceerose v A-Civil Aus Pty Ltd [2023] NSWCA 215, where Payne J held that an error by the adjudicator in identifying a submission as being “duly made” is not jurisdictional.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Submissions not ‘properly made’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In considering documents not ‘properly made’ under the Act, the adjudication decision may be invalidated, depending on the significance of the relevant material: see J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Cada Formwork Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 63, [63] (Peter Lyons J):
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …As mentioned earlier in these reasons, an obvious purpose of s 26(2) is to limit the matters to which an adjudicator might have regard, in determining an adjudication application.  The limitation is significant, not only because it gives effect to the legislative intent that a decision be made quickly, but also because, so far as it permits the adjudicator to consider material originating from a party to the adjudication application, it limits that consideration to material which is to be served on the other party.  Accordingly, at least in the case of material from the claimant, it limits the adjudicator’s consideration to material to which the respondent has had an opportunity to respond.  Where an adjudicator considers something which is not a properly made submission of a claimant, and to which a respondent has not had an opportunity to respond, then it seems to me that the scope and object of the statutory provisions will generally have the consequence that the determination is invalid.  However, this may depend upon the significance of the material thus considered.  It seems to me that the approach taken under the rules of natural justice to the consideration of evidence about which one party has not had an opportunity to comment would provide an appropriate guide… 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In Krongold Constructions (Aust) v SR & RS Wales [2016] VSC 94, an adjudication determination was held to be invalid because the adjudicator had not been provided with a complete copy of the payment claim and therefore was not in a position to comply with the requirements of the Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Hanson Construction Materials Pty Ltd v Brolton Group Pty Limited [2019] NSWSC 1641, Ball J quashed an adjudication determination on the basis that the Adjudicator had attempted to determine an alternate reference date that had not been agreed to by either of the parties. In this case, Brolton alleged that its payment claim, served on 29 August 2019, was supported by a reference date of 25 September 2018. Hanson contended that the payment claim could not be supported by the reference date of 25 September 2018, because it claimed amounts for work done after that date. Further, it could not be supported by a later reference date because no further reference dates arose under the contract following its termination on 3 October 2018. The Adjudicator rejected both submissions and held (erroneously) that a further reference date arose on 23 October 2018. The Court held that there would be a denial of natural justice where the adjudicator determines a dispute on a basis for which neither party has contended without giving the parties an opportunity to make submissions on the matter. As neither party had contended for a reference date of 23 October 2018, the Court held that “the choice of reference date by the adjudicator had a substantial effect on the outcome of the determination.”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Civmec Electrical & Instrumental Pty Ltd v Southern Cross Electrical Engineering Limited & Ors [2019] QSC 300, Mullins J determined that it was within an adjudicator’s jurisdiction under section 88 of the Act to decide whether reasons included in a payment schedule were valid reasons, and any error in dismissing a properly made submission would not be reviewable. In this case, the applicant sought a declaration that the adjudication decision was void for jurisdictional error, or alternatively sought the exercise of the court’s power under section 101(4) of the Act. The Court upheld the Adjudicator’s decision, determining that in any event (at [38]):
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “even if the adjudicator made an error…in concluding that parts of the adjudication response should be rejected on the basis of not being a properly made submission, that would be an error within jurisdiction and not reviewable”.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Something of ‘real relevance’ 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] There is a strong New South Wales Court of Appeal line of authority on an adjudicator’s role to consider something they are aware of, which has ‘real relevance’ to issues arising under section 88(2)(a) and (b) of the Act, but which is not raised in submission ‘properly made’ (or ‘duly made’): The Minister for Commerce v Contrax Plumbing (NSW) Pty Ltd & Ors [2005] NSWCA 142, [33]-[36] (Hodgson JA); John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads & Traffic Authority of New South Wales & Ors [2007] NSWCA 19, [48]-[49] (Hodgson JA).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In the context of the NSW Act, in The Minister for Commerce v Contrax Plumbing (NSW) Pty Ltd & Ors [2005] NSWCA 142, Hodgson JA considered the nature of the equivalent limitation under the NSW Act on considering reasons ‘duly made’. In this context, his Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The only challenge to the primary judge’s decision on the second and third issues identified above was to the effect that the primary judge erred in holding that Contrax was entitled to rely on s.34, when that matter had not been raised in its payment claim. This contention relied on John Holland, and also on a suggested anomaly arising from the prohibition in s.20(2B) on a respondent relying on reasons not included in its payment schedule.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my opinion, this suggested anomaly loses force when one considers the true effect of s.22(2). It is true that paragraph (d) of s.22(2) limits the submissions of the respondent that can be considered under that paragraph to submissions duly made by the respondent in support of the payment schedule; and in my opinion, that does have the effect of excluding, from consideration under that paragraph, reasons included in the adjudication response that were not included in the payment schedule.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] However, paragraphs (a) and (b) of s.22(2) require the adjudicator to consider the provisions of the Act and the provisions of the construction contract; and in my opinion, that entitles and indeed requires the adjudicator to take into account any considerations (other than considerations arising from facts and circumstances of the particular case not otherwise before him or her) that he or she thinks relevant to the construction of the Act, the construction of the contract, and the validity of terms of the contract having regard to provisions of the Act. Thus, in my opinion, if an adjudicator comes to know of submissions of a respondent that he or she thinks to be relevant to these questions (not being submissions based on facts and circumstances of the particular case not otherwise before him or her), he or she can take them into account under paragraphs (a) and (b), even if they cannot be considered under paragraph (d).
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Similarly, in my opinion, an adjudicator could take into account a contention of an applicant that a term of the contract is void by reason of s.34, when considering matters under paragraphs (a) and (b), even if that contention could not be taken into account under paragraph (c).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Hodgson JA, in John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads & Traffic Authority of New South Wales & Ors [2007] NSWCA 19, provided clarification of his earlier views and said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] However, it is to be noted that I said that the adjudicator was required by pars.(a) and (b) of s.22(2) to consider matters “if he or she thinks [they are] relevant to the construction of the Act, the construction of the contract, and the validity of the terms of the contract having regard to the provisions of the Act”. To put this another way, I was saying that the adjudicator should not ignore something which he or she is aware of and also believes is of real relevance to issues arising under pars.(a) and (b), simply because the matter was not raised in submissions duly made by a respondent. Of course, if the matter has not been so raised, there may be questions of natural justice to the claimant that need to be addressed, perhaps by calling for further submissions or by arranging a conference; but that is another issue. However, the requirement for natural justice to the claimant is a further reason why the adjudicator would not be required to consider such matters under pars.(a) or (b) unless he or she thought they were really material to issues arising under those paragraphs.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Thus, in my opinion, any requirement to consider such matters which were not raised in submissions duly made arises only after a threshold is crossed, involving both awareness of the matters in question and a belief that they are of real relevance. In this case, I would infer that the adjudicator became aware of the submissions to the extent necessary for forming a view that they were affected by s.20(2B), but there is no basis for any conclusion that the adjudicator believed they were of substantial relevance to issues arising under pars.(a) and (b) of s.22(2).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The principles from both Contrax and John Holland were considered, and followed in State Water Corporation v Civil Team Engineering Pty Ltd [2013] NSWSC 1879. Sackar J accepted a submission that “even if the submission had not been ‘duly made’, the adjudicator was still permitted to consider the submission provided it was relevant to issues arising under s 22(2)(a) or (b) or both.” His Honour concluded that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] A submission included in an adjudication response contrary to the requirements of s 20(2B) is not "duly made" within s 22(2)(d), and therefore that submission cannot be considered by the adjudicator under s 22(2)(d). Consistent with the purpose for which the Act was introduced, the aim of s 20(2B) is to avoid new submissions being introduced late in a process running on a strict and brief timetable (John Holland Pty Ltd v RTA at [33]).
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] However, it must be remembered that the claimant's and respondent's submissions (duly made) are only two of a number of matters that the adjudicator is required to consider. Accordingly, an adjudicator should not ignore something of real relevance to issues arising under s 22(2)(a) or (b) (or both), simply because the matter was not raised in submissions duly made by the parties (John Holland Pty Ltd v RTA at [47]-[48]). [2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Brodyn Pty Ltd t/as Time Coast and Quality v Davenport & Ors [2004] NSWCA 394, Hodgson JA (Mason P and Giles JA agreeing) set out five ‘basic and essential requirements’ to the existence of a valid adjudication decision:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] What then are the conditions laid down for the existence of an adjudicator’s determination? The basic and essential requirements appear to include the following:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] 1.    The existence of a construction contract between the claimant and the respondent, to which the Act applies (ss.7 and 8).
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] 2.    The service by the claimant on the respondent of a payment claim (s.13).
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] 3.    The making of an adjudication application by the claimant to an authorised nominating authority (s.17).
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] 4.    The reference of the application to an eligible adjudicator, who accepts the application (ss.18 and 19). 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] 5.    The determination by the adjudicator of this application (ss.19(2) and 21(5)), by determining the amount of the progress payment, the date on which it becomes or became due and the rate of interest payable (ss.22(1)) and the issue of a determination in writing (ss.22(3)(a)).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] His Honour then set down what was labelled as ‘more detailed requirements’:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The relevant sections contain more detailed requirements: for example, s.13(2) as to the content of payment claims; s.17 as to the time when an adjudication application can be made and as to its contents; s.21 as to the time when an adjudication application may be determined; and s.22 as to the matters to be considered by the adjudicator and the provision of reasons. A question arises whether any non-compliance with any of these requirements has the effect that a purported determination is void, that is, is not in truth an adjudicator’s determination. That question has been approached in the first instance decision by asking whether an error by the adjudicator in determining whether any of these requirements is satisfied is a jurisdictional or non-jurisdictional error. I think that approach has tended to cast the net too widely; and I think it is preferable to ask whether a requirement being considered was intended by the legislature to be an essential pre-condition for the existence of an adjudicator’s determination.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] His Honour then considered the consequences of non-compliance with the ‘basic and essential’ requirements:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my opinion, the reasons given above for excluding judicial review on the basis of non-jurisdictional error of law justify the conclusion that the legislature did not intend that exact compliance with all the more detailed requirements was essential to the existence of a determination: cf Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority (1998) 194 CLR 355 at390-91. What was intended to be essential was compliance with the basic requirements (and those set out above may not be exhaustive), a bona fide attempt by the adjudicator to exercise the relevant power relating to the subject matter of the legislation and reasonably capable of reference to this power (cf R v Hickman; Ex Parte Fox and Clinton (1945) 70 CLR 598), and no substantial denial of the measure of natural justice that the Act requires to be given. If the basic requirements are not complied with, or if a purported determination is not such a bona fide attempt, or if there is a substantial denial of this measure of natural justice, then in my opinion a purported determination will be void and not merely voidable, because there will then not, in my opinion, be satisfaction of requirements that the legislature has indicated as essential to the existence of a determination. If a question is raised before an adjudicator as to whether more detailed requirements have been exactly complied with, a failure to address that question could indicate that there was not a bona fide attempt to exercise the power; but if the question is addressed, then the determination will not be made void simply because of an erroneous decision that they were complied with or as to the consequences of non-compliance.

[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Sufficiency of reasons
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Section 88(5) of the Act requires an adjudicator to include reasons for the decision, unless the claimant and respondent have both asked the adjudicator not to include the reasons in the decision.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] There is no requirement for the reasons to be lengthy, elaborate or detailed. In SSC Plenty Road Pty Ltd v Construction Engineering (Aust) Pty Ltd [2015] VSC 631, Vickery J said in relation to the VIC Act that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] These circumstances do not call for an exacting standard to be applied to an adjudicator’s reasons. Indeed, the legislative scheme is such that the standard of reasons delivered by an adjudicator within the time constraints required by the Act, and given the nature of the decision to be delivered in this demanding context, and by persons who are often not legally qualified, of necessity means that it falls towards the lower end of the scale.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, ‘a mere indication by an adjudicator of having read the material and expressing a conclusion as to satisfaction as to appropriate value might be inadequate’. Similarly, see SSC Plenty Road Pty Ltd v Construction Engineering (Aust) Pty Ltd [2015] VSC 631 where Vickery J held that, while the adjudicator had provided ‘sufficient’ reasons, the adjudicator had failed to demonstrate any process of assessment of disputed claims other than an adoption of the claimant’s value.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The adequacy or sufficiency of the reasons for the decision given by an adjudicator are to be considered in the context of the task assigned to him or her under the Act, “to provide a speedy interim determination on the papers of disputes about a progress claims [sic].” Also relevant are “other factors such as the qualifications for appointment as an adjudicator”.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Avopiling (NSW) Pty Ltd v Menard Bachy Pty Ltd [2012] NSWSC 1466, Sackar J said the following in the context of the NSW Act:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] As I have already observed s 22(3) requires the adjudicator to give reasons for the determination. There is clearly no requirement for the reasons to be lengthy elaborate or detailed. The reasons should be sufficient to show that the adjudicator has engaged actively with the dispute and dealt with it in a way that is reasoned, not perverse, arbitrary or capricious: Bergemann v Power [2011] NSWSC 1039 at [67] per McDougall J.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] No mechanical formula can be given in determining the precise form the reasons are to take. There is clearly no need to refer to all of the evidence, in the reasons. It has to be clear enough that the relevant evidence or point has been considered. Any issue which is critical should be adverted to, but again the extent to which it needs to be dealt with will be a matter of degree and depends to what extent the issue is canvassed by the parties themselves. It is not necessary for explicit findings to be made on each disputed question of fact provided it is clear by inference what is found. It is often said that subjecting every statement of reasons with a "fine tooth comb" will inevitably lead to the exposure of inadequacies often of no relevance. A statement of reasons should be looked at as a whole. Beale v Government Insurance Office (NSW) [1997] 48 NSWLR 430, per Meagher JA at 441 - 444. What I have said must be of course viewed in the context of the specific requirements of s 22.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The content of the adjudication is obviously the most relevant source as to whether the adjudicator has or has not performed the relevant statutory functions. It is to be recalled that the process may or may not be interactive and will be conducted generally, entirely in writing. Provided it is apparent that the adjudicator has considered pertinent issues in good faith, very considerable latitude in my view should be afforded to an adjudicator as to the manner and form of the determination. To become too pedantic about the way in which the adjudicator has drafted a determination is to introduce an element of artificiality such as might well defeat the object and purpose of the Act and the aim of the process entirely. On the other hand the mere fact an adjudicator blandly says he or she has read "all of the submissions and accompanying documents" or simply that he or she is "satisfied" without more in relation to a particular issue under consideration may not, subject to viewing the determination as a whole survive as adequate reasons. As I have said it will always be a matter of degree. Shell Refining (Australia) Pty Ltd v AJ Mayr Engineering Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 94 at [25] and [26] per Bergin J (as she then was), and McDougall J in Leighton at [94].
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The above statement was referred to with approval in the Queensland decision of JAG Projects Qld Pty Ltd v Total Cool Pty Ltd & Anor [2015] QSC 229 per Bond J.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Ceerose Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd [2023] NSWCA 215, the NSW Court of Appeal considered the adjudicator’s duty to consider the “true merits” of sums claimed in a payment claim. The Court of Appeal overruled Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v JM Hargreaves (NSW) Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 228, and Pacific General Securities Ltd v Soliman & Sons [2006] NSWSC 13 in determining that it will not be jurisdictional error for an adjudicator to award amounts under a payment claim without “addressing its merits”, if there is an absence of material put forward by the respondent.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In BGC Construction Pty Ltd v Citygate Properties Pty Ltd [2016] WASC 88, Tottle J referred to the requirement on an adjudicator to provide reasons for the decision and said the following in the context of the WA Act:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The authorities make it clear that the reasons do not have to be detailed or elaborate but an adjudicator cannot omit to give reasons entirely in respect of significant items and leave the parties to work out for themselves the basis upon which a determination has been made.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Sierra Property Qld Pty Ltd v National Construction Management Pty Ltd [2016] QSC 108 Jackson J considered the extent of the reasons required by section 26(3) of BCIPA and said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …there is no necessary list of matters that must be considered in the reasons. Conversely, there is no list of matters that if considered will in every case satisfy the requirement to include reasons for the written decision. To some extent, each case must depend on its own facts.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In that decision, even though the adjudicator referred to having ‘carefully’ considered the adjudication material, there was no reference made to the extent of the work completed in any of a number of contract categories. His Honour was not satisfied that the adjudicator had fully discharged his obligations under section 26(3) BCIPA. In considering whether the failure to provide reasons amounted to jurisdictional error, his Honour applied the approach adopted in Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority [1998] HCA 28 and said that the true question was one of statutory interpretation and that the detailed steps required by BCIPA are intended to ensure fairness between the parties. His Honour held that the reasons required by s 26(3)(b) of BCIPA are an essential part of an adjudication decision and failing to provide reasons amounts to jurisdictional error.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Justice Bond in Acciona Agua Australia Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 133 declared that the adjudicator had fallen into jurisdictional error by failing to provide proper reasons for his decision as required under section 88(5) of BIFSOPA. The finding of insufficient reasons was borne out of the finding that the adjudicator acted in excess of jurisdiction by improperly considering the new reasons in an adjudication response. Justice Ball held in the circumstances that the reasons were “nothing more than a vote one way”, revealing the adjudicator failed to carry out the “active process of intellectual engagement with the issues and the submissions before him” necessitated by BIFSOPA.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Essentiality of reasons
[CDI Normal] In Cockram Construction Ltd v Fulton Hogan Construction Pty Ltd [2018] NSWCA 107, the NSW Court of Appeal overturned a NSW Supreme Court decision in which Ball J declared an adjudicator’s determination void on the basis that the adjudicator rejected a clause in the construction contract as it was unworkable without providing reasons why, and therefore failed to provide reasons as required by section 22(3)(b) of the NSW Act (equivalent to section 88(5)(b) of the Act). The Court of Appeal held that, when determining whether an adjudicator has complied with the statutory requirement to give reasons, asking whether the adjudicator’s reasons were ‘adequate’ or ‘sufficient’ raises a false issue. The Court of Appeal explained that this is because the statutory question is whether the adjudicator gave ‘their reasons for their determination.’ In this case, the adjudicator believed the relevant clause to be unworkable, which the Court of Appeal held was enough and the adjudicator was not required to provide why the adjudicator thought the clause was unworkable. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Annie Street JV Pty Ltd v MCC Pty Ltd & Ors [2016] QSC 268, the applicant submitted that the adjudicator had committed jurisdictional error due to, inter alia, a denial of natural justice. The applicant referred to the decision of Justice Jackson in Sierra Property Qld Pty Ltd v National Construction Management Pty Ltd & Ors [2016] QSC 108, contending that part of the adjudicator’s reasoning was ‘opaque’. Justice Flanagan did not accept this submission, finding that the applicant’s use of the word “opaque” was taken out of context of the judgment in Sierra. His Honour, in clarifying the decision in Sierra, stated that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Properly understood, his Honour found the reasoning to be “opaque” in the sense that no reasons for decision were revealed. This of course is a recognized basis for finding jurisdictional error. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In CH2M Hill Australia Pty Limited & Anor v ABB Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2016] NTSC 42, Kelly J held, in circumstances where the adjudicator failed to address several key issues in their reasons, that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Granted … the defendant’s characterisation of the adjudicator’s determination as being, by necessity, based (in some circumstances and on some issues) on matters of impression and experience, the adjudicator is nevertheless obliged to make a bona fide attempt to perform the functions conferred on him by the Act.  That includes making a bona fide attempt to provide reasons for the determination.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In MTR Corporation (Sydney) NRT Pty Ltd v Thales Australia Ltd [2020] NSWSC 1147, Stevenson J refused an appeal against an adjudication determination on the basis of jurisdictional error. In that case, the respondent advanced that the adjudicator committed four jurisdictional errors, namely, deciding a claim on a basis which was not advanced by either party; failing to perform its duties as required under s 22(2) of the SOP Act  (NSW) (equivalent to s 88(2)(d) of the BIF Act); failing to consider various claims made by the applicant; and failing to decide on whether a claim for set-off had been made out. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In dismissing each ground of appeal by the respondent, Stevenson J held:
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] 1. the adjudicator was entitled to interpret the claimant’s claim as being under a different clause to that expressly stated in its payment claim, where both parties submitted detailed arguments in regard to that clause;
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] 2. upholding the principles in Cockram Construction Pty Ltd v Fulton Hogan Construction Pty Ltd [2018] NSWCA 107, that where the adjudicator did engage in  “a process of evaluation, sufficient to warrant the description of the matters being taken into consideration”, whether such an “evaluation” was correct would not deprive her of jurisdiction;
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] 3. the adjudicator had given proper consideration to the claims by forming the view that quantum was not in dispute for two of the claims, and dismissing the third on the basis that it did not affect the claim; and
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] 4. the adjudicator was entitled to disregard the respondent’s claim for set-off as the reasons in support of this claim were not advanced in its payment schedule.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] In Nova Builders Pty Ltd v Civil & Civic Corporation Pty Ltd [2022] ACTSC 209, the ACT Supreme Court declined to enforce an adjudication determination on the basis that the adjudicator failed to give proper reasons. The Court held that, among other reasons, the adjudicator did not have an "intelligible basis" on which to find that there was a “construction contract” (as defined in the SOP ACT) between the parties.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.9    Section 88(5) – form
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In John Holland Pty Ltd v Schneider Electric Buildings Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 159, the adjudicator accepted a submission that the claimant has served more than one payment claim in respect of the same reference date, and wrote to the parties expressing this conclusion and stating that he considers not to have jurisdiction to decide the matter. Applegarth J held that this letter from the adjudicator complied with the formal requirements of section 26(3) of BCIPA and constituted a decision of a substantive issue.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In 1155 Nepean Highway Pty Ltd v Promax Buildings Pty Ltd [2020] VSCA 253, the Victorian Court of Appeal held that it will not constitute a failure by an adjudicator to discharge their duties under the SOP Act or a failure to conduct an independent assessment of the payment claim, if an adjudicator relies upon the a document from a claimant to make their decision, even if the reasons are expressed in “brief terms”. In referring to the rough and ready nature of the SOP regime, the Court explained that “brevity in explaining [an] assessment does not necessarily equate to a failure to conduct the assessment”.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Acciona Infrastructure Australia Pty Ltd v Holcim (Australia) Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 1330, Hammerschlag J held that an adjudicator cannot avoid its statutory function of considering the merits of the amounts claimed in a payment claim merely because the respondent did not positively dispute the amount claimed or was not in a position to do so.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.10    Characterisation of the adjudicator’s decision
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] A ‘pressure cooker’ environment?
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] It has been said that an adjudicator’s decision is often one which is made under tight timeframes and in a “somewhat pressure cooker environment.” Such was the observation of Bergin J in Shell Refining (Australia) Pty Ltd v A J Mayr Engineering Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 94, made in the context of the adjudicator stating that a matter had not been considered to be of “sufficient relevance to warrant specific comment”, where her Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Adjudicators are under pressure to produce their determinations within tight timeframes and in large and/or complex cases that pressure can be intense. It also seems that the development of the case law in relation to the Act has not made the adjudicator’s task easier. Indeed Gleeson CJ, in refusing a grant of special leave, recently observed that certain provisions of the Act that had to be construed by the New South Wales Court of Appeal “have occasioned some difficulty and some differences of opinion among judges”: Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v JM Hargreaves (NSW) Pty Ltd & Ors; Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v Climatech (Canberra) Pty Ltd & Ors [2006] HCATrans 9 (3 February 2006). Adjudicators are also under the pressure of knowing that their determinations may well be scrutinised by numerous lawyers and judges.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It is perhaps understandable that some adjudicators whose determinations have been the subject of administrative law challenge may regard it as appropriate to utilise a catch-all statement, similar to the one used in the Determination, to fend off an allegation that they have failed to consider a relevant matter. Notwithstanding the somewhat pressure cooker environment in which adjudicators provide their determinations, it seems to me that it would be unhelpful for adjudicators to develop such a practice. It is assumed that adjudicators will comply with their statutory duties under s 22(2) of the Act, which sets out the matters to which they are to give consideration. A consideration of whether they have so complied is made from the content of their determinations rather than from a statement or claim by the adjudicator in the determination that he/she has so complied. I am not persuaded that the defendant is able to obtain any assistance or support for its submissions from this statement by the adjudicator on the first page of the Determination. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similar comments have been made in Queensland in the context of BCIPA about the time pressures facing adjudicators. In Altys Multi-Services Pty Ltd v Grandview Modular Building Systems Pty Ltd [2008] QSC 26, Skoien AJ made the following observation:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In this case only 21 days elapsed from the lodging of the adjudication application to the making of the adjudication. So the adjudicator (who is not a lawyer) was required to act, and did act, very quickly indeed and any criticism which I may make of his decision must be read in the context of the pressure he must have been under to comply with the time constraints.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.11    Judicial review of an adjudicator’s decision
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The prima facie position is that statutory judicial review is unavailable for chapter 3, part 4 of the Act: Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld) (section 18(2)). However, decisions are not precluded from review in certain circumstances, outlined below in 88.12.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302, Applegarth J suggested the following as grounds to review an adjudicator’s decision:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The Courts have recognised that an adjudication decision is void if:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] 1.    it fails to comply with the basic requirements of the Act; or
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] 2.    it is not a bona fide attempt by the adjudicator to exercise the relevant power; or
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] 3.    there has been a substantial denial of natural justice to a party;
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] and only a declaration regarding its invalidity (and perhaps injunctive relief, if necessary) is needed to give it its quietus.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] TAC Pacific predates the decision in Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531. However, TAC Pacific has been cited post-Kirk.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Nevertheless, the above grounds in TAC Pacific must be considered in the context of the decision in Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531, which is discussed below.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.12    Pre-Kirk judicial review
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Initially it was held that an adjudicator’s decision made under BCIPA was a decision made under an enactment amenable to statutory judicial review under the Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In 2007, the Justice and Other Legislation Amendment Act 2007 (Qld) inserted ‘Building and Construction Industry Payments Act 2004, part 3, decision 2’ under Schedule 1, Part 2 of the Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld). The decision to do so was explained in the Explanatory Note:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] This amendment is consistent with the objective of the Building and Construction Industry Payments Act 2004 to create a dispute resolution process whereby adjudicators can quickly resolve payment disputes between parties to a construction contract on an interim basis. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Under this regime, the Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld) does not apply to decisions made under an enactment mentioned in schedule 1, part 2. Further, the Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld) has since been amended to reflect the introduction of the Act and remove the reference to BCIPA.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Judicial power to review decisions
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531, the High Court held that it is beyond the legislative power of a State to exclude the supervisory jurisdiction of a State Supreme Court to grant relief on account of jurisdictional error.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Following the decision in Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531, the Queensland Court of Appeal in Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2011] QCA 22; [2012] 1 Qd R 525, held that state legislature cannot exclude the supervisory jurisdiction of the Supreme Court as to jurisdictional error and judicial decision-making. The Court of Appeal held that the Supreme Court has supervisory jurisdiction over an adjudicator’s decision under the Act.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.13    Non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Queensland, an adjudicator’s decision is not amenable to judicial review for a non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record. However, in other states the position is not as clear. This is explored below in 88.13.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Probuild Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd v Shade Systems Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 770, Emmett AJA held that an adjudicator’s decision was amenable to judicial review on the face of the record in New South Wales. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Emmett AJA’s decision was later overturned by the New South Wales Court of Appeal in Shade Systems Pty Ltd v Probuild Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd (No 2) [2016] NSWCA 379. The Court of Appeal unanimously held that relief is not available to quash an adjudicator’s determination on a ground other than jurisdictional error. Justice of Appeal Basten, with Bathurst CJ, Beazley P, Macfarlan JA and Leeming JA agreeing, concluded that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It is the unanimous view of cases in this Court that both McDougall J in Musico and this Court in Brodyn concluded that relief is not available to quash an adjudicator’s determination on a ground other than jurisdictional error. (Brodyn may have gone further.) The contrary conclusion would undermine the underlying purposes of the Security of Payment Act, which are manifest in the statement of the object of the legislation (s 3), the scheme revealed by the structure of the Act and, to make the same point from a different perspective, the combined effect of the provisions discussed above. The reasoning has been accepted in numerous cases, not only here but in other jurisdictions. No sufficient reason has been put forward to doubt its correctness.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Before Probuild progressed to the High Court, Stanley J of the South Australia Supreme Court held in Maxcon Constructions Pty Ltd v Vadasz & Ors (No 2) [2016] SASC 156, that a non-jurisdictional error on the face of the record committed by an adjudicator was not amenable to judicial review in South Australia, distinguishing Probuild Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd v Shade Systems Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 770 and adopting the Court of Appeal position.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Stanley J dismissed the applicant’s application to set aside the adjudicator’s decision, stating that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …there is no equivalent to s 69 in either the Supreme Court Act 1935 (SA) or in the Rules. While 6SCR 199(2)(b) provides that the court may make an order for judicial review, including an order setting aside the decision of an authority because of absence or excess of jurisdiction, jurisdictional error or error of law on the face of the record, there is no equivalent provision to s 69(4) of the Supreme Court Act 1970 (NSW) which provides that the face of the record includes the reasons expressed by the court or tribunal for its ultimate determination.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Furthermore, his Honour found that the scheme under the SA Act contraindicates the availability of judicial review on the basis of non-jurisdictional error of law, stating that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Additionally, the Act does not create a statutory right of appeal or review for error of law.  On the reasoning in Kirk, that contraindicates the adjudicator’s determination being amenable to judicial review for non-jurisdictional errors of law.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] I also agree with the reasoning of McDougall J in Musico v Davenport and Hodgson JA in Brodyn Pty Ltd v Davenport & Anor,[29] with whom Mason P and Giles JA agreed, that the scheme of the Act contraindicates the availability of judicial review on the basis of non-jurisdictional error of law.  
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The decision in Maxcon Constructions Pty Ltd v Vadasz & Ors (No 2) [2016] SASC 156 was later appealed in Maxcon Constructions Pty Ltd v Vadasz & Ors (No 2) [2017] SASCFC 2, where Justice Ball (with whom Lovell and Hinton JJ agreed) dismissed the appeal against Stanley J’s decision.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similarly, in Samsung C&T Corporation v Loots [2016] WASC 330, Justice Beech held that: 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …An adjudication determination is not susceptible to judicial review for a non-jurisdictional error, such as error of law on the face of the record.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The decision of Ball J in Future Power Developments Pty Ltd v TJ & RF [2017] NSWSC 232 further considered whether an adjudicator had failed to afford natural justice and procedural fairness in making a determination under the NSW Act. His Honour outlined the principles that should be considered when determining if an adjudicator has ‘adequately considered’ a party’s submissions, as follows: 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] reasoning for an administrative decision should not be read “minutely and finely with an eye keenly attuned to the perception of error”: Collector of Customs v Pozzolanic Enterprises Pty Ltd (1993) 43 FCR 280, cited in PPK Willoughby v Eighty Eight Construction Pty Ltd [2014] NSWSC 760 at [59]-[62]. Rather, a broad reading of the reasoning should be adopted in recognition of the fact that administrative decisions are not made by experts accustomed to legal expression: Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Wu Shan Liang (1996) 185 CLR 259;
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] the reasons should be read in their entirety: PPK Willoughby v Eighty Eight Construction Pty Ltd [2014] NSWSC 760 at [62]; Minister for Immigration and Ethnic Affairs v Wu Shan Liang (1996) 185 CLR 259 at 291;
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] consideration of whether the applicant has been afforded procedural fairness must take into account the scheme prescribed by the Act and the fact that it is “inappropriate” for the court to “sift finely through the reasons of the decision maker in an attempt to find slips warranting the court’s intervention”: Clyde Bergemann Senior Thermal Pty Ltd v Varley Power Services Pty Ltd [2011] NSWSC 1039 at [58]; Watpac Constructions (NSW) Pty Ltd v Austin Corp [2010] NSWSC 168 at [142], [146];
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] a failure to give “lengthy, elaborate or detailed” reasons is not indicative of a denial of natural justice: Clyde Bergemann at [66]. In fact, the Act itself deters adjudicators against giving lengthy reasons by prescribing a compressed (10 business day) timeline for the provision of reasons: s 21(3)(a) of the NSW Act. So long as reasoning demonstrates that the adjudicator has turned his or her mind to the issues raised, natural justice, and the parties interest in expediency, will have been served: Clyde Bergemann at [66]-[67];
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] an honest error in identifying or addressing issues for determination is not the same as making a determination without good faith. The former will not make an adjudicator determination invalid while the latter will: Brookhollow Pty Limited v R & R Consultants Pty Limited and Anor [2006] NSWSC 1 at [58].
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Probuild Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd v Shade Systems Pty Ltd [2018] HCA 4, the High Court (by majority of Kiefel CJ, Bell, Keane, Nettle and Gordon JJ) held that non-jurisdictional errors are not amenable to judicial review and that a clear legislative intention can oust the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court for the review of non-jurisdictional error (at [35], [48], [50]):
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The Security of Payment Act evinces a clear legislative intention to exclude the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court to make an order in the nature of certiorari to quash an adjudicator's determination for non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record. …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] … To permit potentially costly and time-consuming judicial review proceedings to be brought on the basis of error of law on the face of the record, regardless of whether an adjudicator had exceeded the limits of their statutory functions and powers, would frustrate the operation and evident purposes of the statutory scheme. The jurisdiction to make an order in the nature of certiorari to quash an adjudicator's determination for error of law on the face of the record has been excluded. …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] … No inference can be drawn from the fact that the Security of Payment Act does not purport to exclude review for jurisdictional error. As Kirk shows, exclusion of that jurisdiction would be beyond the power of the Parliament of New South Wales70. That the Parliament has not attempted to legislate beyond power says nothing about whether the Security of Payment Act evinces a clear intention to exclude the review jurisdiction of the Supreme Court to the extent that the Parliament had power to do so: relevantly, to prevent an order in the nature of certiorari being made on the basis of non-jurisdictional error of law.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Empire Global Pty Ltd v SA Expert Designs Pty Ltd [2019] ACTSC 244, Murrell CJ held that it would be inconsistent with the objectives of the ACT Act (to provide a “rough and ready” determination) if, on judicial review, the Court was to review mixed questions of law to determine the proper construction of the contract. In this case, the court considered whether an Adjudicator had erred in finding that additional works had been performed “under” the subcontract. The Court cited the decision of Canberra Drilling Rigs Pty Ltd v Haides Pty Ltd [2019] ACTCA 15 where it was held that “the language of s 15(1) means that all issues in relation to the connection between the work done and the relevant contract may form part of the claim rather than being preconditions to it.”
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.14    Grounds of review
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In HM Hire Pty Ltd v National Plant and Equipment Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 274; [2014] 2 Qd R 44, Applegarth J said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Adjudications which do not comply with the essential statutory requirements may be declared void and the court may, when non-compliance has been demonstrated, make declarations and grant injunctions to prevent the adjudication being acted on. The court exercises a supervisory jurisdiction over adjudication decisions under the Act. The court’s supervisory jurisdiction is not affected by s 18(2) of the Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld). The court retains its supervisory jurisdiction to grant relief in the nature of certiorari. Certiorari may be granted when there has been jurisdictional error or where the relevant decision has been obtained through fraud. Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd establishes that an adjudication decision may be challenged in this court if it is established the adjudicator has not acted in “good faith,” failed to accord natural justice or otherwise fallen into jurisdictional error.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In addition to an adjudication decision being open to challenge in the exercise of the court’s supervisory jurisdiction on the grounds of jurisdictional error or being obtained through the fraud of a party, a party who contends that an adjudication decision was obtained by fraud may seek to have the adjudication decision (and any judgment entered in reliance upon it) set aside in the exercise of the court’s equitable jurisdiction.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Other forms of unlawful conduct by a party in the course of making a payment claim, or in proceedings for an adjudication under the Act, may result in the granting of relief under a statutory remedy, for example remedial orders under the Australian Consumer Law for misleading and deceptive conduct or unconscionable conduct. One form of relief might be to set aside the adjudication decision if it was found to be the result of conduct that contravened provisions which prohibit misleading and deceptive conduct or unconscionable conduct.

[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Jurisdictional facts
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The availability of review for jurisdictional error is sometimes framed in terms of the presence or absence of ‘jurisdictional facts’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The nature of jurisdictional facts was considered by McDougall J in Chase Oyster Bar Pty Ltd v Hamo Industries Pty Ltd [2010] NSWCA 190; (2010) 78 NSWLR 393, where his Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] As Gleeson CJ and McHugh J observed in Abebe v The Commonwealth of Australia (1999) 197 CLR 510 at 523–524 [24], “[j]urisdiction is the authority to decide”.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] A “jurisdictional fact” is, in general terms, “a criterion the satisfaction of which enlivens the exercise of the statutory power or discretion in question” (Gedeon v Commissioner of the New South Wales Crime Commission (2008) 236 CLR 120 at 139 [43]).
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Spigelman CJ pointed out in Timbarra Protection Coalition Inc v Ross Mining NL (1999) 46 NSWLR 55 at 63–64 [37] that “[t]he parliament can make any fact a jurisdictional fact, in the relevant sense: that it must exist in fact (objectivity) and that the legislature intends that the absence or presence of the fact will invalidate action under the statute (essentiality)”. As his Honour said at 64[38], those two features “are two inter-related elements in the determination of whether a factual reference in a statutory formulation is a jurisdictional fact in the relevant sense”. The interrelationship arose because essentiality may often suggest objectivity.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Whether something is a jurisdictional fact is ascertained by a process of construction, undertaken in the usual way. The court will have regard to the full statutory context and to the object that the legislation seeks to achieve. One asks, in essence, whether the legislature intended that the presence or absence of the factual condition should invalidate an attempted exercise of power: Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority (1998) 194 CLR 355 at 390–391 [93] (McHugh, Gummow, Kirby and Hayne JJ).
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] A jurisdictional fact may be the existence or non-existence of a specified state of affairs. For simplicity, I will talk only in terms of a jurisdictional fact as the existence of a specified state of affairs. The legislature may specify that the existence of that state of affairs – the jurisdictional fact – is essential to the exercise of statutory power. Or it may go further, and specify as well that the donee of the power is authorised to decide, authoritatively, the existence of the jurisdictional fact.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In the former case, if the exercise of power is challenged on the basis that the jurisdictional fact does not exist, the court must itself inquire into the existence of that fact. It may grant relief against the exercise of jurisdiction if it finds that the jurisdictional fact did not exist.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In the latter case, the court inquires into the primary decision-maker’s decision that the jurisdictional fact exists, but does not itself inquire into the existence of that jurisdictional fact.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Saville v Hallmarc Construction Pty Ltd [2015] VSCA 318, the Victorian Court of Appeal considered the treatment of jurisdictional facts in Chase Oyster Bar. Warren CJ and Tate JA (Kaye JA agreeing) rejected a submission on a narrow interpretation of the decision in Chase Oyster Bar that was to the effect that a decision of an adjudicator is reviewable only in respect of events or circumstances ascertainable mechanically or by means of straightforward calculation. Their Honours said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Jurisdictional facts are thus not confined to events or circumstances which can be ascertained mechanically or by means of a straightforward calculation. Depending upon the statute that confers jurisdiction, jurisdictional facts may require evaluation and assessment.  
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In the context of the Act, the various categories which both have or have not been considered jurisdictional facts, are considered below, under the class of jurisdictional error of ‘proceeding in the absence of a jurisdictional fact’.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] A ‘wrongly decided’ jurisdictional fact
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] An adjudicator cannot ‘endow himself with jurisdiction by wrongly deciding a “jurisdictional fact”’: see McNab NQ Pty Ltd v Walkrete Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 128, [12].
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Fifty Property Investments Pty Ltd v O’Mara (2007) 23 BCL 35; [2006] NSWSC 428, Brereton J held that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] While a decision maker has to decide whether or not facts which are essential preconditions of jurisdiction exist, he or she can not give himself or herself additional jurisdiction by making a wrong decision on the collateral question as to the existence of such facts.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] What will constitute jurisdictional error?
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531, the High Court said that ‘[i]t is neither necessary, nor possible, to attempt to mark the metes and bounds of jurisdictional error’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, the Court in Kirk provided the following summary of the ambit of jurisdictional error as considered earlier in Craig v South Australia [1995] HCA 58; (1995) 184 CLR 163:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] First, the Court stated, as a general description of what is jurisdictional error by an inferior court, that an inferior court falls into jurisdictional error "if it mistakenly asserts or denies the existence of jurisdiction or if it misapprehends or disregards the nature or limits of its functions or powers in a case where it correctly recognises that jurisdiction does exist" (emphasis added).  Secondly, the Court pointed out that jurisdictional error "is at its most obvious where the inferior court purports to act wholly or partly outside the general area of its jurisdiction in the sense of entertaining a matter or making a decision or order of a kind which wholly or partly lies outside the theoretical limits of its functions and powers" (emphasis added).  (The reference to "theoretical limits" should not distract attention from the need to focus upon the limits of the body's functions and powers.  Those limits are real and are to be identified from the relevant statute establishing the body and regulating its work.)  Thirdly, the Court amplified what was said about an inferior court acting beyond jurisdiction by entertaining a matter outside the limits of the inferior court's functions or powers by giving three examples: (a) the absence of a jurisdictional fact; (b) disregard of a matter that the relevant statute requires be taken to account as a condition of jurisdiction (or the converse case of taking account of a matter required to be ignored); and (c) misconstruction of the relevant statute thereby misconceiving the nature of the function which the inferior court is performing or the extent of its powers in the circumstances of the particular case. The Court said of this last example that "the line between jurisdictional error and mere error in the exercise of jurisdiction may be particularly difficult to discern" and gave as examples of such difficulties R v Dunphy; Ex parte Maynes, R v Gray; Ex parte Marsh and Public Service Association (SA) v Federated Clerks' Union.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, following this passage, the Court was careful to note that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] it is important to recognise that the reasoning in Craig that has just been summarised is not to be seen as providing a rigid taxonomy of jurisdictional error…They are not to be taken as marking the boundaries of the relevant field.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Chase Oyster Bar Pty Ltd v Hamo Industries Pty Ltd [2010] NSWCA 190; (2010) 78 NSWLR 393, McDougall J considered the High Court’s three categories of jurisdictional error in Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531. McDougall J interpreted these three categories as follows:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (1) The mistaken denial or assertion of jurisdiction, or (in a case where jurisdiction does exist), misapprehension or disregard of the nature of or limits on functions and powers; 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (2) Entertaining a matter or making a decision of a kind that lies, wholly or partly, outside the limits on functions and powers, as identified from the relevant statutory context; 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (3) Proceeding in the absence of a jurisdictional fact; disregarding something that the relevant statute requires to be considered as a condition of jurisdiction, or considering something required to be ignored; and misconstruction of the statute leading to misconception of functions. (Of this last example, it was said in Craig (at 178) that “the line between jurisdictional error and mere error in the exercise of jurisdiction may be particularly difficult to discern.”) 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In Equa Building Services Pty Ltd (Equa) v A&H Floors 2 Doors Australia Pty Ltd (AH) [2022] NSWSC 152, Hammerschlag J quashed an adjudication determination on the basis that the adjudicator had fallen into jurisdictional error by incorrectly determining that service of the payment claim in issue was valid. The adjudicator determined that service of the payment claim by email to a recipient who the claimant believed to be the representative of the respondent was valid. Hammerschlag J determined that the service of the payment claim was invalid on the basis that the addressee was not an employee of the respondent and was not someone that the respondent had nominated as a representative. In making this determination, Hammerschlag J held that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Service of a document by email under section 31(d) of the NSW SOP Act (no equivalent provision in the BIF Act) requires the person for the service of the document to have nominated the email address for the service;
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] an entitlement on the part of the claimant to believe that the recipient was a representative of the respondent does not make the recipient a representative in the absence of the recipient being specified by the respondent; and
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] a company email address does not indicate the particular status or position of the addressee.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In the context of BCIPA, in BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 346, referring to the decision in Craig, Applegarth J said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …A jurisdictional error may be made by the adjudicator in proceeding to determine an adjudication for a number of reasons.  The determination may be affected by jurisdictional error on one of the grounds discussed in Craig v South Australia. For example, the adjudicator may disregard something which the relevant statute requires to be considered as a condition of jurisdiction or otherwise fall into jurisdictional error by determining something which the adjudicator lacks authority to determine.  A distinction may be made between matters which are “an essential preliminary to the decision-making process” and “matters which can arise during the course of the decision-making process itself”. Where matters are entrusted to an adjudicator to decide, an error of law made in the course of the decision-making process is not, of itself, a jurisdictional error. An error in construing the terms of the contract under which an entitlement is claimed is not, of itself, a jurisdictional error. The position is otherwise where the error causes the adjudicator to make one or more of the jurisdictional errors identified in the leading authorities which consider the issue of “jurisdictional error” in the context of the Act and comparable legislation in other Australian jurisdictions.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similarly, in McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd (Administrators appointed) (Receivers and Managers appointed) & Ors [2013] QSC 223, Boddice J set out the grounds of review of an adjudicator’s decision as follows:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The adjudication process is subject to review by this court, which exercises a supervisory jurisdiction with respect to any adjudication decision found to be infected by jurisdictional error. The concept of jurisdictional error includes an erroneous denial or assertion of jurisdiction, misapprehension or disregard of the nature or limits on the jurisdiction, considering a matter or making a decision that lies wholly or partly outside those limitations and proceeding in the absence of a jurisdictional fact or in disregard of a requirement of the relevant statute.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In the context of the Payments Act, jurisdictional error will arise if the statutory requirements of the Act are not satisfied, including there being in existence a construction contract, service of a payment claim, the making of an adjudication application, the reference of that application to an eligible adjudicator and the determination of that application by that adjudicator.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd v ASAP Plasterers Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 145, where Atkinson J outlined the grounds for jurisdictional error (on the basis of Central Interior) in the following manner:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] White JA in Northbuild at [79]-[80] gave examples of ways in which adjudication might be infected by jurisdictional error:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (1)     where the adjudicator departed from the only matters the adjudicator might consider in reaching a decision, being the provisions of BCIPA, the terms of the construction contract, the payment claim and response and all submissions properly made;
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (2)    where the adjudicator, in departing from that list, considered, for example, what the adjudicator regarded as “a fair thing”;
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (3)    where the adjudicator truly disregarded a claimant’s submissions.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In Demex Pty Ltd v McNab Building Services Pty Ltd [2023] NSWCA 261, the NSW Court of Appeal confirmed that a determination will only be set aside for jurisdictional error, due to an adjudicator’s failure to afford the parties’ procedural fairness if that failure causes substantial injustice to the parties.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The respondent challenged an adjudication determination, alleging that the adjudicator had reached a conclusion, on a basis which was not contended for by either party. 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The contract stipulated that payment for excavated material must be in accordance with the amount of material, measured in cubic metres. In the payment claim, the claim for excavated material was supported by dockets, which measured the material in tonnes. The claimant converted the figures from the dockets to cubic metres and claimed for those amounts (without providing the conversion rate it used to do so). 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The adjudicator performed a reverse calculation, to ascertain the conversion rate used by the claimant to convert the material from tonnes to cubic metres. The adjudicator used that rate to determine the amount payable for excavated material.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The Court of Appeal found that the respondent was correct in its assertion that the rate used by the adjudicator was not put forward by either party. However, the Court of Appeal held that the adjudicator had not fallen into jurisdictional error. This was because:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It was clear, that the claimant had used some form of rate to convert the weight amounts in the dockets, to volume. Therefore, the rate was implicit in the payment claim, and it was not the case, that the adjudicator had reached a conclusion on a basis not contended for by either party.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The implicit existence of the conversion rate in the payment claim meant that the respondent could have made submissions on the claimant’s conversion rate but did not. Therefore, there was no substantial injustice to the respondent, as they had an opportunity to make submissions on the conversion rate.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] On a fair reading of the adjudicator’s reasons, he only employed his industry knowledge in a confirmatory way to accept the conversion rate. Further, because adjudicator’s may be expected to bring their experience and expertise in making their determination, the reliance of the adjudicator on his industry experience further demonstrated that his decision did not give rise to any unfairness or that he was acting outside of his jurisdiction.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Paladin Projects Pty Ltd v Visie Three Pty Ltd & Ors [2024] QSC 230, Paladin applied for judicial review of the adjudication determination, arguing that the adjudicator had considered ‘new reasons’ given in the adjudication response. Williams J held that Paladin failed to establish jurisdictional error on the basis that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The determination of whether new reasons were introduced in the adjudication response requires assessing whether the narrative provided aligns with the scope of the payment schedule's shorthand response. There is no prohibited on articulating the existing reasons for withholding payment identified in the payment schedule.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Even if there were new reasons in the adjudication response, jurisdictional error arises only if the adjudicator actively considered those reasons. The adjudicator must engage in ‘an active intellectual process and a process of evaluation sufficient to warrant the description. meaningful intellectual evaluation.’ In this case, the adjudicator only briefly referenced the respondent’s submissions on the variation in dispute without substantive engagement, failing to meet the required level of consideration under the Act.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Absence of jurisdiction
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386, the Queensland Court of Appeal held that the adjudicator had based his decision on a document which was not a payment schedule, and did not consider the later document, which was the payment schedule. In holding that jurisdictional error had been committed, the Court of Appeal said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The adjudicator lacked authority to decide an adjudication application, where a payment schedule existed and was relied on by the respondent to the application, other than by reference to that payment schedule. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In ACP Properties (Townsville) Pty Ltd v Rodrigues Construction Group Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 45, Bradley J declared an adjudication determination void on the basis that the adjudicator failed to consider the respondent’s adjudication response after incorrectly determining that the respondent’s payment schedule was invalid. In coming to this decision, Bradley J upheld the principle in Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2013] QCA 386 at [59] (Muir JA), in that the adjudicator committed a jurisdictional error by deciding an adjudication application where a payment schedule existed and was relied on by the respondent to the application.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also South East Civil and Drainage Contractors Pty Ltd v AMGW Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 45; [2013] 2 Qd R 189.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Contract has been terminated. McNab NQ Pty Ltd v Walkrete Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 128. A payment claim was made after the construction contract had been terminated. The Court held that the adjudicator had erred in finding that termination was not valid and, in so doing, had made the decision in the absence of jurisdiction.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Licensing under the QBCC Act. In Ooralea Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor [2013] QSC 254 the claimant did not hold a licence to carry out the relevant work under the-then QBSA Act which was the subject of a payment claim. The adjudicator awarded the claimant $969,836.23 (including GST). The Court held that the work the subject of the payment claim was ‘building work’ within the meaning of the-then QBSA Act. Pursuant to section 42(3) of the-then QBSA Act, the Court held that the adjudicator did not have jurisdiction to make an award under the payment claim.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Licensing under the Professional Engineers Act and Electrical Safety Act. Agripower Australia Ltd v Queensland Engineering & Electrical Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 268. The claimant was held to be in breach of both section 56 of the Electrical Safety Act 2002 (Qld) and section 115(1) of the Professional Engineers Act 2002 (Qld). Douglas J followed the decision in Cant Contracting Pty Ltd v Casella [2007] 2 Qd R 13; [2006] QCA 538, setting aside the decision of the adjudicator for jurisdictional error.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Awarding an amount at adjudication which was not claimed in the payment claim. In Samsung C&T Corporation v Loots [2016] WASC 330, an adjudicator awarded the claimant $32.4 million on the grounds that it had been previously certified but wrongly set-off by the respondent. However, Beech J held that the $32.4 million had not been claimed by the claimant in its payment claim. Therefore, his Honour held that the adjudicator had committed jurisdictional error and set the decision aside. 
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Misapprehend or disregard the nature or limits of functions or powers
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] According with the decision in Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531 (above), an adjudicator may fall into jurisdictional error if they misapprehend or disregard the nature or limits of their functions or powers.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 346, [56] (Applegarth J).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The adjudicator failed to perform the minimum content of the statutory task entrusted to him/her In Richard Crookes Construction Pty Ltd v CES Projects (Aust) Pty Ltd (No 2) [2016] NSWSC 1229, McDougall J held that the adjudicator did not carry out the minimum content of the statutory task entrusted to him. His Honour said: 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Adapting what Hodgson JA said in Plaza West at [58] (see at [21] above), it appears that the Adjudicator, having decided that the contractor’s submissions should be disregarded, simply adopted the subcontractor’s valuation of its claim. In doing so, he failed to perform the task required: to determine the amount of the progress payment (if any) to be paid having regard to the matters set out in s 22(2) of the Security of Payment Act. Alternatively, adapting what Vikery J said in SCC Plenty at [100], the Adjudicator’s reasons do not demonstrate any rational assessment of value. They do not disclose any process of reasoning based on the only factual material that the Adjudicator found (even with the flaws that he pointed out) to be persuasive, leading to the conclusion that this aspect of the payment claim had been proved.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Payment for amounts after the reference date. BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 346. The adjudicator awarded amounts for ‘termination costs’. These ‘termination costs’ arose after the reference date identified by the adjudicator of 1 January 2012. The adjudicator did not identify any other contractual basis for the amounts. The Court held that the adjudicator had erred in concluding that ‘simply because the costs had been incurred before the reference date’, the entitlement to termination costs ‘had crystalised’. The Court held the adjudicator to have committed jurisdictional error.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Interpretation of a provision of a contract
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Under section 88(2)(b) of the Act, an adjudicator is to interpret the provision of the construction contract. As such, an error in the identification or interpretation of a term of the construction contract is not ordinarily a jurisdictional error for which the adjudicator’s decision may be challenged: Caltex Refineries (Qld) Pty Ltd & Anor v Allstate Access (Australia) Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 223, [36] (Philip McMurdo J). 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] As a matter of general principle, ‘[a]n adjudicator who misconstrues or misapplies a relevant contractual provision and, as a result, does not correctly decide the amount of the progress payment, if any, to be paid to the claimant does not, for that reason alone, make a jurisdictional error’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Laing O’Rourke Australia Construction Pty Ltd v Samsung C&T Corporation [2016] WASCA 130, the Court of Appeal found that the trial judge was erroneous in his characterisation of the nature of the errors he found that the Adjudicator had made. This was a sufficient reason to uphold the appeal against the decision to set aside the adjudication determination. On the distinction between jurisdictional and non-jurisdictional error, Martin CJ said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] the Adjudicator made error with respect to the identification of the particular term of the contract which governed the dispute which he was required to determine, such an error cannot be properly characterised as a failure to determine the dispute by reference to the terms of the contract; rather, such an error can only be characterised as an error with respect to the proper construction or application of the contract. The utilisation of different descriptive terminology cannot alter the fundamental character of the error which, if it was made, was an error of a kind which falls clearly and unmistakably within the jurisdiction conferred upon an adjudicator by the Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] As the Court said in John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302: 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] [t]he statutory scheme may permit an adjudicator to make unreviewable errors of law in quickly deciding complex legal issues in adjudications of the present kind after considering the parties’ submissions.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similarly, in Laing O’Rourke Australia Construction Pty Ltd v Samsung C&T Corporation [2016] WASCA 130, Martin CJ relevantly said ‘an error of this kind is precisely the kind of departure from contractual and legal precision which the legislature has accepted as part of the 'trade-off' for speed and efficiency.’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, the interpretation of a provision of a contract may constitute jurisdictional error where the interpretation ‘is so unreasonable that no reasonable person could have arrived at it’, or where the interpretation indicates ‘a failure to apply a rational mind to the resolution of the question’: see Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 168, [65] (Byrne SJA).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Jurisdictional error may also arise where ‘it appears that an adjudicator is not meaning to apply the contract, as he or she interprets it, but is instead allowing the claim upon some other basis, the position is different, because the adjudicator is thereby misunderstanding the scope of the adjudicator’s jurisdiction’: Northbuild Construction Sunshine Coast Pty Ltd v Beyfield Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 80, [30] (Philip McMurdo J). See also BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 346.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Diona Pty Ltd v Downer EDI Works Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 480, Stevenson J was required to consider whether an adjudicator failed to address their statutory function, contrary to section 21(4) of the NSW Act (equivalent to section 88(2)(b) BIFSOPA) by failing to properly consider the construction contract. In dismissing the application, Stevenson J held that in circumstances where the adjudicator acknowledged the requirement to consider the contract but did not reference the particular clause submitted by the applicant, it could not be inferred that he did not consider the provision. As a result, while the adjudicator may have come to an incorrect decision about the entitlement under the construction contract, it could not, without more, be a basis to set aside the determination.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Acciona Agua Australia Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 133, Justice Bond held that a mere “logical incongruity” (where the adjudicator initially found he was obliged to ignore an argument for set off but later went on to accept the argument for set off) did not amount to jurisdictional error because the adjudicator nonetheless sought to apply the construction contract consistent with the obligation imposed by section 88(2)(b) of BIFSOPA. Ball J was satisfied that the adjudicator’s failure to “appreciate the logical incongruity” did not amount to jurisdictional error, in circumstances where the other grounds for declaring the decision void for jurisdictional error were made out.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A non-jurisdictional error of interpretation. Caltex Refineries (Qld) Pty Ltd & Anor v Allstate Access (Australia) Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 223. The contract in question was a hire contract for scaffolding equipment. The adjudicator construed a term of Schedule 1 to the contract as providing that the hiree would pay the replacement cost of equipment damage by the hiree. This was an erroneous interpretation on the basis that the particular clause was not a promise by the hiree to pay for defects it causes, but rather, an exception to the hirer’s obligation to replace all defective scaffolding equipment at its own cost. However, as the error was one of interpretation, it was not a jurisdictional error.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A non-jurisdictional interpretation of EOT. Northbuild Construction Sunshine Coast Pty Ltd v Beyfield Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 80. The adjudicator adopted an interpretation of the contract that the claimant had an entitlement to delay costs, notwithstanding that no extension of time claim had been submitted. Properly construed, the extension of time claim under the contract was a precondition to the entitlement to delay costs. The Court held that, while the adjudicator had adopted an erroneous interpretation, the adjudicator was meaning to apply the terms of the contract. It was an error within jurisdiction.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A non-jurisdictional error of calculation. Ooralea Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor [2013] QSC 254. The adjudicator misinterpreted the terms of the contract relating to work claimable at each reference date and, in so doing, erred in calculating the amount which would have been payable. The Court held that this error was not a jurisdictional error.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Failing to apply the terms of the contract. Ball Construction Pty Ltd v Conart Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 124. The adjudicator determined an entitlement to an adjustment to the contract sum for delay without reference to the defined grounds under the contract for claiming an extension of time. The Court held that the adjudicator had failed to assess the claim in accordance with the contract, and had committed jurisdictional error.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Failing to apply the terms of the contract. BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 394. The adjudicator adopted an erroneous interpretation of clause 26.3 of the contract, which provided that any payment for latent conditions was dependent upon the determination of an approved expert, and awarded a payment to the claimant for a claim for latent conditions. The Court of Appeal considered it ‘arguable’ in being a jurisdictional error, but found if unnecessary to decide this point.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Allowing a claim inconsistent with the adjudicator’s own interpretation of the contract. Northbuild Construction Sunshine Coast Pty Ltd v Beyfield Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 80. The adjudicator allowed two variation claims (VO28 and VO29) in circumstances where the adjudicator did not find that the relevant clause on variations (clause 20) had been satisfied, nor did the adjudicator identify a separate contractual basis for the entitlement to these amounts. The Court held that this constituted jurisdictional error.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Adjudicator’s failure to determine the time of service would not constitue a jurisdicational error. In Claire Rewais and Osama Rewais t/as McVitty Grove v BPB Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 1271, McGrath J considered whether an adjudication determination was invalid because the adjudication application was served before the service of the Payment Schedule. 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The timeframes in this case provide important context to understanding the Adjudicator’s decision:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] 1. on 24 April 2024, the Claimant emailed the Respondent its Payment Claim
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] 2. on 22 May 2024, the Claimant alerted the Respondent that it failed to provide a payment schedule within time, and that it intended to proceed to adjudication (as is required under section 17(2) of the NSW SOP Act); and
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] 3. on 11 June 2024, the Respondent received an email from the Claimant which contained copies of the abovementioned 24 April 2024 emails 22 May 2024 letter
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The Claimant commenced adjudication on 13 June 2024, and the Respondent served its payment schedule on 18 June 2024. The Respondent sought to set aside the adjudication determination because the payment claim, notice of intention to seek adjudication application were not served until 11 June 2024. This was because the Respondent was not aware of the 24 April 2024 and 22 May 2024 correspondences until it was sent to the Respondent in the 11 June 2024 email.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The Court did agree that the payment claim and the notice were not served until 11 June 2024, as they had indeed not come to the attention of the respondent. However, the Court held that this mistake by the adjudicator was not a jurisdictional error and upheld the determination.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Disregard of a matter required to be taken into account or taking into account of a matter required to be ignored
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In the context of the Act, this ground of jurisdictional error is given force by the operation of section 88(2) and (3) of the Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] This ground of possible jurisdictional error relates both to:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] failing to take into account a matter required to be taken into account; and
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] taking into account of a matter required to be ignored.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Both of these arose in Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386 where the Court of Appeal held that the adjudicator had based his decision on a document which was not a payment schedule, and did not consider the later document, which was the payment schedule. Adopting the language of Craig v South Australia [1995] HCA 58; (1995) 184 CLR 163, the Court of Appeal held that the adjudicator had committed jurisdictional error in so doing.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] A third type of circumstance of an adjudicator failing to consider a document not provided, was considered in QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd and Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292, where Peter Lyons J said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Further, the duty which the second respondent was required to perform under s 26(2) was to consider the payment claim, the payment schedule, and all submissions, including relevant documentation, provided by the parties in support of those documents.  It is not necessarily a breach of that duty to fail to consider a document not provided to him.  It may perhaps be a breach of that duty to fail to appreciate, from documents provided to an adjudicator, that a document has not been provided, without which the payment claim cannot sensibly be determined; but that is not so in the present case.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Taking into account a matter outside of section 26(2) BCIPA (section 88(2) of the Act). Unifor Australia Pty Ltd v Katrd Pty Ltd atf Morshan Unit Trust t/as Beyond Completion Projects [2012] QSC 252. A payment claim in the form of a tax invoice was supported by a spreadsheet, setting out various costings. By error and unknown to both parties, a wrong version of this spreadsheet – a later version – was included in the adjudication application. Because this spreadsheet was a later version, it did not align with the amounts under the payment claim, and contained higher amounts. The adjudicator awarded the amount of the payment claim as ‘a fair and reasonable value of the additional costs’. The Court held that the adjudicator’s reasons for reaching a valuation were not referable to the contract or any agreement between the parties. Rather, the Court held that the adjudicator’s decision was on the basis of an extraneous consideration outside of section 26(2) BCIPA. The Court held that the adjudicator had committed jurisdictional error ‘by doing something which he lacked authority to so’, namely considering a matter not prescribed by section 26(2) BCIPA and undertaking a valuation not in accordance with section 14 BCIPA (equivalent to section 72 of the Act).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Failing to consider a document not provided to the adjudicator. QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd and Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292. The parties to an adjudication had provided the adjudicator with a covering letter for a variation proposal, but not the variation proposal itself. It was submitted that the adjudicator’s failure to note the absence of the document and request a copy was a failure to perform the functions under section 26(2) of BCIPA. The Court was not satisfied that the adjudicator failed to notice the absence of the variation proposal and was not prepared to draw an inference to that effect.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A spreadsheet not contained in the payment schedule. Syntech Resources Pty Ltd v Peter Campbell Earthmoving (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 293. Two of the respondent’s reasons for withholding payment in the payment schedule was that there should be a deduction of $142,134.25 for the wet hire payments and that it has taken a review of all previous payments and has determined that the claimant has been overpaid to the amount of $127,618.40. In its adjudication response, the respondent included spreadsheets with calculations supporting these reasons. The adjudicator determined that these spreadsheets could not be relied on by the respondent, referring to natural justice considerations. One submission by the respondent was that the adjudicator was required to consider the spreadsheets by reason of section 26(2) BCIPA. The Court held that the adjudicator’s failure to consider the spreadsheets was a breach of an essential requirement and jurisdictional error.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Considering the merits of competing claims not contained in the payment claim the subject of the adjudication. In Samsung C&T Corporation v Loots [2016] WASC 330, Beech J held that an adjudicator had exceeded his statutory function by directing his attention to the merits of competing claims, counterclaims and set-offs made in, or in response to, payment claims other than the payment claim the subject of the adjudication application. His Honour therefore held that the adjudicator had committed jurisdictional error and set his determination aside.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Failing to consider the merits of any counterclaim or set off in submissions. In Cooper & Oxley Builders Pty Ltd v Steensma [2016] WASC 386 Justice Le Miere held that the adjudicator failed to take into account a matter which section 32(1)(a)(ii) of the WA Act (equivalent of section 88(2)(d) of the Act), requires he take into account in determining a payment dispute. In concluding that the adjudicator misconceived the function which he was performing and the extent of his powers, Le Miere J said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] A respondent to an application for adjudication may use its counterclaim or set off as a defence to the claim made against it. The adjudicator is required to take into account the respondent’s response, including the merits of any counterclaim or set off, in reaching his determination.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] His Honour found that the adjudicator did not take into account the applicant’s set off defence raised in its response. Consequently, Le Miere J held that a writ of certiorari should be issued to quash the first and second determination the subject of judicial review in the application. 
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] A failure to consider all submissions properly made
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In the context of the NSW Act, the failure to consider all submissions ‘duly made’ has been held to constitute ‘jurisdictional error, or a failure of a basic and essential requirement, resulting in invalidity of an adjudication’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Queensland, a number of decisions have considered the nature or degree of the failure as relevant in determining whether the failure constitutes jurisdictional error: see Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373, [59] (Ann Lyons J); QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd and Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292, [121], [123] (Peter Lyons J).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads and Traffic Authority of New South Wales & Ors [2007] NSWCA 19, Hodgson JA (Beazley JA agreeing) said that a mere failure to consider a matter required to be taken into account does not, by itself, invalidate the decision. At [55]:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The relevant requirement of s.22(2) is that the adjudicator consider the provisions of the Act, the provisions of the contract and submissions duly made. If an adjudicator does consider the provisions of the Act and the contract which he or she believes to be relevant, and considers those of the submissions that he or she believes to have been duly made, I do not think an accidental or erroneous omission to consider a particular provision of the Act or a particular provision of the contract, or a particular submission, could either wholly invalidate a determination, or invalidate it as regards any part affected by the omission. One could express this by saying that such an accidental or erroneous omission does not amount to a failure to comply with s.22(2), so long as the specified classes of considerations are addressed; or alternatively, if one takes the view that s.22(2) does require consideration of each and every relevant provision of the Act and the contract and each and every submission duly made, the intention of the legislature cannot have been that this kind of mistake should invalidate the determination. In a case where there were 1,000 submissions duly made, an accidental failure to consider one of them could not reasonably be considered as invalidating a whole determination; and there is no basis for partial invalidation of a determination, that is, invalidation only of that part affected by the omitted submission.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] An accidental or erroneous omission is not a failure to comply with section 22(2) of the NSW Act (section 88(2) of the Act) ‘so long as the specified classes of considerations are addressed’. This qualification was noted by Ann Lyons J in Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373, at [55]-[56] in the context of BCIPA.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd and Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292, [114] (Peter Lyons J); Halkat Electrical Contractors Pty Ltd v Holmwood Holdings Pty Ltd [2007] NSWCA 32, [26]-[27] (Giles JA) in relation to this above passage from John Holland.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd and Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292, Peter Lyons J considered the case of non-compliance with section 26(2) of BCIPA, where his Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The requirement to consider matters found in s 26(2) extends to the payment claim, the payment schedule, and, in each case, “all submissions, including relevant documentation, that have been properly made” in support of the payment claim or payment schedule.  The language of the section makes it difficult to conclude that a failure to consider a particular submission is not a breach of the section.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] However, a question then arises as to whether a particular breach would invalidate an adjudication decision.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In Brodyn Pty Ltd v Davenport, Hodgson JA (with whom the other members of the Court agreed) held that for a document purporting to be an adjudicator’s determination to have the strong legal effect for which the New South Wales equivalent of the BCIP Act provided, it must satisfy conditions laid down by the Act as essential for such a determination.  His Honour then identified some “basic and essential requirements” (recognising that his list might not be exhaustive); and noted that there were more detailed requirements, including those found in the equivalent of s 26(2).  His Honour then said that the legislature did not intend that exact compliance with all the more detailed requirements was essential to the existence of a valid adjudication decision, applying Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority.  Project Blue Sky endorsed the approach taken in Tasker v Fullwood which explicitly referred to the extent of the failure to observe a statutory requirement as being relevant in determining the validity of an act purportedly taken under the statute.  In my view, this approach is consistent with the acceptance in oral submissions by Senior Counsel for MCJV that questions of fact and degree are involved.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The submissions made in paragraphs 5.24.21-5.24.23 of the adjudication response are plainly of considerable significance.  Depending upon the construction of cl 32.3(c), they might amount to a complete defence to what is a very substantial claim. Notwithstanding the limited time specified in the BCIP Act for an adjudicator to determine an adjudication application, it seems to me that the failure to consider submissions which are of such significance constitutes a failure by the second respondent to comply with an essential requirement of the BCIP Act for a valid decision.  On that basis, I consider that the adjudication decision fails to comply with one of the “basic and essential requirements” of the BCIP Act, and for that reason is not a valid adjudication decision.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Annie Street JV Pty Ltd v MCC Pty Ltd & Ors [2016] QSC 268, Justice Flanagan distinguished QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd and Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292 in circumstances where a contractual provision not considered by the adjudicator was not raised in a payment schedule as a reason for withholding payment. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] His Honour held that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] By reference to s 24(4), the adjudicator decided that he would not have regard to the time bar issue. It was not jurisdictional error for the adjudicator, being aware of the time bar issue, not to consider it because of his view of the operation of s 24(4). If the adjudicator erred in the application of s 24(4), this is an error committed within jurisdiction. This is because the adjudicator has not failed to consider the time bar issue but rather refused to consider the issue because of the operation of s 24(4).
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The distinction that should be made is between a failure to consider a relevant contractual provision which may constitute jurisdictional error and an adjudicator’s decision not to consider such a provision because of the operation of s 24(4) which, if erroneous, would constitute an error within jurisdiction. No jurisdictional error has been identified.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In considering whether the failure to consider a submission properly made constitutes jurisdictional error, in Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373, Ann Lyons J had regard to the failure to consider a submission properly made, ‘the extent of the failure and the nature, gravity and effect of the error made by the adjudicator’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The reverse of considering a matter outside of those prescribed under section 26(2) BCIPA (equivalent to section 88(2) of the Act) has also been held to constitute jurisdictional error: see Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A failure to consider all submissions properly made. Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373. Submissions were made to the adjudicator concerning Item 1 of an invoice. There was an express concession by the claimant on this Item 1 in the adjudication application, with a recalculation to a lesser amount. The respondent made submission on the errors of calculation and lack of clarity with this Item 1. In valuing Item 1, the adjudicator adopted the full value of the payment claim, notwithstanding the claimant’s concession or the respondent’s submissions (and notwithstanding making an adjustment to Item 2 of the payment claim on the basis of a concession by the claimant). In a letter to the Court, the adjudicator acknowledged that he was ‘fully aware’ of the submissions, but merely ‘forgot’ them during the final drafting. The Court held that the adjudicator had failed to ‘consider’ all submissions properly made at the time of making the decision (at [56]). The Court held that the adjudicator had failed to ‘genuinely attempt to exercise his power in accordance with’ BCIPA, and had committed jurisdictional error (at [60]).
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Proceeding in the absence of a jurisdictional fact
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The following considers categories of jurisdictional facts in relation to the Act and whether such categories have, or have not, been held to be jurisdictional facts.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The existence of a construction contract. The existence of a construction contract between the claimant and the respondent is a jurisdictional fact that operates as an essential precondition to the jurisdiction of an adjudicator: Fifty Property Investments Pty Ltd v O’Mara (2007) 23 BCL 35; [2006] NSWSC 428, [17]-[21] (Bereton J); Walton Construction (Qld) Pty Ltd v Salce & Ors [2008] QSC 235, [7] (McMurdo J); Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Anor [2012] QCA 276, [101] (Philippides J, Holmes and White JJA agreeing); Agripower Australia Ltd v J & D Rigging Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 164, [94] (Wilson J).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The existence of a valid payment claim. In Thiess Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 373, Ann Lyons J said that ‘[i]t is clear therefore that there must be a valid payment claim for there to be a valid adjudication.’ However, her Honour did not expand upon what amounted to a ‘valid payment claim’.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A decision about the scope of a payment claim is within jurisdiction and does not constitute jurisdictional error: Richard Kirk Architect Pty Ltd v ABC [2012] QSC 177, [33]-[34] (Daubney J).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Fitz Jersey v Atlas Construction Group [2017] NSWSC 340 McDougall J said that there is ‘substantial support’ for the view that whether a payment claim has been served within the time required by sub-section 13(4) of the NSW Act is a jurisdictional fact. 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The existence of a valid reference date. It was held in Kembla Coal & Coke v Select Civil [2004] NSWSC 628 that the existence of a reference date was not a jurisdictional fact for the purposes of the NSW Act. McDougall J held that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It is one thing to say that s 8 of the Act specifies the entitlement to a progress payment as something existing “[o]n and from each reference date under a construction contract.” It is quite something else to say that the reference date is thereby made a jurisdictional fact if the matter goes to adjudication. If the payment claim has no reference to a reference date, that may be a valid basis of opposition. But it does not mean that the claimant is anything other than “a person who … claims to be entitled to a progress payment”. It does not mean that the adjudicator is required to make a positive finding as to a reference date, or that the adjudicator can rely on the absence of a reference date to find against the claimant where that point has not been raised by the respondent in its payment schedule or adjudication response.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] If the existence of a reference date were regarded as a jurisdictional fact – something to be proved as a matter of fact before the adjudicator could proceed – then the adjudicator would be required to consider that matter explicitly. Further, it is at least arguable that it would not be a fact that could be established by the agreement, admission or concession of the parties. In other words, the parties could agree, for the purpose of the adjudication, that the claim was made by reference to a reference date under the contract: and yet, if this proved not to be the case, the respondent could later (subject to what I say in para [110] below as to discretionary considerations) take the point in proceedings to quash the determination.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The Act does not specify, expressly or by implication, that an adjudicator is required to be satisfied as to the relevant reference date before proceeding further with the adjudication. Instead, I think, it leaves it to the parties (in the usual case, the respondent) to raise the point if it is appropriate to do so. If the point is raised, then it is one that the adjudicator can determine. For the reasons given by Spigelman CJ in Timbarra at 65 [44], that would suggest that the existence of a relevant reference date is not a jurisdictional fact.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] His Honour’s conclusion in Kembla, that a reference date is not a jurisdictional fact, was affirmed by the New South Wales Court of Appeal in Lewence Construction Pty Ltd v Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd [2015] NSWCA 288, [60] (Ward JA), [119] (Emmett JA), [133] (Sackville AJA).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Contrast the decision in Saville v Hallmarc Construction Pty Ltd [2015] VSCA 318 where the Victorian Court of Appeal held that the fixing of a reference date by the adjudicator was a jurisdictional fact and reviewable for jurisdictional error. Relevantly, the claimant’s right to a progress payment under the VIC Act was, by virtue of the operation of section 14(4)(b) of the VIC Act, dependent on the calculation and fixing of the reference date. The Victorian Court of Appeal held that the adjudicator’s fixing of the reference date was a jurisdictional fact and, hence, reviewable for jurisdictional error.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In J Hutchinson Pty Ltd Glavcom Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 126, Ball J held that following the decision in Lewence Construction Pty Ltd v Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd [2015] NSWCA 288, there is now no doubt that the question of whether a reference date has arisen is not a jurisdictional fact. However, His Honour then said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] But it does not follow from that that any decision taken by the Adjudicator in relation to that question cannot involve jurisdictional error. Section 34 of the Security of Payment Act renders void provisions of contracts that restrict or modify the Act. It was not open to the Adjudicator to seek to apply a provision which was rendered void by s 34; and if the Adjudicator had done so in my opinion he would have made a jurisdictional error because he would have taken into account a matter which the Security of Payment Act made irrelevant.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] On appeal to the High Court of Australia, the conclusion of the Court of Appeal of the Supreme Court of New South Wales in Lewence Construction Pty Ltd v Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd [2015] NSWCA 288 was overturned. in Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd (in Liquidation) v Lewence Construction Pty Ltd [2016] HCA 52, Kiefel, Bell, Gageler, Keane And Gordon JJ held, consistently with the conclusion of the primary judge in Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd v Lewence Construction Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 502, that the existence of a reference date under a construction contract is a precondition to the making of a valid payment claim under the NSW Act.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Brolton Group Pty Ltd v Hanson Construction Materials Pty Ltd [2020] NSWCA 63, the New South Wales Court of Appeal affirmed the decision of the trial judge made in favour of the head contractor that an adjudication determination was void. In dismissing the appeal, Gleeson JA (with whom Meagher and Payne JJA agreed) held that the adjudicator committed jurisdictional error under section 22 of the NSW Act (equivalent to section 88 BIFSOPA) by calculating the Claimant’s entitlement to payment on the basis of an unavailable reference date, thereby failing to address the progress payment to be made on an available reference date. The Court also confirmed the long-standing authority that there will be a denial of natural justice where the adjudicator determines a matter which neither party contended nor was given an opportunity to make submissions on.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Extent and value of ‘construction work’ or ‘related goods and services’ are not jurisdictional facts. ‘[T]he adjudicator’s determination as to the extent and value of the “construction work” or “related goods and services” the subject of the payment claim does not…concern a matter of jurisdictional fact’: Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Anor [2012] QCA 276, [103] (Philippides J, Holmes and White JJA agreeing).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Similarly, it has been held that, as an adjudicator is to determine the extent and value of construction work, ‘ the inclusion of a claim for an obviously irrelevant item for what is not construction work does not deprive the adjudicator of that jurisdiction’: Matrix Projects (Qld) Pty Ltd v Luscombe [2013] QSC 4, [24] (Douglas J).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] However, contrast CKP Constructions Pty Ltd v Evolution Piling Pty Ltd & Ors (Unreported, Supreme Court of Queensland, Dalton J, 24 July 2015) where, under a subcontract for the building of foundation works, a claimant (a subcontractor) was entitled to progress payments for the proportion of the subcontract sum which represented the part of the works satisfactorily completed at the time of the claim. In proceedings to set aside an adjudication decision, Dalton J held that the adjudicator had allowed claims that did not represent the proportion of the work completed. In so doing, Dalton J held that the adjudicator had committed jurisdictional error (and a failure to accord natural justice) due to ‘a total failure to assess the claims made by reference to any contractual entitlement’.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Section 75 of the Act. Strict compliance with the terms of section 17A of BCIPA (equivalent to section 75 of the Act) may not be a ‘basic and essential’ requirement: see Lifestyle Retirement Projects No 2 Pty Ltd v Parisi Homes Pty Ltd [2005] NSWSC 705, [19] (Campbell J); Energetech Australia Pty Ltd v Sides Engineering Pty Ltd [2005] NSWSC 801, [25] (McDougall J).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] However the application of these cases (both decided under the NSW Act) is not certain in Queensland. The equivalent section 13(4) of the NSW Act is drafted providing that a payment claim ‘may be served only within’. In contrast, section 17A(2), (3) of the Act provides that a payment claim ‘must be given before’ the stated periods. The presence of ‘may’ in the drafting of section 13(4) of the NSW Act was a factor emphasised in Lifestyle Retirement Projects No 2 Pty Ltd v Parisi Homes Pty Ltd [2005] NSWSC 705.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Contrast South East Civil and Drainage Contractors Pty Ltd v AMGW Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 45; [2013] 2 Qd R 189 where, in the context of the former section 17(4) of pre-amendment BCIPA (now section 75(2) and (3)), Jackson J held that non-compliance with section 17(4) which was apparent on the face of the claimant’s submissions, invalidated the adjudicator’s decision.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In QC Communications NSW Pty Ltd v CivComm Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 1095, an adjudication application was served to both the adjudicator and the respondent but the supporting material to the application was not. Ball J addressed the question of whether the failure to serve the supporting material to an adjudication application went to the jurisdiction of the adjudicator or constituted a denial of natural justice to the respondent. His Honour doubted it did, citing the reasons of McDougall J in Douglas Aerospace v Indistri Engineering Albury [2014] NSWSC 1445:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] [73]   There may be a question as to whether s 17(5) should be regarded as jurisdictional. It was not identified as a "basic and essential" condition of validity in Brodyn Pty Ltd v Davenport (2004) 61 NSWLR 421. It was not identified as a jurisdictional prerequisite in [Chase Oyster Bay v Hamo (2010) 78 NSWLR 393]. I was not referred to any decision which holds that compliance with s 17(5) is jurisdictional.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Consequently, Ball J held in QC Communications NSW Pty Ltd v CivComm Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 1095 that the failure by a claimant to serve supporting material did not deprive an adjudicator of jurisdiction. As to the question of natural justice, though His Honour determined that the plaintiff had been denied natural justice, His Honour was not satisfied on the particular facts of the case that the denial had any effect on the outcome of the adjudication and thus did not grant any relief to the plaintiff with regards to the defendant’s failure to serve its supporting materials. 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Compliance with section 88(2). In Brodyn Pty Ltd t/as Time Coast and Quality v Davenport & Ors [2004] NSWCA 394, Hodgson JA doubted whether the equivalent to section 88(2) under the NSW Act is a precondition to an adjudicator’s jurisdiction (at [56]).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Section 88(2)(c), (d) – whether submissions are properly made. It has been held that a decision as to whether information was a ‘properly made’ submission is not an error which is a jurisdictional error, being a matter for the adjudicator to decide: Richard Kirk Architect Pty Ltd v ABC [2012] QSC 177, [40] (Daubney J), citing Broad Construction Services (NSW) Pty Ltd v Michael Verdasz t/as Australasian Piling Co [2008] NSWSC 1057; (2008) 73 NSWLR 149.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Section 87 of the Act. In the context of the NSW Act, it has been considered that the equivalent to section 87 of the Act (section 22(4) of the NSW Act), is not one of the “basic and essential requirements”: see Rothnere v Quasar & Ors [2004] NSWSC 1151, [18]-[26] (McDougall J).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Whether a contract was terminated. The effective termination of a contract, where there are no accrued but unused reference dates prior to the date of termination, is a jurisdictional fact for an adjudicator’s jurisdiction: see McNab NQ Pty Ltd v Walkrete Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 128, [10]-[11].
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Whether a service was rendered under a contract. In Roberts Construction Group Pty Ltd v Drummond Carpentry Services Pty Ltd [2024] VSC 246, Roberts Construction, the respondent to an adjudication, sought to quash the adjudication decision on the basis that it was not the person liable to make a payment to Drummond under a construction contract. In this case, Roberts was the head contractor who engaged its related entity Timberworks to provide timber framework (Mr Kerlidis is the sole director of Roberts and Timberworks). Drummond, the applicant to the adjudication, was also engaged for the provision of labour hire under an informal arrangement. Drummond served a payment claim to Roberts after two invoices issued to Timberworks went unpaid. Timberworks subsequently applied for adjudication. The adjudicator found that there was a construction contract between Roberts and Drummond and awarded the claimed amount.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Ultimately, Roberts argued that it was not a person liable to make a payment to Drummond under a construction contract, and whether the payment claim has been served on the right entity is a jurisdictional fact. In dismissing this application, Niall JA found that the question of whether the particular services were or were not rendered under that contract is not a jurisdictional fact. In particular, it was held at [63]-[66] that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] I accept that the existence of a construction contract between the claimant and the respondent is a jurisdictional fact that must exist before a valid payment claim can be made and, as a result, is necessary to enliven the adjudicator’s jurisdiction to make a determination. The consequence of the absence of a relevant construction contract is that an adjudicator’s determination will be void for jurisdictional error.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] However, it is not in dispute that there existed a construction contract between Roberts and Drummond by which the latter agreed to supply ‘related services’ …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Rather, the dispute concerns whether the claimed services were rendered under the Roberts contract, and whether that fact goes to the adjudicator’s jurisdiction. As will be appreciated, Roberts contends that the services were not due under the Roberts contract but were provided under a different construction contract to which it was not a party.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my opinion, having identified a construction contract, the question whether particular services were or were not rendered under that contract is not a jurisdictional fact. Rather, that issue is to be resolved through the statutory processes laid out by the Act.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.16    The effect of jurisdictional error (remittal & severing)
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] If an adjudicator committed jurisdictional error, ‘the decision is “regarded, in law, as no decision at all”’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd [2013] QCA 394, the Queensland Court of Appeal held, in setting aside an adjudication decision, that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] once a court determines that a decision of the type in question is affected by jurisdictional error, the decision cannot give rise to legal consequences.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In so holding, the Court of Appeal referred to the decisions (among others) of the High Court in Minister for Immigration and Multicultural Affairs v Bhardwaj [2002] HCA 11; (2002) 209 CLR 597 and Plaintiff S157/2002 v The Commonwealth (2003) 221 CLR 476 on the effect of jurisdictional error.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Remittal to adjudicator
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Before reaching the High Court, Emmett AJA of the New South Wales Supreme Court held in Probuild Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd v Shade Systems Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 770, that an adjudicator had made an error of law on the face of the record, and ordered that the matter be remitted to the adjudicator for further consideration and determination according to law. In Metacorp Australia Pty Ltd v Andeco Construction Group Pty Ltd (No 2) (2010) 30 VR 141 and Maxstra Constructions Pty Ltd v Gilbert & Ors [2013] VSC 243, Vickery J said that where a court grants relief in the nature of certiorari in respect of an adjudication determination, it is open for the court to remit the matter to the adjudicator.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See section 88.13 in relation the appeal of Probuild Constructions to the High Court, ruling that a non-jurisdictional error of law is not amenable to judicial review.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Further, in SSC Plenty Road Pty Ltd v Construction Engineering (Aust) (No 2) [2015] VSC 680, Vickery J ordered the remittal of parts of an earlier adjudication determination that related to a number of claims (‘Disputed Variation Claims’ and ‘Disputed Deduction Claims’) quashed by the Supreme Court of Victoria for redetermination due to the following factors:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that the earlier adjudication determination contained no adverse findings or observations as to ‘the conduct of either party or the manner in which they presented their respective cases’;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that the earlier adjudication determination did not contain any language ‘which might suggest the formation by the Adjudicator of a preconceived view towards the reliability of any of the materials or a general preference for the submissions of one party over the other’;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that ‘no concerns were raised about the Adjudicator’s management of the matter during the course of making his determination’;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that the Adjudicator’s determination in respect of the Disputed Variation Claims and Disputed Deduction Claims falls short of giving rise to a perception of unfairness to the respondent;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that there was no evidence from the earlier adjudication determination that the Adjudicator might not bring an impartial mind to any remittal issues that favoured an order that the disallowed claims be redetermined by a different adjudicator;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that there was ‘no reasonable foundation for any apprehension that the Adjudicator on a remitted determination would undertake the valuation task otherwise than in good faith and in accordance with the remarks on the function of an adjudicator under the [VIC] Act set out in the principal judgment’;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that the VIC Act has a concern for expeditious adjudication and that this paramount objective should not be too readily displaced;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that the ‘[m]inimisation of delay in the decision-making process promotes a central aim of the [VIC] Act’;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that ‘the Adjudicator is already acquainted with the substantial body of documentation which will inform the evaluation exercises’; and
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] the inconvenience of sourcing another able and available decision-maker.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, in Richard Crookes Construction Pty Ltd v CES Projects (Aust) Pty Ltd (No 2) [2016] NSWSC 1229, McDougall J commented in obiter that: 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It may be accepted, at the level of basic principle, that if the order of an inferior court or tribunal is called up into a superior court, and the decision quashed (which is the process involved in certiorari), the matter may be remitted to the inferior court or to be decided according to law. However, whether that procedure is available, either at common law or on the making of an order in the nature of certiorari under s 69 of the Supreme Court Act, where the “inferior tribunal” in question is an adjudicator appointed under the Security of Payment Act, is in my view a problem that requires some consideration.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similarly, in Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386 the Queensland Court of Appeal considered that ‘[t]here are obvious difficulties in the way of a remittal’. In the absence of a more comprehensive argument on the Court’s power to remit an adjudication to an adjudicator, the Court of Appeal decided on discretionary grounds not to remit the decision, considering the following factors weighed against a remittal:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] ‘the quick, cheap and simple processes envisaged by the Act’;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that circumstances may have changed significantly from when the adjudicator was considering his original decision;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that the payment schedule may be outdated;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that other defects may have come to light;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that the legislature removed adjudicator’s decisions from the scope of the Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld); and
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] that a remittal order would require the adjudicator to make a decision outside the permitted time under the Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 92, Justice Martin remitted parts of an adjudication determination that had been found void back to the adjudicator to be decided according to law. Interestingly, Martin J made the orders without providing any reasoning or consideration. As such, the court may exercise its jurisdiction to remit parts of an adjudication decision back to an adjudicator if the adjudicator falls into jurisdictional error without providing reasoning.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd (No. 2) [2021] QSC 231, Justice Freeburn refused to remit the adjudication determination back to the adjudicator for the third time because there was little utility with respect to costs and delay in that an adjudication determination in effect is only a provisional finding. In relation to the Court’s powers and discretion to remit an adjudication determination back to an adjudicator, His Honour explained that “where the error is a confined, identified error ‘it is appropriate to identify the error and then remit the matter to be dealt with in a way that corrects that specific error’. However, where the adjudication determination is quashed on the basis that the adjudicator lacked jurisdiction to deal with the matter, then the matter should not be remitted back to adjudication”.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd (No 3) [2021] QSC 238, Freeburn J refused to exercise the Court’s discretion to remit parts of a severed adjudication decision that were rendered void by the Court in Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd (No. 2) [2021] QSC 231 to a different adjudicator because there was limited utility. In refusing to remit the void parts back to another adjudicator, Justice Freeburn held that remission to another adjudicator carries with it the grave risk of expanding the fronts of the battle.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Severing part of an adjudication decision
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In BRB Modular Pty Ltd v AWX Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 222, Bond J said that ‘[a] decision attended with jurisdictional error is no decision at all, that is, it is void’, citing Minister for Immigration and Multicultural Affairs v Bhardwaj [2002] HCA 11; (2002) 209 CLR 597, 614 [51] (Gaudron and Gummow JJ).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, this must be considered in the context of the Amendment Act, with section 100(4) of BCIPA (equivalent to section 101(4) of the Act), as inserted, allowing for a discretion to the court to identify and sever a part of an adjudicator’s decision affected by jurisdictional error.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Section 100(4) BCIPA was considered in Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 168 where Byrne SJA said (at [73]):
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The evident purpose of s.100(4) is not difficult to discern. It had been judicially determined that “jurisdictional error which affects one part of a decision will render the whole of [the decision] void”. That rule produced inconvenient consequences. More than one judge urged that the Act be amended to permit so much of an adjudicator’s decision as was not affected by jurisdictional error to stand.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (footnotes omitted)
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Severance remains a possibility in New South Wales, following the decision of YTO Construction Pty Ltd v Innovative Civil Pty Ltd [2019] NSWCA 110, where the New South Wales Court of Appeal held that part of an adjudicator’s determination under section 22 of the NSW Act (equivalent to section 88 of the Act) which was procured by fraud could be severed and remitted for “further hearing or redetermination.” Macfarlan JA, White JA, and Emmett AJA, in allowing the appeal in part, held that the primary judge had erred by failing to consider evidence and arguments forwarded by the head contractor regarding one of two claims of fraud.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 168 the Court held that an extension of time claim in an adjudication involved the re-agitation of issues determined in an earlier adjudication decision. The Court held that the adjudicator in the second adjudication had therefore exceeded his jurisdiction in allowing the extension of time claim and, in so doing, committed jurisdictional error. The Court exercised the power under section 100(4) of BCIPA to sever this part of the adjudication decision.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Icon Co (NSW) Pty Ltd v Australia Avenue Developments Pty Ltd [2018] NSWCA 339, the New South Wales Court of Appeal overturned the decision of the primary judge in Australia Avenue Developments Pty Ltd v Icon Co (NSW) Pty Ltd [2018] NSWSC 1578. At first instance, Parker J declared an adjudication determination void for jurisdictional error on the basis that the adjudicator had awarded amounts to the claimant which were not the subject of the payment claim. The Court of Appeal overturned that decision and confirmed that an error in construing the contract or in understanding the payment claim does not in itself constitute jurisdictional error and therefore cannot form a basis upon which an adjudication can be quashed. At first instance, Parker J decided that the adjudicator erred in considering, and subsequently awarding, “backcharges”, which were not expressly included in the payment claim. Basten JA (Meagher and Leeming JJA agreeing) held that the “backcharges” were clearly before the adjudicator and that this case involved a dispute as to how the payment claim and the payment schedule should be understood. Their Honours considered that in such a case, it is not appropriate for the Court to engage in an analysis of the manner in which the adjudicator dealt with the payment claim in her determination.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Fulton Hogan Construction Pty Ltd v QH & M Birt Pty Ltd [2019] QSC 23, Ryan J held that it was open for an adjudicator to take into account amounts which were not expressly itemised in the relevant payment claim but were otherwise mathematically apparent. In that case, Fulton Hogan issued a payment schedule to Birt, which applied a number of set-offs in response to a payment claim from Birt. The following month, Birt submitted another payment claim which did not expressly refer to Fulton Hogan’s previous set-off amounts, to which Fulton Hogan responded with another payment schedule that maintained its set-off amounts. Birt referred its second payment claim, which did not expressly refer to the set-off amounts, to adjudication. The adjudicator decided that “by extension of mathematics” he was satisfied that both parties were aware that the set-offs were being disputed, despite no express reference to them in Birt’s payment claim. The adjudicator awarded Birt approximately half of what it claimed and reversed Fulton Hogan’s set-offs. Fulton Hogan appealed the adjudication decision, claiming that the adjudicator should not have considered the set-off amounts as they were not expressly included in the payment claim. Ryan J, in dismissing Fulton Hogan’s appeal, confirmed that the adjudicator is empowered to determine the scope of the payment claim and that a reasonable, albeit erroneous, determination of this nature is not reviewable by a court and will not amount to jurisdictional error.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Karam Group Pty Ltd ATF The Karam (No. 1) Family Trust v HCA Queensland Pty Ltd & Ors [2024] QSC 34, the Queensland Supreme Court considered whether an adjudication decision should be set aside in full or in part. Bradley J noted that, “if only part of the decision is affected by the relevant error, then the Court may consider whether to save the balance” (at [9]). Bradley J concluded that discretion should be exercised in the circumstances citing “some utility in allowing the part of the decision” to remain binding on the parties as it would avoid the parties incurring the cost of a further adjudication process in respect of that claim (at [51]).
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.17    Failing to act in good faith
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Good faith and bona fides
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] There are differing authorities as to the “standard” of good faith required to be exercised by an adjudicator, and the consequences of non-compliance. In particular, whether the level of good faith required is to determined by a broad view, or a narrow view.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Queensland Bulk Water Supply Authority v McDonald Keen Group Pty Ltd & Anor [2009] QSC 165, Peter Lyons J adopted a ‘broad’ view to good faith, considering that: 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It may be correct to say that a decision which displays an extreme degree of unreasonableness akin to that described in Associated Provincial Picture House Ltd v Wednesbury Corporation, is not a decision for the purposes of s 26 of the Payments Act. Otherwise, I do not consider an adjudicator’s decision purporting to be made under the Payments Act will be invalid if it is not “reasonable”. The Payments Act seeks to provide a mechanism for obtaining a decision which will be quick, but in a sense, provisional. It does not seem to me, consistent with the general object and tenor of the Act, to impose a requirement of “reasonableness”.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] I am therefore of the opinion that the test advanced on behalf of QBWSA is too widely formulated. If the broad test for good faith is to be adopted, then what is required is a genuine attempt to exercise the power in accordance with the provisions in the Payments Act. Specifically, in relation to a consideration of the construction contract, what is required is a genuine attempt to understand and apply that contract.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] This view of Peter Lyons J was subsequently adopted by Applegarth J in John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Pty Ltd & Ors [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302, and in Rickard Kirk Architect Pty Ltd v Australian Broadcasting Corporation & Ors [2012] QSC 177, [32] (Daubney J).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, on appeal, the Queensland Court of Appeal in Queensland Bulk Water Supply Authority t/as Seqwater v McDonald Keen Group Pty Ltd (in liq) & Anor [2010] QCA 7; [2010] 2 Qd R 322, took a different view on the subject of good faith, adopting a narrower view. Holmes JA (Fraser JA and Fryberg J agreeing) said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The difference between the two tests would only be relevant for present purposes if one took the view that the arbitrator had, without any deliberate, wilful or conscious intent, constructed a contract so different from the one which he was required to consider as to fall short of a genuine attempt at his task. If, instead, one concluded, as QBWSA posited, that the adjudicator had approached the adjudication intent on determining the claim by ensuring that MKG was fairly recompensed, rather than by reference to MKG’s entitlements under the contract, one would inevitably conclude that there was personal fault on his part, and, at best, a conscious preference of “whim or fancy … to considered judgment”.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] I incline to the view that the absence of good faith may not be the exact converse of bad faith, and I agree with the learned primary judge that the content of what is required may vary according to context. But I am not entirely convinced of the significance his Honour attributed to the context here, and, more particularly, to the interim character of the arbitrator’s decision. In that context, of the Payments Act which is designed to provide an expeditious mechanism for payment, and which allows of further proceedings, unaffected by the arbitrator’s decision, to determine the parties’ contractual rights, I think there is a good deal to be said for a narrow approach to questions of good faith. However, as will emerge from what follows, I agree with the learned primary judge that on either test, QBWSA has not demonstrated a want of good faith on the adjudicator’s part.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In McNab Developments (Qld) Pty Ltd v MAK Construction Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QCA 232, the Queensland Court of Appeal discussed the threshold for ‘good faith’ by an adjudicator:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Gotterson JA approved of the approach from Holmwood Holdings Pty Ltd v Halkat Electrical Contractors Pty Ltd & Anor [2005] NSWSC 1129, [46]-[48]  (at [70]);
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Morrsion JA declined to follow the line of authority from Holmwood, noting the reservations that have been expressed about this line of authority (at [88]); 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Jackson J decided the issue on the application of the slip rule under section 28 of the Act and found it unnecessary to go further (at [108]-[111]).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, considering the reasoning of an adjudicator in the framework of ‘good faith’ may, in the view of White JA in Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2011] QCA 22; [2012] 1 Qd R 525, be asking the wrong question. In her Honour’s view, the enquiry ‘should focus more on whether the adjudicator has performed the functions demanded by’ the Act (at [96]). On this point, her Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The discussion in Minister for Immigration and Citizenship v SZMDS concerning the relationship between jurisdictional error in respect of reasoning which is “clearly unjust”, “arbitrary”, “capricious” and “Wednesbury unreasonable” demonstrates that attaching these descriptors to the good faith debate possibly adds little more than did the original understanding of good faith in the review of statutory decision making that the power must be exercised honestly for the purpose for which it was given.  As the New South Wales Court of Appeal did in Holmwood, the enquiry should focus more on whether the adjudicator has performed the function demanded by the Payments Act and less on pursuing elusive synonyms, keeping always in mind that the legislative intent dictates a person with recognised expertise in the area be selected for the task by an informed body and this, necessarily, facilitates the rapid decision making required.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] This view was approved in Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373 by Ann Lyons J (at [53]-[54]) and in James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd v ASAP Plasterers Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 145 by Atkinson J (at [28]).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The question as to whether a bona fide attempt has been made was considered in Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd & Ors [2010] QSC 95 where Martin J said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It is important to establish, before proceeding further, the basis upon which the decision of an adjudicator should be assessed, especially when claims are made that there has not been a bona fide attempt by the adjudicator to deal with the matter. It does not assist in the determination of such a question to simply cherry pick particular paragraphs from a lengthy decision and, by pointing at them alone, attempt to show an absence of bona fides. The correct approach is that which was described in the following two decisions. In Shellbridge Pty Ltd v Rider Hunt Sydney Pty Ltd [2005] NSWSC 1152, Barrett J said at [20]:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “… the whole of the content and tenor of an adjudication may be called in aid in deciding whether particular submissions were considered in the way the Act requires. Inference is permissible. The question is not to be approached solely by reference to the presence or absence of explicit statements referring expressly to the submissions.”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 307, where Philip McMurdo J applied the following passage quoted by White JA in Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2011] QCA 22; [2012] 1 Qd R 525 on the role of an adjudicator:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In summary, what is required of an adjudicator is that he or she make a genuine attempt to understand and apply the relevant contract and to exercise the power in accordance with the Act.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Failure to make a bona fide attempt
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Adopting an ‘abbreviated’ method. Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd & Ors [2010] QSC 95. The payment claim in question contained an itemised claim to building work subdivided into categories of work, each assigned a percentage, and 73 variation claims, the majority of which were less than $5,000. The adjudicator did not individually value each category of work or variation claim. The Court held that the adjudicator had made a genuine attempt in the exercise of his power.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Watpac Construction (Qld) Pty Ltd v KLM Group Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 236. In proceedings challenging an adjudicator’s decision, the respondent alleged that the adjudicator had failed to make a bona fide attempt by merely rejecting its case as to what rates were reasonable and effectively reversed the onus of proof. This submission was rejected by the Court, holding that the adjudicator had adopted the claimant’s rates on an evidentiary basis that was evident from the adjudicator’s reasons.

Specifically, in relation to a consideration of the construction contract, what is required is a genuine attempt to understand and apply that contract.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The view of Peter Lyons J was subsequently adopted by Applegarth J in John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Pty Ltd & Ors [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd and Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292 where, although not necessary to decide, Peter Lyons J would have been prepared to set aside the adjudication decision on the grounds that the adjudicator’s conclusions on a particular matter were so unreasonable that no reasonable person could have made it.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Contrast the view of White JA in Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2011] QCA 22; [2012] 1 Qd R 525 at [96] where her Honour considered that approaching the reasoning in the context of descriptors such as ‘Wednesbury unreasonable’ may be (in effect) asking the wrong question, where the proper inquiry should be on whether the adjudicator has performed the functions demanded by the Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The application of this concept to BCIPA was doubted by Gotterson JA in McNab Developments (Qld) Pty Ltd v MAK Construction Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QCA 232, who said that he ‘would hesitate to adopt the proposition as necessarily correct’. His Honour went on to say, first, that ‘Wednesbury’ unreasonableness has a unique application to discretionary power under statute which is different from the adjudicative functions under the Act, and secondly, that ‘Wednesbury’ unreasonableness was not identified as a species of jurisdictional error in Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, this may be contrasted with Byrne SJA in Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 168 where His Honour was of the view that a ‘perverse, capricious or irrational’ interpretation would establish jurisdictional error, or finding the presence or jurisdictional error sufficient on a finding that the interpretation was so unreasonable that no reasonable person could have arrived at it.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Bouygues Construction Australia Pty Ltd v Southern Cross Electrical Engineering Ltd [2017] NSWSC 1665, Stevenson J granted an injunction restraining the enforcement of an adjudicator’s decision, pending the outcome of an application to have that decision set aside, on the basis that the adjudicator’s decision was so unreasonable that it was beyond their jurisdiction. In the case, the adjudicator based their decision to reject Bouygues right to set-off for liquidated damages on an unsubstantiated purported agreement that Bouygues would not deduct liquidated damages. Stevenson J held that this constituted a serious question to be tried and granted the injunction restraining the enforcement of an adjudicator’s decision. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Ceerose Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd [2022] NSWSC 1487 concerned an application to extend an interim injunction, refraining the defendant from taking steps to enforce a judgment entered in the District Court of NSW, based on an adjudication certificate. The plaintiff asserted there was a serious issue to be tried in extending the interim injunction, as there was a denial of natural justice for the adjudicator have made their determination on a basis other than that which was advocated by the parties, without notifying them of his intention to do so. In refusing to grant the injunction, the Court held that an adjudicator’s finding must be “material” to the outcome in order for an adjudication decision to be overturned based on a denial of procedural fairness. In these circumstances where the plaintiff did not suggest that finding was wrong, the court found the adjudicator’s finding was irrelevant and the strength of the plaintiff’s claim that there was a serious question to be tried was not strong.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.18    Natural Justice
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] A denial of natural justice is a jurisdictional error which can invalidate an adjudicator’s decision under the Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] It has been held that a denial of natural justice must be ‘substantial’. A ‘substantial’ denial of natural justice will render an adjudication decision void, and not merely voidable.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] On the denial of natural justice being ‘substantial’, in John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Ltd [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302, Applegarth J said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The adjective “substantial” has been used in the relevant authorities to capture the principle that the opportunity denied was material, namely that the matter about which the adjudicator did not provide an opportunity to be heard was a point upon which the adjudicator based his or her decision and was significant to the actual determination. In addition, the Court’s concern is with the practical effect of the alleged denial of natural justice. Reference to the High Court’s decisions in Stead v State Government Insurance Commission and Ex parte Aala supports the proposition that even if the Court is satisfied that there has been a denial of natural justice, relief may be denied if it can be shown that compliance with the requirements of natural justice could have made no difference to the outcome. It is probably sufficient in this regard for the applicant for relief to show that there were substantial submissions that, as a matter of reality and not mere speculation, might have persuaded the adjudicator to change his or her mind.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] There will be a substantial denial of natural justice ‘when an adjudicator decides a dispute on a basis for which neither party has contended, unless it can be said that no submission could have been made to the adjudicator which might have produced a different result’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Accordingly, even if there is a denial of natural justice, ‘relief may be denied if it can be shown that compliance with the requirements of natural justice could have made no difference to the outcome’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] A party alleging a denial of natural justice does not need to establish that it would have succeeded in persuading the adjudicator, ‘[i]t is sufficient in this context that there is “something to be put that might well persuade the adjudicator to change his or her mind”’: Northbuild Construction Sunshine Coast Pty Ltd v Beyfield Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 80, [38] (Philip McMurdo J), quoting Trysams Pty Ltd v Club Constructions (NSW) Pty Ltd [2008] NSWSC 399, [45].
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Natural justice under the Act
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] It has been held that ‘[t]he requirement to afford [natural] justice is shaped by the relevant legislation and the particular circumstances of the case’: VK Property Group Pty Ltd & Ors v A A D Design Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 54, [29] (Boddice J).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Regard is to be had to the extent to which natural justice has been afforded or has been denied in the statutory scheme established under the Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v Climatech (Canberra) Pty Ltd & Ors [2005] NSWCA 229, Basten JA said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] the relevant content of procedural fairness must be ascertained by reference to the specific statutory scheme. Where that scheme is inconsistent with some element which might otherwise have been implied under the general law, it is the general law which must give way.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In the context of BCIPA in Queensland, see Abel Point Marina (Whitsundays) Pty Ltd v Uher & Anor [2006] QSC 295 at [15] per Wilson J.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Fifty Property Investments Pty Ltd v O’Mara (2007) 23 BCL 35; [2006] NSWSC 428, Bereton J said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] a denial of natural justice will invalidate an adjudication, but only if the procedure falls short of that measure of natural justice to which a party is entitled under the scheme of the Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similarly, in Brodyn Pty Ltd t/as Time Coast and Quality v Davenport & Anor [2004] NSWCA 394 at [57] per Hodgson JA:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The circumstance that the legislation requires notice to the respondent and an opportunity to the respondent to make submissions (ss 17(1) and (2), 20, 21(1), 22(2)(d)) confirms that natural justice is to be afforded to the extent contemplated by these provisions; and in my opinion, such is the importance generally of natural justice that one can infer a legislative intention that this is essential to validity, so that if there is a failure by the adjudicator to receive and consider submissions, occasioned by breach of these provisions, the determination will be a nullity.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Rhomberg Rail Australia Pty Ltd v Concrete Evidence Pty Limited [2019] NSWSC 755, Ball J held that an adjudicator’s determination under section 22 of the NSW Act (equivalent to section 88 of the Act) will not be void for jurisdictional error for a denial of procedural fairness unless it was “substantial” enough to effect the outcome of the determination. The claimant alleged that a printing error had made it unable to respond to a number of variations which did not appear, as expected, under Tab 7 of the adjudication application, despite a full copy of the variations appearing at Tab 8. Ball J held that “a person acting reasonably would at least have appreciated that there was an inconsistency between Tab 7 and Tab 8 and therefore appreciated that there was at least a risk that the Adjudicator would proceed with his adjudication by reference to Tab 8 rather than Tab 7.” Consequently, in exercising their discretion as to whether natural justice has been denied, the Court will apply an assessment of reasonableness.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Where a denial of natural justice may arise
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Considering material known only to one party. In Fifty Property Investments Pty Ltd v O’Mara (2007) 23 BCL 35; [2006] NSWSC 428, Bereton J said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The receipt and consideration from one party of material, whether in the nature of evidence or submissions, which is not made known to the other, is a denial of natural justice.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Direct correspondence with the adjudicator. J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Cada Formwork Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 63. Following the lodgment of an adjudication application, the claimant sent a series of emails to the authorised nominating authority, with one email in particular (the 10 June email) attaching a site instruction document and explaining its importance. The 10 June email was not seen by the respondent prior to the adjudication decision. The adjudicator placed substantial reliance on this 10 June email, and attached site instruction, and reached a conclusion adverse to the respondent. The Court held that the respondent had been denied natural justice, with the effect that the adjudication decision was void, considering that the respondent should have been afforded an opportunity to address the 10 June email and attached site instruction.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Direct correspondence with the adjudicator. TQM Design & Construct Pty Ltd v Dasein Constructions Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 1216. In dispute was when the adjudication application was received by the respondent. The claimant wrote to the adjudicator, providing evidence of service on a day before the respondent claimed it was received. The respondent was not provided a copy of this correspondence by the claimant or the adjudicator. The adjudicator relied on this evidence to conclude that the adjudication response was delivered out of time. The Court held that there has been a significant denial of natural justice to the respondent. 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A document given to the adjudicator as part of the adjudication application not given to the respondent in the application. Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd v Ian James Ericson t/as Flea’s Concreting & Anor [2011] QSC 327. A letter was given to the adjudicator as part of an adjudication application that was not contained in the hard copy of the adjudication application served on the respondent. The adjudicator relied on the contents of the letter in making his decision. However the Court held that, in the circumstances. the inclusion of the missing letter would not have made any difference to the response. Significant for the Court was that the response was argued other that in relation to the contents of the letter (which, broadly, related to the client’s quantum of amounts claimed). The Court held that it can be said that the lack of opportunity to address the letter could not possibly have produced a different result (at [122]).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Failure to consider matters. Section 88(2) of the Act requires an adjudicator to consider ‘all submissions, including relevant documentation, that have been properly made’ in support of the payment claim (section 88(2)(c)) and the payment schedule (section 88(2)(d)).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A spreadsheet not contained in the payment schedule. Syntech Resources Pty Ltd v Peter Campbell Earthmoving (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 293. Two of the respondent’s reasons for withholding payment in the payment schedule was that there should be a deduction of $142,134.25 for the wet hire payments and that it has taken a review of all previous payments and has determined that the claimant has been overpaid to the amount of $127,618.40. In its adjudication response, the respondent included spreadsheets with calculations supporting these reasons. The adjudicator determined that these spreadsheets could not be relied on by the respondent, referring to natural justice considerations. The adjudicator did not ask for further submissions on this issue. The Court held that, in unilaterally disregarding the spreadsheets without giving the parties notice of his intention, the adjudicator committed a substantial breach of natural justice. The Court held that the claimant had, at the very least, been denied the opportunity to submit that the spreadsheets had been properly received under section 26(2)(d) of BCIPA.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A failure to engage with the evidence. Suprima Bakeries Pty Ltd v Australian Weighing Equipment Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 998. In its adjudication response, the respondent submitted that the work, which was the subject of the adjudication application, was defective and was of no value. This was supported by a number of statutory declarations of employees of the respondent. In his determination, the adjudicator concluded that the system installed by the applicant was not performing to the respondent’s requirements. However, the adjudicator failed to inquire whether, as put by the respondent, the work was of essentially no value. This amounted to a failure to engage with the evidence before him. McDougall J held that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] the Adjudicator did fail to discharge this aspect of his statutory function, and that, by failing to deal in a reasoned way with a fundamental part of [the respondent’s] case, he denied it natural justice.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Richard Crookes Constructions Pty Ltd v CES Projects (Aust) Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 1119 McDougall J referred to his decision in Suprima Bakeries Pty Ltd v Australian Weighing Equipment Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 998 held that the adjudicator had arguably abdicated his statutory function as there was nothing in his reasons that showed any independent rational assessment by him of the two matters in contest: the quantum of work completed and the value of that work. 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Goodwin Street Developments Pty Ltd v DSD Builders Pty Ltd [2018] NSWSC 1229, McDougall J held that an adjudicator will still have carried out their statutory function in good faith under section 10(1)(b)(iv) of the NSW Act (equivalent to section 72(1)(b)(iv) of the Act), regardless of whether their reasons are not entirely logical. In that case, the adjudicator did not provide ‘logical reasons’ as to why they downplayed the significance of the contractor’s claim for defective work. Nevertheless, his Honour held that the adjudicator’s reasons, when read in their entirety, did show that they were not satisfied that there was defective work and the reasons why. McDougall J explained the importance of not ‘overzealously or mechanically applying the description of the adjudicator’s task explained in SSC Plenty Road v Construction Engineering (Aust) Pty Ltd [2015] VSC 631’. His Honour emphasised that it is essential to take into account the extremely compressed timeframe within which adjudicator’s have to work and the subsequent pressure that this generates.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Ceerose Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd (No 2) [2023] NSWSC 345, Richmond J held that part of an adjudicator’s determination was affected by jurisdictional error. The part which was affected by jurisdictional error was the adjudicator’s finding that the claimant was entitled to the return of retention monies. This conclusion was reached on a basis that was not advanced by either party. Richmond J held that because the respondent was not provided with an opportunity to make submissions in relation to the adjudicator’s finding, the respondent was denied procedural fairness. This denial of procedural fairness was deemed by Richmond J to be material to the outcome of the adjudicator’s determination because had the parties been afforded an opportunity to make submissions on the retention monies finding, there was a realistic possibility that the outcome would have been different. As the denial of procedural fairness was material to the determination, it constituted jurisdictional error, and the part of the determination regarding the return of retention monies was set aside.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Considering submissions on jurisdiction. In National Management Group Pty Ltd v Biriel Industries Pty Ltd trading as Master Steel & Ors [2019] QSC 219, Wilson J held that an adjudicator’s refusal to consider submissions about jurisdiction will not lead to practical injustice or a substantial breach of natural justice warranting the setting aside of a determination, if the respondent fails to show that there were substantial submissions that, as a matter of reality and not speculation, might have persuaded the adjudicator to change his mind.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Demex Pty Ltd v McNab Building Services Pty Ltd [2023] NSWCA 261, the NSW Court of Appeal confirmed that a determination will only be set aside for jurisdictional error, due to an adjudicator’s failure to afford the parties’ procedural fairness if that failure causes substantial injustice to the parties.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The respondent challenged an adjudication determination, alleging that the adjudicator had reached a conclusion, on a basis which was not contended for by either party. 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The contract stipulated that payment for excavated material must be in accordance with the amount of material, measured in cubic metres. In the payment claim, the claim for excavated material was supported by dockets, which measured the material in tonnes. The claimant converted the figures from the dockets to cubic metres and claimed for those amounts (without providing the conversion rate it used to do so). 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The adjudicator performed a reverse calculation, to ascertain the conversion rate used by the claimant to convert the material from tonnes to cubic metres. The adjudicator used that rate to determine the amount payable for excavated material.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The Court of Appeal found that the respondent was correct in its assertion that the rate used by the adjudicator was not put forward by either party. However, the Court of Appeal held that the adjudicator had not fallen into jurisdictional error. This was because:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] It was clear, that the claimant had used some form of rate to convert the weight amounts in the dockets, to volume. Therefore, the rate was implicit in the payment claim, and it was not the case, that the adjudicator had reached a conclusion on a basis not contended for by either party.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The implicit existence of the conversion rate in the payment claim meant that the respondent could have made submissions on the claimant’s conversion rate but did not. Therefore, there was no substantial injustice to the respondent, as they had an opportunity to make submissions on the conversion rate.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] On a fair reading of the adjudicator’s reasons, he only employed his industry knowledge in a confirmatory way to accept the conversion rate. Further, because adjudicator’s may be expected to bring their experience and expertise in making their determination, the reliance of the adjudicator on his industry experience further demonstrated that his decision did not give rise to any unfairness or that he was acting outside of his jurisdiction.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Rogers Construction Group Pty Ltd v Mirage Interiors & Construction Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 1344, the NSW Supreme Court dismissed an application to overturn an adjudication determination. The Respondent’s challenge was premised on its assertion the adjudicator made his determination on a basis that was not advanced by either party and which neither could have contemplated. The Respondent argued that the adjudicator failed to make any findings as to the scope of works under the written contract between the parties, and the adjudicator proceeded on the basis that scope of works under an oral contract between the parties was the same as that was under the original contract. However, Stevenson J held that there was no denial of procedural fairness, because in the Respondent’s adjudication response submissions, it was implicit that the Respondent understood there was a scope of works overlap between the two contracts. Therefore, there was no denial of procedural fairness.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In coming to his decision, Stevenson J referred to the NSW Court of Appeal’s comments in Demex Pty Ltd v McNab Building Services Pty Ltd [2023] NSWCA 26 regarding the high bar to overturn an adjudication determination in NSW for procedural fairness. Stevenson J noted that the “…content of the requisite procedural fairness is reduced” and will only result in invalidity of an adjudication determination if there is a “…significant departure from what would ordinarily be the requirements of procedural fairness for a person exercising a statutory power, and where the departure could be characterised as leading to substantial practical injustice in all the circumstances”.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Refer also to the commentary above under jurisdictional error (88.15) on the disregard of a matter required to be taken into account or taking into account of a matter required to be ignored’.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A decision on a basis not contended by either party. It has been considered, in the context of BCIPA, that the natural justice requirements in this context are ‘an incident of the duty in s 26(2)(c) and (d) to consider, inter alia, only the parties’ submissions’: James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd v ASAP Plasterers Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 145, [45] (Atkinson J).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In considering the role of natural justice under BCIPA, in John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Pty Ltd & Ors [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302, 321 [57], Applegarth J said: 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The statutory scheme may permit an adjudicator to make unreviewable errors of law in quickly deciding complex legal issues in adjudications of the present kind after considering the parties’ submissions. The statutory scheme does not permit an adjudicator to determine an adjudication on the basis of a view of the law for which neither party has contended. An adjudicator may be free, as it were, to make an unreviewable error of law based on the submissions of one of the parties. He should not be so free where the error is all his own work and might have been avoided by affording natural justice.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] However, at least two qualifications have been placed on this requirement of natural justice, relating:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] to the ‘materiality’ of the issue on which natural justice is denied; and
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] whether further submissions may have persuaded the adjudicator.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] On this first qualification, in Ball Construction Pty Ltd v Conart Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 124, Douglas J said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The principal [sic] that natural justice requires an adjudicator to notify the parties of an intention to decide a dispute on a basis for which neither party has contended does not necessarily vitiate a decision where the issue is not material to the decision made.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] On this second qualification, in Trysams Pty Ltd v Club Constructions (NSW) Pty Ltd [2008] NSWSC 399, McDougall J said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] [i]t must be at least arguable that meaningful submissions could have been put if an opportunity to put them had been afforded: i.e., that there was something to be put that might well persuade the adjudicator to change his or her mind.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] This above passage was cited by McMurdo J in David & Gai Spankie & Northern Investments Holdings Pty Ltd v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors (no 2) [2010] QSC 166 at [16].
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The threshold for further submissions in this context has been considered to be something ‘that might well persuade the adjudicator to change his or her mind’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Vis Constructions Ltd v Anor v Cockburn & Anor [2006] QSC 416. An adjudicator had determined the existence of a construction contract on the basis of the existence of an “arrangement” of a kind that was not contended by either party. In declaring this decision void, Jones J held that the adjudicator ought to have provided the parties an opportunity to be heard on the issue, thus denying the parties natural justice.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] David & Gai Spankie & Northern Investments Holdings Pty Ltd v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors (no 2) [2010] QSC 166. The adjudicator adopted an interpretation of the liquidated damages clause of the contract (clause 34.7) on a basis that neither party had advanced or addressed in their arguments. The Court held that the adjudicator should have sought further submissions from the parties on the issue, and that a substantial denial of natural justice had occurred. The Court declared the adjudication decision void.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] McNab Developments (Qld) Pty Ltd v MAK Construction Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QCA 232. One ground of appeal before the Court of Appeal was on the adjudicator’s interpretation of, similarly, a liquidated damage clause under the contract (clause 28(j)). The appellant contended that the adjudicator’s decision was on a basis that the parties had not addressed. The Court held, rather, that the parties had each made respective arguments as to clause 28(j) and that the adjudicator preferred a view that accorded with the submissions of the first respondent. This ground of appeal failed.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Caltex Refineries (Qld) Pty Ltd & Anor v Allstate Access (Australia) Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 223. The adjudicator misconstrued a term of the contract in a manner inconsistent with the submissions of the parties. While the applicants had, in their adjudication response, appeared to anticipate a risk that the adjudicator might reach a view favourable to the first respondent on a basis different to the first respondent’s submissions, the Court held that the reasoning adopted by the adjudicator could not have been reasonably anticipated by the applicants, who were not afforded an opportunity of addressing the reasoning adopted by the adjudicator.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd v ASAP Plasterers Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 145. The adjudicator was held to have a ‘fundamental misunderstanding’ of the parties’ submissions in relation to one of the variation claims (variation 24). The result was that the adjudicator decided the claimant’s entitlement to the variation claim on a different basis than contended by either party.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Probuild Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd v Shade Systems Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 770. The adjudicator did not give effect to a deduction of a retention amount in an adjudication application, and therefore arrived at an adjudicated amount greater than the amount claimed in the adjudication application. In rejecting the plaintiff’s claim that there was a denial of procedural fairness by the adjudicator, Emmett AJA held that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The function of the Adjudicator was to determine, in the light of the Payment Claim and Payment Schedule, the amount of the progress payment, if any, that should be made by Probuild to Shade Systems under the Subcontract.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Ostwald Bros Pty Ltd v Jaylon Pacific Pty Ltd & Ors [2016] QSC 240. The adjudicator misconstrued the effect of an extension of time clause and liquidated damages clause, and in doing so, decided the Respondent’s claim for liquidated damages on a basis that was not contended for in the submissions of either party to the adjudication. Justice Burns held that, in circumstances where no submission could have been made to the adjudicator which might have produced a different result, the adjudication decision was void for the adjudicator’s substantial denial of natural justice. 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] INPEX Operations Australia Pty Ltd & Anor v JKC Australia LNG Pty Ltd & Anor [2017] NTSC 45. The adjudicator, prior to making his determination, wrote to both parties requesting further submissions. The adjudicator’s request for submissions was limited to whether the provisions in Division 5 of the Construction Contracts (Security of Payments) Act (NT) was implied into the contract (something which both parties agreed was incorrect), and barred the parties from making submissions about the consequences of those provisions being implied. Justice Kelly held that an adjudicator had denied procedural fairness to INPEX by failing to give INPEX notice of the basis upon which he was going to make his determination, being a basis which neither party had contended for.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] John Holland Pty Ltd v Chidambara [2017] WASC 179. Justice Chaney set aside an adjudication determination on the basis that the adjudicator denied the applicant procedural fairness by his determination on a basis neither party could have reasonably contended for. In that case, John Holland was unaware that the adjudicator was proposing to base his decision on an interpretation that the conduct of the parties had caused the date for completion to be abandoned. His Honour was not satisfied that had the opportunity been provided to John Holland to address the issues relied upon by the adjudicator, its submissions would not have affected the adjudicator’s decision. His Honour ordered the monies paid by John Holland to the subcontractor in result of the determination, to be repaid by the subcontractor. 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Watkins Contracting Pty Ltd v Hyatt Ground Engineering Pty Ltd [2018] QSC 65. Brown J confirmed that, only in circumstances where an adjudicator makes a decision which is clearly on a basis that neither party had contended, will this amount to a breach of natural justice. In that case, Hyatt made submissions in its adjudication application on additional documents that it contended formed part of the entire contract (i.e. in addition to the written contract), with Watkins responding with its own interpretation of the additional documents that it considered comprised the contract. The adjudicator decided that, as neither party had convinced him to stray from the obvious interpretation that the contract comprised only the written contract, none of the additional documents comprised the contract. Brown J held that as each party had contended, and was provided the opportunity to make submissions on, what it considered to be the correct construction of the contract, the fact that the adjudicator did not decide the adjudication directly reflecting either parties’ submission, does not mean that it amounts to a breach of natural justice.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] A failure to request further submissions. In the context of BCIPA, an adjudicator is not obliged to seek further submissions from the parties.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Abel Point Marina (Whitsundays) Pty Ltd v Uher & Anor [2006] QSC 295, despite the adjudicator forming the view that there was insufficient information before him to determine the date for practical completion and the date of practical completion, Wilson J held that the adjudicator did not breach any rules of natural justice in not asking for further submissions. Wilson J found that the adjudicator had complied with the Act in terms of affording procedural fairness, and was not obliged to seek further submissions.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 307, Philip McMurdo J held that the respondent was not denied natural justice because the adjudicator failed to request further submissions in response to an assertion by the claimant that the adjudication response raised new reasons for withholding payment which were not raised in the payment schedule. His Honour held that the respondent was not deprived of an opportunity to respond to the claimant’s assertion, because it could have made submissions to the adjudicator on this point, even though such submissions had not been requested by the adjudicator. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Ku-Ring-Gai Council v Ichor Constructions Pty Ltd [2014] NSWSC 1534, Stevenson J held that given that the ‘object of the Act is to provide a speedy resolution of progress claims’, the adjudicator could not be criticised ‘for failing to seek further information from the parties than that already placed before him’.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Modscape Pty Ltd v Francis [2017] TASSC 55, Chief Justice Blow of the Supreme Court of Tasmania held that where an adjudicator requests further submissions on an issue, it is not a denial of natural justice where the adjudicator determines the issue in dispute in contemplation of a judgment which neither party contended. The adjudicator had invited further submissions from the parties to adjudication regarding a question of ‘issue estoppel’ and subsequently made his determination on that question in consideration of Kugliowski v Metrobus [2004] 220 CLR 363, a case neither party had included in its submissions or further submissions. The question for Blow CJ was whether the adjudicator denied the applicant natural justice by failing to make a second request for further submissions in relation to the decision in Metrobus. Blow CJ relevantly held:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my view the duty to afford the parties natural justice did not require the adjudicator to invite further submissions as to matters of detail relating to the case law as to issue estoppel.  In the words of McDougall J in Musico v Davenport (above) at [107], he did not "come to a … determination on a particular ground for which neither party … contended". He accepted Stowe's submission that Modscape was precluded from re-agitating the issue as to whether it could have any liability to Stowe at all. In the words of Einstein J in Procorp Civil (above), he determined the matter on a basis that had been advanced by a party.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Considering an authority not referred to by either party. In ABC Glass & Aluminium Pty Ltd v Nik Nominees Pty Ltd & Anor [2019] QSC 171, Mullins J held that an adjudicator will not necessarily breach the rules of natural justice/procedural fairness when, first, he or she relies on an authority that was not referred to by either party in their submissions, second,  if he or she fails to provide the parties with an opportunity to make further submissions in respect of the authority, and third, if he or she misstates the effect of the authority. The court found that the applicant had not been denied procedural fairness in the circumstances since the misstated authority did not affect the overall decision of the adjudicator.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.19    Consequences of a denial of natural justice
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] An adjudication decision will be set aside where there is a ‘substantial’ or ‘material’ denial of natural justice: see Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd v Ian James Ericson t/as Flea’s Concreting & Anor [2011] QSC 327, [122] (McMurdo J); Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 168, [20] (Byrne SJA).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also Fifty Property Investments Pty Ltd v O’Mara [2006] NSWSC 428; (2007) 23 BCL 35 where Bereton J said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The result of a denial of natural justice is that the decision is void, even if the decision would not have  been affected by any submissions which might have been made had an opportunity to make them been afforded. While, as a matter of discretion, relief might be declined if it can be shown that the denial of natural justice could not possibly have made a difference to the outcome, all that a plaintiff need establish is that the denial of natural justice deprived it of the possibility of a better outcome, and in order to negate that possibility it is necessary to conclude that a properly conducted adjudication could not possibly have produced a different result
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In Livingstone Shire Council v EarthTEC Pty Ltd and Ors [2018] QSC 271, Lyons SJA declared an adjudication decision void for jurisdictional error on the basis that, among other things, the adjudicator failed to accord Livingstone natural justice by not providing the parties’ with an opportunity to make submissions on the adjudicator’s finding, which was a basis not contended for by either party. In that case, the adjudicator misdirected himself by construing section 26(2)(a) of BCIPA (Qld) (equivalent to s 88(2)(a) of BIFSOPA (Qld)) as prohibiting him from considering the submission made by Livingstone Shire Council that, amounts paid into court in satisfaction of the notices of charge issued pursuant to the Subcontractors’ Charges Act 1974 (Qld) should be taken into account when calculating the adjudicated amount. In declaring the decision void, Lyons SJA followed the authority in Szbel v Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs [2006] 228 CLR 152, finding that the adjudicator is required to advise of any adverse conclusion which was not based on the material before the adjudicator and allow an opportunity to provide further submissions. Her Honour held that, as the basis for the adjudicator’s decision was not raised in either parties’ submissions, and as the adjudicator did not give the parties an opportunity to make the submission in relation to that intended finding, the adjudicator failed to accord Livingstone natural justice.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.20    Fraud
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Hodgson JA in Brodyn Pty Ltd t/as Time Coast and Quality v Davenport & Anor [2004] NSWCA 394; (2004) 61 NSWLR 421 considered fraud to be a sufficient reason to set aside an adjudicator’s decision. Relevantly, his Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] If there is fraud of the claimant in which the adjudicator is also involved, the determination will be void because the adjudicator has not bona fide attempted to exercise the power. If the determination is induced by fraud of the claimant in which the adjudicator is not involved, then I am inclined to think that the determination is not void but voidable; and it is liable to be set aside by proceedings of the kind appropriate to judgments obtained by fraud.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Following the decision in Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531, in Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd v Tan James Ericson t/as Flea’s Concreting [2011] QSC 327, McMurdo J discussed fraud as follows:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The impact of Kirk upon the review of an adjudication under the Act has now been considered by the Court of Appeal in this State in Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd and by the Court of Appeal in New South Wales in Chase Oyster Bar Pty Ltd v Hamo Industries Pty Ltd. To the extent that the JR Act would purport to remove this Court’s power to grant relief for jurisdictional error on the part of an adjudicator, it is invalid as beyond State legislative power.  Certiorari may be granted where a relevant decision has been obtained through fraud, including where the fraud is that of a party which is subject to the decision:  SZFDE v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship. In Kirk, the joint judgment quoted this passage from Colonial Bank of Australasia v Willan where the Privy Council said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “It is, however, scarcely necessary to observe that the effect of [such a privative provision] is not absolutely to deprive the Supreme Court of its power to issue a writ of certiorari to bring up the proceedings of the inferior Court, but to control and limit its action on such writ.  There are numerous cases in the books which establish that, notwithstanding the privative clause in a statute, the Court of Queen’s Bench will grant a certiorari; but some of those authorities establish, and none are inconsistent with, the proposition that in any such case that Court will not quash the order removed, except upon the ground either of a manifest defect of jurisdiction in the tribunal that made it, or of manifest fraud in the party procuring it.”
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] So whilst certiorari could be granted for the “manifest fraud in the party procuring [the decision]”, that was a distinct ground from jurisdictional error.  The same distinction was made in Kirk, where in the joint judgment, fraud as a ground for certiorari was expressly put aside from consideration. Neither Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd nor Chase Oyster Bar Pty Ltd v Hamo Industries Pty Ltd involved the fraud ground.  Therefore, on the present authorities, the expressed exclusion of judicial review of an adjudicator’s decision, according to s 18 of the JR Act (and its Schedule 1, Part 2), may still be in place so far as the fraud ground is concerned.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Where an adjudicator’s decision is affected by fraud, equitable relief may be granted to the extent necessary to avoid the consequence of the fraud: see Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd v Tan James Ericson t/as Flea’s Concreting [2011] QSC 327, [135], [149]-[151] (McMurdo J).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd v Tan James Ericson t/as Flea’s Concreting [2010] QSC 156.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Glavcom Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 126, Ball J considered whether an adjudication decision had been obtained by fraud in circumstances where a subcontractor provided a statutory declaration to the head contractor, as required by the subcontract, which falsely stated that the subcontractor had paid its workers’ compensation insurance premiums. Whilst concluding that the contractual requirement to provide a statutory declaration was void pursuant to s 34 of the NSW Act, his Honour also determined that there had been no fraud on the part of the subcontractor, because when the director of the subcontract signed the statutory declaration, he did not know that the declaration was false and was not recklessly indifferent to its truth or otherwise. In considering the fact that the statutory declaration had been prepared by a person other than the director, his Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …when [the director] swore in the statutory declaration that he was in a position to know the truth of the statements contained in the statutory declaration and that to the best of his knowledge and belief workers compensation premiums had been paid, in my opinion, he was entitled to rely on what he was told by others who were involved in the administration of the company and could be expected to know the relevant facts. The statement that he was in a position to know the relevant facts did not require him to undertake his own investigation of the matter.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In QC Communications NSW Pty Ltd v CivComm Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 1095, Ball J considered whether an adjudication determination awarded in favour of the defendant should be set aside on the ground that it was procured by fraud. His Honour held that the determination was induced by fraud due to the fact that the defendant relied on invoices in support of its adjudication application that it knew were false as they included claims for work that the defendant knew had not been performed. Ball J concluded that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my opinion, the fraud was sufficiently significant and widespread that it had a substantial effect on the Adjudicator’s determination. As a result, the determination should be set aside.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.21    Abuse of process
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Ridgeway v The Queen [1995] HCA 66; (1995) 184 CLR 19, Gaudron J said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Abuse of process cannot be restricted to "defined and closed categories" because notions of justice and injustice, as well as other considerations that bear on public confidence in the administration of justice, must reflect contemporary values and, as well, take account of the circumstances of the case.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also Williams v Spautz [1992] HCA 34; (1992) 174 CLR 509.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In John Holland Pty Ltd v Schneider Electric Buildings Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 159, Applegarth J said that BCIPA ‘as a whole generally manifests an intention to prevent repetitious re-agitation of the same issues’. His Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] I adopt, with respect, the conclusions reached by the Court in Dualcorp that the NSW Act as a whole generally manifests an intention to prevent repetitious re-agitation of the same issues. The same construction should be applied to the Queensland Act. The conclusion that, subject to certain exceptions, the Act precludes the re-agitation of the same issues that have been earlier determined does not mean that the same issue may not arise in a later adjudication. The issue that is later sought to be re-agitated must have been earlier determined for the principle of issue estoppel to apply. As Macfarlan JA observed by way of illustration in Dualcorp, if a progress claim were rejected by an adjudicator because it was premature by reason of the date stipulated for payment by the contract, a later claim made on a timely basis would not be precluded. A further illustration arises in the facts of O’Brien in which the first adjudicator did not determine the date for practical completion and therefore the entitlement of the applicant to liquidated damages. It was therefore open to seek a determination from a later adjudicator about the date of practical completion and a claimed entitlement to deduct any liquidated damages on the hearing of a later, different claim, and on the basis of additional material and submissions.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, as was noted by Peter Lyons J in Spankie & Ors v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2010] QSC 336, the decision in Dualcorp concerned an adjudication decision having been made. His Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …It will be recalled that in Dualcorp there had been a binding adjudication of the first claim. It is, in my view, well understandable and consistent with the correct construction of the provisions of the BCIPA that once an adjudication has occurred, there can be no re-agitation of the same issues.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] That is not the case here. The effect of the decision of Philip McMurdo J is, it is common ground, that there is no adjudication by an adjudicator under the BCIPA of the respondent's earlier claim.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The applicant seeks to contend that the conclusion of Applegarth J is to be adopted and extended to a case where there is no binding determination. I see nothing in his Honour's statement of his conclusion for that view; nor do I see any compelling reason so to extend his Honour's conclusion. Indeed, it seems to me to be inconsistent with s17(6). By that I mean that if an earlier claim includes an amount, and a later claim includes that amount and other amounts, there is, in the sense which the applicants seek to say is prohibited, an authorised re-agitation, at least by a raising of it in the claim, of the liability of a party to the construction contract for the amount common to both claims.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Merely pursuing statutory remedies under the Act (such as payment claims) and other remedies available to a claimant concurrently will not amount to an abuse of process: Civil Mining & Construction Pty Ltd v Isaac Regional Council [2014] QSC 231, [22] (Philip McMurdo J).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The use of the Act to pursue a final payment claim does not amount to an abuse of process: Civil Mining & Construction Pty Ltd v Isaac Regional Council [2014] QSC 231, [12] (Philip McMurdo J), citing Kingston Building (Australia) v Dial D [2013] NSWSC 2010, [19].
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Concurrent arbitration proceedings and payment claim. In Civil Mining & Construction Pty Ltd v Isaac Regional Council [2014] QSC 231, the Court held that it was not an abuse of process for a claimant to pursue a payment claim under the Act concurrently with contractual arbitration. Philip McMurdo J distinguished J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Galform Pty Ltd & Ors [2008] QSC 205 on the basis of the payment claim in the latter case being an abuse of process as a breach of an undertaking given by the claimant to the court.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Referral to arbitration. Vantage Holdings Pty Ltd v JHC Developments Group Pty Ltd [2011] QSC 155. A respondent sought a stay of proceedings to recover amounts under BCIPA on the grounds that the proceeding was ‘in respect of a matter agreed to be referred to arbitration’ (at [37]). The Court considered that the arbitration proceedings, at their highest, concerned the proper interpretation of the contract in relation to variation claims (at [38]) and held that the pursuit of statutory remedies under BCIPA in conjunction with arbitration as under the contract, is consistent with BCIPA (at [44]).
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.22    Issue estoppel and abuse of process
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Dualcorp
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Dualcorp Pty Ltd v Remo Constructions Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 69; (2009) 74 NSWLR 190, Macfarlan JA (Handley AJA agreeing) held that ‘principles’ of issue estoppel were applicable under the NSW Act. Relevantly, his Honour concluded:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Thus the primary judge here was correct in considering that “principles akin to res judicata” or “abuse of process” were applicable. Consistent with that broad description, I conclude that the principles of issue estoppel were applicable. Primarily because temporal considerations are of particular significance in relation to progress claims, the analogy between an adjudicator’s determination and a completed cause of action which the principles of res judicata would require is an incomplete one. It is best that the applicable principles be recognised to be those of issue estoppel. The more general principle of abuse of process is probably also applicable but it is unnecessary to reach a final view about this. This principle involves a broad concept “insusceptible of a formulation comprising closed categories” (Batistatos v Roads and Traffic Authority of New South Wales [2006] HCA 27; (2006) 226 CLR 256 at[9]) but certainly including within its ambit an attempt to “litigate anew a case which has formerly been disposed of by earlier proceedings” (Walton v Gardiner [1993] HCA 77; (1992-3) 177 CLR 378 at 393).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Allsop P concluded that ‘the Act as a whole generally manifests an intention to prevent repetitious reagitation of the same issues’ and found it unnecessary to consider the principles of estoppel. 
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Qualifications of Dualcorp
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Following Dualcorp Pty Ltd v Remo Constructions Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 69; (2009) 74 NSWLR 190, the principles of issue estoppel and abuse of process were further discussed and refined in:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Perform (NSW) Pty Ltd v Mev-Aus Pty Ltd [2009] NSWSC 416;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] The University of Sydney v Cadence Australia Pty Limited [2009] NSWSC 635;
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Urban Traders v Paul Michael [2009] NSWSC 1072; and
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Watpac Constructions v Austin Corp [2010] NSWSC 168.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Firstly, in Perform (NSW) Pty Ltd v Mev-Aus Pty Ltd [2009] NSWSC 416, Rein J followed the approach in Dualcorp, considering that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The contention that a party, having put forward a claim framed in a particular way, when the claim is rejected may serve another claim framed in a different way is in my view entirely inconsistent with the object of the Act and the principle of finality to which Macfarlan JA referred in Dualcorp. In my view the second claim is incompetent because it ventilates issues which have already been decided. As a further ground I respectfully adopt the approach of Allsop P and also find that it is incompetent because the Act permits only one payment claim to be made in respect of the same work, services or goods supplied. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Perform, the claimant had submitted a second claim that was substantially similar with an earlier claim the subject of an adjudication decision and awarded an amount of $nil. Rein J gave five reasons why intervention was appropriate to prevent the second claim from proceeding to adjudication:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (1) The approach taken in Dualcorp is that a second claim is not one that is within the intent of the Act and on the approach taken by Allsop P, a second claim is not one permitted by the Act and hence is not governed by the Act.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (2) As Mr Nicholls pointed out the remedy for abuse of process or issue estoppel is to apply for a dismissal or permanent stay of the proceedings, and there is no mechanism for such an application before an adjudicator.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (3) The continuation of the claim or proceedings is the very matter which the party asserts is the abuse. It is required to expend time and money and "is vexed" with a fresh process when it ought not to be.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (4) The intent of the Act is to provide a means of speedy determination of claims for payment to be made on an interim basis not to burden the parties to a construction contract with a prolonged quasi-litigious process.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (5) I think it is important not to lose sight of the fact that a determination under the Act produces no final result. It is a scheme for interim payment only and a party who is unsuccessful before the adjudicator has rights and remedies at law to correct any deficiencies in evidence or arguments at a final hearing: see s 32 of the Act. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Rein J held that ‘[w]here steps have been taken in breach of and/or against the intent of a statutory scheme I think it is part of this Court's function to step in and prevent that occurring’. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Secondly, in The University of Sydney v Cadence Australia Pty Limited [2009] NSWSC 635, the claimant sought to re-agitate the same claim as part of an expanded second claim. Hammerschlag J concluded that ‘a substantial and unseverable part’ of the second claim had already been adjudicated. His Honour held that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …the first defendant has exhausted its statutory entitlement to claim the delay costs which were the subject of the first claim.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The second claim and the present application accordingly have as their object the obtaining of an advantage beyond what the law offers and together they are an abuse of process.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In addition, given that the Act gives no right to re-make a payment claim which has earlier been made and adjudicated upon, the second claim, to the extent that it seeks to do so is not a payment claim within the meaning of the Act. It may be that where there is duplication of a part but not the whole of an earlier claim, whether there is sufficient duplication to fairly take a second claim outside the definition of payment claim is a matter of fact and degree. If it is, that requirement is clearly satisfied here by reason of the significant degree of overlap. No one suggested that it was possible or feasible for the adjudicator to deal with only part of the present application. Dealing with the present application would require the adjudicator to re-perform a statutory function which he has already discharged. It follows that the first defendant has no right to apply for the adjudication of the second claim and, contrary to what the first defendant put, the second defendant has no jurisdiction to adjudicate it.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] His Honour’s statement ‘that the Act gives no right to re-make a payment claim which has earlier been made and adjudicated upon’ was qualified in Urban Traders v Paul Michael [2009] NSWSC 1072 and Watpac Constructions v Austin Corp [2010] NSWSC 168 by McDougall J.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Thirdly, in Urban Traders v Paul Michael [2009] NSWSC 1072, McDougall J set out the following qualifications on whether a payment claim may constitute an abuse of process:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It does not follow from the decisions to which I have referred that every repetition, in a subsequent payment claim, of a claim made in an earlier payment must amount to an abuse of process. That is so even if that earlier payment claim has been the subject of an adjudicator’s determination. The relevant concept is not abuse of process at large. It is abuse of the processes of the Act: specifically, the processes of the Act designed to ensure that builders and subcontractors (and of course others) received prompt and progressive payment for construction work performed or related goods and services provided. The question of whether there has been an abuse the processes of the Act must take into account relevant provisions of the Act. Specifically:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (1) s 13(6) of the Act recognises that a claimant may include in a payment claim an amount that has been the subject of a previous payment claim; and 

(2) s 22(4) of the Act deals, to an extent, with a repeated claim by providing that if particular construction work or related goods and services have been valued by an adjudicator, an adjudicator in a subsequent adjudication application is to give them the same value unless satisfied that the value has changed since that previous determination. 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Further, whether or not the repetition of a claim amounts to an abuse of process requires consideration of all relevant contextual facts. In addition, it requires consideration of the reasons why the courts intervene to prevent abuse of process. Those reasons include intervention to prevent a person from being vexed by having to reargue an issue already authoritatively decided. Thus, in deciding whether a repetition of a claim amounts to abuse of process, it may be relevant to take into consideration whether, because of fresh claims that are advanced, the respondent will be required to defend itself in any event.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] I do not think that it is possible to state in some exhaustive fashion what combination of factors, including repetition, will lead to the conclusion that there is an abuse of process. (See, as to this, French CJ, Gummow, Hayne and Crennan JJ in Jeffery & Katauskas Pty Limited v SST Consulting Pty Limited [2009] HCA 43 at [27].) My concern is simply to make it clear that, in my opinion, it would be inconsistent with the provisions of the Act to which I have referred above to hold that repetition, by itself and without more, always amounts to abuse of process.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Fourthly, in Watpac Constructions v Austin Corp [2010] NSWSC 168, McDougall J then went on to suggest the further following qualifications:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …that there is no necessary vice in submitting payment claims, incorporating claims for work that were made in earlier payment claims, on fresh reference dates where work is continuing under the contract. The vice arises (if at all) only where work has been completed, and the claimant takes advantage of the 12 month period referred to in s 13(4)(b) to “create” a reference date by serving, as a fresh payment claim, a claim that has been the subject of an earlier payment claim. 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In this context, Mr Kidd relied also on what Macfarlan JA said at [53]: that s 13(6) of the Act did not authorise the inclusion in a later payment claim of an amount that had been the subject of an earlier adjudication. To allow this, his Honour said, would be inconsistent with the carefully structured provisions of the Act dealing with adjudication and other provisions pointing to considerations of finality:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] [53] Secondly, as earlier mentioned, under s 13(5) only one payment claim may be served in respect of each reference date under the construction contract. There is a qualification that the claimant may include in a payment claim an amount that has been the subject of a previous claim. However, taking into account the Act’s objectives and its provisions, I do not consider that that qualification can, or should, be read as authorising the inclusion in a payment claim of an amount which has been the subject of an earlier adjudication. It would in my view be inconsistent with the carefully structured procedures as to adjudication and the provisions, which I am in the course of mentioning, pointing towards finality of adjudication determinations to give it that construction.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] I confess that I have some difficulty with this aspect of the reasoning of Macfarlan JA. Section 13(6) authorises the inclusion in a later payment claim of an amount that has been the subject of an earlier payment claim. Section 22(4) contemplates that the amount that is “re-claimed” might have been the subject of earlier adjudication and provides for the consequences. I accept, of course, that s 22(4) does not deal with all occasions on which amounts are “re-claimed”, and that it is not an exclusive or exhaustive code dealing with the treatment of “re-claimed” amounts. Nonetheless, I do not understand how, in those circumstances, it can be said that the Act impliedly prohibits the inclusion, in a later payment claim, of an amount that has been the subject of a prior adjudication. His Honour’s approach would, with respect, make it difficult for the claimant to seek to have a later adjudicator revalue the work on appropriate evidence; yet the prospect of revaluation is expressly preserved by s 22(4).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] McDougall J considered that, if part of a payment claim is held to be invalid as a repetition of an earlier payment claim, that invalidity should not extend to the whole of the payment claim. His Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] if on the facts of a particular case the consequences of repetition of a payment claim that has been the subject of an earlier adjudicator are, or include, invalidity, those consequences should not extend to, so as infect with invalidity, the whole of the payment claim if and to the extent that it includes claims in respect of work that has not been the subject of a prior adjudication. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Arconic Australia Rolled Products Pty Limited v McMahon Services Australia Pty Ltd [2017] NSWSC 1114, McDougall J held that the defendant’s actions had fallen short of an abuse of process under the Act, regardless of the defendant’s inadequate attempts to get its claims decided by an adjudicator. In that case, the defendant submitted three adjudication applications for payment claims 13 and 14. The first application for claim 13 was withdrawn after the defendant included ‘without prejudice’ documents in their application. The second application for claim 13 failed as the claim that was advanced in the adjudication application was not the same as the claim advanced in the payment claim. The application for claim 14 did not have a supporting reference date and, therefore, also failed. The plaintiff was put to a lot of expense and trouble because of the inadequate attempts made by the defendant to have its claim decided by an adjudicator. McDougall J considered whether the adjudicator could determine payment claim 13. His Honour held that to be an abuse of process, inter alia, the adjudicator must have previously dealt with the claim on its merits. In this instance, the adjudicator had not dealt with payment claim 13 on its merits, but rather found it was not one he could deal with. On that basis, his Honour found that there was no abuse of process and the adjudicator would be free to deal with the previous issues in an upcoming application. 

[Normal] In Harlech Enterprises Pty Ltd v Beno Excavations Pty Ltd [2022] ACTCA 42, the ACT Court of Appeal held that the doctrine of issue estoppel does not apply to adjudication decisions, as such decisions are “interim” (i.e. not final). This case arose in circumstances where an adjudicator in a second adjudication applied the principle of issue estoppel when considering and deciding similar issues determined in an earlier adjudication determination. In finding that issue estoppel did not apply, the Court of Appeal highlighted that: 

[List Paragraph] the second adjudication involved different work that was completed years apart;

[List Paragraph] the ACT Security of Payment Act (s.24(4)) expressly deals with situations where a later adjudicator may be bound to an earlier adjudicator’s determination, and that is limited to “…an adjudication application that involves the working out of the value of the construction work or of the related goods and services… previously decided…” (noting that BIFSOPA has almost identical wording in s.87); and

[List Paragraph] there was no attempt to re-assess or evaluate the same work in the second adjudication, as such the provisions in the Act binding a subsequent adjudicator did not apply. 

[Normal] In its decision, the Court distinguished the facts from those in Dualcorp Pty Ltd v Remo Constructions Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 69 and held that issue estoppel will only apply in the limited instances Allsop J identified in Dualcorp, such as repeated claims and other forms of abuse of process.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Approach in Queensland re Dualcorp Estoppel
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Queensland, the principles of issue estoppel and abuse of process, as qualified by Urban Traders and Watpac, were adopted in AE & E Australia Pty Ltd v Stowe Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 135 in the context of BCIPA.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The decision in Dualcorp was followed by Applegarth J in John Holland Pty Ltd v Schneider Electric Buildings Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 159.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The principles surrounding abuse of process fell for further consideration in Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous & Ors [2016] QSC 96. In that case, Jackson J considered the decision in Cadence and decided that, as a matter of fact and degree, there was not sufficient duplication between the first and second payment claims before him. His Honour said that where the reasoning in Cadence is the basis of an argument that a payment claim is invalid because it includes re-agitated claims, the duplication must be such as to take the second payment claim outside the meaning of what is a payment claim for the purposes of section 17 of BCIPA (equivalent to section 75 of the Act). 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] His Honour further said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] If the label “abuse of process” is attached to facts by which a claimant or respondent purports to engage the processes under the Payments Act invalidly, this court’s power to interfere at the stage before an adjudication decision is made must in most cases involve a threat of jurisdictional error by an adjudicator as the repository of power under the Payments Act.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my view it is unnecessary to resolve any doubt about the authority of Cadence to decide the present case. The Payments Act does not expressly provide what is the consequences of including claims in a payment claim which the Act impliedly prohibits. 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] If such a payment claim proceeds to an adjudication decision, even though the Act impliedly prohibits the adjudicator from including the impermissible claim in a progress payment and adjudication amount, s 100(4) provides that the court may allow the part of the decision not affected by jurisdictional error to remain binding.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The express object of the Payments Act is to ensure that a person is entitled to receive, and is able to recover, progress payments if the person undertakes to carry out construction work under a construction contract and that object is to be achieved by granting an entitlement to progress payments and establishing a procedure for making a payment claim and going through the statutory process to decision by adjudication of a disputed claim.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The purpose of the provisions of the Payments Act, including s 17, as stated in the express objects, is not best achieved by an interpretation of those provisions that the inclusion of an impermissible claim in a payment claim invalidates an otherwise valid payment claim.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority, the plurality said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “A better test for determining the issue of validity is to ask whether it was a purpose of the legislation that an act done in breach of the provision should be invalid. This has been the preferred approach of courts in this country in recent years, particularly in New South Wales. In determining the question of purpose, regard must be had to ‘the language of the relevant provision and the scope and object of the whole statute’.” (footnotes omitted)
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Having regard to these factors, in my view, there is a sufficient difference between this case and Cadence to decide that the alleged duplication in this case was not so significant as to draw the conclusion that Payment Claim 38 was not a payment claim within the meaning of s 17 of the Payments Act.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my view, putting to one side any question of “issue estoppel” or “Anshun estoppel” considered below, there is no discretionary procedural power to stay that arose upon the making of Payment Claim 38, as an abuse of process, and there is no invalidity that attached to the third adjudication application as an adjudication application.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Beno Excavations Pty Ltd v Harlech Enterprises Pty Ltd (No 2) [2021] ACTSC 296, Mossop J determined that the adjudicator had fallen into jurisdictional error in applying the principle of issue estoppel to subsequent adjudication applications involving different works. Mossop J held a narrow view of the application of issue estoppel and found that it only applies where the same claims are being repeated and therefore, the principle of issue estoppel does not apply to subsequent adjudication applications concerning different works.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In AE & E Australia Pty Ltd v Stowe Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 135, Applegarth J set out the principles applicable to issue estoppel in respect of BCIPA in the following way:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] I will avoid repeating the analysis from the New South Wales authorities of the principles of issue estoppel that apply in connection with determinations by adjudicators under the Act. I respectfully adopt the principles of issue estoppel discussed in Dualcorp, Urban Traders and Watpac. They concern a statute in the same terms as the Queensland Act. The legislation manifests a general intention to preclude re-agitation of the same issues. I should follow the interpretation of identical New South Wales legislation unless I conclude that the interpretation is plainly wrong. I do not. To the extent that certain general statements in the judgments in Dualcorp, Performa and Cadence require qualification for the reasons given by McDougall J in Urban Traders and Watpac, I respectfully adopt the interpretation of McDougall J. These qualifications do not have any significance upon the disposition of the present application.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It is sufficient to state the relevant principles that I intend to apply in determining this application:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (a)    The concept of issue estoppel, insofar as it is applicable to determinations by adjudicators under the Act, includes the Anshun principle.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (b)    For the purposes of issue estoppel, the issue determined by the adjudicator is to be ascertained by reference to the payment claim, payment schedule, relevant supporting submissions, the provisions of the contract and the provisions of the Act. Accordingly, the relevant issue for determination by the adjudicator was whether Stowe was entitled to a progress payment pursuant to the statutory mechanisms contained in the Act in respect of the relevant items for the amounts claimed.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (c)    The particular basis upon which the adjudicator determined each contested item and the December Claim in general (whether it be lack of legal entitlement, insufficiency of explanation of the basis of the claim, insufficiency of evidence, a failure to establish an entitlement in the amount claimed or some other basis) does not detract from the proposition that the issue he determined was Stowe’s entitlement to an interim progress payment in the amounts claimed by it, save for claims that were withdrawn.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (d)    The principle of issue estoppel discussed in the majority judgment in Dualcorp applies when the previous adjudicator does not allow a claim for want of evidence.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (e)    The principle of issue estoppel applies when the previous adjudicator determines that an entitlement to be paid has not been made out for one or more of a variety of reasons that include:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (i)    there is no entitlement
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] (ii)    the claimed entitlement is not made out because the basis for it is not demonstrated, or there is insufficiency of proof as to entitlement, valuation or both.Doubting of Dualcorp
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, in Caltex Refineries (Qld) Pty Ltd & Anor v Allstate Access (Australia) Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 223, Philip McMurdo J doubted the applicability and scope of issue estoppel in the context of decisions under BCIPA, stating:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Although Macfarlan JA described the outcome as a result of an issue estoppel, it is clear that his Honour had in mind something less than the operation of the common law doctrine of issue estoppel as it is usually understood.  The doctrine of issue estoppel has been said to reflect “a central and pervading tenet of the judicial system [which] is that controversies, once resolved, are not to be reopened except in a few, narrowly defined, circumstances”. Where the doctrine does apply, then subject to any qualification by legislation or agreement, it precludes the reagitation in any forum of the same issue.  Yet, the estoppel for which Allstate contends, in attempted reliance upon Dualcorp, at its highest is one in which the issue could be reagitated in some forums but not others. It is a remarkable species of issue estoppel where, having regard to s 32 of the New South Wales Act, the “entitlements inter se under the contract” are unaffected by it. The source of this more limited estoppel must be found, if at all, within the legislation. In my view, the legislation does not provide it. A contrary indication is that such an estoppel would be problematical in many ways. Is a future adjudicator, dealing with another claim under that contract, bound by the decision of the earlier adjudication (if not set aside) or that of the court?
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The limited finality described by Macfarlan JA was founded, as his Honour explained, upon the combined effect of several provisions within this statute.  Contrary to the submission for Allstate, the judgment of Macfarlan JA did not hold that the doctrine of issue estoppel applies in this context in all respects.  The judgment identifies a finality of an adjudicator’s decision in the sense of precluding a claimant from pursuing a progress payment inconsistently with determination of an issue by an adjudicator which was fundamental to that decision.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The comments of Philip McMurdo J in Caltex Refineries (Qld) Pty Ltd & Anor v Allstate Access (Australia) Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 223 were referred to with apparent approval by Jackson J in Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous & Ors [2016] QSC 96.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Ingeteam Australia Pty Ltd v Susan River Solar Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 30, the Court held that jurisdictional error in relation to licensing requirements was material because it engaged Dualcorp issue estoppel. [2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Applegarth J accepted that the issue of whether a “construction contract” under the BIF Act existed, so as to give the adjudicator jurisdiction, was a “fundamental” issue in the sense that McMurdo J used that word in Caltex Refineries (Qld) Pty Ltd & Anor v Allstate Access (Australia) Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 223. [2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] 1.    the determination of the licensing issue led the adjudicator to decline jurisdiction; and
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] 2.    it affected the Claimant’s statutory right to make a payment claim and to apply for an adjudication decision of the kind that is sought from the new adjudicator.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The Court also held that Dualcorp issue estoppel did not cease to apply to a finding on the existence or absence of a critical jurisdictional fact simply because an alternative basis existed to find a lack of jurisdiction.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Anshun estoppel
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The concept of an ‘Anshun estoppel arises from the decision in Port of Melbourne Authority v Anshun Pty Ltd [1981] HCA 45; (1981) 147 CLR 589, where the High Court said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] where a given matter becomes the subject of litigation in, and of adjudication by, a Court of competent jurisdiction, the Court requires the parties to that litigation to bring forward their whole case, and will not (except under special circumstances) permit the same parties to open the same subject of litigation in respect of matter which might have been brought forward as part of the subject in contest, but which was not brought forward, only because they have, from negligence, inadvertence, or even accident, omitted part of their case.  The plea of res judicata applies, except in special cases, not only to points upon which the Court was actually required by the parties to form an opinion and pronounce a judgment, but to every point which properly belonged to the subject of litigation, and which the parties, exercising reasonable diligence, might have brought forward at the time.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The application of an Anshun estoppel in the context of security of payment was considered in HM Hire Pty Ltd v National Plant and Equipment Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 274; [2014] 2 Qd R 44, where Applegarth J said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] A party may be estopped from raising a claim which it could have litigated in a previous proceeding if it was unreasonable for the claim not to have been so litigated. An Anshun estoppel is founded on unreasonable conduct, not abuse of process. The concepts are, however, not dissimilar.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The Anshun doctrine is not based on proof of abuse of process. It creates a test of unreasonableness, and requires proof that it was unreasonable not to have brought forward the claim in the earlier proceeding. The question of unreasonableness involves a broad merits-based judgment which takes account of the “public and private interest involved and also takes account of all of the facts of the case”. The earlier quoted observations of Lord Bingham refer to the public interest which underlie various forms of estoppel, namely that there should be “finality in litigation and that a party should not be twice vexed in the same matter.”  This public interest was said to be reinforced by the current emphasis on efficiency and economy in the conduct of litigation, in the interest of the parties and the public as a whole.  More recently, the New South Wales Court of Appeal has confirmed that in deciding whether it can be said to have been unreasonable not to rely upon a matter in the first proceeding, there is a “value judgment to be made referable to the proper conduct of modern litigation”.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] It has been held that ‘[t]he concept of issue estoppel, insofar as it is applicable to determinations by adjudicators under [BCIPA], includes the Anshun principle’: AE & E Australia Pty Ltd v Stowe Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 135, [32] (Applegarth J).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similarly, in Watpac Constructions v Austin Corp [2010] NSWSC 168, McDougall J considered the applicability of the Anshun principle of issue estoppel in the context of the NSW Act, which his Honour called the ‘extended principle’. His Honour held:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my view, both as a matter of principle and because of the clear policy of the Act, as to the finality of determinations, identified by Macfarlan JA, the doctrine of issue estoppel, in so far as it applies to determinations of adjudicators under the Act, includes the extended principle. To conclude otherwise would permit a party to retain the opportunity to resubmit claims, until finally it got an advantageous outcome, by holding back part of its case each time. Except in special circumstances, a party should put the whole of its case, in support of a particular payment claim, before an adjudicator who is charged with the statutory responsibility of deciding that parties’ entitlement to the amount claimed. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, in Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous & Ors [2016] QSC 96, Jackson J referred to the comments of Applegarth J and McDougall J in AE & E Australia Pty Ltd v Stowe Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 135 and Watpac Constructions v Austin Corp [2010] NSWSC 168 respectively, and said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] However, neither case was decided on the extended or Anshun estoppel basis. Accordingly, those statements are obiter dicta. It is not necessary to make any decision about the correctness of the availability of extended or Anshun estoppel as a matter of law in the present case because both the applicant and the respondents argued the case on the basis that Anshun estoppel was available in law. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] His Honour then considered whether any extended or Anshun estoppel arose on the facts before him, and said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Generally, in my view, the court will be slow to conclude that it is unreasonable to pursue a claim for delay costs suffered for a period that was not previously the subject of a determination for delay costs on a basis that has not been previously determined against the claimant so as to preclude such a claim.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 88.23 – Provisions of a construction contract
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In 1155 Nepean Highway Pty Ltd v Promax Buildings Pty Ltd [2020] VSCA 253, the Victorian Court of Appeal held that, despite certain documents forming part of a ‘construction contract’, an adjudicator will not commit jurisdictional error if an adjudicator does not consider that material. In this case, an adjudicator made their determination based on photographs and did not consider, nor did the claimant provide, drawings which formed part of the contract. The respondent, who was prohibited from lodging an adjudication response, argued that the failure by the adjudicator to consider the “provisions of the construction contract” (i.e. the drawings) as required under s.23(2)(b) of the Vic SOP Act (equivalent of s.88(2)(b)BIF Act), amounted to a jurisdiction error. In rejection this argument, the Court of Appeal found that ‘contract documents’ which did not constitute part of the construction contract but were incorporated by reference, are not ‘provisions of the construction contract’ that an adjudicator must consider under s 23(2)(b) of the Vic SOP Act.
