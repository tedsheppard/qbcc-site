[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 72 – Valuation of construction work and related goods and services 
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] A    Legislation
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 72    Valuation of construction work and related goods and services
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] Construction work carried out under a construction contract is to be valued—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] if the contract provides for the matter—in accordance with the contract; or
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] if the contract does not provide for the matter—having regard to—
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] the contract price for the work; and
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] any other rates or prices stated in the contract; and 
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] any variation agreed to by the parties to the contract by which the contract price, or any other rate or price stated in the contract, is to be adjusted by a specific amount; and 
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)]  if any of the work is defective, the estimated cost of rectifying the defect. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] Related goods and services supplied under a construction contract are to be valued—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] if the contract provides for the matter—in accordance with the contract; or
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] if the contract does not provide for the matter—having regard to—
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] the contract price for the goods and services; and
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] any other rates or prices stated in the contract; and
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] any variation agreed to by the parties to the contract by which the contract price, or any other rate or price stated in the contract, is to be adjusted by a specific amount; and
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] if any of the goods are defective, the estimated cost of rectifying the defect.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] For subsection (2)(b), for materials and components that are to form part of any building, structure or work arising from construction work, the only materials and components to be included in the valuation are those that have become or, on payment, will become the property of the party or other person for whom construction work is being carried out. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] In this section—
[1.1 BIFSOPA Body Text] contract price, for a construction contract, means the amount the contracted party is entitled to be paid under the contract or, if the amount can not be accurately calculated, the reasonable estimate of the amount the contracted party is entitled to be paid under the contract.
[1.1 BIFSOPA Body Text] contracted party, for a construction contract, means the party to the contract who is required to carry out the construction work under the contract.
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] B    Commentary
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 72.1    Section 72(1)(a) – ‘in accordance with the contract’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The Act modifies previous wording by replacing ‘under the contract’ with ‘if the contract provides for the matter—in accordance with the contract’. Refer to commentary above under 71.2.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 72.2    Section 72(1)(b) – ‘having regard to’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In has been held, under the NSW Act, that the factors which the adjudicator is to value the construction work ‘having regard to’: 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] ‘represent no more than matters that must be recognised and accepted by an adjudicator as matters to be taken into account in performing the specified valuation task. A provision compelling a decision maker to “have regard to” specified matters in making the particular decision does no more than require that he or she “give weight to them as a fundamental element” in coming to a conclusion’:
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 72.3    Delay Costs or Damages
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Delay costs (or delay damages) are claimable under the Act, even if labelled “damages” where the nature of the delay costs is an amount that becomes due and payable under the contract. In Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v JM Hargreaves (NSW) Pty Ltd & Ors [2005] NSWCA 228 where Hodgson JA said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my opinion, the circumstance that a particular amount may be characterised by a contract as “damages” or “interest” cannot be conclusive as to whether or not such an amount is for construction work carried out or for related goods and services supplied. Rather, any amount that a construction contract requires to be paid as part of the total price of construction work is generally, in my opinion, an amount due for that construction work, even if the contract labels it as “damages” or “interest”; while on the other hand, any amount which is truly payable as damages for breach of contract is generally not an amount due for that construction work.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Under the contract in this case, delay damages are payable only if an EOT is for a compensable cause, that is, in general some act or omission of the head contractor or the superintendent or the sub-contract superintendent; but nevertheless, they are not of their nature damages for breach but rather are additional amounts which may become due and payable under the contract (cl.34.9) and which are then to be included in progress payments (cl.37.1). They are therefore prima facie within s.9(a) of the Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, Hodgson JA drew a distinction between delay costs as representing the increased cost of construction work and representing off-site costs, or other on-site costs. This distinction went to whether the delay costs were “for” construction work, in the following terms:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] If in substance they represent the increased cost or price of construction work actually carried out, in my opinion they are clearly for construction work carried out. If they represent the cost or price of goods or services actually supplied in connection with the construction work under the contract, they are for related goods or services supplied, even if not for construction work carried out.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] If they represent off-site costs (such as office overheads) or other on-site costs, it may be a question of fact and degree whether they are for construction work carried out or for related goods and services supplied. They would in my opinion properly be regarded at least as part of the price for the totality of the construction work when completed. And it would seem artificial to say that they are excluded from the Act if they are not referable to work that has already been carried out, particularly when s.9(b) refers to the value of construction work undertaken to be carried out and related goods and services undertaken to be supplied. However, it is not necessary in this case altogether to exclude the possibility that some delay damages claimed under this contract might possibly not be for construction work carried out or related goods and services supplied within the definition of “claimed amount” in s.4; but it is certainly not obvious that this is so in relation to any of the claims in this case.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] This distinction, on what may be claimable for delay costs, was further considered in ACN 060 559 971 Pty Ltd v O’Brien [2008] 2 Qd R 396; [2007] QSC 91. Relevantly, in that case, the clause conferring an entitlement to delay costs was an amended AS4300-1995 clause 36. As amended, the clause created a liability for “such extra costs as are necessarily incurred by the Contractor by reason of the delay”. Mullins J held that this qualification:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] suggests that the payment for which the principal may be liable under clause 36 of the contract does not extend to recovery of a contribution to off site overheads (which would have been incurred by the contractor in any case) and loss of profits from other opportunities that are unable to be utilised because of the delay. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In the view of Mullins J, extra costs “incurred” by reason of the delay is distinct from extra costs “suffered” by reason of the delay.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 72.4    Preparation costs
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Kembla Coal & Coke Pty Ltd v Select Civil Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 628, McDougall J held that preparation costs were not precluded from being claimed in an adjudication, stating that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Accordingly, in the context of the Act – specifically, having regard to the objects of the Act as stated in s 3 (see paras [95] and [96] above) - and the recognition, through s 9(a), of the primacy of contractual quantification where that is available (see para [98] above, and see also s 3(4): the Act does not limit contractual entitlements), I think that a progress payment may include an amount for preparation costs where the contract so provides. In other words, where the contractual assessment of a progress payment (to which, pursuant to s 9(a), effect is to be given) includes preparation costs as a component of the entitlement, I do not think that the Act should be construed so as to deprive the claimant of that entitlement. It would be an extraordinary result if an assessment of the claimant’s rights by the Superintendent, or other appropriate person having that function under the contract, included (as the contract demands) any applicable amount of preparation costs, but if the adjudicator’s determination could not.

[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 72.6    Valuation other than in accordance with section 72
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Transgrid v Siemens Ltd [2004] NSWCA 395; (2004) 61 NSWLR 521, it was alleged that an adjudicator had failed to make the determination in accordance with the terms of the contract, however, the Court of Appeal held that any such error would not render the adjudicator’s determination invalid.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, it was said by Vickery J in SSC Plenty Road Pty Ltd v Construction Engineering (Aust) Pty Ltd [2015] VSC 631 that, given the ‘severe statutory time constraints placed on adjudicators to perform their tasks prescribed under the Act…judicial review of the valuation exercise documented in an adjudicator’s adjudication determination in Victoria ought not to be approached from an unduly critical viewpoint’. His Honour continued:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Rather, and providing the adjudicator makes the fundamental determinations earlier described, and does so by arriving at a rational assessment of value on the basis of the evidence, the Act calls for a practical and robust approach to the assessment process on the part of adjudicators and their expressed reasons. If the position was to be otherwise, the adjudication mechanism provided for by the Act, in all but the small cases, could not be made to work.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In Krongold Constructions (Aust) v SR & RS Wales [2016] VSC 94, Vickery J said that an adjudicator is bound to comply with the requirements of the VIC Act which includes a requirement to assess the value of the amount claimed. His Honour held that simply adopting the amounts claimed in the payment claim on the basis that they did not appear to be ‘incorrect, unreasonable or excessive’ was not a valid valuation process under the VIC Act. Accordingly, his Honour held that the adjudicator fell into jurisdictional error which rendered the adjudication determination void. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Unifor Australia Pty Ltd v Katrd Pty Ltd atf Morshan Unit Trust t/as Beyond Completion Projects [2012] QSC 252. A payment claim in the form of a tax invoice was supported by a spreadsheet, setting out various costings. By error and unknown to both parties, a wrong version of this spreadsheet (a later version) was included in the adjudication application. Because this spreadsheet was a later version, it did not align with the amounts under the payment claim and contained higher amounts. The adjudicator awarded the amount of the payment claim as ‘a fair and reasonable value of the additional costs’. The Court held that the adjudicator’s reasons for reaching a valuation were not referable to the contract or any agreement between the parties, were not in accordance with section 14 of BCIPA, but was something akin to an assessment on a quantum meruit. The Court held that, in so doing, the adjudicator did not have regard to the matters required under section 14 of BCIPA. The Court held that the adjudicator had committed jurisdictional error ‘by doing something which he lacked authority to do’ (at [35]), namely considering a matter not prescribed by section 26(2) and undertaking a valuation not in accordance with section 14 of BCIPA.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In Goodwin Street Developments Pty Ltd v DSD Builders Pty Ltd [2018] NSWSC 1229, McDougall J held that an adjudicator will still have carried out their statutory function in good faith under section 10(1)(b)(iv) of the NSW ACT (equivalent to section 71(1)(b)(iv) of the Act), regardless of whether their reasons are not entirely logical. In that case, the adjudicator did not provide ‘logical reasons’ as to why they downplayed the significance of the contractor’s claim for defective work. Nevertheless, his Honour held that the adjudicator’s reasons, when read in their entirety, did show that they were not satisfied that there was defective work and the reasons why. McDougall J explained the importance of not ‘overzealously or mechanically applying the description of the adjudicator’s task explained in SSC Plenty Road v Construction Engineering (Aust) Pty Ltd [2015] VSC 631’. His Honour emphasised that it is essential to take into account the extremely compressed timeframe within which an adjudicator has to work and the subsequent pressure generated.
