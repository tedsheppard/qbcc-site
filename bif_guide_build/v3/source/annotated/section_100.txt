[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 100 – Proceedings to recover unpaid amount as debt
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] A    Legislation
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 100    Proceedings to recover unpaid amount as debt
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] This section applies if a claimant starts proceedings in a court under section 78(2)(a) to recover an unpaid amount from a respondent as a debt owing to the claimant.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] Judgment in favour of the claimant is not to be given by a court unless the court is satisfied that—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the respondent did not pay the amount to the claimant on or before the due date for the progress payment to which the payment claim relates; and
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] if the respondent’s liability to pay the amount arises because of a failure to give a payment schedule—the respondent did not give the claimant a payment schedule within the time required to do so under this Act.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] The respondent is not, in those proceedings, entitled—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] to bring any counterclaim against the claimant; or
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] to raise any defence in relation to matters arising under the construction contract.
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] B    Commentary
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 100.1    Section 100(3) – Not entitled to bring any counterclaim or defence
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Traspunt No 5 Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 119; [2011] 2 Qd R 114, the Queensland Court of Appeal considered the scope of the prohibition under section 19(4) (which was amended by the Amendment Act to become19(6) BCIPA, and equivalent to section 100(3) of the Act). Muir JA said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] [i]t is essential, in my respectful opinion, to have regard to the words of s 19(4) in determining whether or not it prevents a defence being raised. It prohibits a respondent to a payment claim, who has failed to duly serve a payment schedule, from bringing a counterclaim against the claimant, or from raising "any defence in relation to matters arising under the construction contract". The prohibition is not against the raising of any defence whatsoever.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my view, s 19(4)(b)(ii) prohibits the raising of a defence only if it can fairly be described as one which relates to matters arising under the relevant construction contract. Counsel for Neumann argue, partly in reliance on observations in Brookhollow, that having regard to the width of the expression "in relation to", there is a sufficient connection between Traspunt's defence and matters arising under the contract. To my mind the connection is tenuous. I do not consider that a defence that the payment claim relied on by the claimant is virtually identical in all relevant respects to a previous one made by the claimant meets the description in s 19(4)(b)(ii), at least, where, as is the case here, the merits of the defence stand to be determined essentially by the comparison of one document with another.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Had the provision been intended to catch all defences having a connection with the construction contract, however remote, all words in s 19(4)(b)(ii) after "to raise any defence" would have been otiose: all payment claims are necessarily concerned with "matters arising under the construction contract". The aim of the provision would appear to be to prevent respondents to claims relying on allegations that the moneys claimed are not owing for reasons referable to the terms of the construction contract and/or to the parties' performance or failure to perform thereunder. If such matters are to be relied on as a defence to a payment claim, they must be included in a payment schedule. This approach to the construction of s 19(4)(b)(ii) is consistent with that taken by Basten JA in Bitannia Pty Ltd v Parkline Constructions Pty Ltd.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Aalborg CSP A/S v Ottoway Engineering Pty Ltd [2017] SASFC 158, the Full Court of South Australia held that sub-s 15(4)(b)(ii) of the South Australian Act (the equivalent to sub-s 19(6)(b)(ii) of BCIPA) does not preclude a respondent from relying upon misleading or deceptive conduct or estoppel, to argue that a payment claim was not appropriately served. In that case, Ottoway served 22 successive payment claims on Aalborg by email and in hard copy to Aalborg’s head office in Denmark. However, Ottoway served its 23rd payment claim only by hardcopy to Aalborg’s registered office in Australia. Aalborg did not provide a payment schedule in response, and Ottoway commenced proceedings for the entire amount claimed in the 23rd payment claim pursuant to s 15(2)(a)(i) of the South Australian Act (the equivalent to sub-s 19(3)(a)(i) of BCIPA). The Full Court held that sub-s 15(4)(b)(ii) did not preclude Aalborg from arguing that the 23rd payment claim had not been appropriately served, on the basis of an estoppel or misleading or deceptive conduct.  
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Somerset Civil Pty Ltd v Eaglerise Developments Pty Ltd; Eaglerise Developments Pty Ltd v Somerset Civil Pty Ltd [2021] QDC 070, the Queensland District Court held that a respondent to a proceeding for summary judgment brought under section 100 of the BIF Act cannot commence proceedings in relation to the same issues and rely on matters that arise under the contract.  
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In that case, the respondent to a claim for summary judgment under section 100 of the BIF Act commenced proceedings against the claimant contending that the claimant was not entitled to the claimed amount because it had carried out unlicensed work under the contract. The respondent brought an application to join both proceedings because the main issues were identical. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In refusing the application, the court concluded that joining both proceedings would be contrary to the intention of the BIF Act as the first proceeding under the BIF Act was for interim payment, whilst the second proceeding sought to finally resolve the rights under the contract. His Honour held that section 100(3) of the BIF Act stands in the way of consolidation because it expressly prohibits the principal from bringing any counter-claim against the builder or any defence against the builder in relation to matters arising under the contract.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In EnerMech Pty Ltd v Acciona Infrastructure Projects Australia Pty Ltd [2024] NSWCA 162, it was confirmed by Basten AJA (with whom Meagher JA and Griffiths AJA agreed) (at [79]) that if a claimant commences proceedings to recover an unpaid amount owing under a payment claim as a judgment debt), the respondent cannot challenge an adjudicator’s interpretation of the relevant construction contract (even if legally erroneous) or an “error of law on the face of the record”.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In 1559 High Street Pty Ltd v Camillo Builders Pty Ltd, Stynes J held that section 28R of the Building and Construction Industry Security of Payment Act 2002 (Vic) (equivalent to section 100(3) BIFSOPA) which limits the ability to raise defences and cross-claims in enforcement proceedings, does not create a constitutional inconsistency with the Australian Consumer Law (ACL) under s 109 of the Constitution. Her Honour found that the SOP Act establishes a distinct and interim statutory entitlement to progress payments that operates without prejudice to the parties' final legal rights. In reaching this conclusion, her Honour applied the reasoning from Birdon Pty Ltd v Houben Marine Pty Ltd (2011) 197 FCR 25, which confirmed that SOP schemes provide administrative mechanisms for provisional payments that do not interfere with federal consumer protection rights. Her Honour also distinguished Bitannia Pty Ltd v Parkline Constructions Pty Ltd [2006] NSWCA 238, observing that unlike in Bitannia, the alleged misleading conduct in this case concerned final legal entitlements, not the SOP payment process itself.

[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Section 42 of the QBCC Act
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Pursuant to section 42(3) of the QBCC Act, if a person carries out, or undertakes to carry out, building work, and does not hold a contractor’s licence of the appropriate class under the QBCC Act, that person is not entitled to any monetary or other consideration for doing so, subject to the amount calculated in accordance with section 42(4) of the QBCC Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] It was held in Cant Contracting Pty Ltd v Casella [2006] QCA 538; [2007] 2 Qd R 13, that (the current) section 19(6)(b)(ii) does not prohibit a respondent to a payment claim from relying on section 42(3) of the QBCC Act. Williams JA held:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Because s 42(3) of the Building Act provides that an unlicensed contractor "is not entitled to any monetary or other consideration" for doing work pursuant to the contract, such a contractor cannot be said to have an entitlement to progress payments pursuant to ss 7, 12 and 17 of the Payment Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Where section 42(1) of the Act is breached it is not possible to ‘sever’ terms of the relevant agreement, the whole agreement is affected: see Dart Holdings Pty Ltd v Total Concept Group Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 158 at [39] where McMurdo J said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …Where s 42(1) is engaged, it is not simply the promise by which the builder undertakes to carry out unlicensed work which is affected.  Rather it is the contract by which that promise is made.  As McPherson JA said in Sutton, in such a case, s 42(1) “prohibits the very process of formation of a contract”. The consequence of this statutory prohibition is that a court will not enforce the contract, at least in favour of the party which contravened the prohibition.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In JAG Projects Qld Pty Ltd v Total Cool Pty Ltd & Anor [2015] QSC 229, there was a submission on behalf of the respondent to the payment claim that section 42(3) operated to restrict the right to payment where the claimant was no longer licenced when the payment claim was made, notwithstanding that the claimant held the relevant licence at the time the work was carried out. This submission was rejected by the Court.
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] In St Hilliers Property Pty Ltd v Pronto Solar Innovations Pty Ltd [2018] QSC 164, Daubney J, having determined that the Respondent had carried out building work without a licence, confirmed the decision in Cant Contracting (above) that an unlicensed contractor will not be entitled to progress payments pursuant to ss 7, 12 and 17 of BCIPA (Qld).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Walton Construction (Qld) Pty Ltd v Plumber by Trade Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 264. An adjudicator made a decision in favour of a claimant for plumbing and drainage services which was ‘building work’ under the-then QBSA Act. The claimant was a partnership of two companies as trustees of two respective family trusts (at [47]). Neither company was licenced under the-then QBSA Act. Holding the case to fall within the decision in Cant Contracting Pty Ltd v Casella [2007] 2 Qd R 13, the Court held both the payment claim and adjudication decision to be void.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Section 55 of the DBCA
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similar principles were applied in Gemini Nominees Pty Ltd v Queensland Property Partners Pty Ltd [2008] 1 Qd R 139; [2007] QSC 20 in the context of section 55 of the (now repealed) Domestic Building Contracts Act 2000 (Qld). Mullins J held that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] It is difficult to reconcile how the scheme under the BCIPA which seeks to facilitate progress payments under a construction contract could apply to facilitate a progress payment in respect of an amount that may be awarded under s 55(4) of the DBCA where the domestic building contract has been made unenforceable pursuant to s 55(3) of the DBCA. As a matter of construction of the BCIPA, the BCIPA does not apply to the plaintiff’s claim under the contract, as the plaintiff is prohibited by s 55(3) from enforcing the contract and therefore cannot claim to be entitled to a progress payment under the contract. This conclusion is supported by the reasoning of the Court of Appeal in Cant and the dicta of Hodgson JA in Brodyn at paragraph[82]. The construction of s 55 of the DBCA shows that it is analogous to s 94 of the Home Building Act.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth)
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Traspunt No 5 Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 119; [2011] 2 Qd R 114 where the Court of Appeal held that the circumstances give rise to an arguable case that the claimant had acted in breach of section 52 of the former Trade Practices Act 1974 (Cth) in changing the basis of its claims without alerting the respondent.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Professional Engineers Act 2002 (Qld); Electrical Safety Act 2002 (Qld)
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Agripower Australia Ltd v Queensland Engineering & Electrical Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 268, Douglas J followed the decision in Cant Contracting Pty Ltd v Casella [2007] 2 Qd R 13; [2006] QCA 538 in the context of the Professional Engineers Act 2002 (Qld) and the Electrical Safety Act 2002 (Qld). 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] His Honour found that the claimant had breached both section 56 of the Electrical Safety Act 2002 (Qld) by not holding the appropriate licence and section 115(1) of the Professional Engineers Act 2002 (Qld) in carrying out professional engineering services without its agents being practising professional engineers. Douglas J held the contract to be illegal and unenforceable, setting aside the adjudicator’s decision.
[2.3 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 4] Not the party liable ‘under the construction contract’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] A defence that a party is not the party liable to a payment claim ‘under the construction contract’ is not precluded by the operation of section 19(6): see Grave v Blazevic Holdings Pty Ltd (2010) 79 NSWLR 132; [2010] NSWCA 324, [36] (McDougall J, Allsop P and Macfarlan JA agreeing).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also Mansouri & Anor v Aquamist Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 209 where the Court of Appeal set aside summary judgment on the ground that there was an insufficiently high degree of certainty that the applicants ‘were or might be liable’ under a construction contract.
