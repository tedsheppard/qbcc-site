[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 109 – Charges in favour of subcontractors
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] A    Legislation
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 109    Charges in favour of subcontractors
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] Each subcontractor for the contract is entitled to a charge over money payable to—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the contractor under the contract; or 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] another subcontractor under a higher subcontract.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] Also, if payment can not be satisfied by a charge mentioned in subsection (1), the subcontractor is entitled to a charge on any security for the contract or higher subcontract. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] However, a security for the contract or higher subcontract is only available to the extent—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] of the security’s maximum possible value for securing performance of the contract or higher subcontract; 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] it is not required to be used for securing the performance of the contract or higher subcontract. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] A charge mentioned in subsection (1) or (2) is a subcontractor’s charge. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] A subcontractor’s charge secures payment in accordance with the subcontract of all money that is payable, or is to become payable, to the subcontractor for the subcontracted work.

[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] B    Commentary
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.1    General
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The previous s 5 SCA appeared to create a separate charge, in favour of each individual subcontractor, to secure payment of the amount owing to it. There was no suggestion that there was to be one charge in favour of numerous subcontractors. It is true that the SCA did not prescribe the way in which charged funds are to be disbursed, but ss 5, 10 and 11SCA strongly suggested they are held for the benefit of the person giving notice: per Dowsett J in Re Radair Pty Ltd [1998] 2 Qd R 539 at 547, per Dowsett J. The entitlement of each subcontractor to a separate charge is reflected in s 109 BIFSOPA (Qld).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] When nothing under the subcontract caused the subcontractor to carry out the performance of work, as distinct from the delivery of goods to, at or upon the land, and where the head contract is being performed then there is no subcontract for which money is payable under the SCA. This is the same under BIFSOPA (Qld). The head contract and subcontract must require the performance of work. It is the nature of the obligation under the contract that is to be examined and which must fall under the definition, not what might in fact be done by the contracting party: see Dowstress (Qld) Pty Ltd v Mission Congregation Servants of the Holy Spirit [1987] 1 Qd R 150 at 151 and 152 per Derrington J, and 156 per Moynihan J; and Walter Wright Pty Ltd v Ronnor Pty Ltd (unreported, QSC, N Williams J, 3318 of 1983, 21 November 1983).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The subsection focuses attention upon the principal contract and does not require performance of work of the kind specified if the preliminary requirement in this section is not satisfied. Destinations and other extraneous matters are irrelevant: Re Peter Fardoulys Pty Ltd [1983] 1 Qd R 345 at 346-7 per Thomas J (as His Honour then was); and Re Austco Pty Ltd (unreported, QSC, Weld M, OS 502 of 1984, 27 April 1984).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Re Radair Pty Ltd [1998] 2 Qd R 539 Thomas J (as His Honour then was) at 540 said the following propositions may be taken as established under the SCA:
[BCIPA Bullet Point] The term every contractor (in s 5(1)) includes every subcontractor in the line of subcontracts.
[BCIPA Bullet Point] The term superior contractor (in s 5(1)) means a contractor or subcontractor further up the line than the immediate contractor or subcontractor with whom the lower claimant subcontractor has a contract.
[BCIPA Bullet Point] Section 10(1) requires a subcontractor who intends to claim a charge to give notice to the employer or superior contractor by whom the money is payable. Section 10(4) provides that if notice is not given pursuant to those requirements, the charge shall not attach.
[BCIPA Bullet Point] The amount to be “retained” by the recipient of the charge is the amount of the claim of which notice has been given.
[BCIPA Bullet Point] A subcontractor may give notice of claim of charge to any contractor superior to the contractor who owes money to that claimant. In other words “leapfrogging” is permitted (s 5(1); Hamilton Australia Pty Ltd v Milson Projects Pty Ltd [1997] 2 Qd R 355).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, His Honour also said that there is no authority recognising any right on the part of such a claimant to moneys other than that which the recipient of the notice is obliged to retain.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] A subcontractor who has given a notice of claim to another party is entitled to a charge as against the amount payable by that party. The source of the funds from which such payment is to be made is not relevant. There is no justification for the view that the SCA assumes that each contractor or subcontractor is to be paid only from such amounts as are ultimately payable by the employer at the top of the chain for the whole of the project: per Dowsett J in Re Radair Pty Ltd [1998] 2 Qd R 539 at 549.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.2    Where a contractor contracts
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] This subsection focuses on the work involved in the head contract: Re Austco Pty Ltd (unreported, QSC, Weld M, OS 502 of 1984, 27 April 1984) per Master Weld at 4.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.3    A “leapfrogging” charge
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] A subcontractor can lodge a “leapfrogging” charge, ie a charge on money due to a contractor further up the line of contracts, despite the subcontractor having no contractual relation with the person to whom the money was otherwise payable, under the SCA prior to the 2002 amendments: see Hewitt Nominees Pty Ltd v Commissioner for Railways [1979] Qd R 256 at 262-263; Hamilton Australia Pty Ltd v Milson Projects Pty Ltd [1997] 2 Qd R 355; Re Radair Pty Ltd [1998] 2 Qd R 539 at 544; and under the amended SCA, see Re University of Queensland [2003] 2 Qd R 577; [2003] QSC 158. However, as Mullins J said in this case, the extent to which a claim is satisfied under one charge must affect the amount of the claim, which can be pursued under the other charge.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Prior to the 2002 amendments, a subcontractor could successfully charge money payable to a superior contractor even if the superior contractor had paid all moneys which were due by it. In other words, there was nothing owing by the superior contractor to the contractor next in line. It was recognised that the intention of the statute was to advantage a subcontractor, at the expense of a contractor further up the line, notwithstanding the absence of any contractual relationship between them: Hamilton Australia Pty Ltd v Milson Projects Pty Ltd [1997] 2 Qd R 355; and Re Radair Pty Ltd [1998] 2 Qd R 539.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Hewitt Nominees Pty Ltd v Commissioner for Railways [1979] Qd R 256 the Court of Appeal found that a subcontractor can lodge a “leapfrogging” charge, ie a charge on money due to a contractor further up the line of contractors, despite the subcontractor having no contractual relation with the person to whom the money was otherwise payable. Further, this dicta in at p 263 has apparently been treated as recognising the ability of a subcontractor to lodge a charge both on his contractor and on a superior contractor in respect of the claim being made by the subcontractor at the same time for the same claim: see Re Radair Pty Ltd [1998] 2 Qd R 539.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Generally, it has long been a feature of the legislation that it enables the subcontractor to recover from the contractor further up the line, even though the superior contractor has no contractual relationship with the subcontractor, and even though the superior contractor has paid all money that is due to the contractor below. In other words, the legislature prior to the 2002 amendments contemplated that if a loss is to fall on one of two innocent parties, it will fall on the superior contractor, rather than the subcontractor. This has been identified In re Williams; Ex parte the Official Assignee (1899) 17 NZLR 712 at 719 – 721 and has been recognised in Queensland in Re Radair Pty Ltd [1998] 2 Qd R 539. It remains to be seen how the courts will interpret the limitations in s 109(3)BIFSOPA (Qld).
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.4    Lump-sum contract
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] An indivisible contract may well be outside the scope of the section if it embraces a substantial degree of work which is not work within the meaning of the SCA: Caldow Properties Ltd v H J G Low & Assoc Ltd [1971] NZLR 311 at 313 per North P.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.5    The meaning of the phrase “money payable under a contract or subcontract”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The phrase “money payable under a contract or subcontract” includes retention moneys under the contract. Thus, charges provided for in s 5 of the SCA apply to retention moneys as well as to other moneys payable under the relevant contract: Re Bunnerin Pty Ltd (in liq) [1990] 1 Qd R 525. In this case, a notice claiming a charge “upon the money that is now or will be payable” under a contract that did not specifically refer to retention moneys, was still effective to charge such retention money.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] It does not necessarily follow from the fact that the balance is, in the ordinary sense of those words, money payable to the respondent under the building contract, that it can be characterised as such within the meaning of s 5(1) of the SCA. Unless there is some basis for reading down the phrase “money payable to the contractor … under the contractor's contract” in that subs (1)(a) it should be given its ordinary meaning. There is nothing, in the context of s 5 or in the general context of the SCA or in the SCA's evident purpose which requires any limitation on the ordinary meaning of the phrase. Indeed, the immediate context of s 5 gives a contrary indication. In contrast with s 5(1), s 5(2) refers to money “payable … to the subcontractor for work done by the subcontractor under the subcontract”: Re A & P Constructions Pty Ltd (in liq) [1999] 1 Qd R 228.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Re Bunnerin Pty Ltd (in liq) [1990] 1 Qd R 525 also makes it clear that a charge can be given over retention money, money owing under the contract other than retention money and money owing under the contract including retention money: see also Rees v Mount Isa City Council [1979] Qd R 553; and Re FFE Group (Qld) Pty Ltd [1984] 1 Qd R 267.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Re A & P Constructions Pty Ltd (in liq) [1999] 1 Qd R 228, the proceeds of a duly realised, unconditional bank undertaking given by a building contractor to the employer in lieu of security and retention money, was money payable to the contractor under the contract (s 5(1) of the SCA) and was therefore chargeable under the SCA. The Court of Appeal distinguished the decision of Road Surfaces Group Pty Ltd v Brown [1987] 2 Qd R 792 where the Full Court found the proceeds of a bank guarantee or undertaking was not money payable under the subcontract because the employer took over the work and, through an amendment of the subcontract, became entitled to utilise money otherwise due under the contract, including retentions and security deposits. As a result, the proceeds of the bank guarantee then became “moneys otherwise due under the (building) contract”.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In George Feros Memorial v Hammat Constructions [2000] NSWSC 808 the question for determination was whether the rights of a subcontractor under the Contractors Debts Act 1997 (NSW) to recover money due by a defaulting contractor from the employer, out of money due by the employer to the contractor, extends to recovery from a security bond from a bank to the employer given in lieu of a retention fund. The Court found the security was not money due to the builder so as to bring the provisions of s 5 of the Contractors Debts Act 1997 (NSW) into operation. All liability depends on the debt falling within s 5 of the Contractors Debts Act 1997 (NSW). The security was not considered to be money but a contract between bank and employer under which money might become available.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Ideal Interior Linings Pty Ltd v Watts & Hughes (Qld) Pty Ltd (unreported, Dist Ct Bne, Wylie DCJ, 413 of 2000, 19 September 2000) there existed an application for cancellation of the respondent's claim of charge under s 21 and payment to the applicant of money held by the court. The respondent did not hold a licence under the QBSA Act and the charge was cancelled as parliament did not intend the provisions of s 42(4) of the QBSA Act to operate retrospectively.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Morton Engineering Co Pty Ltd v Stork Wescon Australia Pty Ltd [2000] 2 Qd R 148; [1999] QCA 61, the question arose as to whether the contractor required a licence under the QBSA Act to install part of the equipment at the site of the Barella Gas Centre. The QBSA Act and its regulations have the effect that building work includes the “erection or construction of … any fixed structure”, with certain exceptions, the relevant one in this case being “the installation of manufacturing equipment or equipment for … conveying … materials or products”. The Court of Appeal found it hard to think of a rational policy which would underlie bringing manufacturing equipment under the regulatory scheme applicable to the building industry and held the word “installation” should be given a wide meaning to encompass all necessary work to produce the installation, and so a licence was not required because fabricating steel structures to support the pipeline was not “building work” within the meaning of s 4 of the QBSA Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Rapid Contracting Pty Ltd (in liq) (unreported, QCA, Thomas JJA, 10856 of 1998, 6 August 1999), the appellant (builder) applied for cancellation of a charge pursuant to s 21 of the QBSA Act. The basis of the application was that the work the respondent carried out was work within the meaning of the QBSA Act, the respondent did not have a licence of the appropriate class under that Act and therefore was not entitled to any monetary or other consideration for the work. The Court found that the respondent was required to construct pre-cast concrete panels, arguably part of the construction of a building, which was sufficient to bring it within the definition of building work. It was held that the respondent was required to have a licence under the Act and this licence would be a concreting licence. The respondent therefore carried out building work in contravention of s 42 of the Act and was not entitled to any monetary or other consideration for doing so. It follows therefore that there was no payment in accordance with the subcontract to which a charge could secure.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In St Hilliers Property Pty Ltd v Pronto Solar Innovations Pty Ltd [2018] QSC 164, Daubney J ordered that a charge be cancelled on the basis that:
[BCIPA Bullet Point] the subcontractor had carried out building work without a licence, thus disentitling the builder from payment pursuant to section 42(3) of the QBSA Act; and
[BCIPA Bullet Point] therefore, no money was payable for work done by the subcontractor under the subcontract as required by section 5(2) of the SCA.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] His Honour followed the reasoning of de Jersey CJ, Davies and Thomas JJA in Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Rapid Contracting Pty Ltd (in liq).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Henry Walker Etlin Contracting Pty Ltd v Mostia Constructions [2001] QSC 089, Ambrose J approved and followed the decisions and held that variation sums claimed by a subcontractor can't be categorised as “money that is payable or is to become payable” in the absence of relevant certificates and the resolution of disputes as to the valuation of variation works required under the subcontract.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Qline Interiors Pty Ltd v Jezer Construction Group Pty Ltd; [2009] 2 Qd R 566; [2002] QSC 88 Muir J found that the charge created by s 5(1) was a charge “on the money payable to the contractor … under the … contract”. The total amount recoverable under the charges of all subcontractors cannot exceed the amount payable to the contractor under the contract. In the event that at a time after the charges attached the employer or superior contractor has ceased to have an obligation to pay moneys to the contractor under the head contract, then no moneys will be payable to the subcontractor.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Mulherin Rigging & Cranes Australia Pty Ltd v Roberts and Schaefer Australia Pty Ltd, [2011] 1 Qd R 139; [2009] QSC 429 the Supreme Court permitted a plaintiff to amend its statement of claim on the basis that the charge was never extinguished and accordingly did not need to be re-enlivened. The Court considered that the charge secured payment of money “payable to the subcontractor for work done” by it under the subcontract.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In S.O.S Plumbing Service (Qld) Pty Ltd v 28 Careel Developments Pty Ltd & Anor [2016] QMC 6, a subcontractor was engaged to undertake earthworks for a development. The subcontractor rendered invoices to the head contractor which were not paid. The subcontractor then served a notice of claim of charge on the developer and subsequently commenced proceedings against the developer seeking recovery of those amounts. The developer did not defend the proceedings and default judgment was entered against it. The developer then applied to have the judgment set aside, on grounds that it had not received the notice of claim of charge, claim or statement of claim and was unaware of the proceedings. In determining whether the judgment should be set aside, Magistrate Carmody considered the developer's prospects of defending the proceedings. The developer argued that when the notice was served, it did not hold any money which was payable to the head contractor, and adduced business records asserting that it had made progress payments totalling $1,400,000 to the head contractor. However, Magistrate Carmody held that this evidence fell well short of the standard required to demonstrate that no money was payable and dismissed the developer's application to have the judgment set aside. This decision was upheld on appeal: 28 Careel Developments Pty Ltd v S.O.S Plumbing Services (Qld) Pty Ltd [2016] QDC 223.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.6    The definition of payable
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] This word used alone without any explanatory or qualifying context means payable there and then as distinguished from payable at some future time. When money is said to be payable at some future time it is to use an ellipsis and really to convey that the money will become payable at some future time: JJ Craig Ltd v Gillman Packaging Ltd [1962] NZLR 201 at 209, per Gresson P. Thus, the repudiation of a contract by the employer may preclude a claim for a charge in respect of work still to be done: Caldow Properties Ltd v H J G Low & Assoc Ltd [1971] NZLR 311 at 315 per Turner J.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] As the SCA refers to money that is to become payable, there is clearly no limitation of security to any money that is presently determined and payable. However, to the extent that the effect of the SCA is to impose a security which is limited to a particular amount of money in the hands of the principal, as a matter of ordinary understanding, the reference to “money to become payable” must refer to a specific sum that will become payable. That specific sum must be objectively determinable at the date of the charge by reference to the contract. For example, if work were to be done in a certain way and on completion to be valued by a certifier, upon which the certified value would be payable, it would not fall within the SCA, even though the value of the work might be capable of assessment otherwise. Accordingly, if the amount payable depends upon the assessment of any factor within the judgment of an intermediary, the specific sum cannot be identified until that determination is made: Riteway Constructions Pty Ltd v Baulderstone Hornibrook Pty Ltd [1998] 2 Qd R 218 at 220, per Derrington J.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.7    Domestic Building Contracts
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Section 45 of Sch 1B to the QBSA Act provides:
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] (2) The Subcontractors' Charges Act 1974 does not apply to domestic building work relating to a detached dwelling if the work –
[BCIPA Bullet Point] is carried out by a building contractor for an individual; and
[BCIPA Bullet Point] is not for a business carried on by the individual, either alone or as a member of a partnership.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.8    Work done by a subcontractor
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Section 5(2) (see now s 109(5)BIFSOPA) limits the charge to work done by the subcontractor and characterises the money payable for which a charge attaches by reference to the work done by the subcontractor: Re Austco Pty Ltd (unreported, QSC, Weld M, OS 502 of 1984, 27 April 1984) at 4-5, per Master Weld.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Raedel v Jezer Construction Group Pty Ltd [2007] QDC 49 the District Court found that the charge of the subcontractor is “on the money payable to the contractor or a superior contractor under the contractors, or a superior contractor's contract or a subcontract”. It is not expressly limited to payments due in respect of work which was the subject of a subcontract under which the subcontractor claims, and there is no reason why the scope of the section should be read down. The charge is to enforce payment of a sum payable under the subcontract in respect of work done under the subcontract, which attaches to any money payable under the head contract. This conclusion was re-affirmed in 28 Careel Developments Pty Ltd & S.O.S Plumbing Services (Qld) Pty Ltd [2016] QDC 223 at [36].
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Sempac Pty Ltd v Stockport (Q) Pty Ltd [2004] QDC 87 the subcontractor gave notice of claim of charge for work under the head contract between the contractor and employer and also work that did not relate to the head contract. The Court said that a charge could only attach to money payable under a subcontract that relates to the head contract between the contractor and the employer:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Section 5(2) must be read as referring to a subcontract relating to the employer's contract with the contractor and section 5(1) must be read as meaning every subcontractor of the contractor under the contractor's contract with the employer. The whole scheme of the Act requires that the subcontract be one related to the head contract between the contractor and the employer.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.9    Objective test
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] As with the head contract, it is not appropriate to consider whether the subcontractor actually performs work, but whether he has a contract with the head contractor for the performance of work. The identifying factor is directed to the nature of the contractual obligation and not to the mode of performance. Such a contract for the performance of work is, for example, in contrast with the contract for the supply of goods, and perhaps services. As in the case of the employer's contract, if the contract be merely for the supply of goods, it is immaterial that the subcontractor may have performed work in their production, even if that work were done on the land of the employer: Dowstress (Qld) Pty Ltd v Mission Congregation Servants of the Holy Spirit [1987] 1 Qd R 150 at 152, per Derrington J. For example, a contract to supply meals to the workers of the head contractor upon the employer's land, whilst it may involve some work in the preparation of such meals, is merely a contract for the supply of goods and is not a contract for the performance of work within the meaning of the SCA: Re Leighton Contractors Pty Ltd [1985] 2 Qd R 377.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.10    Composite obligations
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] If goods are manufactured involving both supply of raw materials and the performance of work, the SCA gives a benefit only in respect of the work done. Due to the difficulty in separating out the money payable for each component, there is an implication that this reference to work is not intended to apply to such a case: Dowstress (Qld) Pty Ltd v Mission Congregation Servants of the Holy Spirit [1987] 1 Qd R 150 at 152, per Derrington J. See also William Andrew Pty Ltd v Santalucia [1983] 1 Qd R 349. In the latter case, a contract for the delivery of crushed rock was found to be only a contract for the sale of goods notwithstanding that the subcontractor crushed the rock and built a service road in order to deliver it.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.11    Apportionment of items of work
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] An indivisible contract for the performance of work is outside the scheme of the contract altogether if a substantial part of it relates to work which is not work within the meaning of the SCA: Bayliss v Wellington City Corp [1957] NZLR 836 at 323, per Richmond J,and at 312 per North P; cited with approval in Winstone Ltd v Wellington City [1972] NZLR 399 at 405,per White J. Thus, where the operation was costed under separate heads, the contract might well have been negotiated following discussion on such figures and the work in question is clearly a separate item and one which is specifically contemplated as the subject of a lien or charge by s 20(1)(c) of the extended definition in s 20(1) of the Wages Protection and Contractors Liens Act 1939 (NZ). It is appropriate that there be an apportionment: Winstone Ltd v Wellington City [1972] NZLR 399; see also Northcoast Forest Products Ltd v Eakins Construction Ltd (1961) 26 DLR 251; and Re Terra Cotta Contracting Co Ltd (1964) 43 DLR 488. If it is not possible to separate the work which falls within the meaning of the SCA from the other duties performed under the contract then there is an implication that the subcontractor will not be entitled to give a charge pursuant to the SCA: Derrington J in Dowstress (Qld) Pty Ltd v Mission Congregation Servants of the Holy Spirit [1987] 1 Qd R 150 at 152.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Leach v Dynac Pty Ltd [2000] QSC 300 the Supreme Court held that work completed by a subcontractor off the land on which the structure in question is being constructed is not work within the meaning of that term in the Subcontractors' Charges Act 1974 and that when a substantial part of the work to be performed is not work within the meaning of that term in the SCA, none of the work is then within the scheme of the SCA.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.12    Secure payment
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Emphasis should be placed on the reference in s 5(2) to “all money that is payable or is to become payable to the subcontractor for work done by the subcontractor under the contract”. This is now, in near identical terms, conveyed in s 109(5) of BIFSOPA (Qld)“[a] subcontractor's charge secures payment in accordance with the subcontract of all money that is payable, or is to become payable, to the subcontractor for the subcontracted work”. This means that security is designed to enforce only sums payable or to be payable under the subcontract for work done under it and this excludes, for example, damages that might be payable for its breach: Groutco (Aust) Pty Ltd v Thiess Contractors Pty Ltd [1985] 1 Qd R 238. This is made clear by s 5(2) where it states that the charge shall “secure payment in accordance with the subcontract”. Accordingly, in order that security apply, the payment must be provided for by the contract itself. In addition, the word “debt” is used in ss 9 and 23 of the SCA (see now s 116BIFSOPA (Qld)) to describe the money payable or secured. This identifies and limits the nature of the sum payable or to become payable that is to be covered by the security. This does not mean that the amount be due and owing at the time of the notice, for it is clear from the sections set out above that it may include amounts that are to become payable. Section 10(2)SCA (now ss 118 and 122BIFSOPA (Qld)) confirms that it is not necessary that the timeframe for the payment of the money, in the event that the claimed charge has arrived, provides that the work is completed: Riteway Constructions Pty Ltd v Baulderstone Hornibrook Pty Ltd [1998] 2 Qd R 218 at 220, per Derrington J. When a subcontract provides a procedure to ascertain the amount payable and a procedure to resolve disputes between parties, until a dispute concerning that amount is so resolved and the amount payable is so ascertained, a charge in respect of that amount cannot be sustained: James Hardie Building Systems Pty Ltd v Epoca Constructions Pty Ltd (1999) 15 BCL 199.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] A subcontractor may be entitled to a claim under s 5(2) (s 109(5)BIFSOPA). Even where the subcontractor is prevented from completing work under the subcontract by the contractor, the contractor can purportedly exercise rights under the subcontract by excluding the subcontractor from the building site: Milgun Pty Ltd v Austco Pty Ltd [1988] 2 Qd R 670.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Section 5(2) (s 109(5)BIFSOPA) establishes that for a charge to be valid, it must secure payment of money in accordance with the subcontract: Barclay Mowlem Constructions Pty Ltd v Ishikawajima-Harima Heavy Ind Co Ltd [2003] QSC 10.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Qline Interiors Pty Ltd v Jezer Construction Group Pty Ltd; [2009] 2 Qd R 566; [2002] QSC 88 Muir J found that in the event that a contractor fails to make an assessment of a subcontractor's claim prior to the termination of the subcontract and the subcontractor is not making a claim for damages, then the amount as assessed by the court would be a claim for payment under the subcontract. See also: Groutco (Aust) Pty Ltd v Thiess Contractors Pty Ltd [1985] 1 Qd R 238 at 247, per Campbell CJ, where his Honour said that “the moneys claimed are moneys which the terms of the subcontract itself provide as being or becoming payable”.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.13    Charge secures
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Mansfield J, in Re Stockport (NQ) Pty Ltd (2003) 127 FCR 291; [2003] FCA 31, said that s 5 of the SCA (now see s 109BIFSOPA (Qld)) operates to create a set of rights in a subcontractor in relation to the money payable by an employer to the principal or superior contractor. Although there is no crystallisation of those rights so as to create specific proprietary interest in the moneys, or the chose in action, being the entitlement to those moneys, by attaching the rights to the specific property, until notice is given under s 10 (now ss 118 and 122BIFSOPA (Qld)). His Honour was of the view that s 5(2) (now s 109(5)BIFSOPA (Qld)) creates what is in effect a floating charge.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.14    Limit on claims
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Section 5(3), being aimed at the situation where several subcontractors claim against the same fund, limits the total amount recoverable under charges upon that fund to the total of that fund: Hamilton Australia Pty Ltd v Milson Projects Pty Ltd [1997] 2 Qd R 355 at 2. See now s 109(3)BIFSOPA (Qld).
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.15    Contract still to be complied with
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Isakka v South Australian Asset Management Corp [2002] QCA 549 the Court of Appeal said that a provision of contract still to be complied with cannot be money, “the payment of which is governed by a provision of the subcontract still to be complied with” unless the subcontractor's right to payment accrued prior to termination and the quantum. If not fixed by the relevant clause, it can be determined by the court pursuant to a principle such as that discussed in Qline Interiors Pty Ltd v Jezer Construction Group Pty Ltd; [2009] 2 Qd R 566; [2002] QSC 88.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.16    Damages
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Barclay Mowlem Constructions Pty Ltd v Ishikawajima-Harima Heavy Ind Co Ltd [2003] QSC 10, Muir J pointed out that s 5(6)(b)SCA expressly excluded damages for breach of contract or a quantum meruit. The notice of charge in question included claims for delay and disruption and force majeure (as submitted in a progress claims to the head contractor). The respondent submitted that this was not a claim for damages, rather for payment in accordance with the subcontract. Muir J held, due to the nature of the claim and the express exclusion under the SCA, the claim of charge was invalid.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd [2004] QSC 198, the subcontractor claimed some $6 million for acceleration and delay claims. The contractor applied to strike out that part of the notice of charge on the basis this was, in effect, a claim for damages for breach of contract. Chesterman J held that this amount should be omitted as it was not clearly identified and was within the exclusions in s 5(6)SCA. This decision has been upheld by the Court of Appeal see Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd [2005] 1 Qd R 610; [2005] QCA 61, (Multiplex 4).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Quality Concrete Pty Ltd v Honeycombes Townsville Pty Ltd [2005] QSC 192, Cullinane J considered a claim by the subcontractor for variation works. His Honour found, on the facts, that the relevant variation was the subject of a collateral contract. It was held that the works were not intended to be performed as a variation to the contract and it was not intended that the subcontractor would charge the main contractor for the works: at [25]. Consequently, the claim for the relevant works was a claim for damages under a collateral contract and could not be claimed pursuant to s 5(6)(b) of the SCA: at [26].
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 109.17    Procedure for Resolution
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd v Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd [2004] 1 Qd R 470; [2003] QCA 501, (Multiplex 2) the applicant applied to strike out two notices of charge on the basis that as the amount claimed had not been certified, the notices were not for moneys payable under the subcontract. Further, it was argued that the claims were in dispute and there was no procedure under the subcontract for resolution of the disputed claims, with the result that the moneys in dispute did not fall within the s 5(6)(a)(ii) inclusion of “moneys payable”. In the Court of Appeal, McPherson JA held that s 5(6) used the word “including” and there was nothing in the SCA to suggest that s 5(6)(a) of the SCA was confined to the instances give in paras (a)(i) – (a)(ii). His Honour described the critical aspect of s 5(6)(a) as the presence in the subcontract of a provision governing the payment of money that is or is to become payable to a subcontractor for work completed under the subcontract, and that this provision is still to be completed.
