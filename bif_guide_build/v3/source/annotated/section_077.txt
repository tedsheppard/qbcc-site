[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 77 – Consequence of failing to give payment schedule
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] A    Legislation
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 77    Consequence of failing to give payment schedule 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] This section applies if a respondent given a payment claim does not respond to the claim by giving the claimant a payment schedule as required under section 76. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] The respondent is liable to pay the amount claimed under the payment claim to the claimant on the due date for the progress payment to which the payment claim relates.
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] B    Commentary
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 77.1    Section 77(2) – ‘is liable’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Fyntray Constructions Pty Ltd v Macind Drainage & Hydraulic Services Pty Ltd [2002] NSWCA 238, the New South Wales Court of Appeal interpreted the phrase ‘becomes liable’ in a wide manner, construing ‘becomes’ to mean ‘became, becomes or will become’. At [48] per Heydon JA (Hodgson JA and Ipp AJA agreeing):
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] … even if a “claimant”, as defined by s 13(1), delays in serving a payment claim beyond a time which is ten business days before the contractual due date in which the “respondent” becomes liable to pay the claimed amount, as a matter of language there is no strain in concluding that, if no payment schedule is provided by the respondent in time, the respondent “becomes” liable to pay at a passed date. If strictness of construction, which the defendant’s approach calls for, is to be applied, that approach is not strict enough. It fastens on the contrast between the present tense of “becomes” and the occurrence of liability to pay in the past. But if the defendant’s construction were correct, the word “becomes” should have been “will become” or, to cover the possibility of a payment claim served precisely ten business days before the due date for the progress payment, “will become or becomes”. Since that language was not used, there is no strain in construing “becomes” to mean “became, becomes or will become”.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In K2M Investments Pty Ltd v Unita (Qld) Pty Ltd [2022] QSC 274, the Court dismissed an application to set aside a statutory demand which was issued following a failure to pay a payment claim or respond with a payment schedule (thus, making the respondent liable to pay the amount claimed under the payment claim).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In his decision, Cooper J referred to Re Douglas Aerospace Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 167, where (in a similar situation) a debt that arose under the NSW Equivalent of s 77 BIFSOPA was found to be a statutory debt by operation of the NSW SOP Act. In that decision, it was held that the only way that a genuine dispute could be raised in respect of such a debt would be by disputing whether there was a failure to provide a payment schedule and/or failure to pay the amount claimed. Cooper J found that in the present case, there was no dispute that the respondent had failed to provide a payment schedule or failure to pay the claimed amount existed, and therefore a genuine dispute could not be raised in relation to the existence of the debt.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In the matter of Nicolas Criniti Pty Ltd (in Liquidation) [2022] NSWSC 1149, the NSW Supreme Court considered when a statutory debt arose under the Security of Payment Act and whether a claimant that was successful at adjudication had a valid “proof of debt” against a respondent in liquidation. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The Court held:
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] a valid statutory debt must arise against the company prior to the appointment of an administrator;
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] the debt of an amount claimed and pursued at adjudication does not arise as a statutory debt until there is an adjudication determination; and 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] as, in this case, that right arose after the respondent entered into voluntary administration, there was no valid “statutory debt” against the company.
