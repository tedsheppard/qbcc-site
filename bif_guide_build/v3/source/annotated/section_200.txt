[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 200 – Contracting out prohibited 
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] A    Legislation
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 200    Contracting out prohibited 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] The provisions of this Act have effect despite any provision to the contrary in any contract, agreement or arrangement. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] A provision of a contract, agreement or arrangement is of no effect to the extent to which it— 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] is contrary to this Act; or
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] purports to exclude, limit or change the operation of this Act; or
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] has the effect of excluding, limiting or changing the operation of this Act; or 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] may reasonably be construed as an attempt to deter a person from taking action under this Act.
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] B    Commentary
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 200.1    Purpose of section 99
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The purpose of section 99 of the Act was summarised by the Queensland Court of Appeal in Gambaro Pty Ltd as Trustee for the Gambaro Holdings Trust v Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd; Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd v Gambaro Pty Ltd [2015] QCA 288 in the following terms:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] the purpose of s 99 is both to put it beyond doubt that the statutory entitlement to progress payments created by Pts 2 and 3 has effect despite any difference between that statutory entitlement and the contractual entitlement to progress payments and to prohibit the exclusion or modification of the statutory right to the adjudicated amount of a progress payment on account of the contractual remuneration.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 200.2    Preconditions to payment
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Refer to the commentary under section 70 of the Act, particularly in relation to the decision in John Holland Pty Ltd v Coastal Dredging & Construction Pty Ltd [2012] 2 Qd R 435; [2012] QCA 150.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Watpac Constructions Pty Ltd v Collins & Graham Mechanical Pty Ltd [2020] VSC 414, Riordan J held that the equivalent of section 200 of BIFSOPA will invalidate a “deemed” termination for convivence clause to the extent that it delays a right to a progress payment, as it would have the effect of “excluding, modifying or restricting the operation of the Act”. In that case, Riordan J considered the validity of a contractual mechanism which had the effect that an entitlement to a deemed termination claim would only arise if a court or other relevant tribunal determined that the purported termination by the principal was wrongful. The result of this contractual mechanism meant that, upon a purported wrongful termination by the principal, a court or tribunal was required to firstly make a ‘determination’ before a reference date could arise under section 9 of the Vic Act (equivalent to section 70 BIFSOPA). Riordan J held that to effectively delay a right to a progress payment until a decision is made by a court or tribunal on a substantial issue such as repudiation is directly inconsistent with the purposes of the legislation and, if applied in its terms, would have the effect of excluding, modifying or restricting the operation of the Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd v Meso Solutions Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 372, a respondent challenged the validity of an adjudication determination, due to the claimant issuing notice pursuant to s 17(2)(a) NSW SOP Act, prior to the satisfaction of a condition precedent under the contract. The contract required any progress claim to be accompanied by certain documents, such as a statutory declaration. Richmond J held that it was irrelevant that the claimant failed to satisfy the preconditions in the contract when issuing the progress claim. This was because the contract could not deprive the claimant of their right to payment under the SOP Act. Richmond J dismissed the respondent’s application and held that s 34 NSW SOP Act (equivalent s 200 BIFSOPA) applied, as the condition precedent would exclude, modify or restrict the operation of the SOP Act.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Martinus Rail Pty Ltd v Co-operative Bulk Handling Ltd [2025] WASC 373, Palmer J considered section 111 of the Building and Construction Industry (Security of Payment) Act 2021 (WA) (equivalent to section 200 BIF Act 2017 (QLD)). His Honour noted that where parties’ make an agreement contained within their contract, that is inconsistent with the Act, the provision will be rendered void. In coming to his decision Palmer J noted that agreeing to contractually lengthen the time for receipt of payment clearly undermines the object of the Act, being to provide a mechanism for fair, expeditious cashflow in the construction industry. Therefore, any such provision will be barred from finding application pursuant to the ‘No contracting out” provision of the Act (s 111).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Roberts Co (NSW) Pty Ltd v Sharvain Facades Pty Ltd (Administrators Appointed) [2025] NSWCA 161, Hammerschlag CJ held that the deeming clause in the contract, which delayed the deemed receipt of documents served after 5:00pm to 9:00 am the next business day, purported to “modify the operation” of the NSW SOP Act and was therefore void under section 34(2) (equivalent to section 200(2) of the QLD Act). However, the Court ultimately did not determine whether the clause was void “for all purposes,” finding it unnecessary to resolve that broader issue. In reaching this conclusion, the Court referred to obiter in All Seasons Air Pty Ltd v Regal Consulting Services Pty Ltd [2017] NSWCA 289, which had suggested that similar clauses may be invalid where they conflict with the Act’s statutory regime.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In York Property Holdings Pty Ltd v Tomkins Commercial & Industrial Builders Pty Ltd [2025] QCA 251, Kelly J held that s 200 is a “general provision embodying the substantive law affecting ultimate rights” and does not condition or limit an adjudicator’s authority. In coming to the decision, his Honour relied on authorities such as Maxcon and Ceerose to confirm that misinterpretation of a general statutory provision is an error within jurisdiction. The court distinguished cases like Southern Han which involved true jurisdictional preconditions tied to reference dates. The case therefore confirms that a mistaken application of s 200 cannot invalidate an adjudication decision.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 200.3    Preconditions to reference dates 
[BCIPA Body Text] Refer to the commentary under section 70 of the Act, particularly in relation to the decisions in Lean Field Developments Pty Ltd v E & I Global Solutions (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor [2014] QSC 293 and BRB Modular Pty Ltd v AWX Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 218.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 200.4    Choice of law clauses
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] A choice of law clause, providing that the construction contract was to be governed by the law of England with the courts of England having exclusive jurisdiction, was held to be void by operation of the equivalent to section 99 under the NSW Act: Proactive Building Solutions v MacKenzie Keck Pty Ltd [2013] NSWSC 1500.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 200.5    Recourse to security
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] It has been held that a provision of a construction contract which provides for recourse to bank guarantees (where recourse was being had by a respondent following an adjudication decision), is not a provision that purports to annul, exclude, nullify, modify, restrict or otherwise change the effect of a provision of the Act: see Fabtech Australia Pty Ltd v Laing O’Rourke Australia Construction Pty Ltd [2015] FCA 1371, [29]-[38] (Besanko J); Patterson Building Group Pty Ltd v Holroyd City Council [2013] NSWSC 1484, [56]-[75] (White J); Duro Felguera Australia Pty Ltd v Samsung C&T Corporation [2016] WASC 119, [37] – [48] (Le Miere J).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Examples:
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Fabtech Australia Pty Ltd v Laing O’Rourke Australia Construction Pty Ltd [2015] FCA 1371. Clause 6.2 of the construction contract provided that ‘[t]he [respondent] may have recourse to the Bank Guarantees…whenever the [respondent] claims to be entitled to claim the payment of monies by the [claimant]’. The respondent sought to have recourse to the Bank Guarantees following an adjudication decision which the respondent alleged was in error. Besanko J held that clause 6.2 ‘does not purport to “annul, exclude, modify, restrict or otherwise change the effect of a provision of [the] Act”’ (at [37], emphasis original). Further, his Honour held that the Act did not directly address the issue of recourse to security and, as such, did not operate to have an ‘effect’ to prevent the enforcement of the Bank Guarantees. Accordingly, his Honour held that clause 6.2 did not have the effect of excluding, modifying, restricting or otherwise changing the effect of a provision of the Act (at [38]).
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Duro Felguera Australia Pty Ltd v Samsung C&T Corporation [2016] WASC 119. Clause 5.2 of the construction contract provided that the respondent could at any time convert security where the ‘[respondent] considers, acting bona fide, that it is or will be entitled to recover the relevant amount from [the claimant] under or in respect of the Subcontract’.  The claimant had previously been successful in obtaining three adjudication determinations against the respondent and applied for an interlocutory injunction restraining the respondent from having recourse to security. Le Miere J held that an adjudication determination does not alter the parties’ contractual rights including its rights to have recourse to security, saying at [47]:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In his reasons for determination, the adjudicator may decide the proper construction of provisions of the construction contract or may make findings of fact. Neither the adjudicator’s construction of the contract or the facts found by the adjudicator alter the proper construction of the contract nor any facts in contest between the parties. 
[2.6 Com-BIFSOPA bullet] Telvent Australia Pty Ltd v Acciona Infrastructure Australia Pty Ltd & Ors [2016] QSC 201. Clause 2.6(f) of the construction contract provided that the respondent had a right to have recourse to the unconditional undertaking provided by the applicant at any time to the extent of any bona fide claim notified in writing by the respondent to the applicant which the respondent may have against the applicant arising out of, or in connection with [the construction contract]. The respondent sought to have recourse to the unconditional undertakings in relation to nine claims it considered it had against the respondent, five of which had previously been dealt with, and rejected, by an adjudicator under the Act. Jackson J followed the decisions in Fabtech Australia Pty Ltd v Laing O’Rourke Australia Construction Pty Ltd [2015] FCA 1371 and Duro Felguera Australia Pty Ltd v Samsung C&T Corporation [2016] WASC 119 and held that the respondent was not precluded from raising claims against the applicant that had been determined against the respondent in adjudication.  
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 200.6    Accord and Satisfaction 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Fitzroy Shopfitting and Building Pty Ltd v. Solene Investments Pty Ltd [2016] VCC 1352, Judge Anderson applied the reasoning of the Court of Appeal in Beba Enterprises Pty Ltd v Gadens Lawyers [2013] VSCA 136 and held that section 48 of the VIC Act, the equivalent of section 99 of the Act, would not apply if there had been accord and satisfaction. His Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] In my view, the policy considerations raised by the Court of Appeal are relevant in the present case. Notwithstanding the special nature of the Building and Construction Industry Security of Payment Act 2002 (Vic), and the clear and direct wording of section 48, if the parties by their words and conduct have shown that their agreement was not directed to the exclusion, modification or restriction of the operation of the Act but rather the genuine resolution of a dispute, the parties should be bound by their agreement and section 48 would have no application.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 200.7    Right to set off
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Grocon (Belgrave St) Developer Pty Ltd v Construction Profile Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 409, Ball J discussed, in obiter, that a particular clause of the contract relating to the right of setoff may have had the effect of permitting the head contractor to have recourse to bank guarantees notwithstanding an adjudication determination which found there was no basis for the particular claim for liquidated damages set off against the payment claim. Justice Ball considered that the clause might constitute the modification or restriction of the operation of the NSW Act because the clause could have the effect of reducing “the amount [the contractor] was entitled to recover (on an interim basis) as a consequence of the adjudicator’s determination.”
