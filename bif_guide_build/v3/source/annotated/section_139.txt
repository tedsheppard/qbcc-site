[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 139 – Application to court by person prejudicially affected
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] A    Legislation
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 139    Application to court by person prejudicially affected 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] A person who alleges to be prejudicially affected by a claim of a subcontractor’s charge may apply to any court of competent jurisdiction for an order that—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the claim be cancelled; or
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the effect of the claim be modified.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] The court must hear and determine the application summarily and may make such order as it considers appropriate.
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] B    Commentary
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 139.1    The meaning of “prejudicially affected”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The circumstances of when a person may be “prejudicially affected” are outlined, without limitation, in s 138 of the BIFSOPA (Qld). Sections 138 and 139 should be read together. The requisite prejudice to the applicant appears to be constituted by a mere retention of moneys by the employer or head contractor: Re Austco Pty Ltd (unreported, QSC, Weld M, OS 502 of 1984, 27 April 1984) at p 11. Where a plaintiff has commenced an action seeking to enforce its claim, a defendant may, on an application or on summons in that very action, seeking that the claim be summarily cancelled, be “prejudicially affected” for the purposes of this section: Ronnor Pty Ltd v D & R Fabrications Pty Ltd [1983] 2 Qd R 455 per Williams J at p 4.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The legislature has endeavoured to remove the problem, which can result in an intermediate contractor paying twice for the same work completed by a subcontractor. This has been done by giving the court the power to cancel a leapfrogging charge which is otherwise valid, or to modify it so as to avoid the intermediate contractor having to pay again for work which is the subject of the leapfrogging charge for which the intermediate contractor has already paid. The court retains a discretion under s 139BIFSOPA (Qld) as to whether or not to make an order cancelling a claim or modifying the effect of the claim, even when satisfied that a person has been prejudicially affected by a claim of charge under the Act. The fact that the legislature has expressly chosen to deem a contractor who has paid “an amount” to be prejudicially affected, is a particularly significant consideration as to whether the discretion conferred by s 139(2) of the BIFSOPA (Qld) is exercised. The legislature has introduced a departure from the approach of protecting the subcontractor in preference to the superior contractor, when the superior contractor has already paid for the work done by the subcontractor, which is the subject of the leapfrogging charge per Mullins J. Re University of Queensland [2003] 2 Qd R 577; [2003] QSC 158.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 139.2    Mode of application
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] If proceedings have not been commenced to enforce the charge, s 21 (see now s 139BIFSOPA (Qld)) together with O 2, r 1 of the Rules, indicates that the appropriate application is by originating summons: Re Austco Pty Ltd (unreported, QSC, Weld M, OS 502 of 1984, 27 April 1984) at p 11.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Multiplex Constructions v Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd [2004] QSC 198, (Multiplex 3) Chesterman J was asked to consider which party carried the onus of proving that a claim of charge should be cancelled or modified. His Honour decided to approach the application on the basis that the applicant (head contractor) must show that the respondent subcontractor had no arguable case, or no fairly arguable case in support of its charge.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In establishing the test to determine whether the court should act in terms of s 21 (now s 139BIFSOPA (Qld)), in Multiplex Constructions v Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd [2004] QSC 198, (Multiplex 3), Chesterman J said that he doubted that it was necessary to import into s 21 a requirement that the court consider the balance of convenience between allowing a charge to continue or cancelling it, and established the test as whether or not there was a serious question to be tried and whether the balance of convenience favoured the continuation or cancellation of the charge. This statement of the test under s 21 has been upheld in the Court of Appeal see Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd [2005] 1 Qd R 610; [2005] QCA 61, (Multiplex 4), per Jerrard JA at [6].
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 139.3    General
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] As s 139(2) states, the court shall hear and determine the application summarily: Ronnor Pty Ltd v D & R Fabrications Pty Ltd [1983] 2 Qd R 455 at p 457. Thus, an application may be inappropriate to determine a contentious factual issue between parties which ought to be determined in the ordinary way by action: Re Watpac Australia Pty Ltd [1996] 1 Qd R 229; see also Concept Constructions (Qld) Pty Ltd v Asphalt Pavements Pty Ltd (in liq) [2000] QSC 269.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 139.4    Modification order
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] If the modification of the amount of a charge is an appropriate option for the court then the onus is on the contractor to establish the amount to which a charge should be modified: Milgun Pty Ltd v Austco Pty Ltd [1988] 2 Qd R 670 per Kelly SPJ.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 139.5    Substitution for charge by bank undertaking
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Where a payment into court pursuant O 58, r 9 of the Rules of the Supreme Court to would attain the same object as a charge imposed by the operation of BIFSOPA, namely to secure payment to a subcontractor in accordance with his subcontract of all money payable, or that was to become payable to him for work done by him under the subcontract. It may be proper to exercise the court's jurisdiction under s 21(2) (now s 139(2)BIFSOPA (Qld)) to order cancellation of the relevant claim of charge upon such payment being made: Re DA Story Pty Ltd [1993] 2 Qd R 355. In certain circumstances, it might be appropriate for the court to exercise its jurisdiction under the subsection to modify the charge so as to charge an unconditional bank undertaking, lodged with the Registrar of the Court and any money received by Registrar pursuant to it to the same effect and extent as the subcontractor had on the employer's moneys prior to the order: Landoro (Qld) Pty Ltd (Admin Apptd) v Jensen International Pty Ltd (unreported, QSC, Demack J, 95 of 1974, 20 August 1999).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The principle upon which the court should proceed in an application under s 21 (equivalent to s 139BIFSOPA (Qld)) is that any substitution for the retention of money in court should disturb, only as far as necessary, the effects of the payment into court. That minimum disturbance will be achieved by permitting the provision of an unconditional bank undertaking instead of the retention of the money in court and by preserving the accrual of interest on the sum in question: see Rapid Contracting Pty Ltd (in liq) v Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd (unreported, QSC, Shepherdson J, 97 of 1014, 27 October 1998).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] As long as the subcontractor, or the rights conferred upon the subcontractor are in no way prejudiced, there seems to be no reason as to why an appropriate order for cancellation or modification ought not be made, where the prejudice pointed to is inability to have access to the funds in question on the part of the person who is prima facie entitled to them in the absence of the charge. The subcontractor will not be prejudiced, in any way, regarding the security of its position, and the head contractor will have access to the funds pending resolution of the dispute if the substitution of moneys paid into court is by a bank undertaking. A broad interpretation should be given to this section in those circumstances: see Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Queensland (unreported, QSC, Williams J, 10661 of 1996, 31 January 1997).
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 139.6    Summary determination
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] An application under s 21(2) (equivalent s 139(2)BIFSOPA (Qld)) may be inappropriate to determine a factual issue between the parties involving their post-contractual actions: Re Watpac Australia Pty Ltd [1996] 1 Qd R 229; followed James Hardie Building Systems Pty Ltd v Epoca Constructions Pty Ltd (1999) 15 BCL 199 per Shepherdson J at p 12.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The mere fact that there is a dispute over whether or not an amount is payable, does not mean a charge cannot attach: per Williams J in Concept Constructions (Qld) Pty Ltd v Asphalt Pavements Pty Ltd (in liq) [2000] QSC 269.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 139.7    Onus of applicant
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Cancellation of the charge on summary proceedings and before the trial of the action should only occur where it is demonstrated to the court that the case for the claim of a charge is clearly untenable (see General Steel Industries Inc v Commissioner for Railways (NSW) (1964) 112 CLR 125 at 130: per Shepherdson J in Re Northbuild Pty Ltd (unreported, QSC, Shepherdson J, OS 6399 of 1998, 25 September 1998) at p 23. However, the subcontractor has an onus of proving the amount claimed in the notice of claim of charge is a debt (ie a liquidated demand): James Hardie Building Systems Pty Ltd v Epoca Constructions Pty Ltd (1999) 15 BCL 199 per Shepherdson J at p 12.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The onus is on the person seeking to cancel or modify the charge. The test to be applied by the court when hearing such an application is whether there is an arguable case at [13], in Multiplex Constructions v Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd [2004] QSC 198, (Multiplex 3). See also Rapid Contracting Pty Ltd (in liq) v Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd (unreported, QSC, Shepherdson J, 97 of 1014, 27 October 1998) where it was held that the test is whether the basis of the charge is untenable. This decision has been upheld by the Court of Appeal see Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd [2005] 1 Qd R 610; [2005] QCA 61, (Multiplex 4).
