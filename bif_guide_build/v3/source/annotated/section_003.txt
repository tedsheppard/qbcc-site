[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 3 – The main purpose of Act
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] A    Legislation
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 3    The main purpose of Act 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] The main purpose of this Act is to help people working in the building and construction industry in being paid for the work they do. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] The main purpose of this Act is to be achieved primarily by—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] requiring the use of project bank accounts for particular building contracts; and
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] granting an entitlement to progress payments, whether or not the relevant contract makes provision for progress payments; and 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] establishing a procedure for—
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] making payment claims; and 
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] responding to payment claims; and 
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] the adjudication of disputed payment claims; and 
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] the recovery of amounts claimed; and
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] enabling the use of a statutory charge in favour of subcontractors for payment of the work they do.
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] B    Commentary
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 3.1    Object of the Act
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The legislative intention behind the NSW Act was considered by Hodgson JA in Brodyn Pty Ltd t/as Time Coast and Quality v Davenport [2004] NSWCA 394; (2004) 61 NSWLR 421; where his Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “The Act discloses a legislative intention to give an entitlement to progress payments, and to provide a mechanism to ensure that disputes concerning the amount of such payments are resolved with the minimum of delay. The payments themselves are only payments on account of a liability that will be finally determined otherwise: ss.3(4), 32. The procedure contemplates a minimum of opportunity for court involvement: ss.3(3), 25(4).”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also Roadtek, Department of Main Roads v Davenport & Ors [2006] QSC 47, [16] per Mackenzie J.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The general nature of the scheme under BCIPA, which is similar in wording to section 3 of the Act, was summarised by White JA in Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2011] QCA 22; [2012] 1 Qd R 525, [52]-[66]. 
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 3.2    A ‘fast track’ vehicle
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In the context of the NSW Act, the scheme established under the NSW Act has been described as ‘a fast track interim progress payment adjudication vehicle’. In Brodyn Pty Ltd t/as Time Coast and Quality v Davenport [2003] NSWSC 1019, Einstein J described this scheme in the following manner:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “What the legislature has effectively achieved is a fast track interim progress payment adjudication vehicle. That vehicle must necessarily give rise to many adjudication determinations which will simply be incorrect. That is because the adjudicator in some instances cannot possibly, in the time available and in which the determination is to be brought down, give the type of care and attention to the dispute capable of being provided upon a full curial hearing. It is also because of the constraints imposed upon the adjudicator…denying the parties any legal representation at any conference which may be called. But primarily it is because the nature and range of issues legitimate to be raised, particularly in the case of large construction contracts, are such that it often could simply never be expected that the adjudicator would produce the correct decision. What the legislature has provided for is no more or no less than an interim quick solution to progress payment disputes which solution critically does not determine the parties rights inter se. Those rights may be determined by curial proceedings, the Court then having available to it the usual range of relief, most importantly including the right to a proprietor to claw back progress payments which it had been forced to make through the adjudication determination procedures. That clawback route expressly includes the making of restitution orders.” (emphasis original)
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] This description by Einstein J has been cited with approval under BCIPA.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Along similar lines, the Court of Appeal has referred to the scheme under BCIPA as ‘an expeditious, simple and cheap means of obtaining progress payments’: Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386, [63] (Muir JA, Gotterson and Morrison JJA agreeing).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The effect of the above is that the use of adjudication proceedings for wholesale construction disputes may be inimical to the object of the Act. In Illawarra Retirement Trust v Denham Constructions Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 1173, McDougall J said that: 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “one of the fundamental purposes that the Act seeks to achieve is the swift and cheap resolution of disputes as to interim entitlements. The practice, all too often seen, and clearly manifested by the parties in this litigation, of treating each adjudication application as a full-blown construction dispute seems to me to be inimical to the objects of the Act.”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The tendency for ‘prolixity’ in adjudication proceedings on complex construction disputes was discussed by Margaret Wilson J in John Holland Pty Ltd v Walz Marine Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 39 where her Honour said (at [58]):
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “[a] great deal of time and expenditure was undoubtedly invested in the preparation of the claim and payment schedule and the adjudication application and adjudication response. The thoroughness with which they were presented bordered on prolixity. I understand that this is a feature of many adjudications under BCIPA. While I appreciate that the quantum of progress claims made under the Act can be very high, and that the outcome of the adjudication can have serious consequences for the liquidity of either or both parties, I doubt that the Legislature envisaged such a development. The adjudication process may be more suited to comparatively small, uncomplicated claims than to large, complex claims.”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Mackenzie J in Roadtek, Department of Main Roads v Davenport & Ors [2006] QSC 47 also made note of the limitations of BCIPA, observing that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “[i]t is essentially a summary process based on written information. …The process is not conducive to resolving questions of fact that cannot be resolved on written submissions or in a conference or by inspection.”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] As per the above extract from Brodyn, judicial observations of BCIPA and its counterparts often refer to or note the speed or informality of the statutory mechanisms: see also Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA Pty Ltd) & Ors [2007] QSC 333, [20] (Chesterman J); Intero Hospitality Projects Pty Ltd v Empire Interior (Australia) Pty Ltd & Anor [2008] QCA 83, [51]-[52] (Muir JA).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also Chase Oyster Bar Pty Ltd v Hamo Industries Pty Ltd [2010] NSWCA 190; (2010) 78 NSWLR 393, [25].
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 3.3    ‘Pay now, argue later’
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] BCIPA (and equivalent security of payment acts around Australia) has been described as a ‘pay now, argue later’ system. The origin of this phrase was under the NSW Act in Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Luikens & Anor [2003] NSWSC 1140 at [96] per Palmer J. This phrase was cited with approval in Milburn Lake Pty Ltd v Andritz Pty Ltd [2016] VSC 3 at [6] per J Forrest J.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Given the similarity of BCIPA to its New South Wales equivalent, this phrase has also been used to describe the nature of the scheme in Queensland.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 3.4    Cash flow
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] A further noted object or purpose of the Act (and its equivalents) is to maintain the flow of cash under construction contracts: see Amflo Constructions Pty Ltd v Anthony Jefferies [2003] NSWSC 856, [25] (Campbell J).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Amflo, Campbell J said (at [25]):
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “A fundamental feature of the legislation is that, apart from the fact that parties to a construction contract cannot contract out of the rights given by the legislation (section 34) nothing in Part 3 of the Act (section 13-32 inclusive) affects any of the rights that parties to a construction contract have (section 32(1)). The concern of the Act is with maintaining the cash flow of claimants, by enabling them to recover quickly amounts which the adjudication process says they are entitled to. It is possible for the person who pays the amount of money which an adjudication has found due to seek to reclaim that money, in court proceedings which decide what the ultimate legal rights of the parties are. An evident purpose of the Act is that, if there is to be such litigation, it will start from a position where the claimant has been paid the amount which the adjudication process has decided should be paid.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] This importance of cash flow is reflected in the second reading speech for the Building and Construction Industry Security of Payment Amendment Bill 2002 (NSW) where the-then Minister said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Cash flow is the lifeblood of the construction industry. Final determination of disputes is often very time consuming and costly. We are determined that, pending final determination of all disputes, contractors and subcontractors should be able to obtain a prompt interim payment on account, as always intended under the Act…”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The role of cash-flow under BCIPA has also been discussed in Queensland cases: see Capricorn Quarries Pty Ltd v Inline Communication Construction Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 388; [2013] 2 Qd R 1 where Jackson J said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “The essential operative effect of BCIPA is that it confers a statutory right to a provisional progress payment upon a claimant who qualifies for that right, with provisions to speedily value and establish the right without curial proceedings and to vindicate the established right by enforcing it as a court judgment. Commercially, that outcome has an important cash-flow effect.”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similar comments were made in HM Hire Pty Ltd v National Plant and Equipment Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 274; [2014] 2 Qd R 44, where Applegarth J said that:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “The purpose of the Act is that the process of adjudication established under it will provide a speedy and effective means of ensuring cash-flow to a party from the party with which it contracts. It reflects an appreciation by the legislature that an assured cash-flow is essential to the commercial survival of builders.”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The significance placed on cash-flow under BCIPA can also be seen in BRB Modular Pty Ltd v AWX Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 218 where it was a factor in Applegarth J holding a statutory declaration to be an invalid precondition to a reference date arising under BCIPA. His Honour held the statutory declaration to have the effect of preventing cash-flow, without any corresponding benefit under BCIPA.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, while the importance of cash-flow has been held to be a purpose of BCIPA, in Patrick Stevedores Operations No. 2 Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2014] NSWSC 1413, Ball J said that the NSW Act:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “is concerned to ensure that contractors are paid progress payments, it is not concerned to ensure that they are paid immediately for any work that they do. Rather, it is concerned to ensure that they are paid periodically and promptly so that they are not out of pocket for significant sums of money while ensuring that excessive administrative burdens are not placed on those liable to pay for the work.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] As I have said, the purpose of the Act is not to ensure that a contractor is paid for work as soon as it is done. Nor do I think it is to ensure that a contractor is paid everything it is owed promptly. Rather, the purpose of the Act is to provide a practical mechanism to ensure that contractors receive progress payments for the work that they do.”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also R J Neller Building Pty Ltd v Ainsworth [2008] QCA 397; [2009] 1 Qd R 390, [40]-[41] (Keane JA).
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 3.5    Interim
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] It has also been described that BCIPA (and its counterparts) are ‘essentially interim in nature’: Northbuild Constructions Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2011] QCA 22; [2012] 1 Qd R 525, 550 [66].
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also HM Hire Pty Ltd v National Plant and Equipment Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 274; [2014] 2 Qd R 44, 55 [30].
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 3.6    Remuneration-progress payment distinction
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] There has been a distinction drawn between the right to remuneration for construction work, and the right to obtain a progress payment. In Roseville Bridge Marina Pty Ltd v Bellingham Marine Australia Pty Ltd [2009] NSWSC 320, Brereton J said:
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] BCIPA (and by extension the Act) does not create a right to remuneration for construction work – that right is created by the construction contract. What the Act does is to create and regulate a right to obtain a progress payment. This distinction was adopted in BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 346, [56] (Applegarth J).
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 3.7    A distinct contractual and statutory regime
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] The function of section 3, in creating a statutory entitlement, was discussed with context to section 8 of BCIPA by Fraser JA in John Holland Pty Ltd v Coastal Dredging & Construction Pty Ltd [2012] QCA 150; [2012] 2 Qd R 435 where his Honour said:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] “In considering that issue, the object of the Act expressed in s 7, and how that object is to be achieved, expressed in s 8, must be borne in mind.  An interpretation of the Act which best achieves its purpose is to be preferred to any other interpretation. Relevantly, s 8 makes it clear that the purpose of the Act is not merely to give statutory force to a contractual entitlement to progress payments.  Rather, the fundamental object of ensuring an entitlement to progress payments is to be achieved by granting a statutory entitlement to progress payments even where the contract itself makes provision for progress payments.  The extent to which those separate contractual and statutory entitlements coincide must be derived from the operative provisions of the Act.”
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Similar observations have been made in New South Wales in the context of the NSW Act: see Falgat Constructions Pty Ltd v Equity Australia Corporation Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 49, [13] (Handley JA, Santow JA and Pearlman AJA agreeing).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] See also Spankie & Ors v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 355, [9] (Fraser JA).
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Refer further to the commentary under 100.2 in relation to this dual regime.
