Enforcing a subcontractor’s charge

Time limit to commence

A subcontractor’s charge does not look after itself. Once the notice of claim has been given under s 122, copied to the contractor under s 123, and the contractor has responded under s 128, the holder of the security is sitting on money that is frozen only until the court does something with it. Section 130(2)(a) makes that explicit: the security is to be retained “until the court in which the subcontractor’s claim is heard makes an order under section 132 about enforcing the subcontractor’s charge over the security”. If you don’t put a proceeding before that court, no s 132 order is ever made and the freeze comes off.

Section 130 BIF Act — retention pending a s 132 court order

130 Use of security for benefit of subcontractor if contractor accepts liability for all claims

(1) This section applies if—

(a) a subcontractor gives a notice of claim to a person under section 122 because the person is obliged to pay money to a contractor under a contract; and

(b) the subcontractor gives a copy of the notice of claim to the contractor under section 123; and

(c) in the contractor’s response to the claim under section 128, the contractor accepts liability to pay the amount claimed; and

(d) the unsatisfied amount for the contract is more than the retained amount for the contract.

(2) The holder of a security for the contract must—

(a) retain the security until the court in which the subcontractor’s claim is heard makes an order under section 132 about enforcing the subcontractor’s charge over the security; or

(b) instead of retaining the security—

(i) if the security is held as an amount of money—pay the amount, up to the difference amount for the contract, to the subcontractor or subcontractors to whom the acceptance of liability relates; or

(ii) if the security is not held as an amount of money but may be converted into an amount of money—convert the security, wholly or partly, into an amount of money and pay the amount, up to the difference amount for the contract, to the subcontractor or subcontractors to whom the acceptance of liability relates.

(5) Subsections (2) and (3) do not stop the holder of the security from exercising an entitlement to use the security for securing the performance of the contract, including by keeping control of the security until the security would be required to be surrendered, wholly or partly, if this section did not apply.

Why the clock matters

The whole architecture of Chapter 4 assumes that a court proceeding is on foot or imminent. The notice of claim under s 122 and the copy under s 123 are the front-end formalities; the contractor’s s 128 response sorts the claim into the s 130 (accepted) or s 131 (disputed) channel; and from that point the security holder is holding either the original security, or money standing in for it, against a future order of the court under s 132. If you let the time to commence pass without filing, the foundation for retention falls away and the security holder is free to deal with the security on the contract’s own terms — including, under s 130(5), surrendering it back up the chain when the contract would otherwise require.

What “commence” means in this context

Commencing in time means filing a proceeding in the court that will hear the subcontractor’s claim — the same court referred to in s 130(2)(a) as “the court in which the subcontractor’s claim is heard”. That proceeding must seek relief under s 132 enforcing the charge over the security. Filing a debt claim against the contractor on its own is not the same thing: a money judgment against the contractor for the underlying debt does not, by itself, engage the s 132 machinery that releases the retained security or the funds paid into court under s 131. The proceeding has to be framed so the court can make the s 132 order the security holder is waiting for.

What the proceeding has to capture

To stop time and keep the charge alive, the originating process should put the s 130/s 132 framework squarely before the court. That means:

  • identifying the contract under which the money up the chain is owed and the work that grounds the underlying debt;
  • pleading the notice of claim under s 122 and the copy to the contractor under s 123, with dates;
  • pleading the contractor’s s 128 response and whether liability was accepted (s 130) or disputed (s 131);
  • quantifying the “unsatisfied amount”, the “retained amount” and the resulting “difference amount” for the contract, since s 130 only engages where the unsatisfied amount exceeds the retained amount; and
  • seeking, as primary relief, an order under s 132 enforcing the charge over the security held by the security holder.

You will normally name both the contractor (as the party against whom the underlying debt is asserted) and the security holder (as the party who has to act on the s 132 order). Without the security holder before the court, an order under s 132 is of limited practical use — it has to bind the entity actually holding the money.

If the contractor accepted liability under s 128

Even where the contractor has accepted liability and the security holder may, under s 130(2)(b), pay the difference amount directly to the subcontractor, you should still treat the time bar as live. The holder may instead choose to retain under s 130(2)(a) pending the s 132 order, and the personal-liability backstop in s 130(3) does not extend the time within which the proceeding has to be on foot. If retention is the chosen route, the order out of the court is what releases the money cleanly.

Practice tips

  • Diary the time-bar date the moment you serve the notice of claim under s 122. Don’t wait for the s 128 response to start the clock in your own system.
  • Plan the proceeding in parallel with the notice. Drafting an originating application that pleads s 122, s 123, s 128 and seeks s 132 relief takes time, and you don’t want to be doing it in the last week of the statutory window.
  • If the contractor accepts liability under s 128, still file. A s 132 order is the cleanest way to get the security released, and s 130(2)(a) contemplates the holder retaining until that order is made.
  • If the contractor disputes and money is going into court under s 131, the proceeding is unavoidable — that money only comes out on a court order. Build the s 132 application around it.
  • Name the security holder as a respondent. The s 132 order has to bind the party actually holding the security; otherwise you have a piece of paper but no mechanism to compel release.
  • If the deadline is genuinely at risk, file a protective originating process and refine the pleading later. A bare-bones filing inside time is recoverable; a perfect pleading filed out of time is not.
