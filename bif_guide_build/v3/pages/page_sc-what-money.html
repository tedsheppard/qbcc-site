Valid subcontractor’s charge

What money can be charged

A subcontractor’s charge does not bite on the world at large. It attaches only to a specific pool of money — the money owing up the contractual chain to the contractor who engaged you, plus any retention held under that contract. Identifying that pool, and describing it accurately in the notice of claim, is what makes the charge work.

Section 122 BIF Act — notice of claim (extract)

(1) To claim a subcontractor’s charge over money payable to the contractor under the contract, the subcontractor must give written notice (a notice of claim) to the person obliged to pay the money under the contract.

(6) The claim may relate only to—

(a) money payable to the subcontractor by the date the notice is given; and

(b) money to become payable to the subcontractor after the date the notice is given if the money is for work done by the subcontractor before that date.

(7) To remove any doubt, it is declared that a subcontractor’s charge on money payable under the contract includes a charge on a retention amount for the contract.

Money payable to the contractor under the contract

The fund the charge attaches to is the money the person above your contractor (the employer or a higher subcontractor) owes that contractor under their contract. You are reaching past your own contractual counterparty and grabbing what they are owed by the level above. The notice goes to that paying party, not to your own contractor: s 122(1).

Two things follow. First, your claim is capped by what is actually owed up the chain — if there is nothing payable to your contractor, there is nothing for the charge to attach to. Second, the moneys must be identifiable. The notice must allow the paying party to work out, on receipt, which contract’s funds are being charged and to what extent. Where a notice covers work spanning two separate head contracts and does not break the amount down between them, the money sought to be charged cannot be ascertained from the notice and the charge will not attach.1

Retention is included — and it changes the timing

Section 122(7) confirms that the charge over “money payable under the contract” includes a charge on the retention amount. You have two practical options. You can lodge a notice that captures money generally payable under the contract (which will sweep in retention as part of that fund), or you can lodge a notice expressly limited to the retention amount only. The choice matters because it changes the deadline.

A notice over money generally payable under the contract must be given within 3 months after practical completion: s 122(5). A notice limited to retention can be given at any time while work is being performed, and must be given within 3 months after the defects liability period expires: s 122(8). So if you are past the 3-month post-completion window and only retention is left, a notice expressed in terms of money generally payable will be too late — you need a retention-only notice.2

The Court of Appeal’s reading in Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd [2005] 1 Qd R 610; [2005] QCA 61 confirms that “completion” for the 3-month rule means completion of the whole subcontract works, not completion of the particular item of work the claim is about. That is the appellate position you should plan around when calculating the cut-off.3

Describing the fund — don’t blur the categories

The charge will fail if the notice is ambiguous about which fund it is reaching for. The leading caution is Re FFE Group (Qld) Pty Ltd [1984] 1 Qd R 267, where McPherson J considered a notice that described the money as “money and/or retention money”:

such a formula led to uncertainty about which money was subject to the statutory charge and so liable to be retained and would therefore eliminate the distinction maintained between the two… It was accordingly a matter of substance and not merely a matter of form.

Compare that with Road Surfaces Group Pty Ltd v Brown [1987] 2 Qd R 792, where Williams J accepted a notice referring to “all moneys, including retention moneys”: a charge can be claimed on retention only, on other moneys only, or on both — the only consequence of the third option is that the tighter 3-month-after-completion deadline applies, not the longer retention deadline.4 The line between the two cases is whether the notice positively claims both funds or leaves the reader to guess.

Scope: what you can claim and when

Section 122(6) defines the temporal scope of the claim. You can claim money already payable to you when you give the notice, plus money that will become payable after the notice for work you did before the notice. You cannot reach forward to charge future work that has not yet been performed. If you keep working and earn more after lodging, that later work needs to be picked up by a fresh claim, not absorbed into the original one. And under s 122(4) the notice can be lodged before payment falls due or before the work is finished — you do not have to wait for an unpaid invoice.

The s 113 anti-avoidance rule

Section 113 closes off the obvious workaround. For the purposes of the charge, the “money payable to the contractor under the contract” is taken to include money the paying party has already paid out in reduction of the contract price to anyone other than you, unless that payment was made in good faith, not for the purpose of defeating the charge, and without contravening s 126. So an employer who hurries a payment to the contractor (or a third party) once it senses a charge coming cannot point to an empty account when the notice lands.

What this means on a project

  • Work out what is actually owed up the chain to your contractor before you draft the notice. The charge can only attach to that fund — if nothing is payable, there is nothing to charge.
  • Send the notice to the paying party (the employer or higher subcontractor), not just to your own contractor. The notice up the chain is the one that creates the charge under s 122(1).
  • Decide deliberately whether you are claiming over money generally payable, retention only, or both — and say so clearly. Avoid “and/or” formulations that blur the two.
  • Calendar both deadlines. 3 months after practical completion of the whole subcontract works for a general notice; 3 months after the defects liability period for a retention-only notice. If you miss the first window and the only money left is retention, lodge a retention-only notice.
  • If your work straddles two contracts between your contractor and the same paying party, split the amount between them in the notice. A single un-allocated figure across two contracts will not be ascertainable.
  • If you suspect the paying party is racing to pay your contractor out before your notice lands, s 113 is your friend — payments made to defeat the charge are still treated as money payable for charge purposes.
