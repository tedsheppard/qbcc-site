Retention trust accounts

Penalties for non-compliance

The retention trust regime in Chapter 2, Part 3 is enforced almost entirely through criminal-style penalties attached to specific obligations. Most of the operative duties — opening the account, depositing the retention, releasing it back to the subcontractor, notifying the commissioner, completing training — carry their own maximum penalty, and the heavier ones include a custodial alternative. Understanding which obligation carries which penalty matters because it tells you where the regulator and the courts are most likely to push back, and where the personal exposure of the trustee’s officers is greatest.

Penalty units across Chapter 2, Part 3 BIF Act

s 34(2) — failure by the contracting party to open a retention trust account at a financial institution within the time required: 500 penalty units.

s 34A(1) — account not held at an approved financial institution: 200 penalty units.

s 34A(2) — account not held under a name that includes the trustee’s name and the word ‘trust’: 200 penalty units.

s 34A(3) — deposits or withdrawals made by methods other than ones that create an electronic record: 500 penalty units.

s 34A(4) — closing the account other than as permitted: 200 penalty units.

s 34B(2) — failure to notify the commissioner of opening, closing, name change or transfer of the account within 5 business days: 200 penalty units.

s 34C(1) — transferring the account to an alternative financial institution otherwise than as permitted: 200 penalty units.

s 35(2) — failure by the contracting party to deposit the retention amount into the retention trust account: 200 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment.

s 35A(1) — depositing money into the account for any purpose other than withholding a retention amount or repaying a withdrawal made in error: 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.

s 36(1) — withdrawing for an unauthorised purpose: 300 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment.

s 36(2) — withdrawing for a defects-correction payment to the trustee before the defects liability period ends: 300 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment.

s 36(3) — failure to repay an amount withdrawn in contravention of s 36(1) as soon as practicable: 300 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment.

s 36A(2) — releasing a retention amount otherwise than by withdrawing it from the trust account and depositing it into the contracted party’s account: 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.

s 40(1) — failure to give the contracted party a notice of retention trust before withholding the amount: 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.

s 40A(2) — failure to notify the contracted party of a deposit or withdrawal within 5 business days, without reasonable excuse: 100 penalty units.

s 41(5) and s 41(6) — failure to ensure that the nominated administrator (or, if none, the trustee itself) completes the prescribed retention trust training within the prescribed time: 100 penalty units.

The structure of the penalty regime

Considered as a hierarchy, the penalties make the legislative priorities apparent. The heaviest exposure — 300 penalty units or two years’ imprisonment — attaches to s 36, which governs what comes out of the retention trust account. Withdrawing for an unauthorised purpose, withdrawing too early for defects work, or failing to repay money withdrawn in breach of the section all attract the same maximum. The next tier — 200 penalty units or two years’ imprisonment — attaches to s 35(2), the deposit obligation: the act that puts money into the trust in the first place. Together, these two sections carry Chapter 2, Part 3. If retention money never reaches the trust, or is taken out for the wrong reason, the protective machinery in Part 3 has nothing to operate on.

A second tier of imprisonable offences — one year, plus 200 penalty units — addresses three integrity rules: s 35A(1) (no foreign deposits into the trust account), s 36A(2) (release must be by trust-to-beneficiary bank transfer, not cash, set-off or in-kind), and s 40(1) (the contracted party must be told, in writing, that a retention trust is being used before the money is withheld). These provisions exist to keep the trust account untainted, to ensure release happens in money rather than by accounting entries, and to put the subcontractor on notice that their retention is held on trust rather than as a general debt.

Penalty-only obligations

The remaining contraventions are punishable by a fine alone. The 500-penalty-unit obligations — s 34(2) (open the account) and s 34A(3) (use only electronic-record methods for deposits and withdrawals) — are the highest of the fine-only band, reflecting that without an account, and without an audit trail, none of the downstream protections function. The 200-penalty-unit band covers the account-naming, account-location, account-closure, commissioner-notification and institution-transfer rules in s 34A and ss 34B–34C. The lightest penalties — 100 penalty units — apply to the beneficiary-notification obligation in s 40A(2) and the training obligation in s 41(5)–(6).

The training and notification penalties being lower does not mean those obligations can be deprioritised: they are the obligations the commissioner is most likely to detect on a routine audit, because they leave the clearest paper trail. Missing the s 34B(2) notice of an account opening is the type of contravention that surfaces by comparison of bank records to commissioner records, with no need for a complaint from a subcontractor.

Imputed conduct under s 36(4)

Section 36(4) extends the withdrawal offence by deeming the trustee to have made a withdrawal where the trustee either authorises another person to make it, or knowingly contributes to it being made. That is significant in a corporate-trustee context: a director who approves a payment instruction, or who knowingly allows the bookkeeper to draw down retention to fund payroll, is within the deeming provision and is exposed to the s 36(1) maximum. The penalty is on the trustee, but the deeming language — “authorises any person”, “knowingly contributes” — is the mechanism by which liability practically extends to the individuals inside the trustee.

Practice tips

  • The two offences carrying imprisonment maxima of two years — s 35(2) (failure to deposit) and s 36(1)/(2)/(3) (unauthorised withdrawal or failure to repay) — are where the regulator’s and the courts’ attention will be sharpest. Build your retention controls around those two events: every retention amount goes in, and nothing comes out except for the s 36(1)(a)–(c) purposes (and only after the defects liability period for the s 36(1)(b) trustee-payment limb).
  • Treat s 36(4) as a personal-exposure provision. A director or officer who authorises or knowingly contributes to a non-compliant withdrawal is within the deeming language. Document who has authority over the trust account and confine that authority to people who understand the s 36(1) purposes.
  • Do not use the retention trust account as a convenience deposit. Section 35A(1) prohibits any deposit other than a retention amount or a repayment of a withdrawal made in error — one year’s imprisonment available. If a deposit is made into the wrong account by mistake, repay it under s 35A(1)(b) rather than leaving it in.
  • Release retention only by bank transfer from the trust account to the contracted party’s account (s 36A(2)). Do not release by set-off, by cheque drawn on the trustee’s general account, or by leaving the money on account. The penalty is 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.
  • Give the s 40 notice of retention trust to the contracted party in writing before retention is withheld. If the account had not been opened in time, the notice must follow within 5 business days of opening (s 40(4)). The penalty for missing s 40 entirely is 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.
  • The lower-tier penalties — account naming and location (s 34A(1)–(2)), commissioner notifications (s 34B(2)), institution transfers (s 34C(1)), beneficiary notices (s 40A(2)) and training (s 41(5)–(6)) — are the items most likely to appear on a commissioner audit. Calendar them and treat them as compliance obligations rather than optional administrative tasks.
  • All deposits and withdrawals must be made by methods that create an electronic record (s 34A(3) — 500 penalty units). No cash, no over-the-counter cheques without electronic record. The audit trail is itself a statutory obligation.
  1. A “penalty unit” takes its dollar value from the Penalties and Sentences Act 1992 (Qld), s 5 and the Penalties and Sentences Regulation 2015; the value is reset annually. The figures in the body of this page are the BIF Act maxima expressed in penalty units, not in dollars.
  2. The deeming rule in s 36(4) covers both authorising a withdrawal and knowingly contributing to one being made. Read with s 36(1), it is the principal route to personal liability for officers of a corporate trustee.
  3. Sections 38 and 39 are not reproduced here as the source files were not available; readers should consult the current reprint of the BIF Act for any obligations and penalties contained in those sections.
