Requirements of a payment schedule

Identify the payment claim

A payment schedule must make clear which payment claim it is responding to. If the document does not tie itself to the payment claim, the schedule may be treated as no schedule at all, exposing the respondent to summary judgment for the full claimed amount and precluding it from raising reasons later in adjudication.

Section 69 BIF Act — meaning of payment schedule (chapeau and (a))

69 Meaning of payment schedule

A payment schedule, responding to a payment claim, is a written document that—

(a) identifies the payment claim to which it responds; and

…

What “identify” actually requires

The Act doesn’t prescribe a form. The leading Queensland statement of the test comes from Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA) Pty Ltd [2007] QSC 333, where Chesterman J said the Act “emphasises speed and informality” and the document is to be assessed on content, not label:

One should not approach the question whether a document satisfies the description of a payment schedule… from an unduly critical viewpoint. No particular form is required. One is concerned only with whether the content of the document in question satisfies the statutory description.

That passage was approved by the Queensland Court of Appeal in Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Traspunt No 5 Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 119 at [24] (Muir JA, Holmes and Chesterman JJA agreeing), so it is the controlling Queensland authority on s 69 generally and on what s 69(a) requires in particular. A document does not need to be headed “payment schedule” or even use the phrase — what matters is whether, read in context, the recipient can tell that this is the response to a specific payment claim.1

How identification is achieved in practice

Identification can be express or by reasonable inference from context. Useful indicators include:

  • quoting the payment claim’s reference number, date or invoice number;
  • responding line-by-line to the items in the payment claim;
  • attaching or referring to the payment claim itself, or a letter of demand that contains it;2 or
  • responding the same day, where there is no other document with which the claim could be confused.3

You don’t need all of these. A short, “relatively informal” email referring to “paperwork, stating I owe you this outrageous amount of money” was enough in Gisley Investments Pty Ltd v Williams & Anor [2010] QSC 178, because it was sent the day the claim arrived and there was nothing else it could have meant. By contrast, an email aimed at one component of a multi-item claim — without making clear it is a response to the whole payment claim — will fail the s 69(a) test.4

Queensland is stricter than New South Wales

The point that often trips respondents up is that s 69(a) requires the schedule to identify the payment claim, full stop. In Melaleuca View Pty Ltd v Sutton Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2019] QSC 226, Brown J held that a respondent’s document failed as a payment schedule because it did not identify the initial payment claim it was supposedly responding to. Her Honour acknowledged the result might be seen as harsh, but emphasised that “the onus was on a respondent to ensure it had complied with the requirements of the Qld Act.” The contrast Brown J drew with the NSW regime is important: s 69 says “state” rather than the looser “indicate” used in New South Wales, and that signals a higher standard of clarity for Queensland respondents across all of s 69(a)–(c).

The take-away is that you can’t rely on the assessor or the court to reconstruct your schedule from external materials. If the document doesn’t identify the payment claim on its face (or with an obvious cross-reference to an attached or recently-served document), it is not a payment schedule.

Practice tips

  • Open every payment schedule with an unambiguous reference: “This is our payment schedule under s 76 of the BIF Act in response to your payment claim dated [date], reference [number], for $[amount].” That single sentence neutralises any s 69(a) argument.
  • If you respond by email or short letter rather than a formal schedule, attach or quote the payment claim, or reply on the same email chain. Don’t leave the recipient guessing which document you’re engaging with.
  • If the payment claim has multiple components or invoices, make the schedule address the whole claim — an item-by-item response, or a clear global statement plus reasons. Responding to only one part risks the schedule being treated as not identifying the claim at all.
  • Don’t rely on a payment certificate issued by the superintendent doubling as your payment schedule unless your contract has a properly drafted deeming clause and the certificate, read with that clause, satisfies s 69 on its face.5
  • If two documents are sent in time, the later one can be the payment schedule. Make sure whichever document you intend to be the schedule clearly identifies the payment claim — don’t leave it to the adjudicator to pick.6
