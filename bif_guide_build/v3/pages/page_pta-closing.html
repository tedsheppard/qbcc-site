Project trust accounts

Closing the project trust

A project trust does not continue indefinitely. Section 21 of the BIF Act sets the conditions the trustee must satisfy before dissolving the trust — when dissolution is permitted, what happens to any money remaining in the project trust account, and the written notice that must be given to the commissioner once dissolution has occurred.

Section 21 BIF Act — Ending project trust

(1) Once a project trust is established for a contract, the trustee may dissolve the trust only if—

(a) there are no longer any subcontractor beneficiaries for the trust; or

(b) the only remaining work to be carried out under the contract is maintenance work.

(2) A project trust is dissolved by the trustee—

(a) closing the project trust account; and

(b) giving written notice to the commissioner of the trust having been dissolved.

(3) A trustee is taken not to dissolve the project trust by closing the project trust account if the account was only closed for the purpose of transferring the account to another financial institution under section 18C.

(4) When dissolving the project trust, the trustee may pay itself the following amounts—

(a) any amount for interest that the trustee is entitled to under section 51D;

(b) any remaining amount that is not owing to a subcontractor beneficiary.

(5) In this section—

maintenance work see section 15D(2).

When the trust may be closed

Dissolution is not at the trustee’s option. Section 21(1) only permits the trust to be wound up in two situations: there are no remaining subcontractor beneficiaries, or the only work still to be carried out under the head contract is maintenance work. Until one of those conditions is satisfied, the trust must continue — the requirement for the project trust runs from the day it is first required and continues until the trust is dissolved under s 21, regardless of any later variation, change in contract price or change in scope.

“No longer any subcontractor beneficiaries” means each subcontractor beneficiary has been paid all amounts owing for subcontracted work. A subcontractor ceases to be a beneficiary only when the contracted party has no further payment obligation to them under the subcontract. If a subcontractor is still owed a retention release, a final claim, an adjudicated amount or a court-ordered amount, that subcontractor remains a beneficiary and the trust cannot be dissolved on the s 21(1)(a) limb.

The maintenance-work limb in s 21(1)(b) reflects the fact that defects-liability and maintenance phases can run for a year or more after practical completion, beyond the point at which any further subcontract work is being carried out. Once the only remaining work is maintenance work as defined in s 15D(2), the trustee may proceed to dissolve the trust even if the head contract itself has not yet ended.

Distribution of remaining funds

Section 21(4) governs the treatment of any amount remaining in the project trust account at the point of dissolution. The trustee may pay itself two categories of money: interest the trustee is entitled to under s 51D, and any remaining amount that is not owing to a subcontractor beneficiary. The order of operations is significant. Subcontractor beneficiary entitlements come first — the trust cannot be dissolved under s 21(1)(a) while a beneficiary is still owed money, and even on the maintenance-work limb the trustee’s entitlement under s 21(4)(b) is confined to amounts “not owing to a subcontractor beneficiary”.

That structure is consistent with the purpose of the chapter, which is to ensure that funds paid to the contracted party for particular contracts are held in trust to protect the interests of subcontractors. The trustee is not permitted to clear the account in its own favour and leave a beneficiary unpaid; the trust funds remain impressed with the trust until beneficiary entitlements are satisfied. Only the residue — head-contract margin, retention released back from subcontract retention trusts that has flowed through the account, surplus on the contracting party’s payments — may be drawn down by the trustee on dissolution.

Interest is dealt with in its own subsection because s 51D entitles the trustee to interest accrued on the project trust account in defined circumstances; that entitlement crystallises on dissolution and is paid out under s 21(4)(a).

Closing the account and notifying the commissioner

Section 21(2) prescribes the two mechanical steps that actually effect dissolution. The trustee must close the project trust account at the financial institution, and must give written notice to the commissioner that the trust has been dissolved. Both steps are required — closing the account without notifying the commissioner does not dissolve the trust for the purposes of the Act, and notifying the commissioner without closing the account is equally incomplete.

There is one carve-out. Under s 21(3), if the only reason the account is being closed is to transfer the project trust to another financial institution under s 18C, the trustee is taken not to have dissolved the trust. That preserves the integrity of the trust during a change of financial institution — the trust continues, the new account takes over, and no notice of dissolution should be given.

Practice tips

  • The project trust account should not be closed at practical completion as a matter of routine. The s 21(1) conditions must be satisfied first — either every subcontractor beneficiary has been paid in full, or the only work still to be carried out is maintenance work.
  • Before dissolving, conduct a final reconciliation against every subcontract: claims paid, retention released, any adjudicated or court-ordered amount, any pending charge under chapter 4. If any amount is still owing to a subcontractor beneficiary, the trust must continue.
  • On dissolution, the trustee may pay itself the s 51D interest and any residue not owing to a subcontractor beneficiary — in that order, and only after beneficiary entitlements are satisfied. Document the calculation; the trust records must be kept under s 52.
  • Two formal steps effect dissolution: close the project trust account at the financial institution, and give written notice to the commissioner that the trust has been dissolved. Doing one without the other does not constitute dissolution.
  • A change of financial institution should not be treated as a dissolution. The s 18C transfer mechanism applies — s 21(3) deems the trust to continue and no notice of dissolution should be given to the commissioner.

The duration of the project trust requirement, and its insulation from contract variations, follows from s 12(4) BIF Act — the requirement “continues until the project trust is dissolved under section 21” regardless of any variation, amendment, change in contract price or change in contracted work.

The protective purpose informing s 21 is stated at s 7 BIF Act — “to ensure that funds paid to the contracted party for particular contracts are held in a trust to protect the interests of subcontractors”.
