Project trust accounts

Deposits into the project trust

Once a project trust has been established, s 19 controls the flow of money in. Every payment the contracting party makes to the contracted party in connection with the project trust contract has to land in the project trust account — not the contracted party’s general operating account, not a director’s loan account, not a related-entity sweep account. The deposit obligation is the mechanism that gives Chapter 2 its protective force: if the funds never reach the trust, the subcontractor beneficiaries in s 11A have nothing to claim against.

Section 19 BIF Act — All payments from contracting party to be deposited in project trust account

(1) This section applies to any of the following amounts paid by the contracting party to the contracted party in connection with a project trust contract—

(a) an amount paid in accordance with the terms of the contract;

(b) an amount paid because the contracting party is liable under section 77 to pay the amount to the contracted party in connection with the contract;

(c) an amount paid under chapter 3, part 4 because of an adjudication of a disputed progress payment relating to the contract;

(d) an amount paid because of a final and binding dispute resolution process relating to the contract;

(e) an amount paid because of a court order relating to the contract;

(f) an amount, paid for any other reason, that reduces the unpaid amount of the contract price for the contract.

(2) The contracting party must deposit the amount into the project trust account for the contract (the deposit obligation) unless—

(a) the amount was due to be paid before the trust was established; or

(b) the amount is paid into court; or

(c) the amount is to be withheld because of a payment withholding request given to the contracting party under section 97B; or

(d) the amount is paid directly to a person under chapter 4 in connection with a subcontractor’s charge; or

(e) the contracting party has a reasonable excuse for failing to deposit the amount into the account.

Maximum penalty—200 penalty units.

(3) Once the amount is deposited into the project trust account, the deposit is taken to be a payment made by the contracting party to the contracted party and discharges the contracting party’s liability to pay that amount to the contracted party.

(4) If an amount is paid to the contracted party or its agent in contravention of the deposit obligation, the contracted party must deposit the amount into the project trust account as soon as practicable after receiving the amount.

Maximum penalty for subsection (4)—200 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment.

What “all payments” actually means

Section 19(1) is drafted to be exhaustive. Read the six paragraphs together: any amount paid “in connection with” the project trust contract has to be deposited, whether the payment is made under the contract’s own progress claim machinery (paragraph (a)), as a default amount triggered by s 77 of the BIF Act (paragraph (b)), as the cash payable on a successful adjudication of a disputed progress payment (paragraph (c)), as a payment made to settle or finalise a dispute through ADR (paragraph (d)), as compliance with a court judgment or order on the contract (paragraph (e)), or for any other reason that has the effect of reducing the unpaid balance of the contract price (paragraph (f)).

Paragraph (f) is the qualification-all, and it is deliberately broad. If a payment’s effect is to reduce what is still owed under the contract price, it is caught — regardless of the label put on the payment in the head contractor’s ledger. A “goodwill” payment from the principal towards an overdue claim, a settlement of a disputed variation, or a release of contested retention all reduce the unpaid amount of the contract price and so all attract the deposit obligation. The form the payment takes does not matter; the function does.

Timing of the deposit

Section 19(2) does not, on its face, prescribe a specific deadline for the contracting party to make the deposit. The obligation is expressed as “must deposit the amount into the project trust account”, and the natural reading is that the deposit must be made by way of the payment itself — in other words, the contracting party pays the contracted party by depositing into the trust account, rather than first paying into a general account and later sweeping. Section 19(3) confirms that mechanism: the deposit into the project trust account is the act that discharges the contracting party’s payment liability to the contracted party. So the timing of the deposit is the timing of the payment itself: whenever the contract or s 77 or the adjudicated decision or the court order calls for payment, that payment must be made into the trust account on that date.

The position is different where money has already been paid the wrong way. Section 19(4) deals with the case where, in breach of s 19(2), the contracting party has paid the amount directly to the contracted party (or its agent) into a non-trust account. In that scenario, the contracted party itself becomes responsible for moving the money into the trust account “as soon as practicable” after receipt. The penalty in s 19(4) — 200 penalty units or two years’ imprisonment — is materially heavier than the s 19(2) penalty on the contracting party, reflecting the fact that the contracted party is the trustee and is in immediate control of the funds the moment they hit its account.

Carve-outs from the deposit obligation

Section 19(2) lists five exceptions. Four are mechanical and tightly drawn:

  • amounts that fell due before the project trust was established (paragraph (a)) — an early progress claim that was due and payable before the trust came into existence under s 17 is not retrofitted into the trust;
  • amounts paid into court (paragraph (b)) — for example, payments made under an order to pay the disputed sum into court pending resolution;
  • amounts withheld in response to a payment withholding request from a subcontractor under s 97B (paragraph (c));
  • amounts paid directly to a third party under Chapter 4 in connection with a subcontractor’s charge (paragraph (d)).

The fifth exception is the soft one: paragraph (e) excuses non-deposit where the contracting party has a “reasonable excuse”. It is not defined in the Act and should not be relied on as a planning tool. Administrative inconvenience, a broken banking integration, or the head contractor having asked for payment to a different account will not, on any sensible reading, qualify. The exception is there to deal with the genuinely abnormal case — not to give the contracting party a discretion to bypass the trust.

Advance and upfront payments

Section 19 does not contain a special rule for advance, mobilisation or upfront payments — and that is the point. The section captures “any of the following amounts paid by the contracting party to the contracted party in connection with a project trust contract”, including in paragraph (a) any amount paid in accordance with the terms of the contract and in paragraph (f) any amount that reduces the unpaid amount of the contract price for any other reason. An advance payment, mobilisation payment, deposit on award, or any other upfront sum paid before work has been physically performed all sit within those words: each is paid in connection with the project trust contract and each, on its own terms, reduces the unpaid amount of the contract price (or, depending on the drafting, sits as a credit against future claims, which has the same arithmetic effect).

The practical consequence is that mobilisation and advance payments must be deposited into the project trust account in the same way as a progress payment. The only escape is the s 19(2)(a) timing carve-out: if the advance was contractually due and was paid before the trust was established under s 17, it sits outside the regime. The first payment from the contracting party to the contracted party is itself one of the events that establishes the trust under s 17(a), so in practice an advance payment paid after the s 12 requirement has been engaged but before the account is opened needs careful handling. The contracted party still has to open the account within 20 business days of the first subcontract (s 18(2)), and any payment that was due to be made after the trust was established has to be deposited; structuring a head contract so the entire mobilisation is paid into a non-trust account before the first subcontract is entered, in order to keep that money out of the trust, will not work where the s 12 requirement is already engaged at the time of payment.

Practice tips

  • Once the project trust account is open, all payments from the principal under the head contract should be made directly into the trust account — not into the head contractor’s general operating account with a later transfer. The deposit is the payment for s 19(3) discharge purposes.
  • Paragraph (f) of s 19(1) is a catch-all. Settlement payments, payments resolving disputed variations, releases of contested retention and any other amounts that reduce the unpaid contract price all attract the deposit obligation, regardless of how they are labelled in correspondence.
  • Adjudicated amounts (s 19(1)(c)) and court-ordered payments (s 19(1)(e)) go into the trust account, not directly to the head contractor’s nominated account. Update payment instructions for the principal’s legal and finance teams accordingly.
  • Mobilisation, advance and upfront payments fall within s 19. Treat them like any other payment under the contract: deposit into the trust account once the trust is established. The s 19(2)(a) carve-out only saves payments that were due before the trust came into existence.
  • If money is mistakenly paid into the contracted party’s general account in breach of s 19(2), s 19(4) puts the obligation back on the contracted party to move it to the trust account “as soon as practicable”. The penalty for getting that wrong is 200 penalty units or two years’ imprisonment — meaningfully heavier than the s 19(2) penalty on the principal.
  • Don’t plan around the “reasonable excuse” exception in s 19(2)(e). It is for the genuinely abnormal case. Banking inconvenience, a head contractor request to pay elsewhere, or a contractual side-letter directing payment to a different account are not reasonable excuses.
  • For payments responding to a subcontractor’s s 97B payment withholding request, or a Chapter 4 subcontractor’s charge, follow the carve-outs in s 19(2)(c) and (d) precisely — those amounts bypass the trust and go where the statute directs.
  1. On when the trust is established — and therefore when the s 19(2)(a) timing carve-out closes — see s 17: the trust is established by the first of (a) a payment from the contracting party to the contracted party under the contract, (b) a payment from the contracted party to a subcontractor beneficiary, or (c) a deposit in the project trust account as required under Chapter 2.
  2. The s 19(2) penalty is 200 penalty units. The s 19(4) penalty for the contracted party failing to redirect a wrongly-received payment into the trust account is 200 penalty units or 2 years’ imprisonment. The disparity reflects the contracted party’s position as trustee under s 11.
  3. On the s 19(2)(c) carve-out for payment withholding requests, see s 97B (the request mechanism a subcontractor uses to require the principal to withhold from the contracted party). On the s 19(2)(d) carve-out for subcontractor’s charge payments, see Chapter 4 of the BIF Act.
  4. The discharge rule in s 19(3) means that, once the deposit is made, the contracting party has paid the contracted party for contractual purposes — the contracted party cannot insist on a separate payment outside the trust on the footing that money in the trust is “not yet” paid to it.
