Retention trust accounts

Records, reporting, training and audit

Running a retention trust account is not just a banking exercise. The BIF Act treats the trustee — usually the head contractor or principal holding cash retention from a subcontractor — as the steward of trust money, and backs that role with hard obligations to keep records, cover shortfalls, and submit the account to independent audit. The penalties for getting it wrong are not theoretical: failure to keep records carries up to 300 penalty units or a year’s imprisonment, and failure to engage an auditor up to 200 penalty units or a year’s imprisonment. This page pulls those obligations together.

Section 52 BIF Act — trust records

The trustee for a project trust or retention trust must—

(a) keep records for the trust as prescribed by regulation; and

(b) retain the records for at least 7 years after the trust is dissolved.

Maximum penalty—300 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.

Keeping and retaining records

Section 52 is the bedrock obligation. Two duties sit inside it. The first is to keep the records — the form and content are pushed out to regulation, but the obligation to keep them is in the Act itself. The second is to retain the records for at least seven years after the trust is dissolved. The clock does not start when the project finishes or when the last retention is released; it starts when the trust is dissolved, and runs for seven years from that point. In practice that means a head contractor cannot purge retention trust records on completion of the works. The records have to be archived in a form that can be produced years later, well after the project team has demobilised.

The seven-year retention period is also why record-keeping cannot be treated as a project-level task. The trustee — the legal entity holding the trust — carries the obligation across all of its retention trusts, and the records have to survive personnel changes, system migrations and corporate restructures.

Shortfalls and the trustee’s personal exposure

Section 51 sits alongside the records obligation and bites the moment the account does not have enough money to pay a beneficiary. If there is an insufficient amount in the trust account to pay an amount due to a beneficiary, the trustee “must immediately deposit an amount equal to the shortfall in the trust account” — called a shortfall deposit. The maximum penalty is 100 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment. The point for a project director is that a retention trust shortfall is not a commercial dispute to be worked through over weeks; it is a statutory trigger that requires the trustee to top the account up immediately from its own funds, and then sort out the underlying cause separately.

Annual auditor review under s 57

The trustee for a retention trust must engage an auditor to carry out a review of the trust account. Section 57 is the operative section and several mechanical points fall out of it:

  • The engagement of the auditor must comply with the requirements prescribed by regulation (s 57(2)).
  • The review must be carried out at the times prescribed by regulation, and the “review period” itself is the period prescribed by regulation (s 57(3)–(4)).
  • Once the review starts, it must be complete within 40 business days (s 57(5)).
  • The auditor must be independent of the trustee and must not have been excluded by the commissioner under s 54E (s 57(6)).

Independence is defined exhaustively in s 57(7). The auditor cannot be an employee of the trustee; if the trustee is a company, the auditor cannot be an executive officer, investor or shareholder of that company; if the trustee is a partnership, the auditor cannot be a partner; and the auditor cannot be a related entity for the trustee. In a corporate group setting that captures the obvious cases — the in-house finance team, a sister company auditor, or the trustee’s usual statutory auditor where there is a shareholding or related-entity link — and rules them out.

When the review is not required

Section 57(8) carves out three situations where the trustee does not need to engage an auditor. The first is where no retention amount was held in the account during the review period and the trustee gives the commissioner, within 10 business days after the end of the review period, a written statement using an approved way explaining why no auditor was engaged. The second is where there are no transactions or changes for the account during the review period and the trustee gives the commissioner, within the same 10-business-day window, a written statement using an approved way confirming that fact. The third is any further circumstance prescribed by regulation. The exceptions are narrow and procedural — they do not get the trustee out of recordkeeping; they only switch off the audit step for a dormant account.

What the auditor produces and where it goes

The output of the s 57 review is the “account review report” defined in s 50 by reference to s 57A(1). Section 58 then deals with what happens if the auditor’s own conduct is problematic. If the commissioner reasonably suspects the auditor’s conduct breaches a professional standard or condition, the commissioner may inform the relevant professional body and give it any information necessary to investigate. Crucially, the commissioner must redact all information identifying the trustee of the project trust or retention trust before passing material on (s 58(3)). For a trustee that is concerned about commercial confidentiality if its auditor comes under scrutiny, that statutory redaction is the protection the Act provides.

Register and approved financial institutions

Two background controls support all of the above. The commissioner must maintain a register of project trusts and retention trusts of which the commissioner has been notified, and may publish information about them (s 53). And trust accounts can only be held at a financial institution the commissioner has approved — approval is conditional on the institution having an agreement with the commission about providing financial services for trust accounts, which can extend to telling the commissioner what is in the accounts and to auditing arrangements (s 55). The register and the approved-institution regime are the framework that makes the audit and records obligations enforceable in practice.

What this means on a project

  • Treat retention trust records as a corporate-level archive, not a project file. The seven-year clock under s 52(b) only starts when the trust is dissolved — design your records system so the file survives demobilisation, system changes and personnel turnover.
  • Build a shortfall protocol into your treasury function. Section 51 requires the shortfall to be deposited “immediately” — you cannot wait for an internal investigation. Make sure someone with payment authority can move funds into the trust account on the day the shortfall is identified.
  • Diary the s 57 review window. The review period and timing are set by regulation; once the review starts you have 40 business days to complete it. Engage the auditor early enough that the 40-day clock is not a problem.
  • Test the auditor’s independence against s 57(7) before engagement. Employees, executive officers, investors, shareholders, partners and related entities are out. If you are using your usual statutory auditor for the trust review, confirm there is no related-entity link.
  • If the account has been dormant or held no retention during the review period, you can use the s 57(8) exception — but only if you give the commissioner the written statement, in the approved way, within 10 business days after the end of the review period. Miss that window and the audit obligation revives.
  • Only deposit retention trust money in a financial institution the commissioner has approved under s 55. The list is published on the commission’s website; check it before opening the account.
