Requirements of a payment claim

The claimant must be entitled to a progress payment

Section 70 is the gateway to the BIF Act payment regime. Before you can serve a valid payment claim, you must be a person who is “entitled to a progress payment” — which means you have actually carried out construction work, or supplied related goods and services, under the contract, and a reference date has arisen.

Section 70 BIF Act — Right to progress payments

From each reference date under a construction contract, a person is entitled to a progress payment if the person has carried out construction work, or supplied related goods and services, under the contract.

The two limbs of entitlement

Section 70 sets up two cumulative requirements. First, you must have carried out construction work (s 65) or supplied related goods and services (s 66) under the contract — that is the work limb. Second, a reference date under the contract must have arisen — that is the timing limb. If either is missing, there is no statutory entitlement and any payment claim served is invalid. The leading authority is the High Court’s decision in Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd (in liq) v Lewence Construction Pty Ltd [2016] HCA 52, which confirmed that the existence of a reference date is a precondition to a valid payment claim, not just a timing rule.1

The 2017 Act simplified the BCIPA wording from “a person is entitled to a progress payment if the person has undertaken to carry out construction work” to “a person who has carried out construction work”. The explanatory notes record that s 70 “essentially replicates” s 12 of BCIPA, so earlier decisions on the BCIPA wording continue to apply.

“From” each reference date

The opening words “from each reference date” have generated a long-running argument in Queensland about whether a payment claim must be served on the reference date or whether it can be served on or after it. In Reed Constructions (Qld) Pty Ltd v Martinek Holdings Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 345 Daubney J read the words narrowly — one opportunity per reference date, and a late claim is postponed to the next one. Fryberg J expressly disagreed in Tenix Alliance Pty Ltd v Magaldi Power Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 7, taking the view that the words create an entitlement “on and after” the reference date.2 The better-aligned position, applied by Mullins J in McNab Developments (Qld) Pty Ltd v MAK Construction Services Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 293, is that the statutory right can be exercised at any time after the reference date arises, subject to the s 75 limits on how often you can claim and the six-month rule.

Preconditions in the contract: what is void and what isn’t

Contracts often try to bolt extra conditions onto the right to a progress payment — a statutory declaration about subcontractor payments, a superintendent’s opinion, a draft claim served first. The key Queensland authority is John Holland Pty Ltd v Coastal Dredging & Construction Pty Ltd [2012] QCA 150; [2012] 2 Qd R 435, where the Court of Appeal held that a contractual provision purporting to defer the statutory entitlement to a progress payment from a reference date that has otherwise arisen is void under what is now s 200 of the BIF Act. Fraser JA (White JA and Peter Lyons J agreeing) put the underlying principle this way:

None of those provisions bear upon the date upon which the statutory entitlement to progress payments accrues. Bearing in mind the statutory object and the role of s 12 and the definition of “reference date” in giving effect to that object, those provisions are incapable of justifying an implication that the date upon which the statutory entitlement to a progress payment accrues may be qualified by contractual provisions other than those captured by the unambiguous terms of the definition of “reference date”.

Applegarth J refined the analysis in Lean Field Developments Pty Ltd v E & I Global Solutions (Aust) Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 293, drawing a distinction that matters in practice. There are two categories: preconditions to making a payment claim where a reference date has already arisen (these are void, because they defer an entitlement that has accrued); and preconditions to the arising of the reference date itself (these can be valid, but only if they don’t make the reference date “more of a theoretical possibility than an actuality”).3 A statutory-declaration precondition has repeatedly been struck down on this approach — see BHW Solutions, BRB Modular and J Hutchinson v Glavcom.4

Termination, insolvency and other edge cases

Once a contract is terminated, no new reference date accrues under it — you can’t generate fresh entitlements out of a contract that no longer exists.5 A reference date that had already accrued before termination, but was unused, can still support a payment claim served afterwards. Insolvency is messier: Façade Treatment Engineering Pty Ltd (in liq) v Brookfield Multiplex [2016] VSCA 247 held the Victorian equivalent of s 70 does not give a company in liquidation an entitlement, while the NSW Court of Appeal in Seymour Whyte Constructions Pty Ltd v Ostwald Bros Pty Ltd (in liq) [2019] NSWCA 11 reached the opposite conclusion on the NSW wording.6 The Queensland District Court in Tantallon Constructions Pty Ltd (in liq) v Santos GLNG [2016] QDC 324 followed the Façade line under BCIPA. The position under the BIF Act has not been authoritatively resolved.

Practice tips

  • Before serving any payment claim, check both limbs: have you actually performed the work, and has a reference date arisen under the contract (or under s 67 if the contract is silent)? Either gap is fatal.
  • If your contract makes payment conditional on a statutory declaration, a superintendent forming an opinion, or a draft claim being served first, those conditions are very likely void to the extent they defer an accrued statutory entitlement. Don’t treat them as a bar to claiming.
  • Don’t serve before the reference date. Even if the work is done, a claim served before the reference date arises is not supported by a valid reference date and will be invalid — Southern Han is clear on that.
  • If the contract has been terminated, identify the last reference date that accrued before termination. That’s the only one you can still use; new ones won’t come into existence.
  • If the claimant is in liquidation, expect a fight. The Queensland position is unsettled and you may need to structure the claim through a deed of company arrangement or pursue it outside the BIF Act regime.
