Requirements of an adjudication application

On the approved form to the registrar

An adjudication application starts with a single, deceptively simple step: lodging the right document, in the right form, with the registrar at the QBCC. Get the form wrong and the adjudicator has nothing valid to determine. The Act spells the requirement out in one line.

Section 79(2)(a) BIF Act — form of the application

An adjudication application—

(a) must be in the approved form; and

What the “approved form” actually is

The “approved form” is the form approved by the registrar for a claimant to apply to the registrar for adjudication. In practice it is the document the claimant lodges with the QBCC — either through the QBCC online portal or in hardcopy — to commence the adjudication process. It is not the receipt or acknowledgement the QBCC generates after lodgement.

That distinction matters because it has tripped up at least one well-resourced claimant. In Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2023] QSC 290, Williams J held that the “QBCC PDF Form” — the PDF the QBCC system automatically generated as a record once the claimant submitted the online “Electronic Form” — was not the approved form for the purposes of s 79(2)(a). The approved form was the Electronic Form itself; the PDF was effectively a receipt. Because the document the claimant gave the respondent (and treated as the application) was the PDF rather than the Electronic Form, the application failed the s 79(2)(a) requirement and the adjudicator had no jurisdiction.1

Two things have happened since. First, the QBCC Registry has changed how it processes adjudication applications, so the workflow that produced the problem in Iris Broadbeach has been reworked. Second, and more importantly, the Residential Tenancies and Rooming Accommodation and Other Legislation Amendment Act 2024 (Qld) was passed on 6 June 2024 with amendments to the BIF Act expressly responding to Iris Broadbeach. The Explanatory Note to the Third Reading recorded that intent. The practical effect is that the legal principle in Iris Broadbeach on the “approved form” point is now nugatory — that particular trap has been closed.

The “must” in s 79(2)(a) is mandatory

The form requirement is not directory or technical. In Kangaroo Point Developments MP Property Pty Ltd v RHG Construction Fitout and Maintenance Pty Ltd [2021] QSC 30, Dalton J read the “must” in s 79(2)(a) and s 79(2)(c) as imposing mandatory requirements, contrasting it with the discretionary “may” in the submissions limb. Non-compliance with s 79(2)(a) is therefore the kind of defect that goes to the validity of the application, not a mere irregularity an adjudicator can overlook.

That said, content errors short of using the wrong form will not always be fatal. In National Management Group Pty Ltd v Biriel Industries Pty Ltd trading as Master Steel & Ors [2019] QSC 219, Wilson J held that an adjudication application satisfied s 79(2)(c) despite a number of errors, because the document still referred to the project name, the reference date and the amount due.2 The point for present purposes is narrower: get the right form — the one the registrar has approved — and complete it accurately.

Practice tips

  • Lodge the current QBCC-approved adjudication application form. If you are using the online portal, the form you fill in and submit is the approved form. The PDF the system generates after submission is a record of lodgement, not the form itself.
  • Keep a complete copy of what you actually lodged with the registrar — ideally a screen-capture or saved version of every page of the online form before you submit. You will need to give the respondent a copy of that same document under s 79(4).
  • Do not assume an old version of the form is still “the approved form”. Check the QBCC website each time; the registrar can update the approved form, and using a superseded version is a needless jurisdictional risk.
  • Treat s 79(2)(a) as mandatory. A wrong-form application is not cured by good content elsewhere, so this is the first thing to confirm before lodgement, not after.
  • If you are advising a respondent, the post-June 2024 amendments mean an “approved form” challenge of the Iris Broadbeach kind will rarely succeed. Energy is better spent on timing (s 79(2)(b)), identification (s 79(2)(c)) and service on the respondent (s 79(4)).
