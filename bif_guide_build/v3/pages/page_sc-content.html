Valid subcontractor’s charge

Content of the notice of claim

A subcontractor’s charge only attaches if the notice of claim itself is up to scratch. The notice has to be in the approved form, identify the contract and the parties, state a certified amount, and set out enough detail of the work for the recipient to know what is being charged. Get the contents wrong and s 122(9) is unforgiving: the notice is of no effect and the charge does not attach.

Section 122(2)–(3) and (9) BIF Act — what the notice must contain

(2) The notice of claim must be made in the approved form and—

(a) state the amount of the claim; and

(b) include details of the work done by the subcontractor, certified as prescribed by a qualified person; and

(c) include the other information prescribed by regulation.

(3) The amount of the claim must be certified by a qualified person, as prescribed by regulation.

…

(9) If the notice of claim is not given in compliance with this section, the notice is of no effect and the subcontractor’s charge does not attach.

The approved form and the certified amount

The first thing the notice has to do is be in the approved form and state the amount of the claim, with that amount certified by a “qualified person” under s 147(1) and the regulation. The form does the heavy lifting on identification: it requires you to name the contractor and the person obliged to pay (the recipient, usually the principal or the next contractor up the chain), to identify the contract under which the money is payable, and to state the money sought to be charged. The Court of Appeal has made clear that there is no specific statutory wording you have to use — the section is “permissive” on form — provided the substance is there.1

Particulars of the work and the money charged

Section 122(2)(b) requires “details of the work done by the subcontractor”. The test the courts apply is one of substance: the contents of the notice must be sufficient to indicate the moneys sought to be charged and the amount of the claim, both ascertainable with reasonable certainty from the face of the notice.2 A bare description of the works subject to the claim will not do. In McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 223 the notice described the moneys claimed as “Monies payable to claimant pursuant to subcontract for the earthworks, concrete and pond liner works on the project”. The Supreme Court held that was insufficient information to identify the work the subject of the claim, and the notice failed to comply with the section.

Two practical points fall out of that. First, where there is more than one contract between the contractor and the recipient, the notice must make clear which contract’s moneys are being charged and to what extent — otherwise the recipient cannot tell from the notice what to retain.3 Second, attaching invoices, payment claims or a statement of account that itemises the work and the amounts is the usual way of meeting s 122(2)(b). If you signal an annexure on the face of the notice and then fail to attach it, the document is incomplete and is not a notice at all until the missing material is supplied.4

Money or retention — pick a lane

The notice must be clear about whether it charges money payable under the contract, retention only, or both. The leading appellate guidance is Road Surfaces Group Pty Ltd v Brown [1987] 2 Qd R 792, where the notice referred to “all moneys, including retention moneys”. Williams J, reading s 10 of the SCA (now s 122 BIF Act) as a whole, held that a charge may be claimed on retention only, on other moneys only, or on both:

a charge may be claimed either on retention money, or secondly on moneys otherwise payable under the contract, or thirdly on retention moneys and moneys otherwise payable under the contract. The only material consequence flowing from the third categorisation is that such notice would have to be given within three months of the completion of the work, and not within the extended period permissible when the charge is sought against retention moneys only.

Hedged language — “money and/or retention money”, or “money and retention money that is now or will be payable” — has been struck down because it leaves the recipient unable to tell which money is subject to the charge.5 If your timing is anywhere near the three-month limit after practical completion, and you can only meet the longer retention-only window in s 122(8), restrict the notice in terms to retention money for the contract.

Identifying the contract and the parties

The recipient of the notice has to be the actual person obliged to pay the contractor under the contract. Naming the wrong legal entity — for example, an executive department instead of the State, or a parent instead of the contracting subsidiary — will usually be fatal, but a notice will still be valid where the inaccuracy can be cured because the moneys to be charged and the amount of the claim are ascertainable with reasonable certainty from the notice itself.6 Likewise, identifying the contract is straightforward where there is one subcontract; where there are several, spell out which one and tie each amount back to it.

Practice tips

  • Use the approved form. Don’t reinvent it. There is no statutory requirement to use any particular wording, but the form prompts the information the section and the regulation require.
  • Get the amount certified by a qualified person under s 147(1) before service. An uncertified amount, or certification by the wrong person, takes you straight to s 122(9).
  • Particularise the work. Attach invoices, payment claims or a statement of account that itemises scope, periods and amounts. Avoid generic descriptions like “monies payable under the subcontract for the works”.
  • Decide before drafting whether the notice charges money payable under the contract, retention only, or both — and say so unambiguously. Avoid “and/or” formulations.
  • Name the recipient and the contractor as the legal entities they are on the contract. Cross-check ABN, ACN and contracting party against the executed agreement.
  • If you reference an annexure in the body of the notice, attach it. A signalled-but-missing annexure means there is no notice until the annexure is provided, and time runs from then.
  • If the first notice is defective, you can serve a fresh, compliant notice within time — provided no charge from an earlier notice has already been extinguished by operation of the Act.7
