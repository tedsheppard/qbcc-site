Project trust accounts

Opening the project trust account

Once a project trust is required for your contract under s 12 of the BIF Act, the contracted party (head contractor or principal contractor) has to actually open a dedicated bank account for that trust. The Act prescribes when the account must be opened, where it can be held, what it must look like, and who has to be told about it. Get any of these wrong and you are exposed to significant penalties — the obligations sit at 500 and 200 penalty units, with imprisonment available for some breaches.

Section 18 BIF Act — Contracted party must open project trust account

(1) If a project trust is required for a contract under section 12, the contracted party must open an account at a financial institution for the trust as required by this section.

Maximum penalty—500 penalty units.

(2) The project trust account must be opened within 20 business days after the contracted party enters into the first subcontract for the contract.

(3) However—

(a) if a project trust is not required for the contract until after an amendment of the contract; and

(b) the contracted party entered into a subcontract for the contract before the amendment of the contract;

the project trust account must be opened within 20 business days after the day the contract is amended.

(4) The project trust account must not be a virtual account or subordinate to any other account at a financial institution.

(5) There must not be more than 1 project trust account for the project trust.

(6) A provision of a contract that provides that the project trust account must be opened less than 20 business days after the contract is entered into is of no effect.

Timing — the 20-business-day clock

The trigger for opening the account is not the head contract or even the first payment. It is the first subcontract. Under s 18(2), the contracted party must open the project trust account within 20 business days after entering into the first subcontract for the project trust contract. That is the date you should be diarising the moment a project trust is required.

There is a separate trigger where the project trust requirement only arises later. If the head contract was not originally caught by chapter 2 but becomes caught after an amendment — for example, a variation pushes the contract price over the $1 million threshold in s 14, or pushes the project-trust-work share over 50% — and you had already entered into a subcontract before that amendment, the 20-business-day clock runs from the date of the amendment, not from the original subcontract date (s 18(3)). The cross-reference in the Act is to s 14A, which deals with how amendments affect the requirement to establish a project trust.

Section 18(6) is a quiet but important provision. A contract clause that purports to require the project trust account to be opened in less than 20 business days after the contract is entered into is “of no effect”. Principals sometimes try to write tighter timetables into their head contracts. They do not bind you. You still have the full 20 business days from the first subcontract.

Note also the relationship with s 17. The project trust itself is “established” by the first of three things happening — a payment from the contracting party to you, a payment from you to a subcontractor beneficiary, or a deposit into the project trust account. So the trust can be established (and your trustee duties switched on) before the account is even opened. Opening the account within the 20 business days does not give you a window to delay establishing the trust; it simply fixes the outer limit for getting the bank account in place.

Where the account can be held — approved financial institution

Section 18(1) requires the account to be opened “at a financial institution”. Read together with the chapter 2 definitions in s 8, an “approved financial institution” is one approved by the commissioner under s 55. The QBCC publishes the list of approved institutions on its website; only those institutions can hold a project trust account. Before you instruct your finance team to open the account, check the current list — it is updated from time to time, and a previously-approved institution coming off the list will affect any new accounts you open.

The account itself has to be a real, stand-alone bank account. Section 18(4) prohibits two structures that banks sometimes offer for cash-management efficiency: it cannot be a “virtual account” (a notional sub-ledger sitting under a parent account number), and it cannot be “subordinate to any other account at a financial institution” (for example, a sweep account that automatically empties into a master operating account overnight). Both structures are common in corporate cash-pooling arrangements. Neither is permitted here. The project trust money has to sit in its own discrete account in its own right.

Section 18(5) imposes a one-account-per-trust rule. There must not be more than one project trust account for a given project trust. You cannot split the funds across two accounts at the same institution, or hedge by holding part of the trust money at one bank and part at another. One trust, one account.

Account name and account purpose

The account must be opened “for the trust” (s 18(1)) — that is, in the contracted party’s name as trustee of the project trust for the relevant contract. In practice, banks approved by the commissioner will require you to open the account with a name that identifies it as a project trust account (commonly in a form such as “[Contracted Party] ATF [Project] Project Trust”). The account is a trust account in the legal sense: the funds in it are trust money held primarily for the benefit of subcontractor beneficiaries (s 11), and they are not available to your general creditors. Treat it accordingly from the moment it is opened — do not co-mingle with operating funds, do not use it as a working-capital float, and do not give the bank any set-off rights over other facilities.

Notifying the subcontractors

Opening the account is not the end of it. Section 23 requires the contracted party to give each subcontractor a written “notice of project trust” about the use of a project trust account. The notice must be in writing, must state that a project trust will be used for making payments to the subcontractor, and must include the further information prescribed by regulation.

The timing of the notice depends on whether the trust is already established when the subcontract is entered into:

  • if the project trust is not yet established when you and the subcontractor enter into the subcontract, the notice must be given within 10 business days after the trust is established (s 23(3)(a));
  • if the project trust is already established at the time of contracting, the notice must be given before you and the subcontractor enter into the subcontract (s 23(3)(b));
  • if the project trust requirement only arises after an amendment of the head contract and you had already entered into the subcontract pre-amendment, the notice must be given within 10 business days after the project trust account is opened (s 23(4)).

Failing to give the s 23 notice attracts 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment. It is one of the few obligations in chapter 2 that carries a custodial maximum, so it should be on the project mobilisation checklist alongside the bank-account paperwork.

Practice tips

  • Diarise the 20-business-day clock from the date of the first subcontract, not from contract award and not from first payment. That is the date that triggers s 18(2).
  • If a variation later pushes the contract over the $1 million / 50% threshold and you already have a subcontract in place, the 20-business-day clock runs from the amendment date under s 18(3). Treat the variation paperwork as the trigger event.
  • Ignore any head-contract drafting that purports to require the trust account to be opened in less than 20 business days from contract execution — s 18(6) renders that clause of no effect.
  • Before opening the account, check the QBCC’s current list of approved financial institutions. The approval can change, and an account at a non-approved institution will not satisfy s 18(1).
  • Make sure the bank product is a real, segregated trust account in the contracted party’s name as trustee. No virtual accounts, no cash-pool / sweep arrangements, no shadow ledgers under a master operating account — s 18(4) prohibits both.
  • One project trust, one account (s 18(5)). Do not split funds across two accounts or two institutions for the same trust.
  • Build the s 23 notice into your subcontract execution pack. For subcontracts signed before the trust is established, send the notice within 10 business days of establishment; for subcontracts signed after the trust is up and running, send the notice before the subcontract is signed. Missing this carries 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.
  • Keep the bank-opening documents, the QBCC approval reference, the first-subcontract date and the s 23 notices together in one project-trust file. If the commissioner ever audits the trust, those are the documents that prove timely compliance with s 18 and s 23.
  1. Definitions of “approved financial institution” and “project trust account” appear in s 8 and s 10 of the BIF Act respectively; the commissioner’s power to approve financial institutions is at s 55.
  2. On when the trust is “established” (as distinct from when the account must be opened), see s 17 BIF Act — the trust comes into existence on the first of a payment from the contracting party, a payment to a subcontractor beneficiary, or a deposit into the project trust account, occurring after the trust is required under s 12.
  3. The information that must be included in a s 23 notice of project trust is prescribed by the BIF Regulation 2018; check the current regulation before finalising your notice template, as the prescribed content has been amended since the chapter commenced.
