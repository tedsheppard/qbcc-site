Valid subcontractor’s charge

Supporting certificate (s 124)

If a third party holds a security for the head contract — typically a financier with a fixed and floating charge, or a surety holding retention or a bank guarantee — your subcontractor’s charge will only bite on that security if the security holder is also given a copy of the notice of claim. Section 124 is the rule that drags the security holder into the loop, and the consequence of skipping it is narrow but real: the charge does not attach to the security, even though it can still attach to the money owed under the contract.

Section 124 BIF Act — copy of notice of claim to the holder of a security

124 Copy of notice of claim to the holder of a security

(1) This section applies if—

(a) a subcontractor gives a notice of claim to a person (the recipient) obliged to pay money to a contractor under a contract; and

(b) a person other than the recipient holds a security for the contract.

(2) The subcontractor must give a copy of the notice of claim to the person who holds the security.

(3) If a copy of the notice of claim is not given as required under subsection (2), the claimed subcontractor’s charge does not attach to the security.

(4) To remove any doubt, it is declared that subsection (3) does not affect a subcontractor’s charge to the extent it otherwise attaches under this chapter.

When the section is engaged

Section 124 only switches on where two facts coincide: you have given a s 122 notice of claim to the person obliged to pay your contractor (the “recipient”), and someone other than that recipient holds a security for the head contract. If there is no third-party security — for example, the principal holds its own retention internally and no separate party stands behind the contract — s 124 has no work to do, and your charge proceeds on the s 122 / s 123 service alone.

The trigger is the existence of a security “for the contract” held by a third party. Common examples in practice are a bank guarantee or insurance bond provided by the contractor and held by the principal’s financier or by a surety; an unconditional undertaking lodged with a separate trustee; or retention monies held by a third-party stakeholder rather than by the recipient itself. If you are unsure whether a third-party security exists, the head contract and any project-specific deed of guarantee will normally tell you — and if there is room for doubt, serve the copy anyway.

What “does not attach to the security” actually means

The drafting of subsections (3) and (4) is deliberately surgical. If you fail to give the copy to the security holder, the only thing you lose is the charge over that security. The charge can still attach to money payable to the contractor under the head contract in the ordinary way under chapter 4. Subsection (4) puts that beyond argument: failure to comply with s 124(2) does not unravel a charge that has otherwise validly attached.

That is a quite different outcome from a failure of service under s 122 or s 123. Under those sections, the notice itself is “of no effect and the subcontractor’s charge does not attach” at all. Under s 124, the charge survives — it just can’t reach the third-party security. In practical terms, you keep your priority over the money the principal still has in its hands, but you lose any claim against, say, the bank guarantee or the externally-held retention bond that you might otherwise have looked to if the principal became insolvent.

How to comply

The section requires that the security holder be given “a copy of the notice of claim” — that is, the same s 122 document that goes to the recipient. There is no separate prescribed form, no separate certification step, and no statutory time limit. As a matter of prudent practice, the copy should go out at the same time as the s 122 notice and the s 123 copy, by the same s 39 / s 109X service route used for the recipient. Section 124 inherits the silence of s 123 on timing, so the safer course is to treat the “commercial urgency” benchmark that applies to the s 123 copy as a working guide for the s 124 copy as well.

Practice tips

  • Before issuing the s 122 notice, ask whether anyone other than the recipient holds a security for the head contract — a financier with a charge, a surety holding a bank guarantee, a trustee holding retention. If yes, s 124 is engaged.
  • Send the security holder a copy of the same notice of claim you gave the recipient. There is no separate form; the “copy” is what the section requires.
  • Use the same service route as for the s 122 notice — head office, registered office or principal place of business under s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld), or registered office / personal director-service under s 109X of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth). Avoid PO boxes and Dropbox-style links.
  • Send the s 124 copy at the same time as the s 122 notice and s 123 copy. The section sets no deadline, but treating the trio as a single mailing avoids argument later.
  • If you only realise after the event that a third-party security exists, serve the s 124 copy as soon as you find out. Your charge over the contract money is unaffected by the earlier omission — s 124(4) protects it — and you may still pick up the security if late service is accepted by the holder.
  • Keep the head contract, any deed of guarantee and any bond paperwork on file with your s 122 notice. If the principal later goes into administration, the security holder’s identity and address will be the first thing you need.
