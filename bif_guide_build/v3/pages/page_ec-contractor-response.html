Enforcing a subcontractor’s charge

The contractor’s response

Once a subcontractor serves a notice of claim of charge and gives the contractor a copy under s 123, the ball is in the contractor’s court. Section 128 forces the contractor to break cover — within ten business days — and say in the approved form whether the claim is accepted or disputed. That response is the hinge of the whole Chapter 4 process: it determines whether the security holder pays you out under s 130 or pays the disputed money into court under s 131, and it sets up the s 132 application that ultimately releases the security.

Section 128 BIF Act — Contractor given copy of notice of claim must respond

(1) This section applies if a subcontractor gives a contractor a copy of a notice of claim.

(2) The contractor must give both of the following persons a written response to the claim within 10 business days after the contractor is given the copy of the notice of claim, unless the contractor has a reasonable excuse—

(a) the person given the notice of claim;

(b) the subcontractor.

Maximum penalty—20 penalty units.

(3) The response to the claim must be made in the approved form and—

(a) accept liability to pay the amount claimed; or

(b) accept liability to pay an amount stated in the response, but otherwise dispute the claim; or

(c) dispute the claim.

Who has to respond, and to whom

The obligation falls on the contractor — the party with which the subcontractor has its contract — once it has been given a copy of the notice of claim under s 123. The response has to go to two people: the person who was given the notice of claim itself (typically the principal or party further up the chain holding money for the contractor), and the subcontractor making the claim. A response that is sent only to the subcontractor, or only to the principal, doesn’t comply.

The clock is ten business days from when the contractor is given the copy. There is no extension mechanism in the section, and missing the deadline carries a maximum penalty of 20 penalty units. The only escape valve is “reasonable excuse”, which is not defined and which a contractor relies on at its own risk — the safer course is always to respond inside the ten-day window, even if the response is provisional and disputes the claim.

Three options, and only three

Section 128(3) closes off any “wait and see” response. The contractor must do one of three things, in the approved form:

  • accept liability to pay the full amount claimed (s 128(3)(a)) — this puts the claim into s 130 territory, and the security holder must then either retain the security pending a s 132 order or pay the subcontractor up to the difference amount;
  • accept liability to pay a lesser amount stated in the response, and otherwise dispute the claim (s 128(3)(b)) — this is a partial acceptance, and the dispute over the balance feeds into s 131; or
  • dispute the claim outright (s 128(3)(c)) — this puts the whole claim into s 131, and the security holder must retain the security or pay the relevant amount into court rather than to the subcontractor.

A response that doesn’t fit one of these three boxes, or that is given outside the approved form, is not a s 128 response. Practically, the most common defects are responses that hedge (“under review”, “awaiting further information”), responses sent by email or letter rather than in the approved form, and responses sent only to the principal without copying the subcontractor.

Why the response matters downstream

The s 128 response is what sorts the claim into one of two statutory channels. Acceptance under s 128(3)(a) (or partial acceptance under s 128(3)(b) for the accepted portion) engages s 130: the holder of the security can pay the accepted amount directly to the subcontractor up to the difference amount, or retain the security against a s 132 order. Dispute under s 128(3)(c) (or the disputed portion under s 128(3)(b)) engages s 131: the holder must either retain the security or pay it into court, where it stays locked until the court orders otherwise.

So the response does two things that the contractor cannot undo later. It locks the contractor into a position on the underlying debt (acceptance is hard to walk back in the subsequent court proceeding), and it determines whether the disputed money sits with the principal/security holder or goes into court under s 131. Once funds are paid into court, only a court order will get them out — the contractor and subcontractor cannot release them by agreement.

If the contractor doesn’t respond

Silence is not neutral. The penalty in s 128(2) bites on a non-responding contractor regardless of the merits of the underlying claim. More importantly, a failure to respond doesn’t pause the Chapter 4 machinery: the subcontractor still has the charge over money in the principal’s hands, and the absence of a response will be evidence in any subsequent s 132 application that the claim should be enforced over the security.

What this means on a project

  • If you are the contractor and you receive a copy of a notice of claim, diary the ten business day deadline immediately. Use the approved form — not a letter, not an email — and send it to both the person given the notice and the subcontractor.
  • Pick one of the three boxes in s 128(3). If you genuinely accept part and dispute part, use the partial-acceptance limb in s 128(3)(b) and state the accepted figure precisely. Don’t leave the response open-ended.
  • If you accept liability under s 128(3)(a), understand the consequence: you have set up s 130, and the security holder may pay the subcontractor directly. Acceptance is also difficult to retract in the s 132 proceeding that follows.
  • If you dispute, dispute clearly under s 128(3)(c). That puts the matter into s 131, the money goes into court rather than to the subcontractor, and only a court order under s 132 will release it.
  • If you are the subcontractor, read the response carefully. It tells you whether you are heading into a s 130 (administrative) or s 131 (contested) application for s 132 relief, and it fixes the accepted amount you can build on in the proceeding. A non-compliant or absent response is itself a fact you will rely on in court.
  1. The trigger for s 128 is the contractor being given a copy of the notice of claim under s 123 — not service of the notice itself on the principal under s 122. The ten business day clock runs from receipt of the copy.
  2. The “approved form” for a s 128 response is the form approved by the commissioner under the Act; a response not made in that form does not satisfy s 128(3) even if it otherwise communicates acceptance or dispute.
  3. The response feeds directly into s 130 (acceptance) and s 131 (dispute), and ultimately into a s 132 application: see s 132(1)–(2), under which the court may make “the order it considers appropriate for enforcing the subcontractor’s charge over the security, including an order for realising the security”.
