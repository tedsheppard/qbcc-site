Retention trust accounts

What money is held in retention trust

The retention trust regime in Chapter 2 of the BIF Act is a cash-only regime. It captures money that the contracting party has actually withheld from a progress payment as security — not every form of security the contract might allow, and not money that simply sits in the contracting party’s general account waiting to be withheld.

Section 35 BIF Act — deposit obligation

35 All retention amounts withheld must be deposited in retention trust account

(1) This section applies if, under section 32, a retention trust is required for a retention amount withheld from payment under a contract.

(2) The contracting party must deposit the retention amount withheld under the contract in a retention trust account (the deposit obligation) as follows—

(a) if the retention trust is required under section 32(1)—when the retention amount is withheld;

(b) if the retention trust is required under section 32(2) and there is an existing retention trust account into which the retention amount may be deposited—within 5 business days after the contract becomes a withholding contract;

(c) if the retention trust is required under section 32(2) and there is no existing retention trust account into which the retention amount may be deposited—when the retention trust account is opened.

Maximum penalty—200 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment.

(3) A term of a contract is of no effect to the extent it is inconsistent with the deposit obligation.

(4) The retention amount to which the deposit obligation relates is inclusive of any GST related to the amount.

Cash retention — the only thing that goes in

The trust is established by the act of withholding. Section 33 says it bluntly: where a retention trust is required under s 32, “the retention trust is established by the contracting party withholding the retention amount from payment.” There is no separate declaration of trust, no instrument to sign — the moment money is held back from a progress payment under a withholding contract, the trust comes into existence over that amount.1

The deposit obligation in s 35 then forces that money into a dedicated bank account. For a contract that was a withholding contract from the start, the cash must hit the retention trust account at the moment of withholding — not at the end of the month, not when the next reconciliation happens. For a contract that becomes a withholding contract part-way through (the s 32(2) case), there is a 5-business-day window if a trust account is already open, or until the new account is opened if it isn’t. The penalty for breach is 200 penalty units or 2 years’ imprisonment, so this is not a back-office tidy-up obligation.

Two features of s 35 are worth pausing on. First, s 35(3) overrides any contrary contract term — you cannot drafting-around the deposit obligation by, for example, agreeing that retention will sit in the head contractor’s working-capital account until practical completion. Second, s 35(4) confirms the amount deposited is GST-inclusive: if you withhold 5% of an invoice, you deposit 5% of the GST-inclusive figure, not the GST-exclusive figure.

Bank guarantees and other non-cash security

The retention trust regime simply does not apply to bank guarantees, insurance bonds, parent-company guarantees or any other non-cash form of security. Sections 33, 34, 35, 35A and 36 all speak in terms of “retention amount” being “withheld from payment” and “deposited” into “an account” at “a financial institution.” The statutory machinery is built around money moving through a bank account. A bank guarantee is an instrument issued by a bank to the principal — nothing has been “withheld from payment” to the subcontractor, and there is no cash to deposit anywhere.

Section 35A reinforces the point from the other direction. It is a criminal offence for a trustee to deposit anything into the retention trust account other than (a) a retention amount withheld from payment under a contract for which the trustee is the contracting party, or (b) a repayment of an amount withdrawn in error. Putting the cash proceeds of a called bank guarantee into the retention trust account would not fall within either limb — the proceeds of a guarantee are not a “retention amount withheld from payment” to the contracted party. The trust account is for cash retention, full stop.

The practical consequence is that if your subcontract permits security in the form of either cash retention or a bank guarantee, only the cash retention engages Chapter 2. If the subcontractor delivers a bank guarantee for the full security amount, no retention trust is required for that contract. If the subcontractor delivers half cash and half guarantee, only the cash half goes into the retention trust account. The guarantee sits outside the regime entirely and is governed by its own terms and the general law of guarantees.

The contracted party’s charge

Money in the trust account is also subject to a statutory charge in favour of the contracted party. Section 33A creates a charge over the retention amount “for securing the release of the amount when the party becomes entitled to the amount.” The charge is enforceable as if it had been given under a written agreement, it is declared to be a statutory interest for the purposes of s 73(2) of the Personal Property Securities Act 2009 (Cwlth) (so it does not need to be registered on the PPSR to be effective), and it is GST-inclusive. The charge is released over a portion of the retention amount only when the contracting party becomes entitled to be paid that portion under the contract — for example, on a properly substantiated set-off for defective work.

Practice tips

  • If you are the contracting party on a withholding contract, the cash you deduct from a progress payment must hit the retention trust account on the day you withhold it. There is no grace period on a fresh withholding contract.
  • The amount deposited is GST-inclusive. Don’t deposit the ex-GST figure and treat the GST as your own working capital — that’s a deposit-obligation breach.
  • Bank guarantees, parent-company guarantees and insurance bonds are outside the regime. You don’t open a trust account for a contract secured entirely by guarantee, and you don’t put the proceeds of a called guarantee into a trust account if you do happen to have one for other contracts.
  • If the security is mixed (part cash, part guarantee), only the cash component goes into trust. Track the two streams separately.
  • A contract clause that says retention will be held in your general account, or released on terms inconsistent with the deposit obligation, is of no effect to that extent under s 35(3). Don’t rely on bespoke retention drafting to displace the regime.
  • Treat the contracted party’s s 33A charge as a real encumbrance on the cash. An attempt to defeat it — for instance, by routing the money through a related entity — is “of no effect” against the contracted party under s 33A(5).
  1. See s 33 BIF Act (trust established by withholding) read with s 35 (deposit obligation) and s 35A (limited purposes for which money may be deposited). The combined effect is that the only money that can lawfully be in a retention trust account is cash retention withheld under a withholding contract for which the trustee is the contracting party, plus repayments of amounts withdrawn in error and interest credited by the financial institution.
