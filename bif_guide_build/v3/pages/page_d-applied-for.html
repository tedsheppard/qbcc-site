Requirements of an adjudicator’s decision

Decide only what was applied for

An adjudicator’s job under s 88(1) is narrow and specific: decide the amount of the progress payment (if any), the date it became or becomes payable, and the rate of interest on it. The adjudicator can’t go higher than the claim, can’t go lower than what the respondent already conceded in the payment schedule, and can’t award amounts the claimant never put in the payment claim. Step outside that envelope and the decision is liable to be set aside for jurisdictional error.

Section 88 BIF Act — adjudicator’s decision (extract)

(1) An adjudicator is to decide—

(a) the amount of the progress payment, if any, to be paid by the respondent to the claimant (the adjudicated amount); and

(b) the date on which any amount became or becomes payable; and

(c) the rate of interest payable on any amount.

(2) In deciding an adjudication application, the adjudicator is to consider the following matters only—

(a) the provisions of this chapter and, to the extent they are relevant, the provisions of the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991, part 4A;

(b) the provisions of the relevant construction contract;

(c) the payment claim to which the application relates, together with all submissions, including relevant documents, that have been properly made by the claimant in support of the claim;

(d) the payment schedule, if any, to which the application relates, together with all submissions, including relevant documents, that have been properly made by the respondent in support of the schedule;

(e) the results of any inspection carried out by the adjudicator of any matter to which the claim relates.

What s 88(1) obliges the adjudicator to decide

Three things, no more and no less. The adjudicated amount, the date it falls due, and the interest rate. The amount can be zero — a determination that no amount is to be paid is still a determination, and is no less valid than one that awards money.1 What the adjudicator can’t do is decline to make the call. Refusing to determine the application, or treating the question as “premature”, is a failure to perform the statutory task and produces a void determination.

That was the position in Parrwood Pty Ltd v Trinity Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd [2020] NSWCA 172. The adjudicator there said in the reasons that he “decline[d] from determining” any entitlement under the payment claim because a finding would be premature. Instead of recording the adjudicated amount as nil or $0, he recorded “No amount”. The NSW Court of Appeal held that the adjudicator had failed to do the “very thing” the Act required, breached the equivalent of s 88(1)(a), and the determination was void for jurisdictional error. The Court also confirmed that the claimant did not have to wait for a court ruling before treating the determination as void and lodging a fresh adjudication — otherwise the claimant would be denied the chance to exercise its statutory rights.

The floor and ceiling: claimed amount and scheduled amount

The amount the adjudicator can award is bounded at both ends. At the top, by the amount actually claimed in the payment claim. At the bottom, by the amount the respondent admitted in the payment schedule. The leading articulation is Blue J’s in Maxcon Constructions Pty Ltd v Vadasz (No 2) [2017] SASCFC 2, [228]:

[228] the adjudicator had no jurisdiction in the circumstances to determine the amount of the progress payment at less than $141,163.55 or more than $204,864.55. Because of the manner in which the parties framed the issues in the payment claim, payment schedule, adjudication application and Maxcon’s submissions in response, the adjudicator was required to determine two discrete issues, namely the retention sum claim and the administration charges setoff.

The reasoning is that the BIF Act process is adversarial: the claimant defines the upper limit of the dispute through the payment claim, and the respondent defines the lower limit through what it concedes in the payment schedule. An adjudicator who awards more than was claimed has exceeded the jurisdiction conferred by the application; an adjudicator who awards less than was admitted has done the same in the other direction. Either way, it’s jurisdictional error.2

The same logic explains why an adjudicator can’t reach into amounts that weren’t in the payment claim at all. In Samsung C&T Corporation v Loots [2016] WASC 330, an adjudicator awarded $32.4 million on the basis that it had been previously certified but wrongly set off. Beech J held the amount had not been claimed in the payment claim before the adjudicator, so the adjudicator had directed his attention to the merits of competing claims and set-offs that lay outside the application — jurisdictional error, and the determination was set aside.3

There is a refinement worth flagging. In Binah Constructions Pty Ltd v PTMG Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 872, Ball J held that where the adjudicated amount exceeds the figure claimed in the payment claim itself but matches an amount the respondent had already certified by default in respect of a prior payment claim, that’s not a jurisdictional defect — the claimant was entitled to roll up previously certified amounts into the application, and it was open to the adjudicator to find those amounts were not in dispute and were payable.4 The principle in Maxcon is about awarding more than the claimant put forward across the application as a whole; it is not a mechanical comparison of the headline figure on the face of the payment claim.

Practice tips

  • Whatever you put as the claimed amount in the payment claim is the absolute ceiling of any adjudicated amount. If you under-claim, the adjudicator can’t fix it for you — you live with the figure you served.
  • Whatever you certify in the payment schedule is the absolute floor. Don’t schedule a figure you’re not prepared to pay if the adjudication goes against you on every other issue; the adjudicator can’t go below it.
  • If you’re a respondent and the adjudicated amount is higher than the payment claim amount, look closely at how the figure was built up. Maxcon and Samsung suggest jurisdictional error; Binah says it depends on whether the extra is a previously certified amount that was implicit in the application. Get advice quickly — the s 101 enforcement clock won’t pause while you think about it.
  • If you’re a claimant and the adjudicator records “no amount” or refuses to decide because the question is premature, treat that as a Parrwood failure and consider lodging a fresh adjudication application under s 87. You don’t need to wait for a court declaration first.
  • The adjudicator must also fix the date the amount became payable and the interest rate. If those are missing from the decision, that’s a s 88(1) gap on its face — raise it before you pay.
