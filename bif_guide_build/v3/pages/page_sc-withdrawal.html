Valid subcontractor’s charge

Withdrawal of a notice

A notice of claim under chapter 4 freezes money in the hands of the payer above your contractor — once the s 122 notice has been served, that party is statutorily required to retain the amount claimed pending resolution. Section 125 lets you turn that off again. It is the formal mechanism by which a subcontractor can dismantle its own charge, in whole or in part, before the matter goes any further.

Section 125 BIF Act — Withdrawing a notice of claim

125 Withdrawing a notice of claim

(1) A subcontractor may at any time withdraw, wholly or partly, a notice of claim by giving notice of the withdrawal, in the approved form, to the person to whom the subcontractor gave the notice of claim.

(2) If a subcontractor withdraws, wholly or partly, a notice of claim under subsection (1), the subcontractor must give a copy of the notice of withdrawal to each of the persons to whom the subcontractor gave a copy of the notice of claim.

What the section actually does

Three features of s 125 are worth pulling out. First, withdrawal is available “at any time”. There is no statutory deadline, no requirement that proceedings have been commenced or not commenced, and no need to show a change of circumstances. If you decide the charge should come off — because you’ve been paid, because you’ve agreed a settlement, because you’ve over-claimed, or because you simply want to walk away — you can withdraw it.

Second, withdrawal can be partial. You can reduce the quantum claimed (for example, where part of the claimed amount has been paid or compromised) without giving up the charge in respect of the balance. The Act treats a partial withdrawal as a valid form of notice in its own right, so the operative figure on the charge becomes whatever remains after the withdrawal takes effect.

Third, withdrawal must be done in the approved form. Section 125(1) doesn’t set out the content; it points to the form approved under the Act for that purpose. Using your own letter or email instead of the approved form risks the withdrawal being ineffective, with the consequence that the charge continues to bite money in the payer’s hands and the payer continues to be exposed under s 122.1

Service mirrors the original notice

Section 125 is built around symmetry with s 122 and s 123. The withdrawal goes to the same person you served the original notice of claim on — the party above your contractor in the chain who is obliged to pay the money — and copies must go to everyone who received a copy of the original notice. In a typical chain that means the payer gets the withdrawal, your contractor gets a copy, and any other party that was copied on the s 123 distribution gets a copy as well.

The same practical service rules that apply to the original notice apply to the withdrawal. Chapter 4 doesn’t prescribe a method, so s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) and, for companies, s 109X of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) do the work: head office, registered office, principal office, or personal delivery to a director. Get the legal entity right, use a method you can prove, and avoid PO boxes and bare cloud-link service. The point of the withdrawal is to release the money — if the payer can’t verify it, the payer will keep retaining.

Why subcontractors withdraw

The most common trigger is payment or compromise. Once the disputed amount has been paid, or a settlement deed has been signed, the subcontractor is contractually obliged to release the charge and a s 125 withdrawal is the mechanism. A second common trigger is recalibration: a subcontractor that has claimed too high a figure in the original s 122 notice will sometimes withdraw and re-issue, or use a partial withdrawal to bring the operative amount back into line with what is genuinely owed. A third is tactical retreat — for example, where it has become clear that the chapter 4 path is the wrong one and the subcontractor wants to fall back on its chapter 3 progress payment rights without the chapter 4 charge complicating the position.

Whatever the reason, the legal effect of a properly served s 125 withdrawal is to lift the statutory retention obligation on the payer to the extent of the withdrawal. The payer is then free to deal with the money in the ordinary course, subject to any other claims or notices it may have received.

What this means on a project

  • Use the approved form. Section 125(1) requires it. A letter, email or settlement deed recital is not a substitute — lodge the form so the payer has something it can act on without legal advice.
  • Mirror your original distribution exactly. Whoever received the s 122 notice gets the withdrawal; whoever received a s 123 copy gets a copy of the withdrawal. Don’t skip the contractor — s 125(2) makes the copy mandatory.
  • Decide whether the withdrawal is whole or partial before you sign. If you are settling part of the claim and pursuing the balance, the withdrawal needs to specify the amount being withdrawn so the operative figure on the remaining charge is clear.
  • Tie the withdrawal to the payment or settlement mechanism. In a deed of settlement, make execution and lodgement of the s 125 withdrawal a condition of payment, not a post-payment promise — otherwise you are relying on the subcontractor’s good faith to lift the charge.
  • Serve the withdrawal the way you served the original. Personal delivery, courier with proof of delivery, post to head office or registered office, or email to a clearly nominated business address. Keep the proof; the payer will want to see it before releasing funds.
  • If you are the payer, don’t release on a partial or informal withdrawal until you have the approved form and have confirmed the chain — your statutory retention obligation under s 122 only falls away to the extent the s 125 withdrawal is valid.
  1. The “approved form” is the form approved by the commissioner under s 199 of the BIF Act for the purpose of s 125. The current form is published on the QBCC website. The same approved-form requirement applies to the original notice of claim under s 122 and to the contractor’s response under s 124, so practitioners working in chapter 4 should keep the current versions of all three forms on hand.
