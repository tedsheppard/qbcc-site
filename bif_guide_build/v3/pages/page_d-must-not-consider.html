Requirements of an adjudicator’s decision

Not consider what is prohibited

Section 88(2) tells the adjudicator what to consider. Section 88(3) is the matching prohibition — the closed list of things the adjudicator must not consider. In practice the live issue is almost always the second limb: a respondent putting up “new reasons” in the adjudication response that were not in the payment schedule. If the adjudicator runs with those new reasons, the determination is exposed to being set aside for jurisdictional error.

Section 88(3) BIF Act — matters the adjudicator must not consider

88 Adjudicator’s decision

(3) However, the adjudicator must not consider any of the following—

(a) an adjudication response, to which the adjudication application relates, that was not given to the adjudicator within the time required under section 83;

(b) a reason included in an adjudication response to the adjudication application, if the reason is prohibited from being included in the response under section 82.

Two prohibitions, one practical issue

Subsection (3)(a) is mechanical. If the response is late under s 83, the adjudicator cannot look at it. There is little case law because the timing question rarely produces a contestable issue — the response either landed in time or it didn’t.

Subsection (3)(b) is where the work happens. It locks together with s 82, which prohibits the respondent from including in the adjudication response any reason for withholding payment that was not included in the payment schedule. Read s 82 and s 88(3)(b) together and the rule is simple: the payment schedule fixes the universe of reasons. The adjudication response can develop, refine and support those reasons, but it cannot add new ones — and if it does, the adjudicator is forbidden from acting on them.

The Queensland authority on “new reasons”

The clearest Queensland statement of the rule is Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 92, where Martin J held that while an adjudicator may consider the provisions of the construction contract, the adjudicator must not consider a reason for withholding payment that relies on a particular contractual provision if that reason is a “new reason” not included in the payment schedule. The point came back in Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd (No. 2) [2021] QSC 231, where Freeburn J declared parts of an adjudication determination void in part because the adjudicator had appeared to acknowledge a respondent’s submission raising a new defence for withholding payment that was not in the payment schedule, and then proceeded as if it were properly before him.1

The most direct recent application of s 88(3) itself is Chevron Park Pty Ltd v Groupline Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2024] QSC 202. Chevron sought judicial review on the basis that the adjudicator had failed to consider payments made before and after the date of the payment claim. Treston J dismissed the application, holding that the adjudicator could not consider the subsequent payments precisely because of s 88(3): the adjudication response cannot include new reasons for withholding payment that were not raised in the payment schedule, and the adjudicator must not act on any such reason if it is included.

What counts as a “new reason”

The line between a new reason and further development of an existing reason is where the cases bite. In Miller v LMG Building Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 995, Ball J observed that the legislation “draws a distinction between the reasons for refusing a payment claim and the material that might be advanced in support of those reasons”, and said the dividing line turns on whether the claimant was sufficiently on notice of the reason that it could, if it wished, address it in the application. On the facts, the adjudicator had erred in excluding material produced after the payment schedule because the material did not introduce a new reason — it explained the overpayment that had already been identified in the schedule.2

The Queensland Supreme Court took the same approach in Paladin Projects Pty Ltd v Visie Three Pty Ltd & Ors [2024] QSC 230, where Williams J held that determining whether new reasons have been introduced requires assessing whether the narrative provided in the adjudication response aligns with the scope of the shorthand response in the payment schedule — there is no prohibition on articulating, in fuller form, reasons that were already there. And jurisdictional error only arises if the adjudicator actively considered the new reasons; brief reference without substantive engagement may not cross the line.3

Why getting this wrong is fatal

If the adjudicator does take a new reason into account, that is a textbook ground of jurisdictional error — the adjudicator has considered something the statute requires to be ignored. In Acciona Agua Australia Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 133, Bond J declared an adjudication determination void on that very basis: the adjudicator had acted in excess of jurisdiction by improperly considering new reasons in the adjudication response, and the reasons given were “nothing more than a vote one way”, revealing a failure of intellectual engagement with the issues.4 The same logic underpins the appellate gloss in NSW — the prohibition on a respondent travelling beyond the payment schedule is “a jurisdictional pre-condition” essential to the “pay now, argue later” scheme of the Act.5

Practice tips

  • If you are the respondent, treat the payment schedule as the closing list of reasons. Anything you want the adjudicator to engage with later must be there in some form — even in shorthand — before the application is made.
  • If you are the claimant reading an adjudication response, mark up every reason and check it back against the payment schedule. Anything genuinely new is a candidate to be flagged to the adjudicator under s 82 / s 88(3)(b).
  • The line is reason vs material. Adding documents, calculations, expert input or further explanation that supports an existing reason in the payment schedule is fine. Adding a defence, a contractual clause or a setoff that was never mentioned is not.
  • For the respondent, the safest practice is to plead reasons in the payment schedule at a level of generality that captures the case you actually intend to run — without misleading the claimant about the basis for non-payment. A bare “Nil” with no reasons gives you nothing to develop later.
  • If you are challenging a determination on this ground, the question is twofold: was a new reason in the response, and did the adjudicator actively consider it? A passing reference is unlikely to be enough; engagement that affected the result is.
