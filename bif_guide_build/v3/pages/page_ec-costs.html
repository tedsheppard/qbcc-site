Enforcing a subcontractor’s charge

Costs

A subcontractor’s charge proceeding is not a free ride. Whoever wins, whoever loses, and whoever drags the matter out longer than it needs to go can end up wearing a costs order. Chapter 4 deals with this in one short provision, but the discretion it confers on the court is broad and it sits on top of the ordinary cost rules that apply to any civil proceeding in Queensland.

Section 145 BIF Act — Costs

145 Costs

Costs of proceedings under this chapter as between party and party are at the discretion of the court.

What s 145 actually does

Section 145 doesn’t set a costs rule of its own — it confirms that the ordinary judicial discretion applies. “As between party and party” means the standard basis on which costs are awarded in civil proceedings: the successful party recovers a proportion of its actual legal costs, not all of them, and not on an indemnity basis unless something special justifies that order. The court’s starting point will be the usual one — costs follow the event — but the section is deliberately open-textured so the court can take account of the way the proceeding has been conducted and the conduct of the parties before the proceeding started.

What s 145 does not do is fence off costs in a Chapter 4 proceeding from the rest of the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules. Offers to settle, Calderbank offers, applications run and lost, unnecessary joinder, unreasonable refusals to accept liability under s 128, and obstructive conduct by a security holder are all available material for the court when it exercises the s 145 discretion.

Who is exposed

A Chapter 4 proceeding typically has three sets of parties: the subcontractor (claimant), the contractor (the party against whom the underlying debt is owed and against whose entitlement up the chain the charge bites), and the holder of the security (often the principal or a head contractor further up). All three are potentially exposed to a costs order.

  • The subcontractor is exposed if the charge fails — for example, because the notice of claim was defective, the time limit was missed, or the underlying debt isn’t made out.
  • The contractor is exposed if it disputed the claim under s 128 without a proper basis, or if it ran the proceeding in a way that drove costs up unnecessarily.
  • The security holder is exposed if it failed to retain the security, paid out in breach of s 130 or s 131, or otherwise required the subcontractor to litigate to get money that should have moved without a fight. The personal-liability machinery in s 130(3) is a separate exposure, but the costs of the proceeding sit on top of it.

Costs and the “prejudicially affected” concept in s 138

Costs in a Chapter 4 proceeding are not just an end-of-trial issue. They feed into the wider scheme through s 138, which defines when a person is “prejudicially affected” by a claim of charge. A person is taken to be prejudicially affected if, because of the claim, the payment of an amount to which they are entitled is delayed or otherwise affected, or the release of a security for a contract is delayed or otherwise affected, and they are the contractor or a subcontractor for a higher subcontract who has already paid another person an amount for the work the subject of the claim.

Section 138 BIF Act — When person prejudicially affected by a claim

138 When person prejudicially affected by a claim

Without limiting the circumstances in which a person may be prejudicially affected by a claim of a subcontractor’s charge, a person is taken to be prejudicially affected by the claim if—

(a) because of the claim—

(i) the payment of an amount to which the person is entitled is delayed or otherwise affected; or

(ii) the release of a security for a contract given by or for the person is delayed or otherwise affected; and

(b) the person is the contractor or a subcontractor for a higher subcontract; and

(c) under the contract or higher subcontract, the person has already paid another person an amount for work the subject of the claim.

Section 138 matters for costs because it identifies the people whose money is being held up by the claim. If the charge ultimately fails — because the work was never done, was already paid for, or the notice was bad — the prejudicially-affected contractor or higher subcontractor has been kept out of money it was already entitled to. That is the kind of fact pattern in which the court’s s 145 discretion can run hard against the subcontractor, including with costs assessed on the indemnity basis where the claim should never have been made.1

Practical drivers of the discretion

Because s 145 is a pure discretion, the way the parties conduct themselves before and during the proceeding tends to drive the outcome more than the bare result does. The factors that typically matter are:

  • whether the notice of claim under s 122 was properly grounded — an inflated or speculative claim weighs against the subcontractor on costs even if some part of the charge is upheld;
  • the contractor’s s 128 response — a bare denial that is later abandoned, or an acceptance that should have come earlier, can shift costs against the contractor;
  • the conduct of the security holder — cooperative compliance with s 130 or s 131 typically keeps the security holder out of the costs firing line; obstructive conduct does not;
  • offers to settle made during the proceeding — the usual UCPR consequences flow, including indemnity costs from the date of a non-accepted offer that the offeree fails to beat;
  • the extent to which the proceeding has caused prejudice within the meaning of s 138 — the longer money is held up and the larger the prejudice up the chain, the more attention the court will give to whether the claim was sensibly run.

What this means on a project

  • Treat a s 122 notice the way you would treat any pleading. Overstated amounts and shaky underlying debts are visible to the court when costs are assessed under s 145, even if you win the substance.
  • If you are the contractor, respond under s 128 on the merits. A reflex denial that you walk away from later is the kind of conduct that costs are made for.
  • If you are a security holder, comply with s 130 or s 131 on its terms. Cooperative compliance keeps you out of the costs argument; non-compliance puts you into it and exposes you to s 130(3) liability on top.
  • Make and consider offers to settle early. The s 145 discretion is wide enough to let the court give full UCPR effect to a Calderbank-style offer or a formal offer that the other side fails to beat.
  • Remember that delay itself is a cost item under the Act. Section 138 treats delayed payment up the chain as “prejudicial effect”, and that prejudice is part of what the court weighs when it decides who pays.
  1. Section 145 BIF Act preserves the ordinary party-and-party discretion; it does not displace the indemnity-costs jurisdiction available where a claim is made or maintained without proper foundation, or where a party’s conduct of the proceeding warrants it.
  2. Section 138 BIF Act — the “prejudicially affected” definition is expressed as inclusive (“without limiting the circumstances”), so the matters listed in paragraphs (a) to (c) are deemed cases of prejudice rather than the only ones the court can recognise.
