Project trust accounts

Withdrawals to pay the contracting party

The project trust exists primarily for the benefit of subcontractors, but the head contractor — sitting in the dual role of contracted party and trustee — is also entitled to draw down its own progress claims out of the same account. The Act doesn’t leave that to commercial discretion: s 20A confines the purposes for which money can come out at all, and s 20B then forces the head contractor’s self-payment to sit behind any current liability to a subcontractor beneficiary.

Section 20A(1) BIF Act — limited purposes for withdrawals

(1) A trustee must not withdraw an amount from the project trust account for any purpose other than—

(a) paying a subcontractor beneficiary an amount the contracted party is liable to pay the beneficiary in connection with its subcontract; or

(b) paying the trustee, as the contracted party, an amount the contracting party is liable to pay the contracted party for contracted work but only to the extent a subcontractor beneficiary is not entitled to be paid for the same work; or

(c) returning an amount paid in error by the contracting party; or

(d) depositing a retention amount into a retention trust account; or

(e) making payment relating to the contract in accordance with an adjudication under chapter 3, part 4; or

(f) making payment relating to the contract as ordered by a court; or

(g) making another payment prescribed by regulation.

The head contractor wears two hats

Under the BIF Act, the “contracted party” for a head contract is the party performing the work — in practice, the head contractor — and that same party is required to open the project trust account and act as trustee (s 18). When the head contractor pays itself out of the trust, it is doing so “as the contracted party”: receiving from the trust the amount the principal (the “contracting party” in BIF terminology) is liable to pay it for its own contracted work. Section 20A(1)(b) is the gateway provision that authorises that withdrawal.

The two limits in s 20A(1)(b)

Section 20A(1)(b) puts two distinct limits on the head contractor’s self-payment. First, the amount drawn must be an amount the contracting party is “liable to pay the contracted party for contracted work” — that is, money owing under the head contract for the head contractor’s own work, certified or otherwise payable. The trustee can’t draw down on amounts that aren’t yet due, and it can’t use the trust as a working capital facility for amounts the principal hasn’t become liable for.

Second — and this is the structural protection for subcontractors — the head contractor can only take out of the trust an amount “to the extent a subcontractor beneficiary is not entitled to be paid for the same work”. Where a portion of the principal’s payment relates to work that a subcontractor performed and is owed for, that portion belongs to the subcontractor, not the head contractor. The head contractor’s slice is the margin and the head contractor’s own work content; the subcontractor’s slice flows through.

Section 20B priority — pay subcontractors first

Section 20B BIF Act — order of priority

A trustee must not withdraw an amount from the project trust account to pay itself, or make another payment prescribed by regulation, unless there would still be a sufficient amount available in the account after the withdrawal to pay all amounts the contracted party is liable to pay subcontractor beneficiaries at the time of the withdrawal.

Section 20B is the hard rule on order of priority. Even where the head contractor is genuinely entitled under s 20A(1)(b) to take an amount, it cannot do so if the withdrawal would leave the account short of what it needs to pay subcontractor beneficiaries who are presently owed money. The test is forward-looking: would a sufficient amount remain “after the withdrawal” to cover all subcontractor liabilities owing “at the time of the withdrawal”? If not, the self-payment is prohibited — and the prohibition carries 300 penalty units or two years’ imprisonment.

That priority is reinforced from the other direction by s 51. If the account is short when a payment to a subcontractor beneficiary falls due, the trustee must immediately deposit an amount equal to the shortfall from its own funds. The Act does not allow the trustee to manage cash by paying itself first and topping the account up later.

Interest and bank fees sit outside the s 20A regime

Two carve-outs sit outside the s 20A purpose-list. Interest earned on amounts held in the project trust account belongs to the trustee under s 51D, and may be withdrawn once every 12 months or on dissolution — subject to the same proviso that the withdrawal must not prevent full payment of another amount that must be paid from the account. Fees charged by the approved financial institution for the project trust account are also outside s 20A (s 20A(4)). Neither category requires a s 20A(1)(b) entitlement, but both effectively defer to the same priority idea: the trust’s capacity to meet subcontractor liabilities comes first.

Withdrawals on dissolution

When the project trust is dissolved under s 21 — either because there are no longer any subcontractor beneficiaries, or because the only remaining work is maintenance — the trustee may pay itself any accrued interest entitlement under s 51D and “any remaining amount that is not owing to a subcontractor beneficiary” (s 21(4)). That is the one moment at which the residual balance can move cleanly into the head contractor’s own hands, because by definition no subcontractor beneficiary is left to be paid out of it.

What this means on a project

  • The head contractor can be paid from the trust, but only under s 20A(1)(b) — for amounts the principal is liable to pay it for its own contracted work, and only to the extent no subcontractor beneficiary is entitled to be paid for the same work.
  • Before each self-payment, run the s 20B test: after the withdrawal, will the account still hold enough to pay every subcontractor amount currently owing? If not, don’t take it. The criminal exposure (up to two years’ imprisonment) sits on the trustee personally.
  • Don’t treat a principal’s progress payment as “all yours” just because it landed in the trust account. Identify the subcontractor portion of the certified work first and ring-fence it; the head contractor can only draw the residue.
  • If the account is short when a subcontractor payment falls due, top it up under s 51 — do not delay the subcontractor payment and do not pay yourself first.
  • Annual interest and bank fees are not s 20A withdrawals; they are dealt with under s 51D and s 20A(4). Diary the 12-month interest sweep and check that it doesn’t collide with a pending subcontractor payment.
  • The clean exit point for any residual balance is dissolution under s 21: once there are no subcontractor beneficiaries left, the trustee can pay itself the remaining amount that is not owing to a beneficiary, plus any s 51D interest entitlement.
