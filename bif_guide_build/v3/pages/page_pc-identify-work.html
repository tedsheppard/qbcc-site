Requirements of a payment claim

Identify the construction work

The first thing s 68 demands of a payment claim is that it identify what the progress payment is for. Get this wrong and the claim is a nullity — no payment schedule is required, no adjudication can follow, and any determination that does follow is open to be set aside for jurisdictional error. The threshold is not high, but it is real.

Section 68(1)(a) BIF Act — what a payment claim must do

68 Meaning of payment claim

(1) A payment claim, for a progress payment, is a written document that—

(a) identifies the construction work or related goods and services to which the progress payment relates…

The “reasonably comprehensible” test

A payment claim must identify the construction work or related goods and services sufficiently to enable the respondent to understand the basis of the claim — or, put another way, the claim must be “reasonably comprehensible” to the recipient. The leading appellate statement is T & M Buckley Pty Ltd v 57 Moss Rd Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 381, where Philippides J (Fraser and White JJA agreeing) reviewed the authorities and adopted the formulation Hodgson JA had used in Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v Climatech (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 229:

What was required was that the payment claim purport in a reasonable way to identify the work the subject of the claim, and a payment claim was not a nullity for failure to identify the work unless the failure was patent on its face. The payment claim did not cease to satisfy the requirement concerning identification because it could be subsequently shown that the payment claim was not entirely successful in identifying all of the work.

Two things follow from that. First, the document is read in a common-sense, practical way and not from an unduly critical viewpoint — the regime emphasises speed and informality.1 Second, “sufficiency” is judged against what the parties already know. The Court of Appeal accepted in T & M Buckley that “the evaluation of the sufficiency of the identification takes into account the background knowledge of each of the parties derived from their past dealings and exchanges of documentation”. So a short line item that would be opaque to an outsider may still be enough between a head contractor and subcontractor who have been trading documents on the same job for months.

How much detail is enough — the rule in KDV Sport

The leading Queensland decision on the practical content of s 68(1)(a) is KDV Sport Pty Ltd v Muggeridge Constructions Pty Ltd [2019] QSC 178. Muggeridge served a payment claim for $2,365,432 supported by a “Trade Breakdown Schedule” listing some 51 categories of work, with a percentage-complete figure against each category and a number of unsubmitted variations tacked on. Brown J set the resulting adjudication aside. Her Honour held that merely citing the category of work and a percentage was not enough to identify the actual work being claimed for: it was not possible to tell what “20% completion” of a category represented as opposed to “30%”, the figures could not be reconciled to the dollars claimed, and the principal’s general familiarity with the project did not cure the deficiency.2

Percentage-only claims are not, however, automatically bad. In the same case Brown J accepted that where a percentage figure is anchored to an identifiable benchmark — for example, work claimed from a zero base — the description can be sufficient. Kelly J approved that distinction in Bridgeman Agencies Pty Ltd v S.E QLD Plumbing & Drainage Pty Ltd [2025] QSC 167, and added that the inquiry “cannot be answered by confining the inquiry to the actual document or documents comprising the payment claim” — supporting documents that have been provided do count, even if they aren’t physically attached.3

Documents in support, and information after the fact

A payment claim can incorporate other material — an earlier spreadsheet referred to in the invoice, a notice of dispute emailed at the same time, contractual line-item references — provided that material was already in the respondent’s hands when the claim was served.4 Information sent after service does not retrospectively rescue an inadequate claim: in Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd [2023] QSC 290, Williams J held that compliance with s 68(1)(a) is judged against information and material provided at or before delivery of the claim, and a previous practice of supplying further detail later was not enough to expand that timeframe.5

Errors and severance

Including an item that is plainly not construction work, or an item that turns out to be wrong, will not by itself invalidate the whole claim. The adjudicator is taken to have jurisdiction to value the work and to discount or sever items that don’t qualify or that are insufficiently identified.6 The exception is where the unidentified or non-qualifying portion is so substantial that the claim as a whole ceases to be reasonably comprehensible — KDV Sport is itself an example, and in Iris Broadbeach Williams J accepted the “big-ticket items” test: if the items that are not sufficiently described add up to a substantial amount of the total claimed, the whole claim falls.7

Practice tips

  • Describe the work, not just the category. A trade name plus a percentage is the classic KDV Sport trap. Tie each line item to the actual scope item, drawing or work package and, where possible, to a dollar value the recipient can reconcile.
  • Where you do use percentages, anchor them. “50% of the driveway concreting” on a single-house contract is fine; “5% of concreting” across 56 townhouses is not.8
  • Use the parties’ shared history. Past payment claims, agreed schedules of rates, the contract’s priced bill of quantities and the project’s document register are all fair game and reduce the description burden.
  • If you rely on a supporting document — a costed-variations spreadsheet, a notice of dispute, a delay claim — serve it with the claim or make sure it has already been delivered. Don’t back-fill after service.
  • Don’t pad with non-construction items (bank guarantees called up, financing costs, pure damages). Including them won’t always kill the claim, but if they swamp the qualifying scope the whole thing can fail.
  • For variations, attach enough particulars at the time of the claim to identify the work, the instruction relied on and the calculation. Unsubmitted variations bolted onto the claim were a key factor in KDV Sport.
