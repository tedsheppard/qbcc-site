Retention trust accounts

Releasing retention back to the subcontractor

Retention isn’t a fund the head contractor gets to sit on indefinitely. Once the subcontractor becomes entitled to the money under the contract — typically at practical completion for the first half, and at the end of the defects liability period for the balance — the trustee must release it, route the payment through the trust account, and tell the subbie what has happened. The same regime that protects the money on the way in controls how it comes out.

Section 36A BIF Act — release of retention amounts from the trust account

36A All retention amounts withheld to be released from retention trust account

(1) This section applies if a contracted party becomes entitled to the release of a retention amount held in a retention trust.

(2) The trustee must not release the retention amount other than by—

(a) withdrawing the amount from the retention trust account; and

(b) depositing the amount into the contracted party’s account at a financial institution.

Maximum penalty—200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.

When the entitlement to release arises

Section 36A is a release-mechanism section, not a release-trigger section. The trigger sits in the underlying contract: the subcontractor “becomes entitled to the release of a retention amount” when the contract says the money is to come back — usually a milestone tied to practical completion (release of the first moiety) and to expiry of the defects liability period (release of the balance). Once that contractual entitlement crystallises, s 36A controls how the money is moved.

The mechanics are tightly drawn. The trustee may not release retention by any path other than withdrawing it from the retention trust account and depositing it into a financial-institution account in the subcontractor’s name. Set-off by journal entry, application against an unrelated debt, or payment in cash all sit outside s 36A and attract the 200 penalty unit / 1 year imprisonment maximum. The companion provision at s 36 reinforces the point from the other side: withdrawals from the trust account are confined to paying the contracted-party beneficiary, paying the trustee for correcting defects (and only after the defects liability period ends), or paying a third party engaged to correct defects.

Notice to the subcontractor on every withdrawal

The release is paired with a separate notification duty. Under s 40A, every time the trustee deposits a retention amount into the trust account, or withdraws all or part of one, the trustee must give the contracted party from whom the amount was withheld a notice of the deposit or withdrawal within 5 business days. The notice has to include the information prescribed by regulation, and the only carve-out is a “reasonable excuse”. The maximum penalty is 100 penalty units.

For the subcontractor, that means a release of retention — whether full or partial — should be matched by a written s 40A notice within a week. If the cash arrives but no notice follows, that is itself a contravention and a useful trigger to ask the head contractor what has been withdrawn and why. The duty also runs the other way: a withdrawal that goes to the trustee (under s 36(1)(b) for defect rectification) or to a third-party rectifier (s 36(1)(c)) still attracts a s 40A notice to the subcontractor, so the subbie should see every movement that affects their money.

Disputing a withdrawal or a refusal to release

The Act doesn’t set up a bespoke retention-dispute tribunal. Disputes about release tend to run on three tracks, and the right one depends on what is actually in issue.

  • Contractual entitlement disputes. If the argument is whether the subcontractor has reached the milestone that releases retention — practical completion, end of defects liability, no outstanding defects — that is a contract dispute. It is resolved through the contract’s dispute mechanism, by adjudication under chapter 3 of the BIF Act if the released retention is claimed in a payment claim, or by court proceedings. Adjudication is often the fastest route because the released retention is a “progress payment” once the entitlement has accrued.
  • Misuse of the trust account. If the trustee has withdrawn money for a purpose outside s 36(1) — for example, applying retention to an unrelated debt, or withdrawing for defect rectification before the defects liability period has ended (prohibited by s 36(2)) — that is a trust-account contravention. The statutory remedies sit with the QBCC: s 36(3) requires the trustee to repay any amount withdrawn in contravention of s 36(1) as soon as practicable, and s 36A and s 36 carry their own penalties. The contracted party also has the benefit of the statutory charge under s 33A securing release of the amount, enforceable as if granted under a written security agreement, and protected against acts purporting to defeat it.
  • Refusal to release once entitlement has crystallised. If the contract clearly says the money is due and the trustee will not pay, the subbie can include the released retention in a payment claim and pursue adjudication, sue for the debt, or report the conduct to the QBCC as a likely breach of s 36A. The statutory charge under s 33A means the retention is treated as secured property in the contracted party’s favour — the charge is a statutory interest under s 73(2) of the Personal Property Securities Act 2009 (Cwlth) — which materially improves the subbie’s position if the head contractor goes into external administration before release.

Closing out the trust

Once every retention amount under a contract has been released to the parties entitled to it, the retention trust for that contract is dissolved (s 37(1)). On dissolution the trustee may pay itself any residual amount that is not owing to another beneficiary (s 37(2)). The retention trust account itself is a different question: under s 34A(4) the trustee must not close the account unless all retention amounts have been released to the parties entitled, or the account is being transferred to another approved financial institution under s 34C.

Practice tips

  • Map your release entitlements off the contract, not the Act. Diary the practical completion date and the defects liability end date for every subcontract, and treat each as the moment the corresponding moiety becomes payable.
  • Expect a s 40A notice within 5 business days of any deposit or withdrawal that touches your retention. If a release lands in your account without a notice, ask for one in writing — the notice is the trustee’s obligation, not a courtesy.
  • If a withdrawal looks like it has gone to the head contractor for “defects” before the defects liability period has ended, that is a s 36(2) issue. Raise it in writing, ask for repayment under s 36(3), and report to the QBCC if it isn’t corrected.
  • To force release of retention you say is overdue, the fastest route is usually to include the amount as a line in your next payment claim and run it through adjudication. The contractual entitlement is the live question; s 36A simply controls how the trustee must pay once it loses.
  • If the head contractor’s solvency is in doubt, remember that the s 33A charge sits over the retention amount in your favour and is protected against acts that would defeat it. That is your principal protection if administration or liquidation intervenes before release.
  • Keep your own ledger of retention withheld, deposit notices and withdrawal notices. When release time comes, the s 40A notices — and the absence of any unexplained withdrawals — are the evidence trail you will rely on.
