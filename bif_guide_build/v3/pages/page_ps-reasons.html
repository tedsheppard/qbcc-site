Requirements of a payment schedule

Reasons for withholding payment

If your scheduled amount is less than the amount claimed — including where you propose to pay nil — s 69(c) requires the payment schedule to state why, and to set out your reasons for withholding any payment. This is the rule that locks in the dispute. Reasons you don’t state in the schedule cannot be raised later in adjudication, so the schedule has to do the work of defining the issues at the time you serve it.

Section 69 BIF Act — meaning of payment schedule (chapeau and (c))

A payment schedule, responding to a payment claim, is a written document that—

…

(c) if the amount proposed to be paid is less than the amount stated in the payment claim—states why the amount proposed to be paid is less, including the respondent’s reasons for withholding any payment…

What “state why” requires

The schedule has to define the dispute clearly enough that the claimant can decide whether to push the claim into adjudication, and what case it has to meet if it does. The classic formulation is from Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Luikens & Anor [2003] NSWSC 1140, where Palmer J said that for a respondent merely to state that a claim is rejected is no more informative than to say that payment is “withheld”:

Section 14(3) requires that reasons for withholding payment of a claim be indicated in the payment schedule with sufficient particularity to enable the claimant to understand, at least in broad outline, what is the issue between it and the respondent. This understanding is necessary so that the claimant may decide whether to pursue the claim and may know what is the nature of the respondent’s case which it will have to meet if it decides to pursue the claim by referring it to adjudication.

That standard governs s 69(c). The reasons don’t have to read like a pleading — an experienced builder and subbie working off the same contract and the same prior correspondence can use abbreviated language — but they have to apprise the parties of the real issues in dispute.1 Brown J in Melaleuca View Pty Ltd v Sutton Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2019] QSC 226 stressed that s 69 deliberately uses “state” rather than the looser “indicate” in the NSW equivalent, signalling that Queensland respondents are held to a higher standard of clarity than their NSW counterparts.

Reasons must cover every part of the claim

The schedule has to address the whole payment claim, not just the parts the respondent wants to argue about. Silence on the rest is fatal. Chesterman J’s warning in Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA) Pty Ltd [2007] QSC 333 still controls: “The machinery for prompt payment and enforcement of payment would break down if a document, said to be a payment schedule, took issue with part only of a claim but was silent as to what it proposed to pay in respect of the balance.” If the payment claim has multiple components, every component needs to be addressed — either with reasons of its own, or by a global response that on its face covers the lot.2

Bare denials, deferrals and clarification requests

The recent NSWCA decision in Joye Group Pty Ltd v Cemco Projects Pty Ltd [2021] NSWCA 211 illustrates how easy it is to fall short. The respondent had told the claimant it would not pay until the work was complete, and the District Court treated that — read with the parties’ background correspondence — as a sufficient reason. The Court of Appeal disagreed: a statement that “we will not pay your claim until…” says when, not whether, payment will be made; it doesn’t identify which items have been done, which haven’t, or what the respondent disputes. The Court of Appeal also held that a payment schedule is not to be reconstructed by reference to external materials in order to give it a particularity it doesn’t have on its face.

The same theme runs through other recent decisions. An email asking the claimant to “please adjust your claim accordingly and resubmit”, or to “expand on the difference between these two quotes”, is a request for clarification, not a reason for withholding payment.3 An email amounting to “little more than a bare assertion that further substantiation is required” doesn’t state a reason at all.4 And a vague reference to “many emails, photos, videos, back charges from builders and other trades, complains [sic] from my clients” isn’t enough — if you incorporate documents by reference, they have to be identified with enough particularity that the claimant knows what is being incorporated.5

What sufficient reasons look like

By contrast, in CPB Contractors Pty Ltd & Ors v MSS Projects (NSW) Pty Ltd t/as MSS Steel & Ors [2025] QSC 239, Johnstone J held that a payment schedule satisfied s 69(c) where the respondent expressly proposed to pay $0 for descoped work, identified the descoping direction, and explained its variation calculations by reference to the figures in the “Certified this Month” column of Schedule 2 of the schedule. Both sides knew what the dispute was and why. As his Honour confirmed, agreeing with Chesterman J in Minimax, there is no particular form required and no obligation to produce a detailed spreadsheet — just enough to define the scope of the dispute.6

One further point: incorporation by reference is permitted, but the assembled document — schedule plus annexures — has to satisfy s 69 on its face. The respondent can’t leave the claimant or the adjudicator to assemble miscellaneous prior correspondence into a payment schedule.7

Practice tips

  • For every dollar you propose to pay less than the claimed amount, write a reason. If the schedule discounts ten line items, say something specific about each one — even if the language is short.
  • Address the whole payment claim. If you say nothing about an item, you risk the document being treated as no schedule at all.
  • Don’t treat “we’ll pay when the work is finished”, “please resubmit a corrected claim” or “we need more information” as reasons. They aren’t. State your actual basis for paying less — defective work, descope, contractual cap, set-off, valuation dispute — on the face of the schedule.
  • Use the contract and the parties’ prior correspondence as shorthand if you like, but make sure the schedule itself is intelligible. Don’t rely on the adjudicator reconstructing your case from emails you didn’t identify.
  • If you incorporate documents (back-charge ledgers, defect lists, superintendent’s assessments, set-off calculations), identify them precisely — date, title, version — and attach them. Vague references to “our records” will not do.
  • Remember the consequence. Reasons not stated in the payment schedule cannot be raised in your adjudication response. Get them all in now — bare denials and conditional language will leave you defenceless when the claim goes to adjudication.
