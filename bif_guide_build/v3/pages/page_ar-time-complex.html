Requirements of an adjudication response

Within time — complex claims (15 + 15 BD)

If the payment claim being adjudicated is a complex payment claim, you have longer to put your response together — and you can apply for a further extension on top. But the windows still run from fixed events, they’re measured in business days, and getting the trigger date wrong is one of the most common reasons a response is rejected as out of time.

Section 83(2) BIF Act — time for an adjudication response to a complex payment claim

(2) If responding to a complex payment claim, the respondent must give the adjudicator the adjudication response within the later of the following to end—

(a) 15 business days after receiving the documents mentioned in section 79(4);

(b) 12 business days after receiving notice of the adjudicator’s acceptance of the adjudication application.

(3) However, if responding to a complex payment claim, the respondent may apply to the adjudicator for an extension of time, of up to 15 additional business days, to give the adjudication response to the adjudicator.

(4) The application must—

(a) be in writing; and

(b) be made within the later of the following periods to end—

(i) 5 business days after receiving the documents mentioned in section 79(4);

(ii) 2 business days after receiving notice of the adjudicator’s acceptance of the adjudication application; and

(c) include the reasons for requiring the extension of time.

The two starting periods

Section 83(2) gives a complex-claim respondent the later of two periods. The first runs 15 business days from when you receive the s 79(4) documents — that is, the copy of the adjudication application the claimant is required to give you. The second runs 12 business days from when you receive notice of the adjudicator’s acceptance of the application. Whichever date falls later is the deadline for the response itself.

Most of the time the acceptance notice arrives after the s 79(4) documents, which means the 12-business-day limb does the work. But not always — if the adjudicator accepts quickly, the 15-business-day limb running from the s 79(4) documents will be the controlling deadline. Calculate both, then use the later one.

“Receiving” is not the same as “serving”

Both limbs of s 83(2) are measured from the date you received something — not the date the claimant or registrar dispatched it. The Act draws a deliberate distinction between service and receipt, and the courts have insisted on it. In CMF Projects Pty Ltd v Masic Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 209 the adjudicator’s notice of acceptance was sent to a post office box rather than the respondent’s registered office. Daubney J held that the adjudicator had erred by treating deposit of the notice into the post office box as receipt; service of a document and receipt of it by the addressee are two different events, and only the latter starts the s 83 clock.1

there is a distinction between the “service” and the “receipt” of documents under the Act — the deposit of a notice into a post office box is not, without more, receipt of the notice by the addressee.

The practical point is unforgiving. If the adjudicator or claimant treats a dispatch date as the receipt date and your response is rejected as late on that footing, the determination is at risk — but so are you, because you’ll need to prove when the documents actually came into your hands. Diary the actual receipt date for both events the moment they happen, and keep the envelope, email header or courier record that proves it.

The 15 business day extension under s 83(3)

Complex claims attract one further safety valve: under s 83(3) you may apply to the adjudicator for up to 15 additional business days. The application must be in writing, must include the reasons you need the extension, and must itself be made in time — the later of 5 business days after receiving the s 79(4) documents, or 2 business days after receiving the acceptance notice (s 83(4)). Those inner windows are tight, so the extension request usually has to be drafted in parallel with the response itself, not after the response is well under way.

The extension is not automatic. The adjudicator decides whether to grant it and may grant less than 15 days. Reasons that tend to land — volume of claim material, genuine availability problems for key witnesses or experts, a claim that pleads a new theory not foreshadowed in the payment schedule — should be set out specifically. Generic “we need more time” requests are easy to refuse.

Out of time means not considered

If the response is given outside the s 83(2) window (as extended under s 83(5) where applicable), the adjudicator must not consider it: see s 88(2). That is what happened in Rise Constructions v El-Hajj [2019] VSC 818, on the Victorian equivalent of s 83(1)(b). Digby J upheld the adjudicator’s decision that the notice of acceptance had been served on a particular date and that, in consequence, the adjudication response had been delivered outside the statutory time. The adjudicator validly declined to consider the response, and the determination stood.2 The lesson is that arguments about when the clock started are best run before the response is due, by way of correspondence with the adjudicator — not after the determination, when the response has already been excluded.

What this means on a project

  • The day you receive the s 79(4) documents and the day you receive the acceptance notice are the two trigger dates that matter. Diary both immediately and calculate both deadlines — 15 BD from the first, 12 BD from the second — and work to the later one.
  • Capture proof of actual receipt, not dispatch. After CMF Projects, the date the document hit your inbox, post office box pickup or registered office is what counts, and you may need to prove it.
  • If the claim material is voluminous or the schedule was tight, decide within the first 2–5 business days whether to apply for the s 83(3) extension. The inner window for the extension request is shorter than people expect.
  • Make the extension request specific. Identify the volume, the experts you need, the reason the standard window won’t do. Don’t assume the adjudicator will grant the full 15 days — they often grant less.
  • Don’t lodge a late response and hope it gets in. Section 88(2) requires the adjudicator to disregard it, and you’ll be left arguing the determination on the application alone.
  • Once the response is given, remember s 83(6): you must give a copy to the claimant within 2 business days. Build that step into the same diary entry as the response itself.
