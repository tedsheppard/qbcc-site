Requirements of an adjudication application

Identify the payment claim and schedule

An adjudication application has to point unambiguously back to the payment claim it is built on, and to the payment schedule it is answering — if there is one. Get this wrong and the adjudicator has no jurisdiction, because the application has not engaged the statute.

Section 79(2)(c) BIF Act — identification

79 Application for adjudication

(2) An adjudication application—

…

(c) must identify the payment claim and the payment schedule, if any, to which it relates…

What “identify” actually requires

The word “must” in s 79(2)(c) is mandatory, not directory. It is in the same league as the requirement in s 79(2)(a) to use the approved form, and it sits in deliberate contrast to the discretionary “may” that governs submissions. An application that fails to identify the right payment claim and the right payment schedule is not just defective — it is incapable of supporting an adjudication decision.1

That doesn’t mean the application has to be perfectly drafted. The leading Queensland decision on s 79(2)(c) is National Management Group Pty Ltd v Biriel Industries Pty Ltd trading as Master Steel & Ors [2019] QSC 219, where Wilson J held that an adjudication application satisfied s 79(2)(c) despite a number of errors, because “the adjudication application document refers to the project name, the reference date and the amount due.” The point is functional: can the respondent and the adjudicator tell, from the four corners of the application, exactly which payment claim and which payment schedule are in play? If yes, s 79(2)(c) is met even if there are imperfections elsewhere.

Identifying the wrong document is fatal

The clearest way to fall foul of the section is to identify the wrong payment schedule. In Kangaroo Point Developments MP Property Pty Ltd v RHG Construction Fitout and Maintenance Pty Ltd [2021] QSC 30, Dalton J set aside an adjudication decision because the claimant had not identified the payment schedule the respondent had actually issued — the application instead pointed to a different document. Her Honour was direct about the consequence:

The word “must” in each of section 79(2)(a) and 79(2)(c) of the BIF Act denotes a mandatory requirement of the Act whereas, in contrast, the word “may” in section 79(2)(e) of the BIF Act is used when the applicant for adjudication in fact has a choice as to whether or not to provide submissions.

So the risk is real even when the underlying claim is good: if the application names a document that isn’t the payment schedule, or omits the payment schedule altogether when one exists, the foundation of the adjudication is gone.

If there is no payment schedule

The words “if any” in s 79(2)(c) recognise that one path into adjudication — the s 79(1)(a) / s 78(2)(b) route — involves the respondent giving no payment schedule at all. In that situation you only need to identify the payment claim. But if a payment schedule did exist, even a late or informal one, you need to grapple with it and identify it; you cannot improve your position by ignoring it.

What this means on a project

  • State the payment claim’s date, claim number, project and claim amount on the face of the application. Attach the claim itself. The Biriel threshold — project name, reference date, amount due — is a sensible minimum, not a target.
  • If a payment schedule was given, identify it the same way: date, scheduled amount, who issued it, and attach a copy. Don’t pick and choose — if there are competing documents that might be the payment schedule, name all of them and explain which one you say is the schedule and why.
  • Don’t identify a different document by mistake. Kangaroo Point is the warning shot: getting the wrong schedule on the application is the kind of error that survives no amount of merit on the underlying claim.
  • If you’re running the “no payment schedule” route under s 79(1)(a), say so explicitly in the application and explain why no schedule was given (e.g. the s 76 deadline passed). That removes any argument that you have failed to identify a schedule that in fact exists.
  • Cross-check the application against the approved form before lodging. The mandatory requirements in s 79(2)(a) and s 79(2)(c) are the two most common jurisdictional knock-out points at the front end of an adjudication.
  1. Kangaroo Point Developments MP Property Pty Ltd v RHG Construction Fitout and Maintenance Pty Ltd [2021] QSC 30 (Dalton J) — “must” in s 79(2)(a) and s 79(2)(c) denotes a mandatory requirement, in contrast to the discretionary “may” in s 79(2)(e) governing submissions.
  2. National Management Group Pty Ltd v Biriel Industries Pty Ltd trading as Master Steel & Ors [2019] QSC 219 (Wilson J) — s 79(2)(c) satisfied despite errors where the application referred to the project name, the reference date and the amount due.
  3. Kuatro Build Pty Ltd v Elite Formwork Group Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 372 (Hmelnitsky J) — adjudication application validly made under s 17 of the NSW SOP Act (equivalent to s 79 BIF Act); no jurisdictional issue with lodgement or identification raised.
