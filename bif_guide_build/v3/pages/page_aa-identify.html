Requirements of an adjudication application

Identify the payment claim and schedule

An adjudication application must point unambiguously to the payment claim on which it is based, and to the payment schedule it answers, if there is one. If this requirement is not met the adjudicator has no jurisdiction, because the application has not engaged the statute.

Section 79(2)(c) BIF Act — identification

79 Application for adjudication

(2) An adjudication application—

…

(c) must identify the payment claim and the payment schedule, if any, to which it relates…

What “identify” actually requires

The word “must” in s 79(2)(c) is mandatory, not directory. It is of the same character as the requirement in s 79(2)(a) to use the approved form, and stands in deliberate contrast to the discretionary “may” that governs submissions. An application that fails to identify the correct payment claim and the correct payment schedule is not merely defective; it is incapable of supporting an adjudication decision.1

The application need not be perfectly drafted. The leading Queensland decision on s 79(2)(c) is National Management Group Pty Ltd v Biriel Industries Pty Ltd trading as Master Steel & Ors [2019] QSC 219, where Wilson J held that an adjudication application satisfied s 79(2)(c) despite a number of errors, because “the adjudication application document refers to the project name, the reference date and the amount due.” The test is functional: whether the respondent and the adjudicator can tell, from the four corners of the application, which payment claim and which payment schedule are in issue. If they can, s 79(2)(c) is satisfied even if there are imperfections elsewhere.

Identifying the wrong document is fatal

The clearest way to contravene the section is to identify the wrong payment schedule. In Kangaroo Point Developments MP Property Pty Ltd v RHG Construction Fitout and Maintenance Pty Ltd [2021] QSC 30, Dalton J set aside an adjudication decision because the claimant had not identified the payment schedule the respondent had actually issued; the application instead pointed to a different document. Her Honour was direct about the consequence:

The word “must” in each of section 79(2)(a) and 79(2)(c) of the BIF Act denotes a mandatory requirement of the Act whereas, in contrast, the word “may” in section 79(2)(e) of the BIF Act is used when the applicant for adjudication in fact has a choice as to whether or not to provide submissions.

The risk therefore remains material even when the underlying claim has merit: if the application names a document that is not the payment schedule, or omits the payment schedule altogether when one exists, the foundation of the adjudication is lost.

If there is no payment schedule

The words “if any” in s 79(2)(c) recognise that one path into adjudication — the s 79(1)(a) / s 78(2)(b) route — involves the respondent giving no payment schedule at all. In that situation only the payment claim need be identified. If a payment schedule did exist, however, even a late or informal one, the claimant must engage with it and identify it; the claimant cannot improve its position by ignoring it.

Practice tips

  • State the payment claim’s date, claim number, project and claim amount on the face of the application, and attach the claim itself. The Biriel threshold — project name, reference date, amount due — is a sensible minimum, not a target.
  • If a payment schedule was given, identify it in the same way: date, scheduled amount, issuer, and attach a copy. Where there are competing documents that might be the payment schedule, name each and explain which is said to be the schedule and why.
  • Avoid identifying a different document in error. Kangaroo Point illustrates the point: identifying the wrong schedule is an error that cannot be cured by the merits of the underlying claim.
  • If proceeding under the “no payment schedule” route in s 79(1)(a), state this explicitly in the application and explain why no schedule was given (for example, the s 76 deadline passed). This forecloses any argument that the claimant has failed to identify a schedule that does in fact exist.
  • Cross-check the application against the approved form before lodging. The mandatory requirements in s 79(2)(a) and s 79(2)(c) are the two most common jurisdictional points of failure at the front end of an adjudication.

  2. National Management Group Pty Ltd v Biriel Industries Pty Ltd trading as Master Steel & Ors [2019] QSC 219 (Wilson J) — s 79(2)(c) satisfied despite errors where the application referred to the project name, the reference date and the amount due.
  3. Kuatro Build Pty Ltd v Elite Formwork Group Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 372 (Hmelnitsky J) — adjudication application validly made under s 17 of the NSW SOP Act (equivalent to s 79 BIF Act); no jurisdictional issue with lodgement or identification raised.
