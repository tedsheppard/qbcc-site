Requirements of a payment claim

Not for unlicensed work

The BIF Act payment regime sits on top of the Queensland licensing scheme in the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991 (QBCC Act). Section 42 of the QBCC Act makes it unlawful to carry out, or undertake to carry out, building work unless you hold a licence of the appropriate class. If your scope is “building work” under the QBCC Act and you weren’t licensed to perform it, the QBCC Act bars you from any monetary recovery for that work — and a BIF Act payment claim is one of the things that bar reaches.

Section 42 QBCC Act — Unlawful carrying out of building work

42 Unlawful carrying out of building work

It is an offence under the QBCC Act for a person to carry out, or undertake to carry out, building work unless that person holds a contractor’s licence of the appropriate class. The Act extends the prohibition to specific trades through s 42C (fire protection work) and s 42CA (mechanical services work), and to operating without a nominee under s 42B. A short carve-out for newly created licence classes is preserved by s 42A. The financial-loss consequences of unlicensed work are dealt with separately at s 42E (avoidance of contractual obligations causing significant financial loss).

How s 42 bites on a BIF Act payment claim

Section 42 doesn’t just create an offence. It strips the unlicensed party of the right to monetary recovery for the unlawful work — including a contractual right to be paid, a quantum-meruit recovery, and (most importantly here) a statutory entitlement to a progress payment under s 70 of the BIF Act. The BIF Act regime is designed to enforce a statutory entitlement to be paid for construction work performed under a construction contract. If the QBCC Act says no enforceable entitlement to payment exists at all because the work was carried out without a licence, the BIF Act has nothing to operate on.

The practical consequence on a payment-claim challenge is two-fold:

  • If the entire scope of the contract is “building work” under the QBCC Act and the claimant is unlicensed for that scope, the payment claim is incapable of supporting a valid adjudication. There is no underlying entitlement to a progress payment for the adjudicator to determine.
  • If only part of the scope is unlicensed — for example, a head contractor licensed for civil works who picks up some plumbing or fire-protection work without holding the relevant trade licence — only the unlicensed part is barred. The licensed scope remains claimable. But the payment claim has to be capable of being read so that the unlicensed component can be cleanly separated; if it can’t, the claim is exposed in its entirety.

What the QBCC Act treats as “building work”

The QBCC Act’s definition of “building work” is broader than most people expect. It picks up not only the construction, alteration, repair and removal of buildings, but also site work directly attached to those activities, fire-protection work (s 42C), and mechanical services work (s 42CA). The licence categories that match these scopes are detailed in the QBCC Regulation, and the “appropriate class” question turns on what your contracted scope actually involved — not what you ended up doing or the title of the trade.

Three points come up repeatedly on projects:

  • The licence has to cover the work, not just the entity. A company that holds a builder’s licence is not automatically licensed for fire-protection or mechanical-services work. Sections 42C and 42CA stand on their own; you need the trade licence as well.
  • Unlicensed nominee work. Section 42B makes it an offence to carry out building work without a properly nominated nominee. A licensed company that loses or fails to nominate a nominee is, for the period of the gap, in the same position as an unlicensed contractor for s 42 purposes.
  • The s 42A grace period only applies to newly created licence classes within the 12-month transitional window. It is not a general defence to operating without a licence.

Section 42E and significant financial loss

Section 42E sits alongside s 42 and is the provision that most directly addresses the money side of unlicensed work. It restricts contractual recovery where unlicensed work has caused a party significant financial loss, and operates independently of s 42’s offence provisions. From a payment-claim perspective, the working assumption should be that any contract or part of a contract executed in breach of the licensing rules will be vulnerable on both fronts: an offence under s 42 and a recovery limit under s 42E. A BIF Act payment claim does not rehabilitate either.

What this means on a project

  • Before serving a payment claim, check that the entity issuing the claim holds the appropriate QBCC licence for every part of the scope being claimed. The licence has to be current and the right class — not just any licence.
  • If you have a mixed scope (e.g. building plus fire-protection plus mechanical services), make sure each trade is held by an entity that is licensed for it, or is sub-contracted to a properly licensed party. Sections 42C and 42CA are easy traps on integrated fit-out and services jobs.
  • If a nominee role has lapsed or hasn’t been filled, treat any payment claim served during the gap as exposed under s 42B. The fix is to regularise the nominee position before serving, not after.
  • If you are a respondent and the claimant is unlicensed for some or all of the scope, raise the s 42 / s 42E point in the payment schedule. Payment-schedule reasons drive what the adjudicator can consider, and licensing is a recurring respondent ground that has to be put in early.
  • For owner-builder work, confirm whether the s 43E permit was required and held. An owner-builder operating outside the permit conditions falls back into the s 42 prohibition and is in the same position as any other unlicensed contractor.
  • Don’t assume the BIF Act’s “construction work” definition in s 65 maps perfectly onto the QBCC Act’s “building work”. They overlap heavily but they are not the same; work that is “construction work” for BIF Act purposes can still be unlicensed “building work” under the QBCC Act and therefore unrecoverable.
