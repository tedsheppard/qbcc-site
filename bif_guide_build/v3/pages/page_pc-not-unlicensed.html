Requirements of a payment claim

Not for unlicensed work

The BIF Act payment provisions operate alongside the Queensland licensing scheme in the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991 (QBCC Act). Section 42 of the QBCC Act makes it unlawful to carry out, or undertake to carry out, building work unless the person holds a licence of the appropriate class. If the scope is “building work” under the QBCC Act and the person was not licensed to perform it, the QBCC Act bars any monetary recovery for that work, and a BIF Act payment claim is within the scope of that bar.

Section 42 QBCC Act — Unlawful carrying out of building work

42 Unlawful carrying out of building work

It is an offence under the QBCC Act for a person to carry out, or undertake to carry out, building work unless that person holds a contractor’s licence of the appropriate class. The Act extends the prohibition to specific trades through s 42C (fire protection work) and s 42CA (mechanical services work), and to operating without a nominee under s 42B. A short carve-out for newly created licence classes is preserved by s 42A. The financial-loss consequences of unlicensed work are dealt with separately at s 42E (avoidance of contractual obligations causing significant financial loss).

The effect of s 42 on a BIF Act payment claim

Section 42 does more than create an offence. It removes the unlicensed party’s right to monetary recovery for the unlawful work, including any contractual right to be paid, a quantum-meruit recovery, and a statutory entitlement to a progress payment under s 70 of the BIF Act. The BIF Act is directed at enforcing a statutory entitlement to be paid for construction work performed under a construction contract. If the QBCC Act provides that no enforceable entitlement to payment exists because the work was carried out without a licence, the BIF Act has no entitlement on which to operate.

The practical consequence on a payment-claim challenge is two-fold:

  • If the entire scope of the contract is “building work” under the QBCC Act and the claimant is unlicensed for that scope, the payment claim is incapable of supporting a valid adjudication. There is no underlying entitlement to a progress payment for the adjudicator to determine.
  • If only part of the scope is unlicensed — for example, a head contractor licensed for civil works who undertakes plumbing or fire-protection work without holding the relevant trade licence — only the unlicensed part is barred. The licensed scope remains claimable. The payment claim must, however, be capable of being read so that the unlicensed component can be separated; if it cannot be, the claim is at risk in its entirety.

What the QBCC Act treats as “building work”

The QBCC Act’s definition of “building work” is broader than is commonly assumed. It includes not only the construction, alteration, repair and removal of buildings, but also site work directly connected with those activities, fire-protection work (s 42C), and mechanical services work (s 42CA). The licence categories corresponding to these scopes are detailed in the QBCC Regulation, and the “appropriate class” question turns on what the contracted scope involved, not on the work ultimately performed or the title of the trade.

Three points recur on projects:

  • The licence must cover the work, not merely the entity. A company that holds a builder’s licence is not, on that basis alone, licensed for fire-protection or mechanical-services work. Sections 42C and 42CA operate independently; the relevant trade licence is also required.
  • Unlicensed nominee work. Section 42B makes it an offence to carry out building work without a properly nominated nominee. A licensed company that loses or fails to nominate a nominee is, for the period of the gap, in the same position as an unlicensed contractor for s 42 purposes.
  • The s 42A grace period applies only to newly created licence classes within the 12-month transitional window. It is not a general defence to operating without a licence.

Section 42E and significant financial loss

Section 42E operates alongside s 42 and is the provision that most directly addresses the financial consequences of unlicensed work. It restricts contractual recovery where unlicensed work has caused a party significant financial loss, and operates independently of s 42’s offence provisions. From a payment-claim perspective, the working assumption should be that any contract or part of a contract performed in breach of the licensing rules will be exposed on two grounds: an offence under s 42 and a recovery limit under s 42E. A BIF Act payment claim does not cure either.

Practice tips

  • Before serving a payment claim, confirm that the entity issuing the claim holds the appropriate QBCC licence for every part of the scope being claimed. The licence must be current and of the correct class.
  • For mixed scopes (for example, building plus fire-protection plus mechanical services), confirm that each trade is held by an entity licensed for it, or is sub-contracted to a properly licensed party. Sections 42C and 42CA are recurring sources of difficulty on integrated fit-out and services projects.
  • If a nominee role has lapsed or has not been filled, any payment claim served during the gap is exposed under s 42B. The position should be regularised before service, not after.
  • For a respondent where the claimant is unlicensed for some or all of the scope, the s 42 / s 42E point should be raised in the payment schedule. Payment-schedule reasons determine what the adjudicator may consider, and licensing is a recurring respondent ground that must be raised at that stage.
  • For owner-builder work, confirm whether the s 43E permit was required and held. An owner-builder operating outside the permit conditions falls within the s 42 prohibition and is in the same position as any other unlicensed contractor.
  • The BIF Act’s “construction work” definition in s 65 should not be assumed to map precisely onto the QBCC Act’s “building work”. The definitions overlap substantially but are not identical; work that is “construction work” for BIF Act purposes may still be unlicensed “building work” under the QBCC Act and therefore unrecoverable.

  2. For owner-builder work, see QBCC Act s 43E (when owner-builder permit is required) and ss 44–44J (application, criteria, conditions and cancellation of owner-builder permits). Work outside the permit conditions falls back into the s 42 prohibition.
