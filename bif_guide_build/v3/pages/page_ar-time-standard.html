Requirements of an adjudication response

Within time — standard claims (10 BD)

If the adjudication you’re responding to relates to a standard payment claim, s 83(1) gives you a tight window. You have to lodge the adjudication response within the later of two periods — 10 business days from when the application documents arrive, or 7 business days from notice of the adjudicator’s acceptance. Miss that window and the adjudicator can’t consider your response at all.

Section 83(1) BIF Act — time for making adjudication response (standard claims)

(1) If responding to a standard payment claim, the respondent must give the adjudicator the adjudication response within the later of the following periods to end—

(a) 10 business days after receiving the documents mentioned in section 79(4);

(b) 7 business days after receiving notice of the adjudicator’s acceptance of the adjudication application.

Two clocks, whichever ends later

Section 83(1) sets up two periods running in parallel and tells you to use whichever ends later — the opposite of the s 76 payment-schedule rule, which uses whichever ends first. The 10 business day clock starts when you receive the s 79(4) documents (the application itself, plus the supporting submissions and any annexures the claimant elects to serve on you). The 7 business day clock starts when the adjudicator’s notice of acceptance reaches you. In practice the 10-day clock will normally be the operative one, because applications are usually served on you the same day or close to the day they go to the registrar. But if the registrar takes time to refer the application, or the adjudicator delays accepting, the 7-day clock from notice of acceptance can run later and give you the longer runway. Diary both dates the moment they happen and work to whichever falls later.

For a complex payment claim the periods are different (15 / 12 business days under s 83(2), with a discretionary extension of up to 15 additional business days under s 83(3)). There is no equivalent extension mechanism for a standard claim — under s 83(1) the 10/7 windows are what you’ve got.

Receipt, not service, fixes the clock

The trigger words in s 83(1)(a) and (b) are “receiving” — not “service”. The Act treats those concepts as distinct, and for s 83 purposes it is the date on which the documents (or the notice of acceptance) actually come into your hands that controls. The point matters because a document deposited somewhere you can’t practically retrieve it isn’t “received” just because the sender has done what their service clause requires. In CMF Projects Pty Ltd v Masic Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 209, an adjudicator’s notice of acceptance was sent to a post office box rather than the respondent’s registered office. Daubney J held that the adjudicator had erred in equating deposit of the notice into the post office box with receipt of the notice — service and receipt are not the same thing under the Act, and the clock under the equivalent timing provision did not start running until the notice was actually received.

The corollary of this is that fixing the date of receipt accurately is the single most important thing you can do as a respondent. If the adjudicator gets it wrong — deciding that the notice of acceptance, or the s 79(4) documents, were received earlier than they actually were — your response can be ruled out of time and excluded from consideration, with limited scope to overturn the result. In Rise Constructions v El-Hajj [2019] VSC 818, Digby J dismissed an application to quash an adjudication determination on procedural-fairness grounds where the adjudicator had decided that a notice of acceptance was served on a particular date and, on that finding, treated the adjudication response as having been lodged outside the equivalent of s 83(1)(b). His Honour concluded the adjudicator’s decision on the date of service was made within jurisdiction and on sufficient material, so the consequential decision not to consider the late response stood. The case is a salutary one: once an adjudicator has made a finding on receipt and treated the response as out of time, undoing it on review is hard.

What this means on a project

  • The day you receive the application documents under s 79(4), diary 10 business days. The day you receive the notice of acceptance, diary 7 business days from that date. Work to whichever is the later date — that is your s 83(1) deadline.
  • Keep contemporaneous evidence of when each document was actually received — envelope, email metadata, courier docket, file note. After CMF Projects v Masic, what controls is receipt, not what the claimant’s service clause says was deemed to happen.
  • Don’t serve the response on a registered-office address or PO box that nobody monitors and assume the same indulgence will be extended to you on the inbound documents. If you nominate an address for service, make sure mail to it is actually being collected and dated.
  • Count in business days under the BIF Act definition, not calendar days and not the contract’s “working days”. Public holidays and the Christmas shutdown can compress an already tight 10-day window.
  • Remember s 83(6): once the response is given to the adjudicator, you have 2 business days to give a copy to the claimant. That deadline runs separately from s 83(1) and is easy to overlook in the rush to lodge.
  • If you think the adjudicator has miscalculated your start date, raise it immediately and in writing. After Rise Constructions, an adjudicator’s finding on the date of receipt — even one made on thin evidence — is hard to disturb on review.

The case is the leading Queensland authority for the service / receipt distinction relevant to s 83(1) and is dealt with more fully in the commentary to s 102 (meaning of “receipt” under the Act).
