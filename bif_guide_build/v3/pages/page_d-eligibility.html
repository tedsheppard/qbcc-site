Requirements of an adjudicator’s decision

The adjudicator must be eligible

An adjudicator’s decision is only valid if the person making it is eligible to adjudicate the application. Eligibility is dealt with at the front end of the adjudication process — before the adjudicator accepts the referral — but it stays live throughout, because a disqualifying conflict that emerges later still bites on the decision. Two disqualifying matters are spelt out in the Act: being a party to the contract, and having a conflict of interest prescribed by regulation.

Section 80 BIF Act — when adjudicator ineligible to adjudicate

An adjudicator is not eligible to adjudicate an adjudication application if the adjudicator—

(a) is a party to the construction contract to which the application relates; or

(b) has a conflict of interest as prescribed by regulation.

Party to the contract

The first limb is straightforward. An adjudicator who is a party to the construction contract under which the payment claim arises cannot adjudicate the dispute about that claim. In practice this is rarely an issue on its face — an adjudicator on the QBCC register is unlikely to be a contracting party to the project — but it can surface in less obvious ways. If the adjudicator’s firm or related entity is the contracting party (for example, a consultancy practice that signed the head contract), or if the adjudicator personally signed an associated agreement that forms part of the contractual matrix, the s 80(a) bar is engaged. The disqualification is automatic; it does not depend on actual bias or on the parties objecting.

Conflict of interest prescribed by regulation

The second limb depends on the regulation. The Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Regulation prescribes the categories of conflict that disqualify. Typical prescribed conflicts include a current or recent professional or commercial relationship with a party, a financial interest in the outcome, or a personal relationship that would cause a fair-minded observer to apprehend bias. Because the conflict categories sit in the regulation, they can be amended without amending the Act — so check the current Regulation rather than relying on a remembered list.

Eligibility under s 80 sits alongside the broader common-law obligation on an adjudicator to act impartially and to afford the parties natural justice. The two overlap but are not identical: s 80 sets out the categorical bars to taking the appointment; the natural-justice obligations govern how the adjudicator must conduct the process once on foot. A breach of either will expose the decision to challenge.

Timing — when eligibility is tested

Eligibility is tested at the point the adjudicator accepts the application under s 81, but it is a continuing requirement. If a disqualifying matter emerges mid-adjudication — say, the adjudicator realises a party is a former client, or a prescribed conflict only becomes apparent once the submissions arrive — the adjudicator must withdraw. A decision delivered after a s 80 disqualification has crystallised is liable to be set aside on judicial review for jurisdictional error, because the Act conditions the adjudicator’s authority to decide on their being eligible.

Practice tips

  • When the registrar refers the application and the adjudicator notifies acceptance, check who has been appointed. A quick conflict check on the adjudicator — firm name, recent engagements, any prior dealings with either party — can save a costly judicial review later.
  • If you spot a s 80 issue, raise it in writing with the adjudicator and the registrar promptly. The adjudicator should withdraw and the registrar will refer the application to another adjudicator. Sitting on the point and using it later as a backup ground of challenge is risky — courts may treat delay as waiver of the conduct ground, even if the jurisdictional bar itself cannot be waived.
  • Distinguish s 80 ineligibility from a natural-justice complaint about how the adjudicator has run the process. The former goes to whether the adjudicator could decide at all; the latter goes to whether they decided fairly. They have different remedies and different evidentiary requirements.
  • If you are challenging a decision after the fact on s 80 grounds, you are in jurisdictional-error territory — not an appeal on the merits. Frame the challenge as a Supreme Court application for a declaration that the decision is void, and act quickly.
  • Check the current Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Regulation for the prescribed conflict categories before alleging a s 80(b) breach. The list is set by regulation and may have been updated since you last looked.
  1. Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 (Qld), s 80. The two limbs — party to the contract (s 80(a)) and prescribed conflict of interest (s 80(b)) — are cumulative grounds of ineligibility, either of which is sufficient to disqualify.
  2. Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Regulation 2018 (Qld) prescribes the conflict-of-interest categories for the purposes of s 80(b). Consult the current Regulation for the operative list.
