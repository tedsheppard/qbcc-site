Requirements of a payment claim

The work must be construction work or related goods and services

A construction contract only attracts the BIF Act regime if what you are doing under it is “construction work” or the supply of “related goods and services”. The two terms are defined separately at s 65 and s 66, and together they set the outer boundary of who can claim under the Act.

Sections 65 and 66 BIF Act — construction work and related goods and services

65 Meaning of construction work

(1) Construction work means any of the following work—

(a) the construction, alteration, repair, restoration, maintenance, extension, demolition or dismantling of buildings or structures, whether permanent or not, forming, or to form, part of land;

(b) the construction, alteration, repair, restoration, maintenance, extension, demolition or dismantling of any works forming, or to form, part of land, including walls, roadworks, powerlines, telecommunication apparatus, aircraft runways, docks and harbours, railways, inland waterways, pipelines, reservoirs, water mains, wells, sewers, industrial plant and installations for land drainage or coast protection;

(c) the installation in any building, structure or works of fittings forming, or to form, part of land, including heating, lighting, air-conditioning, ventilation, power supply, drainage, sanitation, water supply, fire protection, security and communications systems;

(d) the external or internal cleaning of buildings, structures and works, so far as it is carried out in the course of their construction, alteration, repair, restoration, maintenance or extension;

(e) any operation that forms an integral part of, or is preparatory to or is for completing, work of the kind referred to in paragraph (a), (b) or (c), including—

(i) site clearance, earthmoving, excavation, tunnelling and boring; and (ii) the laying of foundations; and (iii) the erection, maintenance or dismantling of scaffolding; and (iv) the prefabrication of components to form part of any building, structure or works, whether carried out on-site or off-site; and (v) site restoration, landscaping and the provision of roadways and other access works;

(f) the painting or decorating of the internal or external surfaces of any building, structure or works;

(g) carrying out the testing of soils and road making materials during the construction and maintenance of roads;

(h) any other work of a kind prescribed by regulation.

(2) To remove doubt, it is declared that construction work includes building work within the meaning of the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991.

(3) However, construction work does not include any of the following work—

(a) the drilling for, or extraction of, oil or natural gas;

(b) the extraction, whether by underground or surface working, of minerals, including tunnelling or boring, or constructing underground works, for that purpose.

66 Meaning of related goods and services

(1) Related goods and services, in relation to construction work, means any of the following—

(a) goods of the following kind— (i) materials and components to form part of any building, structure or work arising from construction work; (ii) plant or materials (whether supplied by sale, hire or otherwise) for use in connection with the carrying out of construction work;

(b) services of the following kind— (i) the provision of labour to carry out construction work; (ii) architectural, design, surveying or quantity surveying services relating to construction work; (iii) building, engineering, interior or exterior decoration or landscape advisory services relating to construction work; (iv) soil testing services relating to construction work;

(c) goods and services, relating to construction work, of a kind prescribed by regulation.

(2) In this chapter, a reference to related goods and services includes a reference to related goods or services.

How the courts read these definitions

A single contract can mix “construction work” with work that isn’t. The Act applies to the part that qualifies. The starting point in Queensland is that the s 65 and s 66 definitions get a “natural” reading — not a strained pro-claimant or pro-respondent one. In Capricorn Quarries Pty Ltd v Inline Communication Construction Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 388; [2013] 2 Qd R 1, Jackson J rejected the argument that the predecessor Act should be given a “remedial” or liberal construction. His Honour held:

The language chosen by Parliament to define “construction contract”, “construction work” and “related goods and services” has no purpose other than to draw the line between who is in and who is out of those classes. There seems to be little logic in seeking to stretch that language either way.

The practical takeaway is that you can’t expand “construction work” just because the Act is protective of contractors, and a respondent can’t shrink it just because the Act imposes obligations. Read the words as written.

“Forming, or to form, part of land”

Most paragraphs of s 65(1) hinge on whether the building, structure or works form, or are to form, part of land. The leading Queensland authority is J & D Rigging Pty Ltd v Agripower Australia Ltd [2013] QCA 406, where the Court of Appeal rejected attempts to import common law fixtures concepts. Applegarth J (Holmes JA and Boddice J agreeing) held that the inquiry is a “practical inquiry into the physical relationship between an item and land” — what matters is the present physical state of things, not the parties’ subjective intention years earlier and not the law of fixtures. Degree of annexation is relevant; ownership rules are not.1 The phrase has been treated as one “of wide meaning”,2 and there is no requirement that the works be intended to be permanent.3

Preparatory and integral operations — s 65(1)(e)

Paragraph (e) sweeps in operations that are preparatory to, integral to, or for completing, work in paragraphs (a)–(c). It only catches an operation because of its close connection to that other construction work, so the connection to land in (a)–(c) carries through.4 Site clearing and grubbing fall within (e)(i),5 and prefabricated components — for example a shiploader and tripper assembled off-site for installation onto a wharf — fall within (e)(iv).6

The mining and resources carve-out — s 65(3)

Section 65(3) excludes drilling/extraction of oil or natural gas, and extraction of minerals (including tunnelling, boring or underground works “for that purpose”). Coal counts as a mineral.7 The exclusion is read narrowly: it is the actual process of extraction that is carved out, not work that is merely preparatory to opening or operating a mine. Work done to open up a mine, or to construct access to underground levels before extraction begins, has been held to remain within “construction work”.8

Related goods and services — the “for use in connection with” test

Section 66 picks up materials, plant, labour and a defined list of professional services. The phrase “for use in connection with the carrying out of construction work” in s 66(1)(a)(ii) is purposive: it has to be satisfied at the outset of the transaction, by reference to what the plant or materials were intended to be used for at the time of the contract or supply — not just by what happened to be done with them later.9 Architectural and project management services have been accepted as “related”,10 but a bare licence to use design drawings has not.11 Plant and materials a contractor orders simply to put itself in a position to perform — and the cost of demobilising equipment off site — have also been held to fall outside both s 65 and s 66.12

What this means on a project

  • If your scope is mainstream building, civil or fit-out work that ends up attached to land, you are squarely within s 65. Treat the question as settled and move to the next element.
  • For mixed contracts (e.g. construction plus pure equipment supply, or construction plus a software licence), separate the qualifying scope from the rest before you frame the payment claim. Only the qualifying scope can be claimed under the Act.
  • For off-site prefabrication, accept that fabrication of components destined for the works is captured by s 65(1)(e)(iv) — you don’t lose BIF Act coverage just because the work happens in a yard rather than on site.
  • On resources projects, draw the line carefully. Roads, dams, accommodation, processing plants and access tunnels built before extraction are likely to be construction work; the actual extraction of coal, minerals, oil or gas is not.
  • For plant hire, supply or design services, identify the construction project the supply is “for” at the time of the deal. If the connection to a specific construction project can’t be shown at the contract date, s 66 may not be available.
  • Don’t pad a payment claim with items that aren’t construction work or related goods and services (e.g. demobilisation costs, standalone IP licences). Including non-qualifying amounts won’t kill the whole claim, but it gives the respondent and adjudicator easy ground to cut value.
