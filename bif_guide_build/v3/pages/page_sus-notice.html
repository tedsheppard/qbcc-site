Statutory suspension of works

Notice of intention to suspend (2 BD)

The right to walk off site under the BIF Act is not self-executing. Before you can lawfully suspend, you must first give the respondent a written notice of your intention to suspend, and then wait at least 2 business days. The notice has to do two things at once: it must come from a triggering non-payment event under s 78 or s 92, and it must say on its face that it is given under this Act. Get either of those wrong and the suspension that follows is just a breach of contract.

Section 78(3)–(4) BIF Act — notice after non-payment of a payment claim

(3) In addition to the action mentioned in subsection (2), the claimant may give the respondent written notice of the claimant’s intention to suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under the relevant construction contract under section 98.

(4) The notice to suspend work must state that it is made under this Act.

Section 92 BIF Act — notice after non-payment of an adjudicated amount

(1) If the respondent fails to pay the whole or any part of the adjudicated amount to the claimant as required under section 90, the claimant may give the respondent written notice of the claimant’s intention to suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under the construction contract under section 98.

(2) The notice about suspending work must state that it is made under this Act.

What the notice has to do

The notice is the gateway to the suspension right in s 98. Section 98(1) only allows you to down tools once at least 2 business days have passed since you gave the respondent notice of intention to do so “under section 78 or 92”. Two consequences follow. First, you cannot give a valid notice in the abstract — it has to be anchored to either a s 78 trigger (non-payment of an amount owed under a payment claim, on or before the due date) or a s 92 trigger (non-payment of the whole or part of an adjudicated amount as required under s 90). Second, the notice itself has to state that it is made under this Act. That sentence is not optional dressing; it is what converts an ordinary commercial letter into a statutory notice that engages the s 98 right.

The clearest way to comply is to spell out the trigger and the statutory basis on the face of the document — for example, “This is a notice given under s 78(3) of the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 (Qld) of the claimant’s intention to suspend carrying out construction work under the construction contract under s 98 of that Act.” A notice that is silent on the Act, or that is framed only as a contractual default notice, runs the obvious risk of being treated as something other than the statutory notice the Act requires.

The 2 business day clock

Section 98(1) requires “at least 2 business days” to have passed since the notice was given. The day the notice is given is day zero; the next business day is day one. Suspension cannot lawfully begin until at least the day after day two — in practical terms, you should not suspend earlier than the third business day after the day of the notice. Weekends and Queensland public holidays do not count as business days, and neither do the days in the statutory end-of-year shutdown period under the Act. If your notice goes out late on a Friday afternoon before a public holiday Monday, the earliest you can stop work is materially later than “in two days’ time”.

What “payment” means for the trigger

Whether the s 78 or s 92 trigger has actually been met turns on whether the respondent has “paid” the relevant amount. The leading reading of that word in this context is Beckhaus Civil Pty Ltd v Brewarrina Shire Council (No 2) [2004] NSWSC 1160, where Master Macready treated “to pay” as the act of a debtor transferring by way of satisfaction whatever is owed to a creditor:

To give (a person) what is due in discharge of a debt, or as a return for services done, or goods received, or in compensation for injury done; to remunerate, recompense.” In turn, ‘payment’ is defined as “[t]he action, or an act, of paying.” From this it can readily be concluded that the touchstone of the ordinary and natural meaning of the expressions ‘to pay’ and ‘receives payment’ in the present context is the act of a debtor transferring by way of satisfaction whatever is owed by him or her to a creditor.

The point matters for the notice because money tendered on conditions — for example, against a secured bank guarantee, or with strings that prevent the claimant obtaining the benefit of the funds — is unlikely to amount to “payment” for s 78 or s 92 purposes. If the respondent has wired the amount but locked the claimant out of using it, the trigger for the notice is still alive.1

Once the notice is given, when does the right end?

The notice is the entry point, but its effect runs through to the duration of the suspension itself. Section 98(2) ties the right to suspend to the day the claimant receives payment of the amount mentioned in s 78(1) or s 92(1), with a further 3 business days’ tail. The appellate gloss on what counts as the end of the right is in On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163, where Crowe AJ held that, following an adjudication determination in favour of a claimant, the right to suspend continues until either the outstanding amount is set aside or there is an unequivocal communication acknowledging discharge of the payment claim, adjudicated amount or provisional judgment debt.2 The practical message for the claimant drafting the notice is to be precise about which trigger is engaged, because the trigger you nominate is the trigger you will need to show has been discharged before your suspension right falls away.

What this means on a project

  • You cannot lawfully suspend without first giving the s 78 or s 92 notice and waiting out the 2 business day period in s 98(1). A “suspension” without that notice is just a walk-off and a likely breach of contract.
  • State on the face of the notice that it is made under the BIF Act, and identify the trigger — either non-payment of the amount owed under s 78(1), or non-payment of the adjudicated amount under s 92(1). Don’t leave the statutory character of the notice to inference.
  • Diary the 2 business day period carefully. The day of the notice is day zero, weekends and Queensland public holidays do not count, and the end-of-year shutdown period drops out as well.
  • If the respondent has tendered money on conditions that stop you obtaining the benefit of it, you have probably not been “paid” for s 78 or s 92 purposes — the trigger for the notice is still live. Get advice before treating a conditional tender as discharging the trigger.
  • Keep proof of how and when the notice was given. The validity of any later suspension turns on whether the 2 business day clock actually started, which in turn requires you to show the date the notice was given to the respondent.
  1. Beckhaus Civil Pty Ltd v Brewarrina Shire Council (No 2) [2004] NSWSC 1160 (Master Macready) — ordinary meaning of “pay” and “payment” in the security of payment context; conditional tender does not necessarily amount to payment where the claimant cannot obtain the benefit of the funds.
  2. On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163 (Crowe AJ) — on the equivalent of s 98(1), the right to suspend following non-payment of an adjudicated amount continues until the amount is set aside or there is an unequivocal communication acknowledging discharge, with the further 3 business day tail running from that point.
