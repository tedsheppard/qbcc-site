Statutory suspension of works

Trigger — failure to pay scheduled amount

The right to suspend work under the BIF Act is not free-standing. It hangs off s 78 — the section that tells you what your options are when a respondent doesn’t pay an “amount owed” on a payment claim. Before you can serve a notice of intention to suspend under s 98, you need to be in the position s 78 contemplates: a payment claim has been made, the due date has passed, and the respondent hasn’t paid the amount owed in full. This page deals with the trigger where the respondent did issue a payment schedule but then failed to pay the scheduled amount by the due date.

Section 78(2)(a) BIF Act — recovery of unpaid scheduled amount

(1) This section applies if a respondent given a payment claim for a progress payment does not pay the amount owed to the claimant in full on or before the due date for the progress payment.

(2) The claimant may either—

(a) recover the unpaid portion of the amount owed from the respondent, as a debt owing to the claimant, in a court of competent jurisdiction; or

(b) apply for adjudication of the payment claim under part 4.

(3) In addition to the action mentioned in subsection (2), the claimant may give the respondent written notice of the claimant’s intention to suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under the relevant construction contract under section 98.

(5) In this section—

amount owed, to a claimant for a payment claim, means—

(a) if the respondent did not respond to the payment claim with a payment schedule as required under section 76—the amount claimed under the payment claim; or

(b) if the respondent did respond to the payment claim with a payment schedule as required to do so under section 76—the amount proposed to be paid under the payment schedule.

What the trigger looks like

The scheduled-amount trigger has three moving parts. First, you served a valid payment claim under s 75. Second, the respondent responded with a payment schedule under s 76, and that schedule proposed to pay some figure — often less than what you claimed, but a positive figure all the same. Third, the due date for the progress payment came and went and the respondent didn’t pay the scheduled amount in full. At that point s 78(1) is engaged, and s 78(5)(b) tells you the “amount owed” for the purposes of the section is the amount the respondent itself proposed to pay. That is the unpaid figure you can recover as a debt under s 78(2)(a), pursue in adjudication under s 78(2)(b), and use as the foundation for a notice of intention to suspend under s 78(3) and s 98.

The point worth holding onto is that the respondent has, by issuing the payment schedule, conceded the scheduled amount as payable. There is no live dispute about that figure — the dispute, if any, is about the gap between the claim and the schedule. So the s 78(2)(a) debt remedy on a scheduled amount is, in practice, a strong claim: you are recovering money the respondent has admitted it owes.

The election in s 78(2) and the suspension overlay

Section 78(2) presents the two recovery routes — debt action or adjudication — as alternatives. The leading appellate analysis is Seymour Whyte Constructions Pty Ltd v Ostwald Bros Pty Ltd (In liquidation) [2019] NSWCA 11, decided on the materially identical NSW provision. Ostwald had lodged an adjudication application that turned out to be invalid, and then sought to recover the scheduled amount as a debt due. Seymour Whyte argued the two limbs of s 78(2) were mutually exclusive and that, by going to adjudication first, Ostwald had elected and was locked out of the debt route. The Court of Appeal accepted the election principle in general terms — once an adjudication application has actually been “made”, the claimant cannot also pursue the unpaid scheduled amount as a debt — but held that an invalid application is not a “made” application for these purposes. So Ostwald was still entitled to debt recovery under the equivalent of s 78(2)(a).

where a claimant makes an adjudication application they are subsequently precluded from attempting, in addition, to recover the unpaid portion of the scheduled amount from the respondent as a debt due to the claimant.

The suspension right in s 78(3) sits outside that election. It is expressed as “in addition to” the action mentioned in s 78(2), so choosing the debt route or the adjudication route does not consume the right to give a notice of intention to suspend. You can sue for the scheduled amount and serve a s 98 notice; you can lodge an adjudication application and serve a s 98 notice. The trigger for the notice is the same underlying failure to pay the amount owed by the due date.

Defending the debt claim — what doesn’t work

Because the scheduled amount has been admitted by the respondent, defences to a s 78(2)(a) debt claim are narrow. The point is illustrated by Marques Group Pty Ltd v Parkview Constructions Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 625, where Rees J considered a respondent’s defence on a summary judgment application brought under the NSW equivalent of s 78(2)(a). Parkview argued the claimant had engaged in misleading and deceptive conduct by stating in the statutory declaration accompanying the payment claim that subcontractors had been paid (which was untrue), and that but for that conduct it would have scheduled nil. Rees J described the defence as “inherently unattractive” and as appearing to “strike at the heart” of the security of payment scheme — the very point of the scheme is that having proposed a figure to pay, you pay it — but found that on the strict summary-judgment threshold she could not say it was “so clearly untenable that it cannot possibly succeed”. The application for summary judgment was struck out at [20]. The takeaway for a claimant is that misleading-conduct style defences will rarely succeed at trial, but they are difficult to dispose of summarily; budget for the time and cost.

Court of competent jurisdiction

If you choose the debt route, s 78(2)(a) directs you to a “court of competent jurisdiction”. In Queensland, that means the Magistrates Court, District Court or Supreme Court depending on the size of the unpaid scheduled amount.1 The dollar figure in the payment schedule sets your jurisdiction; you don’t need to add interest or costs to clear a threshold.

What this means on a project

  • Check the basics first. There must be a valid payment claim under s 75, a payment schedule under s 76, and a due date that has passed without the scheduled amount being paid in full. If any one of those is missing, s 78 is not engaged and the suspension trigger is not available on this footing.
  • Identify the “amount owed” precisely. Under s 78(5)(b) it is the amount the respondent proposed to pay in its schedule, not the amount you originally claimed. That figure drives the debt action, the adjudication application, and the notice of intention to suspend.
  • The suspension right under s 78(3) is “in addition to” the recovery routes in s 78(2). You don’t lose the ability to serve a s 98 notice by choosing to sue, or by going to adjudication.
  • Don’t run a debt action and an adjudication for the same scheduled amount in parallel. Once an adjudication application has been validly made, the s 78(2)(a) debt route is closed for that amount; only an invalid application leaves the debt route open.
  • If you are the respondent and the cash isn’t there to pay the scheduled amount by the due date, expect the worst combination — a debt claim or an adjudication for the admitted figure, plus a notice of intention to suspend on top. The defences are narrow because you have already conceded the figure in your own schedule.
  1. Northside Roofing Pty Ltd v Pires Constructions Pty Ltd [2007] QDC 172 (Durward SC DCJ) — on the BCIPA predecessor to s 78, the only courts of competent jurisdiction to enforce the statutory debt are the Magistrates Court, District Court and Supreme Court.
  2. Kyle Bay Removals Pty Ltd v Dynabuild Project Services Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 334 (Meagher JA) — where no valid payment claim has been served, liability to pay does not attach and there can be no binding election between the two limbs of s 78(2). A premature court action on an invalid claim does not preclude a later adjudication when a valid claim is made.
  3. Canterbury-Bankstown Council v Payce Communities Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 1419 (Henry J) — running court proceedings under the NSW equivalent of s 78 while the dispute is also before the Supreme Court is not, without more, an abuse of process; the threshold is “exceptional circumstances” and the onus on the respondent is “a heavy one”.
