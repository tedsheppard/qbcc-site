Requirements of an adjudication response

Confined to reasons in the payment schedule

The adjudication response is your chance to argue your case to the adjudicator — but only on the reasons you already put in the payment schedule. Section 82(4) draws a hard line: any reason for withholding payment that wasn’t in the schedule when you served it is a “new reason” and must be left out. Get this wrong and the adjudicator can require you to resubmit, can disregard the new material, or — if they consider new reasons anyway — the determination can be set aside for jurisdictional error.

Section 82 BIF Act — adjudication response (subsections (2)–(5))

(2) However, the respondent must not give an adjudication response if the respondent failed to give the claimant a payment schedule as required under s 76.

(3) The adjudication response—

(a) must be in writing; and

(b) must identify the adjudication application to which it relates; and

(c) may include the submissions relevant to the response the respondent chooses to include.

(4) However, the adjudication response must not include any reasons (new reasons) for withholding payment that were not included in the payment schedule when given to the claimant.

(5) The adjudicator may require the respondent to resubmit the adjudication response without the new reasons.

Why the rule exists

The point of s 82(4) is fairness on a tight timetable. Once the payment schedule is served, the claimant decides whether to apply for adjudication based on the reasons it discloses. If the respondent could spring fresh reasons in the response, the claimant would be ambushed — and the Act does not give the claimant a right of reply. The leading statement is from Sackar J in State Water Corporation v Civil Team Engineering Pty Ltd [2013] NSWSC 1879, who emphasised the “obvious and powerful policy considerations in ensuring that the respondent discloses all of its reasons for non-payment in its payment schedule”, given that the claimant has no right to reply to whatever the respondent files in adjudication.1

Submissions vs new reasons

The prohibition is on new reasons, not on new submissions. You can elaborate, amplify and add evidence in support of a reason already stated in the schedule — you just can’t introduce a fresh basis for non-payment. McDougall J’s formulation in TQM Design & Construct Pty Ltd v Dasein Constructions Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 1216 has been adopted in Queensland and remains the working test:

[30] a respondent cannot include in its adjudication response any reason for withholding payment that has not been included in its payment schedule. However, it would be open to a respondent to argue, either in greater detail or with perhaps more supporting evidence, reasons that had been advanced… the adjudication response may do more than parrot the terms of the payment schedule.

That distinction explains why expert reports, spreadsheets and other supporting documents can usually be included in the response even if they weren’t attached to the schedule, provided they go to a reason already raised. In Syntech Resources Pty Ltd v Peter Campbell Earthmoving (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 293, Daubney J held that “submissions” in the BCIPA equivalent of s 82(3)(c) was not to be construed narrowly and could include relevant documentation in support — the spreadsheets in that case were “explanatory” of reasons already in the schedule, not new reasons.2 The same principle let in the expert report in Trysams Pty Ltd v Club Constructions (NSW) Pty Ltd [2007] NSWSC 941, where the report was “foreshadowed” in the schedule and “did not represent any shift from that position”.3 Where new material does cross the line into raising a fresh reason, the position flips: in Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 307, Philip McMurdo J was unable to find that an EOT expert report did not raise a reason not in the schedule, and treated it accordingly.4

“Reasons for withholding payment” is read broadly

Don’t look for a narrow reading of what counts as a “reason for withholding payment”. In John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads & Traffic Authority of New South Wales & Ors [2007] NSWCA 19, Hodgson JA held that any basis on which the respondent says it doesn’t have to pay an amount — non-performance of work, defective work, set-offs, cross-claims, contractual caps, statutory limits, “or indeed any other suggested justification” — is a reason for withholding payment and must be in the schedule. Anything else, his Honour said, would “do violence to the language ‘withholding payment for any reason’, and be contrary to the plain purpose” of the prohibition. Philip McMurdo J adopted that reasoning in Wiggins Island for the Queensland Act.5

Jurisdictional points are different

Section 82(4) bars new reasons for withholding payment. It does not bar arguments going to whether the adjudicator has jurisdiction at all — for instance, that there is no reference date, that the claim is out of time, or that no construction contract exists. Those points couldn’t sensibly have been put in a payment schedule and can be raised in the response.6 The line between a “jurisdictional” point and a substantive reason has to be watched: in Watkins Contracting Pty Ltd v Hyatt Ground Engineering Pty Ltd [2018] QSC 65, Brown J accepted that detailed termination submissions in the response were an elaboration of a “no reference date” reason already in the schedule, and noted in obiter that even if they had been new, they were directed at jurisdiction and the s 82(4) bar would not have applied.

The consequences of getting it wrong

Where an adjudicator considers new reasons that should have been excluded, the affected part of the determination is liable to be declared void. In Acciona Agua Australia Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 133, Bond J found jurisdictional error where the adjudicator accepted set-off bases tied to alleged breaches of an implied contractual term that had “no mention” in the payment schedule; part of the determination was declared void and the balance left to stand. Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 92 (Martin J) is to similar effect, where the adjudicator applied a contractual clause raised for the first time in the response.7 But characterisation is for the adjudicator in the first instance: in Baguley Build Pty Ltd v Olcon Concrete & Construction Pty Ltd [2025] QSC 126, Copley J confirmed that whether reasons are “new” is a matter for the adjudicator to determine, and an error in mischaracterising submissions is an error within jurisdiction, not a ground for setting the determination aside.8

What this means on a project

  • Treat the payment schedule as your final pleading on reasons. Anything you might want to argue at adjudication — defective work, descope, set-off, cross-claim, contractual cap, time bar, valuation dispute — needs to be on the face of the schedule. There is no second bite.
  • You can amplify and support those reasons in the response with extra detail, calculations, photos and expert reports. You cannot use the response to add a basis for non-payment that the schedule didn’t flag.
  • Before lodging the response, run a line-by-line comparison with the schedule. For each reason in the response, ask: is this reason — not just this evidence — in the schedule? If not, take it out, or be prepared to defend it as either an elaboration or a jurisdictional point.
  • Expert reports and spreadsheets are usually fine if they go to a reason already raised. Check that the schedule actually flagged the underlying reason — e.g., if you want to run an EOT-based set-off in the response, the schedule needs to have raised the EOT issue, not just a generic “we dispute the claim” line.
  • Jurisdictional objections (no reference date, no construction contract, claim out of time, multiple applications) can be run in the response even if they weren’t in the schedule. Plead them clearly as jurisdictional, not as substantive reasons for withholding payment.
  • If you are the claimant and the response includes new reasons, draw the adjudicator’s attention to s 82(4) and ask that the response be resubmitted under s 82(5) without them, or that the new reasons be disregarded.
