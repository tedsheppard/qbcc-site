Requirements of an adjudication application

Within time (windows depend on trigger)

Once the entitlement to apply for adjudication has crystallised, you don’t have long. Section 79(2)(b) sets three different time windows, and the one that applies depends on which trigger you’re running on — no payment schedule, short-paid schedule, or short schedule. Miss the window and you can’t apply at all.

Section 79(2) BIF Act — time for an adjudication application

(2) An adjudication application—

(a) must be in the approved form; and

(b) must be made within—

(i) for an application relating to a failure to give a payment schedule and pay the full amount stated in the payment claim—30 business days after the later of the following days—

(A) the day of the due date for the progress payment to which the claim relates;

(B) the last day the respondent could have given the payment schedule under section 76; or

(ii) for an application relating to a failure to pay the full amount stated in the payment schedule—20 business days after the due date for the progress payment to which the claim relates; or

(iii) for an application relating to the amount stated in the payment schedule being less than the amount stated in the payment claim—30 business days after the claimant receives the payment schedule; and

(c) must identify the payment claim and the payment schedule, if any, to which it relates; and

(d) must be accompanied by the fee prescribed by regulation for the application.

Pick the right window

Three triggers, three deadlines. The first thing to do is identify which subparagraph of s 79(2)(b) you’re inside, because the calculation method differs:

  • No payment schedule and no payment — s 79(2)(b)(i): 30 business days after the later of (A) the due date for the progress payment, or (B) the last day the respondent could have given a payment schedule under s 76. The “later of” mechanic matters: if your contract has a long payment period, the due date can fall after the s 76 schedule deadline, and you need to use whichever of the two lands later.
  • Payment schedule given but full scheduled amount not paid — s 79(2)(b)(ii): 20 business days after the due date for the progress payment. This is the shortest window and it’s easy to miss — the trigger here is non-payment of an amount the respondent has already accepted.
  • Payment schedule short-pays the claim — s 79(2)(b)(iii): 30 business days after the claimant receives the payment schedule. The clock runs from receipt, not from service — the Act distinguishes the two, and the receipt date is the one that counts.

All three are measured in business days under the BIF Act definition, not calendar days. Diary the deadline as soon as the trigger event occurs.

The deadline is mandatory — an out-of-time application is no application

The leading Queensland authority on the consequence of missing the s 79(2)(b) window is De Neefe Signs Pty Ltd v Build1 (Qld) Pty Ltd; Traffic Technologies Traffic Hire Pty Ltd v Build1 (Qld) Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 279, where Fryberg J held that the equivalent timing requirement in the predecessor Act was an essential precondition to the existence of an adjudication application at all. His Honour’s reasoning, which has been applied to s 79(2) of the BIF Act, was that the natural meaning of the words “an adjudication application … cannot be made” produces a jurisdictional result:

If by statute an application cannot be made, then anything purporting to be one cannot be an application. The existence of an application is a basic and essential requirement of an adjudicator’s determination.

The same conclusion was reached, on different reasoning, in Nebmas Pty Ltd v Sub Divide Pty Ltd & Ors [2009] QSC 92, where McMurdo J held that the time limit in the predecessor provision used “emphatic” language equivalent to the “cannot be made” formula and was therefore an essential requirement.1 The practical consequence is that an adjudicator who proceeds on an out-of-time application has no jurisdiction, and the determination will be quashed if challenged.

Picking the wrong trigger can save you

A more forgiving line of authority deals with claimants who, in good faith, treat an ambiguous communication as something other than a payment schedule and apply on a different trigger. In Gisley Investments Pty Ltd v Williams & Anor [2010] QSC 178, the claimant was originally required to apply by 3 December 2009 on the assumption that a “relatively informal” email from the respondent was a payment schedule. Treating it instead as no schedule at all, the claimant didn’t apply until 1 February 2010, well after the (b)(iii) window would have closed. Douglas J upheld the validity of the resulting determination, reasoning that the imprecision in the respondent’s own email had caused the difficulty, and the real objects of the Act — notice of the claim and an opportunity to respond — had been met.2 The lesson is narrow but useful: where the trigger itself is genuinely uncertain because of the respondent’s conduct, the courts will not necessarily kill the application on a strict timing point.

What this means on a project

  • The day the trigger event happens, identify which subparagraph of s 79(2)(b) you’re running on and diary the deadline immediately. Don’t wait until you’ve briefed lawyers — the 20 business day window under (b)(ii) can disappear quickly.
  • For the (b)(i) “no schedule, no payment” window, calculate both the due-date deadline and the s 76 schedule deadline, and use whichever is later. Don’t pick the earlier one and lose time you’re entitled to.
  • For the (b)(iii) short-paid schedule window, the clock runs from receipt, not from service. Keep evidence of when the schedule actually came into your hands — receipt is the date that controls.
  • Count in business days, and be careful around public holidays and the Christmas shutdown. The Act’s business-day definition is not the same as “working days” under your contract.
  • If the respondent has sent you something that looks ambiguous — an email that could be a schedule or could be informal correspondence — take advice early. Gisley is a forgiving authority but you don’t want to be relying on it.
  • If you miss the deadline, don’t lodge anyway in the hope it slides through. After De Neefe the application is a nullity and you’ll wear the registrar’s fee, the adjudicator’s fees and likely the costs of a successful challenge.
