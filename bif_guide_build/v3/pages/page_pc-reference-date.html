Requirements of a payment claim

A valid reference date must have arisen

Every payment claim under the BIF Act has to be tied to a “reference date”. If no reference date has arisen, or if you have already used the only one available, the document you serve is not a payment claim under the Act and any adjudication that follows is liable to be set aside for want of jurisdiction.

Section 67 BIF Act — meaning of reference date

(1) A reference date, for a construction contract, means—

(a) a date stated in, or worked out under, the contract as the date on which a claim for a progress payment may be made for construction work carried out, or related goods and services supplied, under the contract; or

(b) if the contract does not provide for the matter—

(i) the last day of the month in which the construction work was first carried out, or the related goods and services were first supplied, under the contract; and

(ii) the last day of each later month.

(2) However, if a construction contract is terminated and the contract does not provide for, or purports to prevent, a reference date surviving beyond termination, the final reference date for the contract is the date the contract is terminated.

The two routes to a reference date

Section 67 gives you a contractual route and a statutory default. Under s 67(1)(a), if the contract states a date or supplies a formula from which a date can be calculated, that is your reference date. Under s 67(1)(b), if the contract is silent, the default applies: the last day of the month in which work was first carried out, and the last day of every later month. Section 67(2) then provides a final reference date on termination, where the contract itself doesn’t deal with survival.

“Worked out under” the contract means worked out by applying facts to a formula in the contract — not construed from the words alone. A clause that ties the reference date to a milestone, the submission of a document, or another post-formation event is permissible, even if that event is not certain to occur. What you cannot do is hide a payment-claim entitlement behind a contractual condition that effectively prevents a reference date arising at all; s 200 of the BIF Act will strike that down.1

Lean Field Developments

The clearest exposition is Applegarth J’s judgment in Lean Field Developments Pty Ltd v E & I Global Solutions (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor [2014] QSC 293. His Honour confirmed that “worked out” carries its ordinary meaning of a process of calculation, and that parties are free to agree a formula that operates on post-contract conduct or events:

[30] The ordinary meaning of “worked out” in this context connotes a process of calculation… the statutory definition of “reference date” would seem to allow a reference date to be worked out by applying a formula to facts that are capable of being ascertained… The first and subsequent reference dates are worked out by reference to the conduct of the contractor after the formation of the contract in commencing work.

The corollary, also from Lean Field, is that a clause that makes the reference date illusory — for example, by deferring it indefinitely or making it conditional on something one party will never trigger — can be invalidated under s 200. The contract clause is read against that statutory backstop.

One reference date, one payment claim

You only get to claim once per reference date. A clause that “deems” an early payment claim to have been served on the next reference date does not save a premature claim — the date is the date.2 If you serve before a reference date has accrued, the document is not a payment claim under the Act, and an adjudicator who proceeds on it acts without jurisdiction.

The same single-shot rule applies after termination. Section 67(2) gives you a final reference date on the day the contract ends, but only one payment claim can be made off it. A subcontractor who has already served a post-termination claim cannot serve another one and run it to adjudication.3

The default in s 67(1)(b)

If the contract is silent, the default reference date is the last day of the month. For that date to arise, some construction work has to have been done in that month — you can’t generate a reference date out of an idle month.4 And if an adjudicator has to perform “surgical intervention” on the contract’s wording to spell out a reference date, the contract is treated as not providing for the matter, and the default applies.5

Practice tips

  • Before serving a payment claim, identify the reference date you are claiming on. Write it on the front of the claim. If you can’t identify one, don’t serve.
  • If your contract states a reference date (e.g. “the 25th of each month”), serve on or after that date — never before. Deeming clauses don’t cure an early claim.
  • If the contract is silent, you’re on the s 67(1)(b) default: last day of the month, and only if you actually did work that month.
  • One reference date, one claim. Don’t double-up by serving two claims against the same reference date — the second is not a payment claim under the Act and any adjudication of it is void.
  • If the contract has been terminated, you have one final reference date under s 67(2) and one final payment claim. Make it count; you won’t get another.
  • Watch for contractual conditions that effectively block a reference date arising. A clause that makes the entitlement illusory is vulnerable to s 200, but it is far cleaner to negotiate it out than to fight about it later.
