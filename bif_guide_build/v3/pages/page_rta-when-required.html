Retention trust accounts

When a retention trust is required

A retention trust is the BIF Act’s mechanism for ring-fencing cash retention held back from progress payments. The regime piggybacks on the project trust framework in Chapter 2: if you are already inside the project trust net, every dollar of cash retention you withhold has to sit in a dedicated trust account, not in your operating funds. The trigger sits in s 32 and pulls its threshold concepts from the eligibility rules for project trusts in s 14 and the carve-outs in subdivision 3.

Section 32 BIF Act — When retention trust required

(1) A retention trust is required for a retention amount withheld from payment under a contract if—

(a) the contract is a withholding contract at the time the retention amount is withheld; and

(b) the retention amount is withheld by the contracting party in the form of cash.

(2) Also, a retention trust is required for a retention amount withheld from payment under a contract if—

(a) the contract was not a withholding contract at the time the retention amount was withheld but the contract has since become a withholding contract; and

(b) the contract is a first tier subcontract; and

(c) the retention amount was withheld by the contracting party in the form of cash; and

(d) the retention amount had not been released to the parties entitled to it at the time the contract became a withholding contract.

(5) This section does not apply to a retention amount withheld from payment under a contract if—

(a) the contracting party is the State, the Commonwealth, a state authority, a local government or another entity prescribed by regulation; or

(b) the contract price for the contract is at least the minimum contract price.

(6) In this section—

withholding contract means—

(a) a project trust contract that is—

(i) a head contract; or

(ii) a subcontract that is eligible for a project trust under section 14C or 14D; or

(b) a project trust subcontract for a project trust contract mentioned in paragraph (a).

The trigger turns on three things

Reading s 32(1) on its own, a retention trust is required when three conditions all hold at the moment cash is held back. The contract has to be a “withholding contract”; the retention has to be in cash (a bank guarantee or insurance bond is outside the section); and the contracting party — the party doing the withholding — has to be the one taking the deduction. The trust is established the moment the cash is withheld (s 33), and the obligation to have an account open at an approved financial institution runs in parallel (s 34).

The work in s 32 is being done by the definition of “withholding contract” in s 32(6). That definition pulls the retention regime onto the same spine as the project trust regime: a withholding contract is either a project trust contract (a head contract, or a subcontract that is eligible under s 14C or s 14D), or a project trust subcontract for one of those head contracts. So a retention trust requirement only ever attaches where a project trust requirement is already in play above it — the two regimes are joined at the hip.

Project value thresholds — inherited from s 14

Because the retention trust trigger keys off project trust contracts, the threshold question collapses back into s 14. A contract is eligible for a project trust if, when it is entered into, more than 50% of the contract price is for project trust work and the contract price is $1 million or more. That same $1 million / 50% gateway is what brings cash retention under a contract into the s 32 net — if the head contract above you is not a project trust contract, no retention trust is required for retention you withhold under it.

Section 32(5) then layers a separate price ceiling on top. The retention trust requirement does not apply if the contract price is “at least the minimum contract price”, which is defined in s 30 as the contract price amount prescribed by regulation. That carve-out is the Act’s way of removing very large contracts from the retention trust regime — the policy view being that on contracts above the prescribed ceiling the parties have other tools (bonds, parent-company guarantees) for managing security.1

Catch-up trusts on contracts that become withholding contracts later

Section 32(2) handles the awkward case of a contract that wasn’t a withholding contract when the cash was first held back, but became one later — for example, because of an amendment that pushes the contract price or scope mix across the s 14 line, or a related-entity subcontract that engages s 14C. If all four limbs of s 32(2) are satisfied (the contract is now a withholding contract, it is a first tier subcontract, the retention was held in cash, and the cash had not yet been released back), a retention trust is required for the existing retention as well. In that situation s 34(2)(b) gives the contracting party 20 business days from the day the contract becomes a withholding contract to open the account, and s 35(2)(b)–(c) sets the deposit timing.

Once the requirement starts, it stays on

Section 32(3) fixes the start date as the first day the contracting party withholds the retention amount from payment. Section 32(4) then locks that requirement in: it continues until all of the retention amount has been released to the parties entitled to it under the withholding contract, regardless of any variation or other amendment of the contract or any change in the contracted work. Releasing scope, dropping the price, or restructuring the work part-way through does not switch the trust off. Only release of the retention to the contracted party does.

Government and prescribed-entity carve-outs

Section 32(5)(a) takes the State, the Commonwealth, state authorities, local governments and other entities prescribed by regulation out of the requirement entirely — but only when one of those entities is the contracting party (i.e. the party withholding). A private-sector head contractor that subcontracts to a government-owned entity is not exempted, because the entity is on the contracted-party side of the deduction. The exemption is for the withholder, not the recipient.

The other set of carve-outs sits one layer up, in the project trust subdivision 3 exemptions that feed into the “withholding contract” definition. If the head contract above you is exempted under s 15A (contracts with prescribed entities), s 15B (contracts only between the State and a state authority), s 15C (small-scale residential construction work for less than 3 living units), s 15D (maintenance-only work) or s 15E (architects, engineers, building designers and landscape architects on advisory, design or contract administration work), the contract is not a project trust contract and therefore not a withholding contract under s 32(6) — so the retention trust requirement never engages. Section 15F — the “less than 90 days to practical completion” carve-out — is in the same family.

What this means on a project

  • If your head contract isn’t a project trust contract, you don’t need a retention trust for cash retention you withhold under it. The retention trust regime sits entirely on top of the project trust regime — s 32(6) makes the link explicit.
  • If your head contract is a project trust contract under s 14 (more than 50% project trust work and contract price of $1 million or more, GST-exclusive), and you withhold cash retention from a subcontractor, the retention trust requirement engages from the day you make the deduction (s 32(3)).
  • Bank guarantees, insurance bonds and similar non-cash security sit outside s 32 — the section only bites on retention “withheld by the contracting party in the form of cash” (s 32(1)(b), s 32(2)(c)). If you switch a project from cash retention to bank guarantees, you can take that contract out of the retention trust regime even though the project trust above it remains.
  • Watch the s 32(2) catch-up. If a contract was below the project trust line at execution but tips over later because of an amendment that engages s 14A or a related-entity subcontract under s 14C, any cash retention already held has to be moved into a retention trust account — you have 20 business days under s 34(2)(b) and the deposit windows in s 35(2) to do it.
  • The s 32(5)(b) ceiling for contracts “at least the minimum contract price” is a regulation-prescribed number; check the current BIF Regulation before assuming a very large contract is exempt.1
  • The exemption for government and prescribed entities under s 32(5)(a) only operates when the government party is the one withholding. A private head contractor subcontracting to a government-owned trading entity gets no benefit from that limb.
  • Once the requirement is on, it stays on. Variations, descopes and price drops don’t end it — only release of the retention to the parties entitled to it under the contract does (s 32(4)).
  • One retention trust account can hold retention amounts withheld under any number of contracts the same contracting party is on (s 34(3)); you don’t need a separate account per project. But that operational economy doesn’t change when the duty to have the account open is triggered.
  1. The “minimum contract price” for s 32(5)(b) is the contract price amount prescribed by regulation: BIF Act s 30 (definition). The figure sits in the BIF Regulation 2018 and is updated from time to time — verify the current prescribed amount before relying on the s 32(5)(b) carve-out.
  2. On the project trust eligibility threshold that feeds the “withholding contract” definition: BIF Act s 14 ($1 million / more than 50% project trust work, tested at contract formation); s 14A (amendments that bring a contract into eligibility post-formation); s 14B (multiple contracts at the same site or adjacent sites taken to be a single contract); s 14C (first-tier subcontracts with related entities); s 14D (contracts of a type prescribed by regulation).
  3. The subdivision 3 carve-outs that take a contract out of the project trust regime — and therefore out of the “withholding contract” definition in s 32(6) — are: s 15A (contracting or contracted party is a prescribed entity); s 15B (contracts between the State and a state authority only); s 15C (small-scale residential construction work for less than 3 living units); s 15D (maintenance work only); s 15E (advisory, design or contract administration work by architects, engineers, building designers and landscape architects); s 15F (less than 90 days between the trigger date and expected practical completion).
  4. On the mechanics flowing from a s 32 trigger: s 33 establishes the trust the moment the cash is withheld; s 33A creates a statutory charge over the retention amount in favour of the contracted party; s 34 obliges the contracting party to open a retention trust account at an approved financial institution (before withholding under s 32(1), or within 20 business days under s 32(2)); s 35 sets the deposit obligation; s 40 requires written notice of the retention trust to the contracted party before withholding.
