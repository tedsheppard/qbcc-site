Project trust accounts

Notifications to QBCC, principal and subcontractors

Once a project trust is required, the trustee’s job isn’t just to open the account and start moving money. Chapter 2 builds a notification scaffolding around the trust: the commissioner has to be told when the trust opens, when it closes and when key things change; subcontractors have to be told that a trust is being used to pay them; and the contracting party has to flag related-entity arrangements. Miss any of these steps and you have a compliance breach sitting on top of an otherwise functioning trust account.

Section 17 BIF Act — establishment of project trust

Once a project trust is required for a contract under section 12, the trust is established by the first of the following being made after the trust is required—

(a) payment of an amount from the contracting party to the contracted party under the contract;

(b) payment of an amount from the contracted party to a subcontractor beneficiary for subcontracted work under the contract;

(c) a deposit in the project trust account as required under this chapter.

Telling the commissioner the trust has opened

The trust is “established” under s 17 by operation of law — you don’t establish it by filing a form. What triggers establishment is the first payment in or out, or the first deposit into the project trust account, after the trust becomes required under s 12. The account itself must be opened within 20 business days after the contracted party enters into the first subcontract for the contract (s 18). Where the trust requirement only kicks in after a contract amendment, the 20 business day clock runs from the day the contract is amended instead.1

The notification to the commissioner that the trust has been established sits in the surrounding administrative provisions of the chapter and is to be given “using an approved way” — in practice, through the QBCC’s online portal. The same approved-way framing appears in the related-entity provisions at s 24 and s 25, which require notification within 5 business days of the contracting party becoming aware of a related-entity subcontractor beneficiary, or the contracted party entering into a subcontract with a related entity. Both are penalty-bearing obligations.

Changes to the trust during the project

The trust requirement, once it starts, “continues until the project trust is dissolved under section 21, regardless of any of the following changes” — a variation or other amendment, a change in the contract price, or a change in the contracted work (s 12(4)). That continuity rule is important: variations don’t reset the obligation, and a price reduction below the eligibility threshold doesn’t end the trust. Where the trust account itself needs to move — for example, the trustee wants to change financial institutions — closing the existing account is not treated as dissolving the trust, provided the closure is for the purpose of transferring to another financial institution under s 18C (s 21(3)).

The contracting party’s related-entity reporting obligation under s 24 is triggered as soon as the party knows, or ought reasonably to know, that a subcontractor beneficiary is a related entity for the contracted party. The 5 business day clock runs from when the party first becomes aware, or ought reasonably to have become aware. The contracted party’s mirror obligation under s 25 runs from entering into the subcontract with the related entity. These are ongoing notification duties, not one-off disclosures at the start of the project.

Closing the trust

Dissolution is tightly controlled. The trustee can only dissolve the trust if there are no longer any subcontractor beneficiaries, or the only remaining work is maintenance work (s 21(1)). Dissolution itself happens in two steps: closing the project trust account, and giving written notice to the commissioner that the trust has been dissolved (s 21(2)). On dissolution, the trustee may pay itself any interest it is entitled to under s 51D, and any amount remaining that isn’t owing to a subcontractor beneficiary (s 21(4)).

Notifying subcontractors that a trust is being used

The contracted party must give every subcontractor a written “notice of project trust” under s 23. The notice has to state that a project trust will be used for making payments to the subcontractor and include the information prescribed by regulation. Timing depends on the sequencing of trust establishment and subcontract entry:

  • If the project trust is not yet established when the subcontract is entered into — within 10 business days after the trust is established.
  • If the project trust is already established when the subcontract is entered into — before the subcontract is entered into.
  • If the trust requirement is triggered by a later contract amendment, and the subcontract was entered into before the amendment — within 10 business days after opening the project trust account.

The penalty for failing to give a compliant notice is 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment, which puts s 23 in the same penalty band as the obligation to pay subcontractors only from the project trust account under s 20. Treat it as a hard, non-negotiable step in the subcontract execution process — not a paperwork formality to catch up on later.

Principal-side information

The contracting party (the principal, or the head contractor where the trust sits at the subcontract layer) carries two distinct information duties. The first is the related-entity report under s 24, discussed above. The second flows from s 19: every payment the contracting party makes to the contracted party in connection with the project trust contract has to be deposited into the project trust account, unless one of the carve-outs applies (payment into court, a payment withholding request under s 97B, a subcontractor’s charge payment under chapter 4, or a reasonable excuse). The principal’s “information” obligation in practical terms is to know the project trust account details and direct payments accordingly — if it pays the contracted party direct in contravention of the deposit obligation, the contracted party then has to deposit the amount into the project trust account as soon as practicable, with the higher penalty (200 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment) attaching to that downstream step.

What this means on a project

  • Diarise the 20 business day clock for opening the project trust account from the date you enter into the first subcontract (s 18(2)). If a contract amendment is what triggered the trust, the clock runs from the day of amendment, not the day of the original first subcontract.
  • Notify the commissioner through the QBCC’s online portal when the trust opens. Use the same channel for related-entity reports under s 24 and s 25 — both have a 5 business day window and both carry penalties.
  • Build the s 23 notice of project trust into your subcontract execution pack. If the trust is already established, the notice has to be given before the subcontract is entered into — you can’t backfill it.
  • Don’t treat variations or price changes as a reason to revisit the trust requirement. Once it’s on, it stays on until s 21 dissolution.
  • When you close the trust, do both steps: close the account and give written notice to the commissioner under s 21(2)(b). Closing the account on its own is not dissolution, and an unnotified closure leaves the trust technically extant.
  • If you are the principal, make sure your accounts payable team has the project trust account details and pays into that account — not the contracted party’s general operating account — for every payment captured by s 19(1).
  1. Section 18(3) BIF Act. The 20 business day rule for opening the account is mandatory; s 18(6) renders of no effect any contract provision that purports to require the account to be opened less than 20 business days after the contract is entered into. The maximum penalty for failing to open the account as required is 500 penalty units (s 18(1)).
  2. Section 24 BIF Act (contracting party to report related entities — 50 penalty units); s 25 BIF Act (contracted party to report related-entity subcontracts — 200 penalty units). Both obligations are discharged “using an approved way”, which in practice is the QBCC online portal.
  3. Section 23(2)(c) BIF Act requires the notice of project trust to include “the information prescribed by regulation” in addition to the statutory statement that a project trust will be used. The penalty for failing to give a compliant notice is 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment (s 23(1)).
