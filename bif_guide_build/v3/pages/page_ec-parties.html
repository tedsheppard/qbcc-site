Enforcing a subcontractor’s charge

Necessary parties / joinder

A proceeding to enforce a subcontractor’s charge under Chapter 4 of the BIF Act is not a simple debt claim. It has to deliver an order under s 132 that binds the people actually holding, owing or contesting the money — otherwise the order can’t do its job of releasing the security or the funds paid into court. Getting the parties right at the start is therefore as important as getting the notice of claim right at the start of the charge process.

Section 131 BIF Act — use of security where contractor disputes the claim

131 Use of security for benefit of subcontractor if contractor does not accept liability for all claims

(1) This section applies if—

(a) a subcontractor gives a notice of claim to a person under section 122 because the person is obliged to pay money to a contractor under a contract; and

(b) the subcontractor gives a copy of the notice of claim to the contractor under section 123; and

(c) in the contractor’s response to the claim under section 128, the contractor disputes the claim (does not accept liability to pay the amount claimed); and

(d) the unsatisfied amount for the contract is more than the retained amount for the contract.

(2) The holder of a security for the contract must—

(a) retain the security until the court in which the subcontractor’s claim is heard makes an order under section 132 about enforcing the subcontractor’s charge over the security; or

(b) instead of retaining the security—

(i) if the security is held as an amount of money—pay the amount, up to the difference amount for the contract, into court; or

(ii) if the security is not held as an amount of money but may be converted into an amount of money—convert the security, wholly or partly, into an amount of money and pay the amount, up to the difference amount for the contract, into court.

(3) If the holder of the security does not comply with subsection (2), the holder is personally liable to pay the subcontractor the amount of the subcontractor’s claim to the extent that the security would have been capable under this chapter, if the holder had complied with the subsection, of satisfying the claim.

(7) An amount paid into court under subsection (2)(b) may be paid out only under an order of the court.

Who has to be in the proceeding

Section 131 sets up a triangle. The subcontractor has the charge and is the moving party. The contractor is the entity to whom the money up the chain is owed under the upstream contract, and is the party whose s 128 response either accepts or disputes the claim. The holder of the security — usually the principal or the next party up the contractual chain — is the entity sitting on the money or the bond and is the party who must comply with s 131(2). All three corners of that triangle have to be before the court if the s 132 order is to do anything useful.

The subcontractor’s position is straightforward: it sues as plaintiff or applicant on the underlying debt and asks for an order enforcing the charge over the security. The contractor is joined because the underlying debt is owed by the contractor — you cannot get a s 132 order without first establishing that there is, in fact, money owing for which the charge can attach. And the holder of the security has to be joined because s 131(2) imposes a personal obligation on the holder to retain the security or pay it into court, and s 132 is the mechanism by which the court tells the holder what to do with it.

Why the security holder has to be joined

The security holder is not optional. Section 131(2) makes the holder — not the contractor — the entity who must retain the security or convert and pay it into court. If the holder fails to comply, s 131(3) makes the holder personally liable to the subcontractor up to the amount the security would have satisfied. That personal liability is a powerful lever, but it also means the holder has its own distinct legal interest in the proceeding: the holder needs to know what to do with the security to discharge that liability under s 131(4).

Section 131(7) reinforces the point. Where the holder has paid the difference amount into court under s 131(2)(b), that money “may be paid out only under an order of the court”. No agreement between the contractor and the subcontractor will release it. So the proceeding has to be framed so that the s 132 order, when it lands, both binds the holder (so the holder can safely act on it under s 131(4)) and authorises the registry to pay out the funds in court. Joining the holder achieves both.

Why the contractor has to be joined

The contractor is the debtor on the underlying contract and the party whose s 128 response shapes the whole proceeding. Without the contractor in the proceeding, the court can’t finally determine the underlying debt, can’t test whether the s 128 response was correct, and can’t make findings that would bind the contractor in any later argument about the “unsatisfied amount” or about the contractor’s rights to the security under s 131(5).

Section 131(5) preserves the holder’s entitlement to use the security for securing the performance of the contract — that is, the contractor’s contractual obligations to the holder. The contractor has a real interest in how that entitlement plays out alongside the subcontractor’s charge, because every dollar that goes out under a s 132 order is a dollar no longer available to satisfy the contractor’s own contractual obligations to the holder. The contractor is therefore a necessary party on its own contractual interest, separate from being the underlying debtor.

Multiple subcontractors and competing claims

Where more than one subcontractor has given a notice of claim against the same contractor on the same upstream contract, the “unsatisfied amount” under s 131(8) is the total of all the relevant claims of charge, and the “retained amount” aggregates everything the holder is retaining or has paid into court for the contract. If the difference amount won’t cover everyone, the court has to allocate. That is only sensible if the competing claimants are before the court — either as co-plaintiffs, by joinder, or by consolidating the proceedings — so the s 132 order can rank and apportion the available money in a single hearing rather than producing inconsistent outcomes across multiple cases.

Effect of contractual provisions about the security

Section 131(6) overrides any provision in the contract, in another arrangement, or in the security itself that would either stop the holder from complying with s 131(2) or operate to the holder’s detriment if it does comply. That matters at the parties stage because it kills off any argument by the holder that, for example, a bond instrument or a deed up the chain entitles a third party to call on the security ahead of the subcontractor’s charge. You don’t need to join those third parties merely because the contract says they have rights over the security; the BIF Act has already neutralised those rights to the extent they conflict with s 131(2).

What this means on a project

  • Plead and join three parties: the contractor (debtor on the upstream contract and the party who responded under s 128), and the holder of the security (who must comply with s 131(2) and against whom the s 132 order will run). You are the third corner.
  • Don’t shortcut by suing only the contractor. A judgment against the contractor on the debt is not an order under s 132, and it doesn’t bind the holder or get money out of court under s 131(7).
  • If you suspect other subcontractors have lodged notices on the same upstream contract, raise consolidation or joinder early. The “unsatisfied amount” and “difference amount” in s 131(8) are calculated across all claims, so a one-claimant proceeding can produce a misleading result.
  • Identify the holder by name. The holder is the entity actually sitting on the bond, retention or money — not necessarily the entity named on the contract you have. Confirm before issuing.
  • Don’t be drawn into joining every party named in the security instrument or in upstream contracts. Section 131(6) flattens contractual rights that would frustrate compliance with s 131(2); you only need the parties whose presence is necessary for a working s 132 order.
