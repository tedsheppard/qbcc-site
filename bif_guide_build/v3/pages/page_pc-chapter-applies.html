Requirements of a payment claim

Chapter 3 must apply (and not be excluded)

Even where there is a construction contract, Chapter 3 of the BIF Act — the part conferring the right to make a payment claim and to pursue adjudication — operates only where the contract is one to which the chapter applies. Section 61 sets out the gateway: a wide general application, followed by a list of exclusions that take particular contracts (or particular parts of them) outside the regime. Where a contract falls within an exception, the payment claim and any adjudication that follows are jurisdictionally exposed.

Section 61 BIF Act — Application of chapter

(1) Subject to subsections (2) to (4), this chapter applies to construction contracts—

(a) whether written or oral, or partly written and partly oral; and

(b) whether expressed to be governed by the law of Queensland or a jurisdiction other than Queensland; and

(c) whether entered into before or after the commencement of this section…

(2) This chapter does not apply to—

(a) a construction contract to the extent that it forms part of a loan agreement, a contract of guarantee or a contract of insurance under which a recognised financial institution undertakes [to lend, guarantee or indemnify in connection with the work]; or

(b) a construction contract for the carrying out of domestic building work if a resident owner is a party to the contract…; or

(c) a construction contract under which it is agreed that the consideration payable… is to be calculated other than by reference to the value of the work carried out or the value of the goods and services supplied.

(3) This chapter does not apply to a construction contract to the extent it includes [employee provisions, loan-conditional provisions, or lend/guarantee/indemnity provisions].

(4) This chapter does not apply to a construction contract to the extent it deals with construction work carried out outside Queensland or related goods and services supplied for construction work carried out outside Queensland.

The default position is broad application

Section 61(1) is deliberately wide. The chapter applies whether the contract is written, oral, or partly each; whether it is governed by Queensland law or the law of another jurisdiction; and whether it was entered into before or after commencement. The choice-of-law point is significant on national projects: a contract expressed to be governed by NSW or Victorian law remains subject to the BIF Act where the work is carried out in Queensland. The Act cannot be contracted out of by selection of a foreign proper law.

The exceptions in s 61(2) to s 61(4) operate as exclusions from this broad rule. A party seeking to keep a contract outside the Act bears the onus of bringing it within an exception.1 That onus has practical significance: a respondent who wishes to rely on, for example, the “domestic building work” exclusion must establish the facts that engage it.

Strict compliance and the “rough and ready” regime

The Act establishes what McDougall J in Musico v Davenport [2003] NSWSC 977 described as “a somewhat rough and ready way of assessing a builder’s entitlement to progress claims” (at [107]). The corresponding requirement, given that speed, is strict observance of the procedural requirements of the chapter. As Lyons J observed in F.K. Gardner & Sons Pty Ltd v Dimin Pty Ltd [2007] 1 Qd R 10; [2006] QSC 243 at [24], the regime “depends on strict compliance with the provisions in the Act”.2 A claimant who seeks the benefit of the statutory procedure — rapid payment, adjudication, judgment debt — must bring the claim within its terms. The chapter operates alongside ordinary contractual rights: it creates a parallel statutory regime, not a replacement for the contract.3

The exclusions encountered in practice

Most of the s 61 exclusions are evident on their face — pure loan, guarantee or insurance arrangements, employment-style provisions, and the like. Three exclusions arise sufficiently often on commercial projects to warrant closer examination.

Domestic building work for a resident owner (s 61(2)(b)). The chapter does not apply to a contract for domestic building work where a “resident owner” (defined by reference to schedule 1B of the QBCC Act) is a party. The exemption is construed consistently with the policy of protecting end-consumers from a rapid statutory regime designed for industry participants, and an owner may have more than one residence for this purpose.4 The exception does not apply where the owner holds (or should hold) an owner-builder permit, or is a building contractor, under the QBCC Act.

Consideration not calculated by reference to value (s 61(2)(c)). Where the price is a fixed lump unrelated to the value of the work or supplies — for example, a management fee structured purely as a percentage of head-contract amounts — the contract may fall outside the chapter.5

Work carried out outside Queensland (s 61(4)). The chapter does not apply “to the extent” the contract deals with work outside Queensland. The leading recent decision is Lendlease Building Pty Ltd v BCS Airport Systems Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 164, which arose from the Gold Coast Airport project — a site that physically straddles the Queensland–NSW border. Lendlease sought to quash an adjudication on the basis that some of BCS’s work was performed in NSW. Sullivan J rejected the argument. Section 61(4) displaces the BIF Act only where the construction work is “wholly” outside Queensland; the relevant consideration is the location of the building, structure or works, not the location of every individual activity. A payment claim is not invalidated merely because it includes a component referable to work outside the State, and the Act does not require a cross-border claim to identify the location of each work item with precision.

The same approach was taken earlier in Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd [2015] QSC 307, where Philip McMurdo J held that fabrication of a shiploader and tripper in Malaysia, although physically performed offshore, was “an integral part of construction work undertaken inside Queensland” at the Wiggins Island terminal, with the result that s 3(4) of BCIPA (the predecessor to s 61(4)) did not displace the Act. The territorial limit concerns whether the contract has a “relevant association with Queensland”, defined by reference to the location of the relevant land.

Indemnity-only claims fall outside the chapter

Section 61(3)(c)(iii) excludes provisions under which a party undertakes “to provide an indemnity relating to construction work carried out, or related goods and services supplied”. In Forte Sydney Carlingford Pty Ltd v Li [2022] FCA 1499, Stewart J held that where the entire payment claim was founded on an indemnity under the contract — rather than for the construction work itself — the equivalent NSW provision excluded the whole claim from adjudication, rendering the payment claim invalid. The lesson for Queensland is the same: if the substance of what you are claiming is an indemnity payment dressed up as a progress claim, s 61(3)(c)(iii) is likely to bite.

Practice tips

  • Start by checking whether your contract falls within s 61(1). Written, oral, partly each, governed by foreign law, pre- or post-commencement — all are caught. The foreign-law point is important: choosing NSW law in your contract does not get you out of the BIF Act for Queensland work.
  • If you are a respondent wanting to rely on an exception, remember the onus is on you. Plead the facts that engage the carve-out and have evidence ready.
  • For cross-border projects (Gold Coast Airport, the Tweed, Wiggins-style fabrication offshore), do not assume s 61(4) gives the respondent a knockout. After Lendlease v BCS, the Act applies unless the work is “wholly” outside Queensland, and offshore fabrication that forms an integral part of a Queensland structure stays within the regime.
  • Watch s 61(3)(c)(iii) on claims that are really about indemnities — rectification cost recovery, third-party liability passes-through, insurance shortfalls. If the substance of the claim is an indemnity, it is excluded, and an adjudicator who decides it anyway is acting without jurisdiction.
  • Domestic-build claims involving a resident owner generally fall outside the Act — but only if the owner is genuinely a resident owner and is not themselves a building contractor or holder of an owner-builder permit. Check the QBCC Act schedule 1B definition before assuming the exemption applies.
  • The price-calculation exception in s 61(2)(c) is narrow but real. If your contract pays you on something other than the value of work or supplies, take advice before issuing a payment claim — an unsuccessful adjudication on a contract outside the chapter is wasted cost.
  • Treat the procedural requirements of the chapter as mandatory. The courts have consistently said that the price of the statutory shortcut is strict compliance.
