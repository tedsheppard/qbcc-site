Statutory suspension of works

When suspension may begin

If a respondent has not paid the scheduled or adjudicated amount on time, the BIF Act gives a claimant a statutory right to down tools. The right is powerful but it is gated — you cannot simply walk off site the day after payment is due. Section 98 sets out exactly when the right applies, how long it runs, and what protection you carry with you while the suspension is in force.

Section 98 BIF Act — Claimant’s right to suspend work

(1) A claimant may suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under a construction contract if at least 2 business days have passed since the claimant gave notice of intention to do so to the respondent under section 78 or 92.

(2) The right conferred under subsection (1) exists until the day on which the claimant receives payment from the respondent of the amount mentioned in section 78(1) or 92(1), and continues for another 3 business days immediately following that day.

(3) If, in exercising the right to suspend carrying out construction work or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract, the claimant incurs a loss or expense because the respondent removes any part of the work or supply from the contract, the respondent is liable to pay the claimant the amount of the loss or expense.

(4) A claimant who suspends carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract under subsection (1) is not liable for any loss or damage suffered by the respondent, or by any person claiming through the respondent, because of the claimant not carrying out that work or not supplying those goods and services, during the suspension.

The two business day notice gate

The right to suspend does not arise the moment payment is missed. Section 98(1) requires the claimant to first give a notice of intention to suspend — under s 78 if the respondent has failed to pay a scheduled amount, or under s 92 if the respondent has failed to pay an adjudicated amount — and then to wait at least 2 business days from the giving of that notice. Only after those 2 business days have run can work lawfully stop. Walk off earlier and you lose the statutory protection in s 98(4); the suspension is then just a breach of contract by you.

The other prerequisite is hidden in the cross-reference. A s 78 or s 92 notice can only be given where the underlying conditions are satisfied — broadly, a payment claim that has crystallised into a debt (a scheduled amount that has gone unpaid by the due date, or an adjudicated amount the respondent has failed to pay). Suspension is a remedy that sits on top of an established non-payment; it is not a self-help tool for live valuation disputes.

How long the right lasts — and what counts as “payment”

Under s 98(2) the right runs until the day the claimant “receives payment” of the s 78(1) or s 92(1) amount, and then for a further 3 business days. The 3-day tail is deliberate — it gives the claimant time to remobilise rather than being expected to be back on the tools the same afternoon the funds clear.

What counts as “receives payment” was considered by Master Macready in Beckhaus Civil Pty Ltd v Brewarrina Shire Council (No 2) [2004] NSWSC 1160. Drawing on the dictionary meaning of “pay” — “[t]o give (a person) what is due in discharge of a debt… to remunerate, recompense” — his Honour held that the touchstone is the act of a debtor transferring by way of satisfaction whatever is owed by him or her to a creditor, and that a claimant will not have “received payment” in circumstances where the claimant is unable to obtain the benefit of that payment due to conditions placed on its receipt (such as the provision of a secured bank guarantee). The practical point: a payment that lands in your account encumbered by conditions that prevent you from drawing on it does not start the 3-business-day clock.

The point was taken further in On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163. Crowe AJ held, in respect of the ACT equivalent of s 98(1), that a claimant’s right to suspend continues until either the outstanding amount is set aside or there is an unequivocal communication acknowledging discharge of the payment claim, adjudicated amount, or in that case the provisional judgment debt. A subsequent expert determination during litigation, which concluded the claimant in fact owed the respondent more than the adjudicated amount, did not end the suspension right; the right ran on until consent orders setting aside the adjudication amount were made, and then for 3 business days after.1

Protection while suspended

While the suspension is on foot, s 98(4) provides that the claimant is not liable for any loss or damage suffered by the respondent (or anyone claiming through the respondent) because the work is not being carried out. That immunity is the whole point of the section — without it, the principal could simply call the suspension a repudiation and terminate. Section 98(3) goes the other way: if the respondent uses the suspension as cover to descope or remove work from the contract, the respondent must pay the claimant for any loss or expense caused by that descoping.

Practice tips

  • Do not stop work the day payment is missed. The right to suspend only arises once you have served a s 78 or s 92 notice of intention to suspend and at least 2 business days have passed since you gave it.
  • Diary the notice carefully. Record the date and time of service, the mode of service, and count business days from the day after service. If you down tools too early, s 98(4) does not protect you.
  • The right runs until you receive payment of the scheduled or adjudicated amount — plus 3 business days. Use that tail to remobilise; you are not expected to be back on site the moment funds clear.
  • “Receives payment” means money you can actually use. A transfer that lands subject to conditions you cannot satisfy (a bank guarantee or similar) does not start the 3-day clock.
  • If the respondent removes scope while you are off the tools, s 98(3) entitles you to your loss or expense from the descoping. Capture the costs (demobilisation, idle plant, restocking) contemporaneously.
  • If the respondent disputes that you ever received payment or argues the amount has been set aside, hold position until there is an unequivocal acknowledgement of discharge or a court order setting the amount aside — the right does not lapse on the strength of a contrary expert determination.
  1. On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163 (Crowe AJ) considered s 26(2)(b) of the ACT Act, the equivalent of s 98(1) of the BIF Act, and is persuasive on the duration of the suspension right under the BIF Act.
