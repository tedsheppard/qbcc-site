Requirements of an adjudicator's decision

The adjudicator must accept the referral

Once the registrar refers an adjudication application to a nominated adjudicator, that adjudicator has a short, statutory window to decide whether to accept the referral. Without acceptance under s 81, no adjudicator is appointed and there is nothing for an adjudicator to decide. Acceptance is the formal step that converts a referred application into one that an adjudicator has been appointed to decide.

Section 81 BIF Act — Appointment of adjudicator

(1) If the registrar refers an adjudication application to an adjudicator for a decision, the adjudicator must, unless the adjudicator has a reasonable excuse, accept or reject the referral within 4 business days after the referral is made.

(2) The adjudicator may accept the referral by serving written notice of the acceptance on the claimant, the respondent and the registrar.

(3) The adjudicator may reject the referral by notifying the registrar of the refusal.

(4) If the adjudicator rejects the referral or does not accept it within the time required under subsection(1)—

(a) the registrar must refer the adjudication application to another adjudicator within 4 business days after becoming aware of the refusal or failure; and

(b) no fee is payable for referring the adjudication application to another adjudicator.

(5) The other adjudicator may accept the referral by serving written notice of the acceptance on the claimant, the respondent and the registrar.

(6) The other adjudicator may reject the referral by notifying the registrar of the refusal.

(7) On accepting a referral of an adjudication application under subsection(1) or (5), an adjudicator is taken to have been appointed to decide the application.

How acceptance works

The mechanism is short and prescriptive. The registrar refers the application to an adjudicator. That adjudicator then has 4 business days to either accept by serving written notice on the claimant, the respondent and the registrar, or reject by notifying the registrar. Failure to act within the 4 business days has the same effect as a rejection — the registrar must re-refer the application to another adjudicator within 4 business days of becoming aware of the failure, and no further fee is payable for that re-referral.

Acceptance is also the point of appointment. Section 81(7) provides that, on accepting the referral, the adjudicator “is taken to have been appointed to decide the application”. Until that point the nominated adjudicator has no decision-making role; from that point, time begins to run on the decision deadlines in s 85.

Service of the notice of acceptance — agents and the post

Two practical issues recur around s 81: who can sign and send the notice of acceptance, and what happens if a party says they never received it. Both were addressed in Peekhrst Pty Ltd v Wallace & Anor [2007] QSC 159, which considered the equivalent provision under the predecessor Act. Douglas J held that an adjudicator may authorise an agent to accept on his or her behalf:

There is nothing in the section to suggest that an adjudicator may not authorise an agent to accept on his behalf. Given the speed with which these claims are to be dealt with, it may well be the case from time to time that a nominated adjudicator may not be available immediately to sign and serve a notice of acceptance personally. That does not mean that those steps may not be taken by an agent.

His Honour added that, even if the use of an agent had constituted a breach of the Act, “there is nothing to suggest that the use of an agent in circumstances like this should lead to invalidity”.1 A challenge to a notice of acceptance on the sole basis that it was signed or sent by an authorised nominee or staff member of the adjudicator’s office is therefore unlikely to succeed.

The second recurring issue is non-receipt. In Randhawa v Serrato [2009] NSWSC 170 the adjudicator’s notice of acceptance was served by post. The plaintiff led evidence that it had not received the notice. The Court held that “if delivery is not disproved the fact of non-receipt does not displace the result that the delivery is deemed to be effected at the time at which it would have taken place in the ordinary course of the post” (citing Fancourt v Mercantile Credits (1983) 154 CLR 87 at 97). The plaintiff’s evidence went only to non-receipt, not to non-delivery, and the challenge to the validity of the determination failed.2 A bare assertion of non-receipt will not, on its own, unwind the appointment.

Practice tips

  • Once an application is referred, expect the notice of acceptance from the adjudicator within 4 business days. The decision period under s 85 runs from acceptance, not from referral.
  • If a notice of acceptance does not appear within the 4 business day window, contact the registrar. Section 81(4) requires re-referral to another adjudicator within 4 further business days, with no additional fee.
  • A notice of acceptance should not be challenged solely because it was signed by someone in the adjudicator’s office rather than the adjudicator personally — Peekhrst forecloses that argument.
  • Retain registered post receipts and email delivery confirmations. To dispute receipt of an acceptance notice, evidence must go to non-delivery, not merely non-receipt — Randhawa is clear on that point.
  • Record the date of the notice of acceptance: it is the date of appointment under s 81(7) and the trigger for the s 85 decision-time calculation that follows.

Peekhrst Pty Ltd v Wallace & Anor [2007] QSC 159 (Douglas J — an adjudicator may authorise an agent to accept the referral on his or her behalf; use of an agent does not lead to invalidity), referring to Project Blue Sky v Australian Broadcasting Authority (1998) 194 CLR 355, 390–391 at [93] on the consequences of statutory non-compliance.
  2. Randhawa v Serrato [2009] NSWSC 170 — postal service of notice of acceptance; non-receipt does not displace deemed delivery in the ordinary course of post; following Fancourt v Mercantile Credits (1983) 154 CLR 87 at 97.
