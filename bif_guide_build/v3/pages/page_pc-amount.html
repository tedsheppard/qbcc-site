Requirements of a payment claim

State the claimed amount

A payment claim has to put a number on the table. Section 68(1)(b) requires the written document to state the amount — called the “claimed amount” — of the progress payment that the claimant says is payable by the respondent. Without that figure the document is not a payment claim and the rest of the Chapter 3 machinery never engages.

Section 68(1)(b) BIF Act — states the amount

(1) A payment claim, for a progress payment, is a written document that—

…

(b) states the amount (the claimed amount) of the progress payment that the claimant claims is payable by the respondent…

What the requirement is asking for

This is the most mechanical of the s 68(1) requirements. The document must state, on its face, the dollar amount the claimant says it is owed. It does not need to state the “correct” amount — whether the figure is in fact payable is a matter for the payment schedule and, if it gets that far, for the adjudicator. It is enough that the document states the amount the claimant claims is payable.1

The Act does not prescribe any particular form. There is no requirement for a single grand-total line, no required label, and no rule against breaking the claim down across attachments. The courts have consistently said the document is not to be read from an unduly critical viewpoint — the only question is whether the content satisfies the statutory description.2

Multiple invoices or amounts spread across documents

A claim does not fail simply because the claimant did not bother to add up its own numbers. In Camporeale Holdings Pty Ltd v Mortimer Construction Pty Ltd & Anor [2015] QSC 211 the claimant served four invoices under a single covering email. The respondent argued the email had to cite a single total and that without it there was no “claimed amount”. Henry J disagreed:

The absence of a statement of the mathematical total of those amounts does not make the statements of the four amounts considered in combination any less a statement of the amount of the progress payment claimed… a payment claim which states the amounts of the progress payment claimed to be payable will comply with s 17(2)(b), as long as those amounts are stated as part of one payment claim.

His Honour reached that conclusion partly through s 32C of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) (singular includes plural) and partly through a common-sense reading of the bundle — when the documents were considered together it was “readily discernible” that the combined total was the amount claimed. The principle carries straight over to s 68(1)(b): you can state the claimed amount as a single figure, or as a series of amounts that, taken together, add up to the claim, provided the documents are served as one payment claim.

What does not satisfy s 68(1)(b)

The opposite case is MWB Everton Park Pty Ltd as trustee for MWB Everton Park Unit Trust v Devcon Building Co Pty Ltd [2024] QCA 94. The builder sent the principal an email with attached spreadsheets describing percentage progress against various trade items. The Court of Appeal (Dalton JA, Brown J and Kelly J agreeing) gave three independent reasons the documents were not a payment claim. The s 68(1)(b) reason was simply that the documents attached to the email did not state an amount which was the claimed amount of the progress payment. Percentages of trade categories, without a stated dollar figure that the claimant was asserting was payable, were not enough.

The point sounds obvious but it is worth stating: the figure stated has to be the figure claimed as payable. A spreadsheet of valuations, a running ledger of costs incurred, or a list of percentages against a schedule of rates is not, by itself, a statement of the claimed amount. There has to be a number, presented as the amount the claimant says the respondent owes for this progress payment.

Including amounts already paid into trust or held back

Section 68(2) expands what the claimed amount may include. It can pick up an amount the respondent is liable to pay the claimant under s 98(3), and an amount held under the construction contract by the respondent that the claimant says is due for release — typically retention or security being called on. Those amounts can be rolled into the figure stated under s 68(1)(b); they do not have to sit in a separate document.

Practice tips

  • Put a dollar figure on the face of the claim. A single line stating the total claimed amount is the safest format. If your invoicing system produces multiple invoices for one claim period, serve them together under one cover so they are unmistakably one payment claim.
  • Don’t rely on the respondent to add up your numbers for you. Camporeale says they have to, but only if the documents make clear they form one claim. Make that obvious — same email, same date, same cover sheet.
  • A schedule of percentages or trade progress without a stated dollar amount is not a payment claim. After MWB Everton Park there is direct Court of Appeal authority on that point; do not assume the principal will infer the figure from the percentages.
  • The figure does not have to be correct. State what you in fact claim is payable; the contest over whether it is actually owing belongs in the payment schedule and the adjudication, not in the validity of the claim.
  • If you are claiming retention, security or amounts the respondent is liable to pay you under s 98(3), include them in the claimed amount — s 68(2) expressly allows this.
