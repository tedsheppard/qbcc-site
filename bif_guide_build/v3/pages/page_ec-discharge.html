Enforcing a subcontractor’s charge

Discharge of the charge

A subcontractor’s charge under Chapter 4 is a creature of statute, and so is the way it ends. Section 135 isn’t about the substantive rights you bring to court — it is the “one proceeding for everyone” rule. Once any subcontractor commences an action to enforce a charge, that action is treated as also brought on behalf of every other subcontractor whose charge over the same money is still alive. The practical consequence: you don’t need to start your own proceeding to protect your charge, but you do have to join in if you want to share in the money.

Section 135 BIF Act — enforcing subcontractor’s charge on behalf of other subcontractors

135 Enforcing subcontractor’s charge on behalf of other subcontractors

(1) An action to enforce a subcontractor’s charge under this chapter may be brought by or on behalf of any number of subcontractors claiming subcontractors’ charges.

(2) Every action brought by a subcontractor to enforce a subcontractor’s charge is deemed to be also brought on behalf of every other subcontractor—

(a) who has given a notice of claim under section 122; and

(b) whose charge has not been extinguished under section 136(3); and

(c) who becomes a party to the action in accordance with Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 1999 and subsection (3).

(3) Subject to the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 1999, a subcontractor may become a party at any time before the date appointed for the hearing or any adjournment of the hearing.

One action, many claimants

Section 135(1) lets a single proceeding be brought by, or on behalf of, any number of subcontractors. That keeps the dispute about the same pool of retained money in one place and stops a principal or security holder from being chased through parallel proceedings over the same security. Where several subcontractors down the same chain have served notices of claim under s 122, the sensible course is one action with all of them as parties — the court can then sort out priorities and quantify each charge on the same evidence.

The deeming rule and why it matters

Subsection (2) is the critical provision for project managers. Once any subcontractor commences an action to enforce a charge, that action is “deemed to be also brought on behalf of every other subcontractor” who has given a s 122 notice and whose charge has not been extinguished under s 136(3). You do not need a fresh originating process of your own. The first proceeding off the rank carries the others.

That is a real protection — but it is also a trap. The deeming only operates while three conditions are simultaneously met: a valid notice of claim under s 122; a charge that has not been extinguished under s 136(3); and the subcontractor actually becoming a party to the existing action under the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 1999 and s 135(3). Miss the third step and the deeming is empty: you may be “deemed” to be on the action, but if you never join it, you cannot take an order under s 132 in your own favour and you cannot share in any money paid out.

The cut-off for joining

Section 135(3) sets the joinder window. Subject to the UCPR, a subcontractor may become a party “at any time before the date appointed for the hearing or any adjournment of the hearing”. The window is generous — it runs right up to the door of the courtroom — but it is finite. Once the hearing starts (or, if the hearing is adjourned, once the adjourned hearing starts), the gate closes for any subcontractor who has not yet joined. The leading appellate treatment of the predecessor provision under the Subcontractors’ Charges Act 1974 is Ex parte Williams [1980] Qd R 67, which confirmed that the deeming and joinder mechanism is the route by which other charge-holders share in the proceeding — they do not get an automatic distribution merely by holding a charge. The same logic carries into s 135.

The link to extinguishment under s 136(3)

Section 135(2)(b) ties the deeming to s 136(3). If your charge has been extinguished — typically because the statutory life of the charge has run out without you starting or joining a proceeding — the deeming in s 135 cannot resurrect it. The discharge of the charge is dealt with in detail on its own page; the point here is that s 135 is not a workaround for a dead charge. It only carries live charges into the existing action.

What this means on a project

  • If you have served a notice of claim under s 122 and another subcontractor down the same chain has already started a proceeding, you don’t need to commence your own. But you do need to join that proceeding under s 135(3) before the hearing date if you want a s 132 order in your favour.
  • Treat the deeming in s 135(2) as a placeholder, not a payment. Until you become a party, you have no standing to take an order or to receive money out of court.
  • Diary the hearing date as soon as you know there is an existing action. The joinder window closes when the hearing (or adjourned hearing) starts — not when judgment is given.
  • Check your charge is still alive. If the s 136(3) extinguishment has bitten, s 135 cannot help you — you fall outside subsection (2)(b) and the deeming does not apply.
  • Where several subcontractors hold charges over the same retained money, coordinate. One properly pleaded action with all charge-holders as parties is faster and cheaper than parallel proceedings, and it gives the court the full picture for the s 132 order.
  1. Section 135(2)(a) BIF Act requires that the deemed-in subcontractor has given a notice of claim under s 122. Without a valid s 122 notice, there is no charge to be carried by the existing action, and the deeming has nothing to operate on.
  2. Section 135(2)(b) ties the deeming to s 136(3) extinguishment. The statutory life of a subcontractor’s charge and the conditions on which it ends are dealt with separately under s 136.
  3. Ex parte Williams [1980] Qd R 67 (Full Court) — predecessor provision under the Subcontractors’ Charges Act 1974; confirms that the deeming/joinder mechanism is the route by which other charge-holders take a share of the proceeding rather than receiving an automatic distribution.
  4. Joinder under s 135(3) is “subject to the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 1999”, so the procedural route is the ordinary UCPR joinder rules, applied within the statutory cut-off of the hearing date or any adjourned hearing date.
