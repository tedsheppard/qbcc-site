Project trust accounts

When a project trust is required

A project trust is the BIF Act’s mechanism for ring-fencing payments flowing down a head contract for the benefit of subcontractors. It is not optional, and it is not a contractual structure you opt into — once your contract crosses the eligibility line in s 14 and you enter a subcontract, the trust is required by force of statute. Three things have to line up: the contract must be eligible (s 14), it must not be exempted, and the contracted party must enter a subcontract for some or all of the work (s 12).

Section 12 BIF Act — When project trust required for a contract

(1) This section applies to a contract entered into on or after the commencement of this section.

(2) A project trust is required for a contract if—

(a) the contract is eligible for a project trust under subdivision 2; and

(b) the contract is not exempted under subdivision 3; and

(c) the contracted party enters into a subcontract for all or part of the contracted work.

(3) The requirement starts on the first day a project trust is required under subsection (2).

(4) The requirement continues until the project trust is dissolved under section 21, regardless of any of the following changes—

(a) a variation, or any other amendment, of the contract;

(b) a change in the contract price;

(c) a change in the contracted work.

(5) If a project trust is required for a contract and a project trust is also required for a subcontract of the contract, separate project trusts are required for the contract and the subcontract.

Section 14 BIF Act — Eligibility of contract for project trust when contract entered into

A contract is eligible for a project trust if, when it is entered into—

(a) more than 50% of the contract price is for project trust work; and

(b) the contract price is $1 million or more.

The two eligibility limbs

Section 14 sets the threshold test. Both limbs have to be satisfied at the time the contract is entered into. The first limb is qualitative: more than half of the contract price has to be for “project trust work” (the Chapter 2 expression for the categories of construction work captured by the regime — defined separately at s 8A and worked through on the next page). The second limb is purely numerical: the contract price has to be $1 million or more, calculated exclusive of GST under s 9(2). A $990,000 contract is not eligible. A $1,000,000 contract is.

Both limbs are tested at contract formation. That is the assessment date the section fixes — “when it is entered into”. If your contract starts at $900,000 and you sign it on that footing, s 14 is not satisfied at formation; later variations that push the price above $1 million do not retrofit eligibility under this section. The Act handles post-contract upward movement separately through the amendment provisions referenced in s 18(3) (s 14A on amendments affecting the trust requirement). The point for project teams is that you cannot ignore eligibility at execution and re-test later: if the contract is eligible at formation, the s 12 obligation is engaged the moment a subcontract is entered.

Eligibility is not enough on its own

Section 12 makes clear that eligibility under s 14 is a necessary but not sufficient condition. Two further things have to be true. First, the contract must not be exempted under subdivision 3 — the carve-outs sit outside this page and are dealt with separately, but they include the categories Parliament thought should sit outside the regime regardless of value. Second, and this is the trigger most often missed on a project, the contracted party must actually enter into a subcontract for all or part of the contracted work. A head contractor performing a $5 million project entirely with its own labour, plant and direct supply does not need a project trust under s 12, because s 12(2)(c) is not engaged. The trust is for the protection of subcontractors; if there are no subcontractors, the statutory mechanism does not switch on.

The flip side is timing. Once eligibility is satisfied and the first subcontract is entered, the requirement starts that day (s 12(3)) and the contracted party has 20 business days to open the project trust account (s 18(2)). That window is short. A head contractor that signs an eligible head contract today and lets its first subbie on site next week has effectively three to four weeks to have the account open at an approved financial institution.

Subcontracts are presumptively outside the regime

Section 15 cuts off a question that is otherwise easy to over-think. A project trust is not required for a subcontract unless the subcontract falls within one of the limited categories the Act picks out (s 14C and s 14E). The default position for a first-tier or second-tier subcontract is that no project trust is required for that subcontract, even if the head contract above it does have one. So a $2 million subcontract under a $20 million eligible head contract does not, of itself, need its own project trust. That is a deliberate policy choice — the regime puts the trust obligation at the top of the contractual chain, where the funds first arrive, and lets those funds cascade down through s 19 (deposit into the head trust) and s 20 (payments out to subcontractor beneficiaries from that trust).

Where s 14C or s 14E does engage a subcontract, s 12(5) confirms that you can end up with stacked trusts: a separate project trust for the head contract and a separate project trust for the subcontract. They do not consolidate. Each trust is administered, accounted for and dissolved separately under s 21.

The requirement, once engaged, is durable

Section 12(4) is worth reading carefully. Once the trust requirement is on, it stays on until the trust is dissolved under s 21. Variations, amendments, changes to the contract price, changes to the scope of the contracted work — none of those events switch the requirement off. A head contractor cannot escape the project trust regime by deleting scope or executing a deed of amendment that drops the price below $1 million. Conversely, the trust does not need to be re-established every time a variation is signed. The trust attaches to the contract and follows it through its life until either there are no subcontractor beneficiaries left, or the only outstanding work is maintenance work (s 21(1)).

Practice tips

  • Test eligibility at contract execution against both limbs of s 14: more than 50% of the price for project trust work, and a contract price of $1 million or more (GST-exclusive). Both limbs, both at formation.
  • If both limbs are satisfied, identify whether any subdivision 3 exemption applies. If not, the trust is required as soon as the contracted party enters its first subcontract for any part of the contracted work.
  • If the head contractor will self-perform the entire scope with no subcontractors at all, s 12(2)(c) is not engaged and no project trust is required — but in practice this is rare on contracts above $1 million.
  • Diary the 20-business-day window in s 18(2) from the date of the first subcontract. Open the account at an approved financial institution before the clock expires; a contract clause purporting to push that window out is of no effect (s 18(6)).
  • Don’t assume your first-tier subcontract needs its own trust. Section 15 says it doesn’t, unless s 14C or s 14E applies. Check those sections expressly before establishing a stacked trust.
  • Once the trust is on, treat it as on for the life of the contract. Variations, descopes and price reductions don’t turn it off — only dissolution under s 21 does, and only on the limited grounds in s 21(1) (no remaining subcontractor beneficiaries, or maintenance-only work).
  • For projects sitting just below the $1 million line, watch the formation-date trap: the s 14 test is taken at execution. Structuring a contract to land at $999,000 and then varying it up after signature does not cleanly avoid the regime — the amendment provisions in s 14A are designed to catch exactly that kind of arrangement.
  1. The contract price for s 14(b) purposes is calculated exclusive of GST: BIF Act s 9(2). On reasonable estimates where the price cannot be accurately calculated at formation, see s 9(1) — the threshold is tested against the reasonable estimate of the amount the contracted party is entitled to be paid.
  2. On post-formation movements in price or scope, see BIF Act s 14A (referenced in the s 18(3) note) — amendments of contracts can engage the trust requirement after formation. Section 12(4) then locks the requirement in: once on, it does not switch off because of later variations, amendments, price changes or scope changes.
  3. On the trust’s purpose and beneficiary class, see s 7 (main purpose — protecting subcontractor interests in funds paid to the contracted party) and s 11(b) (the trust is “primarily for the benefit of subcontractors for the project trust contract”).   4. Stacked trusts under s 12(5) are not consolidated for administration: each trust has its own account (s 18(5) — not more than one project trust account per project trust), its own deposit obligations under s 19, and its own dissolution under s 21.
