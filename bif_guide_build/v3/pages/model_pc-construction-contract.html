Requirements of a payment claim

A construction contract must exist

The BIF Act payment regime only switches on if there is a “construction contract” between you and the other side. Without one, no payment claim can be made and any adjudication that follows has no foundation.

Section 64 BIF Act — “construction contract”

construction contract means a contract, agreement or other arrangement under which 1 party undertakes to carry out construction work for, or to supply related goods and services to, another party.

What counts

The definition does three things at once. It captures any contract or agreement; it extends to an “other arrangement” beyond a contract; and it removes any requirement that the agreement be in writing. An oral agreement is enough, and so is a partly-oral / partly-written exchange — for example, a series of emails confirming scope and price, or a sequence of agreed meeting minutes.1

The work being undertaken must be construction work, or the supply of related goods and services in connection with construction work. Those concepts are defined separately at s 65 and s 66 and are dealt with on the next two pages.

The “other arrangement” limb

The “other arrangement” words exist to catch dealings that don’t quite fit the legal definition of a contract — for instance, a head contractor’s direction to a subcontractor to perform variation work where the parties are operating informally — without letting in every situation where someone has done work for someone else. Drawing the line has produced the most case law under this part of the Act.

The current position is that an “other arrangement” must give rise to either:

  • a legally binding obligation (not necessarily a contract — an estoppel or other source of legal compulsion can suffice, provided some legal consequence flows from it); or
  • a “concluded state of affairs” involving reciprocity — that is, both parties have accepted mutual rights and obligations relating to payment or price for the works.

The most recent appellate guidance is Bettar Holdings Pty Ltd trading as Hunt Collaborative v RWC Brookvale Investment Pty Ltd as trustee for Brookvale Development Trust [2025] NSWCA 242. The pleading in Bettar alleged only a common law contract or, in the alternative, an estoppel preventing denial of a contract. The NSW Court of Appeal held that wasn’t enough — what was missing was a separate allegation that the claimant had “undertaken” to perform work on some basis other than a common law contract. McHugh JA (Bell CJ and Kirk JA agreeing) put the test for an “other arrangement” in these terms:

[139] a legally binding obligation… or a concluded state of affairs… involving some element of reciprocity or acceptance of mutual rights and obligations relating to payment or price for the works.

His Honour expressly declined to resolve the earlier first-instance conflict between Lendlease Engineering Pty Ltd v Timecon Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 685 (Ball J — required a legally binding obligation) and Crown Green Square Pty Ltd v Transport for NSW [2018] NSWSC 1080 (McDougall J — adopted a less stringent reciprocity-based test),2 because the pleading in Bettar failed under either test. So which test applies in close cases remains open at appellate level.

The practical consequence is that a bare quantum-meruit claim — where work has been done but there is no agreement at all about price or payment terms — is unlikely to qualify. The “arrangement” requires reciprocal payment obligations, not merely the fact that work was performed.

Practice tips

  • If you have a signed contract, a construction contract exists. Move on to the next element.
  • If your engagement is by exchange of emails, purchase orders or agreed meeting minutes, that’s still capable of being a construction contract — provided there is offer, acceptance and an intention to be bound. Document what was agreed and when; you may need to prove it later.
  • For variation or out-of-scope work without a written variation order, capture the instruction and your response in writing as soon as possible. Most “other arrangement” disputes turn on whether there is evidence of agreement on price or rate.
  • A bare quantum-meruit case — just a claim for the value of work done, with no agreement — will probably fall outside the Act. You’d need to show reciprocal payment obligations, not just the fact of work done.
  • If you anticipate a challenge to the existence of a contract, plead the arrangement carefully: the material facts of the agreement, the undertaking to carry out work, and the payment obligation. After Bettar the appellate courts are scrutinising those pleadings closely.
  1. That a written instrument is not required follows from the words “contract, agreement or other arrangement” in s 64: see Okaroo Pty Ltd v Vos Construction & Joinery Pty Ltd [2005] NSWSC 45 (Nicholas J); Machkevitch v Andrew Building Constructions Pty Ltd [2012] NSWSC 546 (McDougall J).
  2. Lendlease Engineering Pty Ltd v Timecon Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 685, [29]–[33] (Ball J); Crown Green Square Pty Ltd v Transport for NSW [2018] NSWSC 1080 (McDougall J). For Queensland, Capricorn Quarries Pty Ltd v Devcon Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 28 (Daubney J) is the leading reading of the contract limb of the BIF Act definition.
