Project trust accounts

Records and reporting

Running a project trust is not just a banking exercise. Chapter 2, part 3 of the BIF Act layers a bookkeeping regime, an external review regime, and a long-tail retention obligation on top of the account itself. Get any of those wrong and you are exposed to penalty units, imprisonment risk for individuals, and follow-on regulatory action by the commissioner — quite apart from the civil consequences of a beneficiary being short-paid.

Sections 51, 52 and 57 BIF Act — shortfalls, records, audit

51 Trustee to cover shortfalls

(1) This section applies if there is an insufficient amount available in a trust account to pay an amount due to be paid to a beneficiary of the trust.

(2) The trustee must immediately deposit an amount equal to the shortfall in the trust account (a shortfall deposit). Maximum penalty—100 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.

52 Trust records

The trustee for a project trust or retention trust must—

(a) keep records for the trust as prescribed by regulation; and

(b) retain the records for at least 7 years after the trust is dissolved. Maximum penalty—300 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.

57 Engaging auditor for review of trust account

(1) The trustee for a project trust or retention trust must engage an auditor to carry out a review of the trust account as required by this section. Maximum penalty—200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.

(5) The review must be complete within 40 business days after starting the review.

(6) The review must be carried out by an auditor that is independent of the trustee and has not been excluded by the commissioner under section 54E.

Bookkeeping requirements

Section 52 imposes the core bookkeeping duty: the trustee “must… keep records for the trust as prescribed by regulation”. The substantive content of those records sits in the BIF Regulation and the commissioner’s approved forms — deposits and withdrawals tied to each beneficiary, the underlying payment instruction, the source contract, and the running balance — but the obligation to maintain them at all is statutory, and breach carries a maximum penalty of 300 penalty units or one year’s imprisonment. That is the heaviest individual penalty in the records-and-reporting suite, which gives some indication of how seriously the legislature treats the bookkeeping foundation.1

The records do real work. They are the audit trail an auditor needs to complete a s 57 review; they are the evidence the commissioner relies on if a complaint is made; and they are how the trustee proves it has actually paid each beneficiary the amount the trust held for them. They also feed the s 51 shortfall obligation: if reconciliation against the records shows the account is short of the amount due to a beneficiary, the trustee must “immediately” deposit a shortfall amount into the account. You cannot identify a shortfall, or defend an allegation that one existed, without contemporaneous records.

Trust account reviews and audits

Section 57 requires the trustee to engage an auditor to carry out a periodic review of the trust account. The engagement must comply with regulation requirements; the review must be carried out at the times prescribed by regulation; and once started, it must be completed within 40 business days. The review period itself — the slice of time the auditor is reviewing — is the period prescribed by regulation, not a period of the trustee’s choosing.

Independence is non-negotiable. Under s 57(6) the auditor must be independent of the trustee, and s 57(7) spells out what that means: not an employee, not (for a corporate trustee) an executive officer, investor or shareholder, not (for a partnership) a partner, and not a related entity. The auditor must also not have been excluded by the commissioner under s 54E. An “auditor” for these purposes is a person registered as an auditor under part 9.2 of the Corporations Act, or a person prescribed by regulation (s 50).

There are three narrow situations in which a trustee does not have to engage an auditor for a given review period. Two of them assume an essentially dormant account: no retention amount was held during the review period, or there were no transactions or changes for the account during the review period. In each case the trustee still has to give the commissioner a written statement, in an approved way, within 10 business days after the end of the review period explaining or confirming that fact. The third is whatever circumstances are prescribed by regulation. Outside those exceptions, the audit obligation is mandatory and carries a maximum penalty of 200 penalty units or one year’s imprisonment.

The output of the audit is an “account review report” (s 50, picking up s 57A(1)). If the commissioner reasonably suspects the auditor’s conduct has breached a professional standard or condition, s 58 lets the commissioner pass redacted information to the relevant professional body for investigation — with all information identifying the trustee removed before disclosure. The audit regime is therefore policed at both ends: the trustee is policed through the audit, and the auditor is policed through the professional bodies.

Retention of records

The retention period is the trap most trustees underestimate. Section 52(b) requires records to be retained “for at least 7 years after the trust is dissolved”. The clock does not start when the project finishes, when practical completion is reached, when the last payment is made, or when the bank account is closed. It starts when the trust is dissolved — and a project trust is only dissolved once the dissolution conditions in chapter 2 have been satisfied. In practice this means trust records routinely have to be kept for well over a decade from the date the underlying contract was signed.

Treat the seven-year period as a floor, not a ceiling. Limitation periods for contract claims, security-of-payment recovery and statutory demand processes can run longer in particular fact patterns, and the records the trustee is keeping under s 52 are often the same records you will want if a beneficiary later alleges short-payment or improper withdrawal. Destroying them on the seven-year anniversary, then being asked to prove a payment in year eight, is a foreseeable problem.

Practice tips

  • Set the trust bookkeeping up at the same time the trust account itself is opened. Section 52 starts to bite from day one of the trust, not from the first payment claim — and the 300 penalty unit / 1 year maximum applies to the duty to keep records, not just to record falsification.
  • Reconcile the trust account against the records on a regular cycle. That is what surfaces a s 51 shortfall in time to make the “immediate” deposit the section requires — and it is the same reconciliation the auditor will redo at review time.
  • Engage the auditor early in the prescribed review period, not at the end. The 40-business-day completion clock under s 57(5) starts when the review starts; an auditor brought in late has less room to chase missing records before the deadline.
  • Confirm the auditor’s independence in writing before engagement. Walk through the s 57(7) categories — employee, executive officer, investor, shareholder, partner, related entity — and check the commissioner’s exclusion list under s 54E.
  • If you are relying on the “no retention amount held” or “no transactions” exceptions in s 57(8), diarise the 10-business-day deadline for the written statement to the commissioner. Missing that statement is what converts an exempt review period into a breach.
  • Calendar record destruction off the date the trust is dissolved, not off the date the project finishes. Until you can identify dissolution, the seven-year clock has not started.
