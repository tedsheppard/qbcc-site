Retention trust accounts

Set-off / contract claims against retention

Cash retention sitting in a retention trust account is the contracted party’s money, held on trust, secured by a statutory charge in its favour under s 33A. The contracting party can only reach into that account for the narrow purposes listed in s 36 — and the most important of those, in practice, is using retention to fund defect rectification or to back the head contractor’s contract claims for set-off. The Act does not give an independent right of recourse; it tells you when a recourse the contract already provides for can be exercised against trust money.

Section 36 BIF Act — Limited purposes for which money may be withdrawn from retention trust account

(1) A trustee must not withdraw an amount from the retention trust account for any purpose other than—

(a) paying a beneficiary who is a contracted party from whom a retention amount was withheld from payment; or

(b) paying the trustee, as contracting party, for the purpose of correcting defects or omissions in contracted work, or otherwise to secure, wholly or partly, the performance of a contract; or

(c) paying another person for the purpose of correcting defects or omissions in contracted work.

Note—As the contracting party for a contract, the trustee’s ability to make a payment mentioned in paragraph(a), (b) or (c) would be governed by the contract under which the retention amount was withheld.

(2) A trustee must not withdraw an amount from the retention trust account for a payment mentioned in subsection(1)(b) until after the defects liability period, applying to the amount, ends.

(3) The trustee must repay all amounts the trustee withdraws in contravention of subsection(1) as soon as practicable after withdrawing the amount.

(5) This section does not apply to that part of a retention amount withheld from payment under a contract that is beyond the amount that may be lawfully withheld under the contract.

The contract has to authorise the recourse first

The note to s 36(1) is doing real work. It says that the trustee’s ability to make a payment under paragraphs (a), (b) or (c) “would be governed by the contract under which the retention amount was withheld”. Section 36 is permissive, not enabling: it tells you the categories of withdrawal the BIF Act will tolerate from the trust account, but it does not itself give you a right to set off, to rectify, or to call on the retention. That right has to be in the contract — usually a defects-liability clause, a set-off clause, or a security/recourse clause. If the contract does not give you a right to apply the retention to a contract claim, s 36 will not manufacture one.

So before any withdrawal under s 36(1)(b) or (c), the contracting party needs to be able to point to: the contractual right being exercised (e.g. a clause permitting set-off of moneys due or to become due, or a clause permitting the principal to have defects rectified at the contractor’s cost and recover that amount from retention); the factual trigger that has engaged it (a notified defect not rectified within the allowed period, an established cross-claim, a debt now due); and the quantum, calculated honestly. A withdrawal that goes further than the contract allows is a contravention of s 36(1) and must be repaid as soon as practicable under s 36(3), with the trustee exposed to a maximum penalty of 300 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment.

Defect rectification — s 36(1)(b) and (c)

Paragraphs (b) and (c) are the two defect-rectification limbs. Under s 36(1)(b) the contracting party can pay itself out of the trust account where it has done the rectification work in-house, or wants to apply the retention to secure performance of the contract more generally — for example, to fund completion after termination, where the contract permits. Under s 36(1)(c) the contracting party can pay a third party who is doing the rectification work — the rectification contractor or trade engaged to fix the defect.

Section 36(2) imposes an important timing brake on s 36(1)(b) only: the trustee cannot withdraw under that paragraph until after the defects liability period applying to the amount has ended. The policy is straightforward — while the defects liability period is still running, the contracted party should be given the chance to come back and rectify itself. Paying yourself out of trust to do work the subcontractor is still entitled to do under the contract is not allowed. Note that s 36(2) only applies to s 36(1)(b) — payments to a third-party rectifier under s 36(1)(c) are not subject to the same defects-liability-period gate, reflecting that those payments are usually made when the contracted party has failed or refused to rectify.

Set-off for other contract claims

The words “or otherwise to secure, wholly or partly, the performance of a contract” in s 36(1)(b) are broader than defects. They pick up the standard set-off use case: the contracting party has a contract claim against the contracted party — liquidated damages, back-charges, an overpayment on a previous claim, damages for breach — and the contract permits that claim to be set off against money the contracting party would otherwise owe, including retention. Where the contract supports it, the retention can be applied to that claim under s 36(1)(b). The s 36(2) defects-liability-period restriction still applies to a withdrawal under that paragraph, so the timing point matters: the contracting party can hold the retention against the claim, but cannot actually withdraw it from the trust account until the defects liability period has ended.

Two practical limits sit on top of this. First, s 36(5) carves out from the whole of s 36 any part of a retention amount that exceeds “the amount that may be lawfully withheld under the contract” — a reference back to the QBCC Act part 4A retention caps. Money over-withheld in the first place was never properly trust money, and s 36 doesn’t prevent its return. Second, the QBCC Act minimum financial requirements and the unfair-contract-terms regime in the QBCC Act both bear on whether the contractual set-off clause being relied on is itself enforceable.

Dispute mechanism

The BIF Act does not set up a bespoke dispute procedure for retention set-off. If the contracting party gives notice that it intends to apply retention to a contract claim, and the contracted party disputes that — whether on the merits, on quantum, or on the timing under s 36(2) — the contracted party has the same suite of remedies it would have for any unpaid retention amount. The most direct route is usually a payment claim under Chapter 3 for the disputed retention as a progress payment, followed by adjudication if the payment schedule disputes it. Adjudicators routinely value retention sums and rule on whether a claimed set-off has been made out: the question is whether, on the contract and the facts, the principal had a present right to apply the retention against the claim it has identified.

Alongside the Chapter 3 route, the contracted party can sue on the statutory charge in s 33A — that charge sits in its favour over the retention amount, secures release when the party becomes entitled, and is enforceable as if granted by written agreement (s 33A(4)). The trustee’s obligation under s 36A — that release must be by withdrawal from the trust account and direct deposit to the contracted party’s account — reinforces that the money is the contracted party’s once entitlement crystallises. And s 36(3) gives the contracted party a self-help repayment claim if the contracting party has already withdrawn money in contravention of s 36(1): the trustee must repay it “as soon as practicable” after the contravention.

If the contracted party contends the contracting party is taking retention contrary to s 36 — for example, withdrawing under s 36(1)(b) before the defects liability period has ended, or paying itself for work the subcontractor was never given the chance to rectify — a complaint to the QBCC about the trustee’s conduct is also available. The QBCC commissioner’s audit and direction powers over retention trustees are the supervisory backstop; the criminal penalties in s 36(1) and (3) are there to deter, not to remedy, and on a live project the adjudication route is the practical one.

Practice tips

  • Section 36 controls when retention can leave the trust account, but it does not create a right of set-off. Find the right in the contract first — a defects-rectification clause, a set-off clause, or a security/recourse clause — before drawing on the retention.
  • For defect rectification done in-house, withdrawals are under s 36(1)(b) and cannot occur until the defects liability period for that retention has ended (s 36(2)). Until then, the contracted party gets the first chance to rectify.
  • For defect rectification done by a third-party rectifier, withdrawals are under s 36(1)(c) and the s 36(2) timing brake does not apply — reflecting that you usually only engage a third party because the contracted party hasn’t rectified.
  • For other contract claims — liquidated damages, back-charges, overpayments — the “otherwise to secure, wholly or partly, the performance of a contract” words in s 36(1)(b) cover set-off where the contract permits it. The defects-liability-period brake in s 36(2) still applies; you can hold the retention against the claim but cannot actually withdraw it until that period ends.
  • Document the entitlement before you withdraw: identify the contract clause, the defect notice or back-charge, the cost, and the calculation. The trustee record-keeping obligations apply, and an unjustified withdrawal must be repaid as soon as practicable under s 36(3).
  • Section 36(5) is a useful reminder that any over-withheld retention — above the caps in part 4A of the QBCC Act — was never properly trust money and is not protected by s 36 against being returned.
  • If the contracted party disputes a proposed or completed set-off, the dispute is resolved on the contract: usually by a Chapter 3 payment claim for the disputed retention, with adjudication on whether the set-off was contractually open. Section 33A gives a parallel charge enforceable as if under a written agreement, and the QBCC has supervisory jurisdiction over the trustee’s conduct.
  • The criminal penalty for a wrongful withdrawal is serious — 300 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment for contraventions of s 36(1) and s 36(3). Treat the retention trust account as a regulated trust account, not a working capital line.
