Retention trust accounts

Notifications

Running a retention trust under chapter 2 of the BIF Act is not just a matter of opening the bank account and parking the money. The Act layers three separate notice obligations on top: a notice to the QBCC commissioner whenever you open, close, transfer or rename the account; a notice to the contracted party telling them their retention will be held in trust before you withhold a cent; and a transactional notice to that contracted party every time you deposit money into, or withdraw money from, the account on their behalf. Each carries its own penalty unit exposure and its own short timing window, and each is drafted in mandatory terms.

Sections 34B, 40 and 40A BIF Act — the three notification obligations

34B Notice of retention trust account’s opening, closing or name change

(1) This section applies if a trustee, or another person on behalf of the trustee, takes any of the following actions in relation to the retention trust account—

(a) opens the account;

(b) changes the name of the account;

(c) closes the account;

(d) transfers the account.

(2) Within 5 business days after taking the action, the trustee must, using an approved way, give the commissioner a notice—

(a) stating the action taken; and

(b) including the information prescribed by regulation.

Maximum penalty—200 penalty units.

40 Notice of retention trust before withholding retention amount

(1) If, under section 32, a retention trust is required for a retention amount withheld from payment under a contract, the contracting party must give the contracted party a notice about the use of a retention trust (notice of retention trust) as required by this section.

Maximum penalty—200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.

(2) The notice of retention trust must—

(a) be in writing; and

(b) include a statement that a retention trust will be used for withholding retention amounts under the contract; and

(c) include the information prescribed by regulation.

(3) The notice of retention trust must be given to the contracted party before withholding the retention amount.

(4) However, if the retention trust account was not opened before the retention trust is required under section 32, the notice of retention trust must be given to the contracted party within 5 business days after opening the account.

40A Beneficiary to be informed of transactions affecting retention amount

(1) This section applies if a trustee—

(a) deposits a retention amount into the retention trust account; or

(b) withdraws all or part of a retention amount held in the retention trust account.

(2) Within 5 business days after making the deposit or withdrawal, the trustee must give to the contracted party from whom the retention amount was withheld a notice of the deposit or withdrawal that includes the information prescribed by regulation, unless the trustee has a reasonable excuse.

Maximum penalty—100 penalty units.

(3) This section does not apply to the deposit of a retention amount if the trustee has informed the contracted party of the deposit under section 23A.

Notifying the commissioner about the account itself

Section 34B is the regulator-facing notice. Whenever the trustee, or somebody acting on the trustee’s behalf, opens the retention trust account, renames it, closes it or transfers it to another financial institution, the QBCC commissioner must be told within 5 business days. The notice has to be given “using an approved way” — in practice the QBCC’s online portal — and it has to state the action taken and include whatever further information the regulation prescribes. The penalty for getting this wrong is 200 penalty units. The trigger is the action itself, not when you got around to documenting it; if you open the account on a Monday, the clock starts running that day.

The s 34B obligation pairs with s 34C (change of financial institution), which separately requires the trustee to inform every contracted party whose retention is held in the account about a transfer to an alternative financial institution. So a transfer event generates two notifications — one to the commissioner under s 34B(1)(d), and one to each affected contracted party under s 34C(1)(c) — and both have to be in hand within their respective windows.

Telling the subcontractor before you withhold

Section 40 is the front-end notice to the contracted party. Before you withhold a single dollar of retention under a contract that triggers a retention trust under s 32, you must give the contracted party a written “notice of retention trust” saying that retention amounts under the contract will be held in a trust, plus the prescribed information. This is not a courtesy; the maximum penalty is 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.1

The default rule is that the notice goes out before the first withholding. There is one carve-out in s 40(4): if the retention trust account had not been opened before the retention trust was required under s 32 — for example, where an existing contract becomes a withholding contract under s 32(2) and the trustee opens the account in response — the notice can be given within 5 business days after opening the account. That is the only relief from the “before withholding” sequencing.

Practically, the s 40 notice should be embedded in the back-office workflow so it goes out at the same moment the contract is executed (or, for a contract that becomes a withholding contract mid-stream, at the same moment the account is opened). Because a term of a contract that is inconsistent with the deposit obligation in s 35 is of no effect, you cannot contract around the notice either — even a sophisticated subcontractor cannot “waive” their entitlement to it.

Transactional notices for deposits and withdrawals

Section 40A is the running, transaction-level notice. Every time the trustee deposits a retention amount into the account, or withdraws all or part of one, the contracted party from whom that amount was withheld must be given a notice of the deposit or withdrawal within 5 business days, including the information prescribed by regulation. The penalty is 100 penalty units, but there is a “reasonable excuse” defence built into the section.

Two features of s 40A are worth flagging. First, the obligation runs per transaction and per contracted party, not in a periodic statement form — if you make ten withdrawals across a month, that is potentially ten separate notices. Second, s 40A(3) carves out duplicate notices: if the trustee has already informed the contracted party of a deposit under s 23A (the project trust deposit-notice provision), a fresh s 40A notice for the same deposit is not required. That avoids double-handling where the same trustee operates a project trust and a retention trust on the same project.

Practice tips

  • Build a single notification register that tracks all three streams: s 34B (commissioner notices for the account), s 40 (one-off notice to each contracted party at the front of the contract), and s 40A (one notice per deposit and per withdrawal). Each stream has a 5-business-day clock except the s 40 default, which runs to “before withholding”.
  • For s 40, send the notice as part of contract execution — the first progress claim is too late if any of it includes retention. If you are catching an existing contract under s 32(2), open the account first, then issue s 40 notices within 5 business days under s 40(4).
  • For s 34B, treat “opens, names, closes, transfers” as four discrete trigger events and don’t bundle them. A rebrand of the trustee company that flows through to the account name is a name change under s 34B(1)(b) and triggers its own 5-business-day clock.
  • For s 40A, automate the deposit/withdrawal notices off the bank feed if you can. The 100 penalty unit exposure is per breach, and it scales quickly across a portfolio of subcontractors.
  • Use the “approved way” for the s 34B notice — the QBCC portal — not email or post. The s 40 and s 40A notices are not constrained to an “approved way”, but they must be in writing for s 40 and must include the regulation-prescribed information for both.
  • Keep evidence of dispatch and of the prescribed-information content for every notice. The penalties for the front-end s 40 notice include imprisonment, and proof of compliance will sit with the trustee.
  1. The s 40 notice is one of the few obligations in chapter 2 that carries an imprisonment exposure (1 year), reflecting its role as the gateway notice that puts the contracted party on notice that the retention regime is engaged. The deposit obligation in s 35 and the limited-purpose withdrawal restriction in s 36 carry comparable 2-year imprisonment maxima, underlining that the retention trust regime is treated by parliament as a quasi-fiduciary scheme rather than a purely administrative one.
