Requirements of a payment claim

Only one claim per reference date

Each reference date under your construction contract gives you one shot at a payment claim. Serve a second claim for the same reference date and it is invalid — even if the second one is more carefully prepared, more comprehensive, or fixes errors in the first. The flip side, in s 75(5), is that nothing stops you re-claiming an amount in a later claim tied to a later reference date.

Section 75(4)–(5) BIF Act — Making payment claim

(4) The claimant can not make more than 1 payment claim for each reference date under the construction contract.

(5) A payment claim may include an amount that was included in a previous payment claim.

The one-claim rule

The leading Queensland authority on s 75(4) is Tailored Projects Pty Ltd v Jedfire Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 32; [2009] 2 Qd R 171. Douglas J held that “where more than one payment claim issues for a reference date, the first must be considered to be the payment claim for the reference period so that any subsequent claims for that reference period are invalid”. The first claim out the door consumes the reference date. Anything that follows for the same reference date is a nullity, and an adjudication built on it has no foundation.

Kellett Street Partners Pty Ltd v Pacific Rim Trading Co Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 298 illustrates how unforgiving the rule can be on long-running projects. The claimant’s obligation to perform work had ended by 28 March 2012. Payment claim 10 was served on 31 August 2012 and was held to be the claim for the March 2012 reference date. Claims 11 to 14, served afterwards, were invalid — the claimant had used up its only opportunity for that reference date and no new reference date had arisen.1

What counts as “more than one” claim

Not every set of documents served on the same day is multiple claims. A covering email attaching a number of invoices — or a payment summary sheet with separate invoices behind it — will normally be read as one composite payment claim. In Tailored Projects, Douglas J treated 18 invoices behind a covering payment-claim document as a single claim, observing that to read it otherwise would be “the triumph of form over substance”. The same approach was applied in Camporeale Holdings Pty Ltd v Mortimer Construction Pty Ltd [2015] QSC 211 (covering email plus four invoices) and in J.R. & L.M. Trackson Pty Ltd v NCP Contracting Pty Ltd [2019] QSC 201 (one email, three invoices, one project).2

The most recent Queensland decision is Taringa Property Group Pty Ltd v Kenik Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 298. Hindman J held that of a series of emails sent by the claimant — eleven separate variation-claim emails followed by a twelfth attaching a formal invoice labelled “Payment Claim 31” with a supporting statement — only the final invoice was the payment claim. The earlier emails lacked the features of a payment claim (a clear demand for payment, a supporting statement) and were properly read as supporting documents. Her Honour held that a payment claim must be objectively recognisable as a single demand for payment.

Two qualifications matter on the ground. First, re-sending the same payment claim — even with additional supporting material — is not a fresh claim and does not breach s 75(4): Eco Steel Homes Pty Ltd v Hippo’s Concreting Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 135. Second, where two documents are served separately and only one is endorsed or framed as a payment claim under the Act, only the endorsed document is the payment claim: JAG Projects Qld Pty Ltd v Total Cool Pty Ltd [2015] QSC 229.

Section 75(5) — repeating an amount

Section 75(5) lets a later payment claim include an amount already claimed in an earlier one. The leading appellate reading is Spankie v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 355, where Fraser JA held that the equivalent of s 75(5) is “wholly inapt to impose any restriction” on the right to make a payment claim — its only function is to confirm that re-claiming an unpaid amount is permitted. So if a progress claim includes work or an amount that has previously been claimed and not paid, that does not by itself invalidate the later claim.

The limit on this is practical, not statutory. Re-agitating the same amount over and over invites resistance on issue-estoppel and abuse-of-process grounds — the Court of Appeal in Spankie expressly left those doors open. And once an adjudicator has determined an amount, the determination has the consequences set out in chapter 3 part 4; you cannot simply re-run it by lodging another payment claim.

Withdrawing and replacing a claim

A payment claim can be withdrawn and replaced, but only with the respondent’s consent — which can arise by necessary implication from conduct, but won’t be inferred lightly. Unilateral withdrawal is doubtful; in Kitchen Xchange v Formacon Building Services [2014] NSWSC 1602, McDougall J said that, given the consequences of failing to respond to a payment claim, “the circumstances must make it very clear to the respondent that a payment claim is to be withdrawn, if it is intended that withdrawal should occur”. The safer course is to get the other side’s agreement in writing before issuing a replacement.3

Practice tips

  • Treat each reference date as a single chance. Decide what you are claiming, get the supporting material together, and serve it once. A second “cleaner” version for the same reference date will be invalid and you cannot adjudicate on it.
  • If you need to bundle invoices, send them together — ideally as attachments to one covering email or document that makes plain a single demand for payment is being made. Stagger them across separate emails and you risk being told you served multiple claims.
  • An amount left unpaid from an earlier claim can be rolled into a later claim under s 75(5). You don’t lose the right to claim it just because you didn’t adjudicate the first time.
  • If you discover a problem with a payment claim after service, do not simply send a “replacement”. Either get written agreement from the other side that the first claim is withdrawn, or wait until the next reference date and serve afresh.
  • If you are receiving a stream of documents from a claimant, look for the one that is objectively a single demand for payment with a supporting statement. After Taringa, that document — not the earlier emails — is the payment claim, and your s 76 schedule clock runs from when it is given.
