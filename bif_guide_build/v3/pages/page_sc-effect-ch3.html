Valid subcontractor’s charge

Effect on the Chapter 3 process

A subcontractor’s charge under Chapter 4 and a progress-payment claim under Chapter 3 are two different ways to chase the same money — and the BIF Act does not let you run both at once. The moment you give a notice of claim of charge, s 62 reaches sideways into the Chapter 3 regime and shuts down the payment-claim, adjudication and enforcement machinery for the work caught by the notice.

Section 62 BIF Act — effect of giving notice of claim for subcontractors’ charges

(1) This section applies if a person gives a notice of claim under chapter 4 in relation to construction work or related goods and services the subject of a construction contract.

(2) Proceedings or other action may not be started or continued by the person under part 3 for all or part of the construction work or related goods and services.

(3) Without limiting subsection (2), if the person gave a respondent a payment claim for all or part of the construction work or related goods and services before or at the same time as giving the notice of claim—

(a) the respondent is not required to pay an amount to the person under section 77(2) in relation to the claim; and

(b) amounts may not be recovered by the person as a debt owing to the person in any court of competent jurisdiction in relation to the claim; and

(c) if the person made an adjudication application in relation to the claim and the application has not been decided by an adjudicator before the notice of claim is given, the person is taken to have withdrawn the application; and

(d) if the person made an adjudication application in relation to the claim and the application has been decided by an adjudicator before the notice of claim was given—

(i) the respondent to the application is not required to pay the adjudicated amount under section 90; and

(ii) the registrar must not give the person an adjudication certificate under section 91 relating to the adjudication; and

(iii) any adjudication certificate provided in relation to the adjudication can not be enforced by the person under section 93 as a judgment of a court; and

(e) the person may not suspend, or continue to suspend, carrying out all or part of the construction work or the supply of the related goods and services under section 98.

(5) This section does not stop the person serving, under this chapter, a payment claim in relation to all or part of the construction work or related goods and services and taking other action under this chapter in relation to that claim, if the notice of claim in so far as it relates to the construction work or related goods and services, or part, is withdrawn.

What s 62 actually freezes

Section 62 is triggered by a single act — giving a notice of claim under Chapter 4 — and its effect is matched to the “construction work or related goods and services” described in that notice. It is not all-or-nothing for your contract; it is scoped to whatever you have charged. Within that scope, s 62(2) blocks any further “proceedings or other action” under Part 3, which is the part of Chapter 3 that runs the payment-claim, payment-schedule, adjudication and enforcement process.

Section 62(3) then spells out the consequences for a payment claim already in flight when the notice is given. The respondent is released from its s 77(2) obligation to pay the claim by the due date; the claimant cannot recover the claim as a debt in court; a pending adjudication application is taken to have been withdrawn; and a decided but un-enforced adjudication is neutralised — the respondent doesn’t have to pay the adjudicated amount under s 90, the registrar must not issue an adjudication certificate under s 91, and any certificate already provided cannot be enforced under s 93 as a judgment. The s 98 right to suspend work for non-payment also drops away.

The freeze is not permanent. Under s 62(5), if you withdraw the notice of claim — or withdraw it to the extent it covers particular work — the Chapter 3 machinery is available again for that work. You can serve a fresh payment claim and run it through to adjudication and enforcement in the ordinary way.

Why a defective notice still bites

The trigger in s 62(1) is the giving of “a notice of claim under chapter 4”. That raises the obvious question: does a notice that is technically defective — wrong form, missing particulars, served on the wrong party — still flip the s 62 switch? The Court of Appeal addressed the equivalent provision under the predecessor BCIPA in Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386, holding that it is implicit in the legislation that a notice of claim of charge must be a valid one before it engages the freezing provision.1 An invalid notice does not stop a Chapter 3 claim because, in legal terms, no notice of claim has been given at all.

The Court of Appeal also addressed what it takes to lift the freeze where a notice has been given. In the same decision, on the meaning of “withdrawn” in the equivalent of s 62(5), the Court said:

There is an even stronger argument that the reference to a notice of claim of charge being “withdrawn” in s 4(6) of the Act is a reference to the withdrawal of a notice of claim of charge in such a way that is effective in respect of all relevant parties regardless of whether it is in the approved form.

The practical takeaway is that withdrawal is judged by substance, not form. A clear written notification to the respondent and the higher party that the charge is withdrawn for the relevant work should be enough to re-open the Chapter 3 route, even if the original notice was not in the approved form.

What this means on a project

  • Decide which regime you are running on before you serve anything. A notice of claim of charge under Chapter 4 will freeze your Chapter 3 rights for the same scope of work the moment it is given — you can’t hedge by running both in parallel.
  • If you have already served a payment claim and you now want to charge, understand the cost of switching: any pending adjudication application is treated as withdrawn, the respondent’s s 77(2) due-date payment obligation falls away, and you lose the s 98 right to suspend.
  • If you have a live adjudication decision or certificate, giving a notice of charge for the same work disables enforcement under s 93. Don’t serve a notice of charge over work where you are about to file the adjudication certificate as a judgment unless you accept that consequence.
  • Match the scope of the notice to the scope of the dispute. Section 62 only freezes Chapter 3 for the work or services described in the notice, so if your charge covers only part of the contract, the rest of your Chapter 3 rights remain on foot.
  • Withdrawal under s 62(5) re-opens Chapter 3 for the withdrawn portion. If the charge isn’t getting traction up the contractual chain, withdrawing it — clearly and to all relevant parties — lets you serve a fresh payment claim and run a Chapter 3 process for the same work.
  • If you are the respondent and you have just received a notice of charge, the Chapter 3 clock for the work covered by the notice has stopped. Don’t pay, don’t adjudicate and don’t enforce against that scope while the notice is on foot — but check the validity of the notice itself, because an invalid notice does not engage s 62 at all.
  1. Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386 (Queensland Court of Appeal) — on the predecessor s 4 of BCIPA, the Court held it is implicit in the words of the section that a notice of claim of charge must be valid before it engages the freezing provision, and that “withdrawal” under the equivalent of s 62(5) is judged by substance and is effective against all relevant parties whether or not the original notice was in the approved form.
