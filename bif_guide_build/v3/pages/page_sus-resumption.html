Statutory suspension of works

Resumption when payment is made

Once a claimant has lawfully suspended under s 98, the suspension does not end the moment payment is received. The Act provides a buffer: the right to remain off site until the outstanding amount is actually received, and then for another 3 business days. Identifying precisely when the right ends — and what constitutes “receiving payment” — is significant because demobilising and remobilising a site is expensive, and resuming too early may give the respondent an argument that the suspension was abandoned.

Section 98 BIF Act — Claimant’s right to suspend work

(1) A claimant may suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under a construction contract if at least 2 business days have passed since the claimant gave notice of intention to do so to the respondent under section 78 or 92.

(2) The right conferred under subsection (1) exists until the day on which the claimant receives payment from the respondent of the amount mentioned in section 78(1) or 92(1), and continues for another 3 business days immediately following that day.

(3) If, in exercising the right to suspend carrying out construction work or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract, the claimant incurs a loss or expense because the respondent removes any part of the work or supply from the contract, the respondent is liable to pay the claimant the amount of the loss or expense.

(4) A claimant who suspends carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract under subsection (1) is not liable for any loss or damage suffered by the respondent, or by any person claiming through the respondent, because of the claimant not carrying out that work or not supplying those goods and services, during the suspension.

When does the right end

Section 98(2) sets two endpoints. The first is the day on which the claimant actually receives payment of the amount referred to in s 78(1) (an unpaid scheduled amount) or s 92(1) (an unpaid adjudicated amount). The second is 3 business days after that day. Until both have occurred, the suspension is lawful and s 98(4) continues to immunise the claimant against claims for loss or damage flowing from the stoppage.

The 3 business day tail is deliberate. Sites cannot restart instantly — labour, plant and subcontractors must be recalled, materials reordered, and access reorganised. Parliament incorporated that reality into the section so that a respondent cannot pay at 4:55pm on a Friday and require crews to be back on site at the start of business on Monday.

What counts as “receives payment”

The trigger is receipt of payment, not the issuing of a cheque, the lodgement of a transfer, or a promise to pay. In Beckhaus Civil Pty Ltd v Brewarrina Shire Council (No 2) [2004] NSWSC 1160, Master Macready examined the ordinary meaning of “pay” and “receives payment” in the equivalent NSW provisions:

To give (a person) what is due in discharge of a debt, or as a return for services done, or goods received, or in compensation for injury done; to remunerate, recompense.” In turn, ‘payment’ is defined as “[t]he action, or an act, of paying.” From this it can readily be concluded that the touchstone of the ordinary and natural meaning of the expressions ‘to pay’ and ‘receives payment’ in the present context is the act of a debtor transferring by way of satisfaction whatever is owed by him or her to a creditor.

His Honour went on to hold that a claimant has not “received payment” where the money is encumbered — for instance, where it is paid in but conditioned on the provision of a secured bank guarantee — because the claimant cannot in fact obtain the benefit of it. The corollary is that the respondent has not “paid” in those circumstances either. A payment subject to conditions that prevent the claimant from drawing on it therefore does not start the 3 business day clock.1

Payment after a court challenge

A more subtle problem arises where the adjudicated amount has been paid (or paid into court) but the parties remain in dispute about whether it was ever properly owing — for example because a later expert determination or final account reverses the balance. The ACT Supreme Court addressed this scenario in On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163. The claimant suspended after a respondent failed to pay an adjudicated amount; while proceedings were on foot, an expert determination under the contract concluded the claimant in fact owed the respondent more than the adjudicated sum. The respondent argued the right to suspend had ended on the date of the expert determination.

Crowe AJ rejected that argument. The right to suspend continued until either the outstanding amount was set aside, or there was an unequivocal communication to the claimant acknowledging discharge of the payment claim, adjudicated amount or (in that case) provisional judgment debt. On the facts the right ended only after consent orders set the adjudication amount aside, with the 3 business day tail running from that date.2 The position for a claimant is that ambiguity about whether the debt has been discharged is resolved in favour of continued suspension.

Practice tips

  • The suspension lawfully continues until you actually receive the s 78(1) or s 92(1) amount, plus 3 business days. Don’t demobilise the demobilisation early.
  • “Receipt” means money you can use. If the payment is conditional — held in trust pending a guarantee, paid into court, or paid subject to set-off claims you haven’t agreed — take advice before treating the clock as having started.
  • Diary the resumption date the moment cleared funds hit the account. Count 3 business days from (and not including) the day of receipt; that’s the earliest day you have to be back on site.
  • If the respondent removes scope from the contract while you’re suspended, s 98(3) makes them liable for the loss or expense. Keep contemporaneous records of standby costs, demobilisation costs and any subcontractor charges.
  • If the underlying entitlement is being challenged in court or by expert determination, don’t resume on the basis of an interim ruling unless the order or communication unequivocally discharges the amount. Until then s 98(4) continues to protect you.
  • When you do resume, give the respondent written notice of the resumption date and the basis on which it’s been calculated. It’s a small piece of paper that prevents a later argument that you abandoned the suspension or returned late.

  2. On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163 (Crowe AJ) — on s 26(2)(b) of the ACT Act (equivalent to s 98(1) BIF Act): the right to suspend continues until the outstanding amount is set aside or the claimant receives an unequivocal acknowledgement of discharge, with the 3 business day tail running from that point.
