Project trust accounts

Subcontractor beneficiaries

The project trust exists for the benefit of a defined class of subcontractors — the “subcontractor beneficiaries” of the trust. Knowing who is in that class, and from when, matters because it controls who must be paid out of the project trust account under s 20, when the trust is established under s 17, when it can be dissolved under s 21, and what reporting obligations are triggered if a beneficiary is a related entity of the contracted party under s 24 and s 25.

Section 10 BIF Act — Definitions for part

In this part—

head contract means a contract for project trust work that is not also a subcontract of another contract.

project trust account means the account for a project trust at a financial institution.

subcontractor beneficiary, for a project trust, means a subcontractor who is a beneficiary of the trust under section 11A.

Section 11 BIF Act — What is a project trust

A project trust is a trust—

(a) over amounts—

(i) payable in connection with a project trust contract or project trust subcontract; and

(ii) required to be deposited in the project trust account under this chapter; and

(b) primarily for the benefit of subcontractors for the project trust contract.

Who counts as a subcontractor beneficiary

The defined-term route runs through two provisions. Section 10 tells you that a “subcontractor beneficiary” for a project trust is a subcontractor who is a beneficiary of the trust under s 11A. Section 11A then identifies which of the contracted party’s subcontractors actually fall within the beneficiary class for the particular project trust. The two work together: s 10 supplies the label, s 11A supplies the substantive test.

The starting point is s 11(b). The trust is “primarily for the benefit of subcontractors for the project trust contract”. That ties the beneficiary class to subcontractors of the head contract that has triggered the trust — not subcontractors of some other contract the contracted party happens to be performing, and not the contracting party (i.e. the principal) above. The trust runs downstream from the contracted party to the people the contracted party has engaged to perform parts of the contracted work.

Practically, that means a person becomes a subcontractor beneficiary of a particular project trust by being engaged by the contracted party under a subcontract for some or all of the work under the project trust contract. A subbie engaged on a different job, or by a different head contractor, is not a beneficiary of this trust. A second-tier subcontractor — engaged by a first-tier subbie, not by the contracted party — is also not a beneficiary of this project trust; the protection at second-tier sits inside any separate project trust required for the first-tier subcontract under s 14C or s 14E (see s 12(5) and the “stacked trusts” structure).

When subcontractor status engages

The Act treats the existence of a subcontractor beneficiary as the operative event for several other obligations. Section 12(2)(c) requires the contracted party to enter into a subcontract before a project trust is required at all — so the first subcontractor brought on board is the event that flips the trust requirement on (assuming eligibility under s 14 and no exemption). Section 17 then identifies how the trust is established, and one of the trigger events is “payment of an amount from the contracted party to a subcontractor beneficiary for subcontracted work under the contract” (s 17(b)). Section 23 requires the contracted party to give each subcontractor a written notice of project trust before entering into the subcontract (or within 10 business days of the trust being established, where the trust comes later).

The point is that beneficiary status is not declared by the contracted party or negotiated in a subcontract — it follows by force of statute from the fact of being a subcontractor of the contracted party for the project trust contract. You don’t opt in. Equally, you can’t opt out: a contract term that purports to disclaim beneficiary status, or to require the subcontractor to be paid from a different account, is incompatible with s 20(1), which says the contracted party may only pay an amount to a subcontractor beneficiary from the project trust account and into an account controlled by the beneficiary.

The class is dynamic

Subcontractor beneficiaries are not a fixed list locked in at the start of the job. The class moves with the project. New subcontractors engaged by the contracted party during the works become beneficiaries when they are engaged; subcontractors whose work is complete and who have been paid out drop away. Section 21(1)(a) reflects this directly: a project trust may be dissolved by the trustee if “there are no longer any subcontractor beneficiaries for the trust”. Until that point — or until the only remaining work is maintenance work (s 21(1)(b)) — the trust must continue, the account must remain open, and s 20 payment discipline applies to every payment out.

Related-entity subcontractors

The Act anticipates that a contracted party may engage a related entity (typically a subsidiary, sister company or other commonly-owned vehicle) as a subcontractor on a project trust job. There is no prohibition on doing so — but the regime imposes a transparency overlay so that the commissioner can see when trust funds are flowing within a corporate group rather than out to genuinely independent subcontractors.

Section 25 puts the obligation on the contracted party. Where a project trust is established for the contract and the contracted party enters into a subcontract with a related entity, the contracted party must inform the commissioner about the subcontract, using an approved way, within 5 business days after entering into it. Maximum penalty 200 penalty units. Section 24 mirrors that obligation onto the contracting party (the principal): if the contracting party knows, or ought reasonably to know, that a subcontractor beneficiary is a related entity of the contracted party, the contracting party must inform the commissioner within 5 business days. Maximum penalty 50 penalty units.

The two sections sit on either side of the contracted party. Section 25 catches the inside knowledge the contracted party has at the point of awarding a subcontract; s 24 catches what an attentive principal will work out from the contractor’s payment claims and supporting documentation. Together they let the regulator scrutinise intra-group payment flows without prohibiting them outright.

What this means on a project

  • Treat every subcontractor engaged by the contracted party for any part of the project trust contract’s scope as a subcontractor beneficiary of the project trust by force of s 10 and s 11A — status flows from the engagement, not from a contractual label.
  • Pay every subcontractor beneficiary strictly in accordance with s 20: out of the project trust account, into an account the beneficiary controls, and (if the beneficiary has its own project trust for its subcontract) into that trust account.
  • Don’t treat second-tier subbies as beneficiaries of the head trust — they aren’t. Their protection sits in any separate project trust required at the first-tier subcontract level under s 14C or s 14E, or in retention trust arrangements under s 31–s 32.
  • Maintain a live register of current subcontractor beneficiaries. You need it to track s 23 notice obligations, to monitor when the class has emptied for s 21(1)(a) dissolution, and to identify any related-entity subcontractors for s 25 reporting.
  • If any subcontractor beneficiary is a related entity of the contracted party, diary the 5-business-day s 25 reporting clock from the date the subcontract is entered into. Use the approved way prescribed by the commissioner; an internal note or email to the principal is not a substitute.
  • Principals (contracting parties) on project trust jobs should build a check into their payment-claim review: if you know, or it’s reasonably obvious, that a paid subcontractor is in the contractor’s corporate group, your own s 24 reporting obligation engages within 5 business days of you becoming aware.
