Requirements of a payment schedule

Within time (15 BD or shorter contractual)

Once a payment claim lands, the clock starts. Under s 76 you have to give a payment schedule within whichever ends first — the period in the contract, if any, or 15 business days after the payment claim is given to you. Miss that window and the consequences are severe: you can’t serve a schedule at all, and the claimant becomes entitled to recover the full claimed amount as a debt.

Section 76 BIF Act — Responding to payment claim

(1) If given a payment claim, a respondent must respond to the payment claim by giving the claimant a payment schedule within whichever of the following periods ends first—

(a) the period, if any, within which the respondent must give the payment schedule under the relevant construction contract;

(b) 15 business days after the payment claim is given to the respondent.

Maximum penalty—100 penalty units.

(2) However, the respondent is not required to give the claimant the payment schedule if the amount claimed in the payment claim is paid in full on or before the due date for the progress payment to which the payment claim relates.

(3) If the respondent gives the claimant a payment schedule, the respondent must pay the claimant the amount proposed in the payment schedule no later than the due date for the progress payment to which the payment schedule relates.

Two clocks, whichever ends first

Section 76(1) sets up two competing periods and tells you to use whichever runs out first. The statutory backstop is 15 business days from when the payment claim is given. If the contract specifies a shorter period for the respondent to give a payment schedule, that contractual period takes over. There is no scope for a contract to extend the 15-business-day cap — the Act sets the longest available window, and parties can only shorten it.

Whether a contractual clause actually displaces the statutory period is not always obvious. The courts require “clear contextual support for a necessary implication” that the contract has supplanted the Act’s timeframe.1 A clause that talks about the head contractor’s representative issuing a “payment certificate” under the contract may not be a clause about giving a statutory payment schedule at all — the two documents perform different functions and a certificate clause won’t automatically shorten the s 76 clock.

The Queensland appellate position

The leading Queensland decision is Allencon Pty Ltd v Palmgrove Holdings Pty Ltd trading as Carruthers Contracting [2023] QCA 6. The subcontract there required the Main Contractor’s Representative to issue a payment certificate “within 21 days after receipt of a claim for payment”, setting out the calculations and, where the certified amount differed from the claim, the reasons. The respondent argued the contract said nothing about a payment schedule and that it could rely on the full 15 business days under s 76(1)(b). The claimant said the 21-day contractual period applied and ran out first. The Court of Appeal sided with the claimant: because the contract stipulated a period of 21 calendar days for the issue of the certificate, that was the date by which the payment schedule had to be given, and the respondent — having missed it — was liable for the full amount claimed.

The Court distinguished the New South Wales decision in Thiess Pty Ltd v Lane Cove Tunnel Nominee Co Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 53 on the basis that the Thiess contract required an Independent Verifier’s certificate to accompany progress payments, which showed the relevant clause was a “reply to the contractual progress claim and not a reply to a statutory payment claim”. The point that matters for project teams is this: if your contract has a certification clause that, in substance, performs the work of a payment schedule — setting out what will be paid, the calculations and reasons for any difference — expect a court to read that period as the s 76(1)(a) period, even if the clause never uses the words “payment schedule”.

Contractual deeming clauses can’t buy more time

The other recurring trap is the deeming clause — a contractual provision that pretends a document was served later than it actually was, often to give the recipient extra working time. These don’t work to extend the statutory clock. In Roberts Co (NSW) Pty Ltd v Sharvain Facades Pty Ltd (Administrators Appointed) [2025] NSWCA 161, Hammerschlag CJ held that the equivalent NSW 10-business-day period commences from the date the payment claim is actually served and that parties may only agree to shorten, not lengthen, that period. The payment claim there was served on 28 February 2025 and the schedule issued on 17 March was held to be out of time, with the contractual deeming clause that purported to postpone service to 3 March having no effect on the operation of the statutory timeframe. Roberts became liable for the full amount claimed.

Service mechanics that affect the deadline

How and when the payment claim was “given” to you fixes the start of the count, so the service rules matter. Email service outside business hours still counts. In Queensland, an email payment schedule sent at 11:45pm on the last day of the period was held to have been validly given at the moment it became capable of being retrieved, on the strength of the contract permitting email service and s 24(1)(a) of the Electronic Transactions Act 2001 (Qld).2 Service through solicitors can also be effective, but only where the solicitor’s authority to give or receive the document is established — don’t assume it.3

Practice tips

  • Diary the s 76 deadline the moment a payment claim is received. Count 15 business days from the date the claim was given, then check the contract for any shorter period. Work to whichever date is earlier.
  • Read the contract’s certification or assessment clauses with Allencon in mind. A clause that requires the head contractor’s representative to assess and certify within (say) 10 or 14 days will likely be treated as the s 76(1)(a) period, even if it doesn’t use the words “payment schedule”.
  • Don’t rely on a deeming clause to push out the start date. After Roberts v Sharvain, if the claim was actually given on Day 0, the clock runs from Day 0 regardless of what the contract pretends.
  • If you serve by email, the moment the message becomes capable of being retrieved is the moment of service — including 11:45pm on the deadline day. That cuts both ways: it can save a late-day schedule and it can shorten your time to respond to a claim served at 4:55pm.
  • If the deadline is going to be missed, consider s 76(2): paying the claimed amount in full on or before the due date removes the obligation to give a payment schedule altogether. That is sometimes the cleaner commercial answer to a small or undisputed claim.
  • Late schedules are not “late but valid” — they are no schedule at all. The downstream consequences (judgment debt under s 78, no adjudication response without a s 82 second-chance notice) flow from the missed deadline, not from anything wrong with the schedule itself.
