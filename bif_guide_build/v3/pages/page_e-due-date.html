Enforcing an adjudicator's decision

Due date for paying the adjudicated amount

An adjudicator’s decision in your favour is not self-executing — the respondent has to pay it. Section 90 fixes when payment must happen and creates a statutory offence if it doesn’t. The due date is also the practical pivot point for everything that follows: the right to suspend work, the right to file the decision as a judgment debt, and the disciplinary consequences for licensed contractors all hang off whether the adjudicated amount has been paid by the s 90 due date.

Section 90 BIF Act — Respondent required to pay adjudicated amount

(1) This section applies if an adjudicator decides that a respondent is required to pay an adjudicated amount.

(2) The respondent must pay the amount to the claimant on or before—

(a) the day that is 5 business days after the day on which the adjudicator gives a copy of the adjudicator’s decision to the respondent; or

(b) if the adjudicator decides a later date for payment under section 88(1)(b)—the later date.

Maximum penalty—200 penalty units.

(3) If the respondent pays the amount to the claimant, the respondent must—

(a) notify the registrar, using an approved way, within 5 business days after making the payment; and

(b) provide the registrar with evidence the payment was made.

Maximum penalty—20 penalty units.

Calculating the due date

The default due date under s 90(2)(a) is 5 business days after the day on which the adjudicator gives the respondent a copy of the decision. The trigger is delivery of the decision to the respondent, not delivery to the claimant and not the date the decision was signed. “Business day” takes its ordinary BIF Act meaning, so weekends and Queensland public holidays drop out of the count, as do the days in the statutory end-of-year shutdown period under the Act.

The day on which the adjudicator gives the respondent the decision is day zero; the next business day is day one. So a decision delivered to the respondent on a Monday (with no intervening public holidays) is due for payment by close of business the following Monday. If you are the claimant, ask the adjudicator or the registrar to confirm in writing the date the decision was given to the respondent — you will need that date to prove the due date in any subsequent enforcement step.

Where the adjudicator fixes a later date

Section 90(2)(b) lets the adjudicator override the 5-business-day default by fixing a later date for payment under s 88(1)(b). That power is sometimes used where the adjudicator considers a longer payment period is appropriate — for example, where the claim involves a large sum, or where the contract itself contemplated a longer payment cycle. If the adjudicator has decided a later date, that later date is the due date for s 90 purposes; the 5-business-day default does not apply and is not an alternative. Read the operative part of the decision carefully on first receipt and diary the date the adjudicator has fixed.

The penalty — and the QBCC disciplinary overlay

Failure to pay the adjudicated amount on or before the due date is an offence carrying a maximum penalty of 200 penalty units. That penalty sits on top of the civil consequences — the right to recover the amount as a judgment debt, the right to obtain an adjudication certificate, and the right to suspend work — rather than replacing them.

For licensed contractors and building companies, there is a further consequence that often matters more in commercial terms than the penalty. A failure to pay an adjudicated amount on or before the due date is a ground for taking disciplinary action under the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991. That can mean licence conditions, suspension or, in serious or repeated cases, cancellation. If you are the respondent and the cash isn’t there, the worst response is silence: deal with the payment, or with the QBCC, before the due date passes.

The respondent’s notification obligation under s 90(3)

Once the respondent pays, s 90(3) imposes a separate, free-standing obligation: notify the registrar in an approved way within 5 business days after making the payment, and provide evidence that the payment was made. The maximum penalty for non-compliance is 20 penalty units. This is an administrative step, but it is not optional — it allows the registrar to keep an accurate picture of compliance with adjudicators’ decisions across the industry. If you are the claimant and you have been paid, it is worth confirming with the respondent that the s 90(3) notification has been lodged; an unlodged notification can be a warning sign that the respondent is treating the payment, and the obligations around it, casually.

Practice tips

  • The due date is 5 business days after the day the adjudicator gives a copy of the decision to the respondent — unless the adjudicator has fixed a later date under s 88(1)(b), in which case that date governs.
  • Confirm in writing the date the decision was given to the respondent. You will need that date to calculate the due date and to prove non-payment in any later enforcement step.
  • Read the operative part of the decision on first receipt for any later date the adjudicator has fixed under s 88(1)(b). Diary the actual due date, not just “5 business days”.
  • If you are the respondent and you cannot pay by the due date, don’t go silent. Non-payment is both an offence (200 penalty units) and a ground for QBCC disciplinary action against your licence. Engage early.
  • If you are the respondent and you have paid, lodge the s 90(3) notification with the registrar within 5 business days, with evidence of payment. Failure to do so is a separate offence (20 penalty units).
  • If you are the claimant, the due date is the gateway to the next steps in this chapter — the adjudication certificate, filing the decision as a judgment debt, and the right to suspend work all turn on whether the adjudicated amount has been paid by the s 90 due date.
  1. The penalty under s 90(2) is 200 penalty units; the penalty under s 90(3) is 20 penalty units. The current value of a penalty unit is fixed by the Penalties and Sentences Act 1992 (Qld) and is updated annually.
  2. The note to s 90 records that a failure to pay an adjudicated amount on or before the due date is also a ground for taking disciplinary action under the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991. That disciplinary overlay is, in practice, the most significant consequence for licensed respondents.
  3. The power to fix a later date for payment is found in s 88(1)(b) BIF Act, which is dealt with in the adjudicator’s decision chapter.
