Retention trust accounts

Beneficiaries (whose retention it is)

A retention trust is not the head contractor’s money parked in a special account. It is held on trust for the party from whom the retention was withheld — the contracted party. That is who the beneficiary is, and the whole of Part 2A is built around protecting that person’s interest in the cash until they earn the right to be paid it back.

Sections 33, 33A and 36 BIF Act — how the trust is established and who benefits

33 Establishment of retention trust

(2) The retention trust is established by the contracting party withholding the retention amount from payment.

33A Charge over retention amounts held in retention trust

(2) In addition to the retention trust, the retention amount is also subject to a charge in favour of the contracted party for securing the release of the amount when the party becomes entitled to the amount.

36 Limited purposes for which money may be withdrawn from retention trust account

(1) A trustee must not withdraw an amount from the retention trust account for any purpose other than—

(a) paying a beneficiary who is a contracted party from whom a retention amount was withheld from payment…

The contracted party is the beneficiary

The trust is created automatically the moment the contracting party (the head contractor, or anyone above the subcontractor in the chain who is doing the withholding) holds back a retention amount from payment. There is no need for a deed, a declaration, or any positive act of settlement — s 33(2) does the work. The beneficial interest sits with the contracted party: the subcontractor, supplier or trade whose money it is. They are referred to in the Act as the “contracted party from whom a retention amount was withheld from payment”, and in s 36(1)(a) they are named as “a beneficiary” of the trust.

So on a typical project the beneficiaries of the head contractor’s retention trust account are all of the subcontractors whose progress payments have been clipped for retention. Each of them has a separate beneficial interest, contract by contract, in the portion of the pooled account that traces back to their withheld cash. The head contractor is the trustee; it is not the beneficiary of its own account.

What the beneficial interest carries with it

Being the beneficiary is more than a label. Three protections attach to that status under Part 2A:

  • A statutory charge. Section 33A gives the contracted party a charge over the retention amount “for securing the release of the amount when the party becomes entitled to the amount”. The charge is enforceable by the contracted party as if it had been given under a written agreement,1 any act done to defeat it is of no effect against the contracted party, and it is declared to be a statutory interest under s 73(2) of the Personal Property Securities Act 2009 (Cwlth) — meaning it does not need to be registered on the PPSR to bite.
  • A locked withdrawal regime. The trustee can only take money out of the account to pay the beneficiary, to fund correction of defects or omissions in that beneficiary’s contracted work, or to secure performance of that beneficiary’s contract (s 36(1)). Money cannot be moved between subcontractors’ entitlements, and it cannot be applied to the trustee’s general business.
  • Transactional transparency. Every deposit into and withdrawal out of the retention trust account must be notified to the contracted party from whom that retention amount was withheld within 5 business days (s 40A). Beneficiaries are entitled to see the trust moving, not just the closing balance at release.

Release: when the beneficiary actually gets paid

Identifying the beneficiary matters most at release. Once the contracted party becomes entitled to the retention — typically at practical completion, or at end of the defects liability period for the second tranche — the trustee “must not release the retention amount other than by… withdrawing the amount from the retention trust account; and… depositing the amount into the contracted party’s account at a financial institution” (s 36A(2)). Release has to be a direct trust-to-beneficiary electronic transfer. Set-off by journal entry, payment to a related party, or netting against another debt are not release.

And when the trust over a particular retention amount is exhausted — everything has been released to the contracted party who was entitled to it — the trust is dissolved (s 37(1)). Only at that point can the trustee pay itself any residue, and only to the extent the residue is “not owing to another beneficiary” (s 37(2)). Until then the cash belongs, beneficially, to the subcontractor.

What this means on a project

  • If you are the subcontractor whose retention has been withheld, you are the beneficiary. The money in the head contractor’s retention trust account that traces to your withheld payments is held on trust for you, not as part of the head contractor’s working capital.
  • You have a statutory charge under s 33A in addition to the trust. You don’t need to register anything on the PPSR — the charge is a statutory interest under s 73(2) of the PPSA. If the head contractor goes under, that charge is your security for getting the retention back.
  • If you are the head contractor running the account, you are the trustee, not the beneficiary. You cannot dip into the account for general cash flow, you cannot use one subcontractor’s retention to fix another subcontractor’s defects, and you cannot release retention by book entry — release has to land in the subcontractor’s nominated bank account (s 36A).
  • Keep the deposit and withdrawal notices under s 40A flowing to each beneficiary. Failure to notify is a 100-penalty-unit offence and is also an early-warning signal to QBCC that the trust is being run loosely.
  • At release time, line up the entitlement (under the contract) with the statutory release mechanic (under s 36A). The contract tells you when the beneficiary becomes entitled; the Act tells you how the release has to be effected.
  1. Section 33A(4) BIF Act: “The contracted party may enforce the charge as if the charge had been given to it under a written agreement between it and the contracting party.” The s 33A charge sits alongside the s 33 trust — the beneficiary therefore has both a beneficial interest in the trust fund and a statutory charge over the retention amount.
  2. For the trustee’s residual interest after all beneficiaries have been paid out, see s 37(2) BIF Act: on dissolution “the trustee may pay itself all amounts held in the trust that are not owing to another beneficiary”. The trustee’s entitlement is residual only — subordinate to every contracted party’s claim.
