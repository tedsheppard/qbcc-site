Requirements of a payment claim

Within six months

A payment claim has a shelf life. Section 75(2)–(3) tells you the longest period during which you can give a payment claim, measured from the last day work was carried out (or, for a final claim, from completion or the end of the defects liability period). Serve outside that window and the claim is out of time.

Section 75(2)–(3) BIF Act — Time limits for payment claims

(2) Unless the payment claim relates to a final payment, the claim must be given before the end of whichever of the following periods is the longest—

(a) the period, if any, worked out under the construction contract;

(b) the period of 6 months after the construction work to which the claim relates was last carried out or the related goods and services to which the claim relates were last supplied.

(3) If the payment claim relates to a final payment, the claim must be given before the end of whichever of the following periods is the longest—

(a) the period, if any, worked out under the relevant construction contract;

(b) 28 days after the end of the last defects liability period for the construction contract;

(c) 6 months after the completion of all construction work to be carried out under the construction contract;

(d) 6 months after the complete supply of related goods and services to be supplied under the construction contract.

How the rule works

Section 75(2) is the default rule for ordinary progress claims. You take the longer of the period in your contract (if it specifies one) and six months after the construction work to which the claim relates was last carried out, or the related goods and services were last supplied. The contract can extend the period — it can’t shorten it below the statutory six months.1

Section 75(3) is the final-payment variant. A “final payment” is defined in s 75(9) as the progress payment that is the final payment for construction work carried out, or for related goods and services supplied, under the contract. Here you measure from the longest of four candidates: the contract period; 28 days after the end of the last defects liability period; six months after completion of all construction work; or six months after the complete supply of related goods and services.

Section 75(2)–(3) replaced the previous 12-month limit under BCIPA. The shorter window matters — older Queensland authorities and interstate cases decided under 12-month equivalents are still good law on the principle, but the actual deadline you have to work to is half what it was.

“The construction work to which the claim relates”

The most-litigated question under this provision is what counts as “the construction work to which the claim relates”. Three readings have been argued: any work under the contract; any work that forms part of the payment claim; or every item of work in the claim. The leading appellate authority is the NSW Court of Appeal’s decision in Estate Property Holdings Pty Ltd v Barclay Mowlem Construction Ltd [2004] NSWCA 393, which adopted the middle reading. Hodgson JA, dealing with the equivalent NSW provision, held:

“construction work … to which the claim relates” … is also the construction work for which payment is claimed in the claim; and accordingly, the requirement … is that some of that construction work be carried out in the relevant twelve month period … ss.13–15 of the Act do not provide any basis for dividing up the construction work to which the claim relates into items which may be considered discrete, and asking in respect of each such item whether some work was carried out in the twelve month period.

The practical effect: if the payment claim relates to a body of work and at least some of it was carried out within the six-month window, the claim is in time, even if other items in the same claim go further back. You don’t have to dissect the claim line by line. But the work in the window must be work for which payment is being claimed — you can’t reach back twelve months simply because you’ve recently done unrelated work under the same contract. Estate Property Holdings has been followed in Queensland and the ACT, and was applied (as to the principle, before the limit was reduced to six months) in Canberra Drilling Rigs Pty Ltd v Haides Pty Ltd [2018] ACTSC 282.2

Final payment claims and the s 75(3)(c) trigger

For final payment claims, the timing question shifts: when does the six-month clock under s 75(3)(c) start running? The answer is “completion of all construction work to be carried out under the contract” — not the date the contract was terminated, and not the date you stopped going to site. In Ventralia Pty Ltd v Redline Water Infrastructure Pty Ltd [2023] QSC 291 Freeburn J held that s 75(3)(c) “should not be read as if the time limit was six months from the date the contract was terminated”. Termination may set the outer limit for any further work under the contract, but it doesn’t supply the start date for the six months — that has to be measured from the point work was actually complete. His Honour expressly cautioned against over-reading S.H.A. Premier Constructions Pty Ltd v Niclin Constructions Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 307, where Bond J had observed that termination brings about the “completion” of all work under a contract for s 75(3)(c) purposes.3 The reconciliation is that termination is useful evidence of when work ended, not a substitute for the inquiry.

Two related points come from the same line of authority. First, retention claims at the end of a job sit comfortably within s 75(3): in EHome Construction Pty Ltd v GCB Constructions Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 291 Bond J held that a payment claim seeking release of retention amounts that had been deducted from earlier progress claims is “plainly” a final payment claim within s 75(3), provided it is given within the longest of the four periods. Second, while a contract is on foot the possibility of further rectification work (which is itself construction work for the period of the defects liability period) means “completion” under s 75(3)(c) is unlikely to have occurred — unless you can show that on a particular earlier date the works were complete and free of defects.

Non-compliance and the adjudicator’s role

Compliance with s 75(2)–(3) is a jurisdictional question for the adjudicator. In South East Civil and Drainage Contractors Pty Ltd v AMGW Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 45 Jackson J held that a respondent’s failure to take the timing point in the payment schedule does not authorise an adjudicator to ignore it where the issue is apparent on the face of the material the adjudicator must consider. So even if the payment schedule is silent on timing, the adjudicator has to satisfy themselves the claim was given in time.4 The flip side is that the adjudicator’s opinion on whether the claim was given within the period is generally treated as a matter within jurisdiction — the courts will review whether the adjudicator formed an opinion at all, but will not readily revisit the correctness of that opinion.5

Practice tips

  • For an ordinary progress claim, the deadline is the longer of any period in the contract and six months after the work to which the claim relates was last carried out. If your contract is silent, six months is your hard limit.
  • If you’re running close to the limit, the date that matters is the last day work to which the claim relates was actually carried out on site (or the related goods and services were last supplied) — not the date you raised the invoice, and not the reference date.
  • For a final claim, identify which of the four s 75(3) periods is longest in your circumstances. If the contract has a defects liability period, the “28 days after the end of the last defects liability period” trigger is often the most generous and is easy to overlook.
  • If the contract has been terminated, don’t assume the six months runs from termination. After Ventralia you measure it from when the work was actually complete — which may be earlier or later than the termination date.
  • If you want to recover retention at the end of the job, structure it as a final payment claim and serve it within the s 75(3) window. EHome confirms a retention-only claim qualifies.
  • For mixed claims spanning a long period, make sure at least some of the work for which payment is claimed was carried out within the last six months. After Estate Property Holdings that is enough to pull the older items along with it — provided they form part of the same claim.
  • Don’t rely on the respondent missing the timing point. Even if it isn’t in the payment schedule, an adjudicator who can see the issue on the face of the materials has to deal with it.
