Enforcing an adjudicator’s decision

What the respondent cannot raise as a defence

Once an adjudication certificate has been filed as a judgment, the respondent’s options for resisting payment are deliberately narrow. If the respondent commences proceedings to set the judgment aside, s 93(4) shuts the door on the usual contractual defences and counterclaims, and forces the disputed money into court as security. The point of the regime is to keep cash flowing to the claimant while the underlying merits are sorted out elsewhere.

Section 93(4) BIF Act — bar on defences and counterclaims, security for the unpaid amount

93 Filing of adjudication certificate as judgment debt

(4) If the respondent commences proceedings to have the judgment set aside—

(a) the respondent is not, in those proceedings, entitled—

(i) to bring any counterclaim against the claimant; or

(ii) to raise any defence in relation to matters arising under the construction contract to which the adjudication certificate relates; or

(iii) to challenge the adjudicator’s decision; and

(b) the respondent is required to pay into the court, as security, the unpaid portion of the adjudicated amount pending the final decision in those proceedings.

The three things you cannot do

Section 93(4)(a) lists three prohibitions, and they need to be read together. You cannot bring a counterclaim against the claimant in the set-aside proceeding. You cannot raise a defence to “matters arising under the construction contract” — in practice, the contractual arguments you would normally run about scope, value, defects or back-charges. And you cannot “challenge the adjudicator’s decision” on the merits as part of resisting the judgment. The matching obligation in s 93(4)(b) is that the unpaid portion of the adjudicated amount has to be paid into court as security while the proceeding runs.

The bar is not absolute, and two narrow categories of argument do still get through. The first is jurisdictional error — if the adjudicator’s decision is void, there is, in truth, no “decision” for s 93(4)(a)(iii) to protect, and the judgment built on it can be set aside. The second is a class of factual contention that goes to what is actually owing under the certificate (rather than to the underlying contract).

What counts as a “defence in relation to matters arising under the contract”

The clearest recent appellate guidance on the scope of s 93(4)(a)(ii) is the New South Wales Court of Appeal’s decision in Hynash Constructions Pty Ltd v BRP Industries Pty Ltd [2025] NSWCA 14, dealing with the equivalent NSW provision. The respondent there had failed to provide a payment schedule. Faced with a claim for the unpaid portion, it argued that an earlier 10% “establishment and mobilisation” payment it had made under the contract should be credited against the payment claim, so that the “unpaid portion” was reduced. The Court of Appeal rejected the argument: the “unpaid portion” refers to amounts unpaid in response to the payment claim itself, not unrelated prior payments under the contract. If the respondent wanted credit for a prior contractual payment, it had to put that case in a payment schedule. Trying to run it as a defence at enforcement was, in substance, a defence in relation to a matter arising under the construction contract, and so caught by the bar.

The same line is taken with equitable set-off. In Infinity Formwork Group Pty Ltd v Adobe Construction Group Pty Ltd [2023] NSWDC 220 the District Court held that an equitable set-off was within the scope of the bar — if the respondent wanted to rely on it, the place to do so was in a payment schedule, not at the summary-judgment stage. By contrast, a misleading-or-deceptive-conduct claim under the Australian Consumer Law is not a “matter arising under the construction contract” and is not caught: Marques Group Pty Ltd v Parkview Constructions Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 625.1 A factual contention that part of the claimed amount has already been paid — for example, where the claimant accepts that a direct payment to a third-party supplier should reduce the debt — is also not a counterclaim or defence within s 93(4); it goes to the unpaid portion as a matter of fact.2

Jurisdictional error and the “no decision” route

Section 93(4)(a)(iii) bars a challenge to “the adjudicator’s decision”, but it does not protect a purported decision that is void for jurisdictional error. The orthodox analysis comes from Brodyn Pty Ltd t/as Time Cost and Quality v Davenport [2004] NSWCA 394 — once the certificate is filed, the resulting judgment is not itself void, but an application can be made to set it aside on the basis that there is, in truth, no adjudicator’s determination. That route was applied in Harlech Enterprises Pty Ltd v Beno Excavations Pty Ltd t/as Benex Pipeline [2024] NSWDC 151, where Montgomery DCJ set aside a judgment and a garnishee order after the underlying determination had been quashed for jurisdictional error in a separate Supreme Court proceeding. The statutory scheme does not contemplate the enforcement of a judgment debt where the determination is infected with jurisdictional error.

The practical consequence is that if you want to resist enforcement, you generally need to be running a parallel attack on the determination itself — void for jurisdictional error — rather than trying to re-litigate the contract under the cover of the set-aside proceeding.

Pay-in as security

Section 93(4)(b) is a hard requirement, not a discretion. If you commence proceedings to set aside the judgment, the unpaid portion of the adjudicated amount has to go into court as security. That obligation reflects the same legislative policy that runs through the rest of Chapter 3: the claimant’s cash flow is preserved while the merits are determined elsewhere, and the risk of the claimant’s insolvency is, prima facie, allocated to the respondent. Stays and injunctions to hold up enforcement are available, but only where there is a serious question to be tried (typically jurisdictional error) and the balance of convenience favours intervention — and even then the courts have been emphatic that “considerable caution” should attend any such order.3

What this means on a project

  • If a payment claim has been served and you want to dispute any part of it — including by way of set-off, back-charge, prior payment or quality argument — the place to do that is the payment schedule, on time. Once you are at the s 93 enforcement stage, those arguments are barred.
  • Don’t expect to resist enforcement by re-running the contract dispute. A defence framed as “we don’t actually owe this under the contract” is exactly what s 93(4)(a)(ii) is aimed at.
  • The realistic challenges at the enforcement stage are (a) jurisdictional error in the underlying determination, and (b) factual contentions about what is actually unpaid on the certificate (e.g. payments since made, accepted third-party payments).
  • Non-contractual causes of action — misleading or deceptive conduct under the Australian Consumer Law is the obvious one — can sometimes be run, because they are not “matters arising under the construction contract”. They are still hard to win on a summary-judgment timetable.
  • If you are commencing set-aside proceedings, budget for paying the unpaid portion into court. That is a precondition of running the proceeding, not an optional step. If you cannot fund it, an undertaking to do so or a bank guarantee may sometimes be acceptable, but you will need to negotiate it.
  • If you are the claimant, the discipline is the mirror image: keep your enforcement clean, file the certificate with a proper s 93(2) affidavit, and be prepared to resist any stay application by pointing to the legislative policy and the s 93(4)(b) security requirement.
