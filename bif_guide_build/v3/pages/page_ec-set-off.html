Enforcing a subcontractor’s charge

Set-off and counterclaim by the contractor

When a subcontractor lodges a notice of claim of charge, the contractor will often look for ways to chip the claim back — cross-claims for defective work, liquidated damages, back-charges, or a security the subcontractor has lodged that the contractor wants to apply against the very money the charge attaches to. Chapter 4 lets ordinary set-offs and counterclaims run, but it draws a hard line at one specific manoeuvre: a contractor cannot use a subcontractor’s own security to defeat the contractor’s performance obligations to a third party. Section 133 is the provision that polices that line.

Section 133 BIF Act — particular subcontractor securities of no effect

133 Particular subcontractor securities of no effect

To the extent that something is given by or for a subcontractor for securing, wholly or partly, the performance by a contractor of the contractor’s contract it is of no effect.

What s 133 actually voids

Section 133 is narrow and surgical. It catches anything — cash retention, bank guarantee, parent company guarantee, charge over assets, indemnity — that is “given by or for a subcontractor” and that secures, wholly or partly, the performance by the contractor of the contractor’s contract (i.e. the head contract or the next contract up the chain). To the extent it does that work, it is “of no effect”. The drafting is deliberately pitched at the function of the security, not its label: it doesn’t matter what the document is called or how the parties have characterised it, if it operates to underwrite the contractor’s performance to a higher party using the subcontractor’s money or credit, that operation is statutorily neutralised.

What s 133 does not do is wipe out every set-off or counterclaim the contractor might assert. A genuine cross-claim arising under the subcontract — defective work, delay damages, overpayments, unreturned plant — survives s 133 untouched. Those are debts owed by the subcontractor to the contractor in the ordinary course; the section says nothing about them. It is only the artificial device of leveraging a subcontractor-supplied security upstream that is targeted.

How this interacts with the charge

The mischief s 133 addresses is obvious once you map it onto the Chapter 4 machinery. A subcontractor serves a notice of claim under s 122; money up the chain is frozen under s 126; the security holder retains funds against a s 132 order. If the contractor could point to a security “given by or for” the subcontractor and apply it to discharge the contractor’s own obligations to the principal, the subcontractor’s charge would be stripped of its substance — the very money meant to back the charge would be siphoned off to perform the contractor’s contract one level up. Section 133 closes that exit.

The provision works alongside the prohibition in s 200 on contracting out of the Act. A clause in a subcontract that purports to let the contractor apply the subcontractor’s retention or bank guarantee against the contractor’s own performance obligations to the principal is, to that extent, a nullity under s 133, and is independently unenforceable as a contracting-out under s 200.1

Set-offs that do survive

Outside the s 133 prohibition, the contractor’s ordinary entitlements are unaffected. The court in a proceeding to enforce the charge will quantify the “unsatisfied amount” for the contract, and that figure is the net amount actually owing to the subcontractor after legitimate cross-claims have been taken into account. If the contractor has a real claim for defective work or liquidated damages, it can be pleaded in answer to the underlying debt, and it will reduce or extinguish the amount the charge can attach to. The charge attaches only to what is properly owing.

The point was put squarely by McMurdo J (as his Honour then was) in Karam Group Pty Ltd v HCA Queensland Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 95, dealing with the equivalent provisions of the predecessor Subcontractors’ Charges Act 1974 (Qld). His Honour treated the charge as attaching to the balance “justly owing” to the subcontractor, after taking into account proper cross-claims under the subcontract.2 Section 133 doesn’t stop that exercise; it only stops the contractor reaching across to a security the subcontractor itself has put up to make good the contractor’s performance further up the chain.

What this means on a project

  • If you are a subcontractor, your retention or bank guarantee cannot be used by the contractor to perform the head contract or any contract above it. Any subcontract clause to that effect is void to that extent under s 133.
  • Read any “set-off” or “application of security” clause in the subcontract carefully. A clause limited to set-off of cross-claims under the subcontract is fine; a clause that lets the contractor apply your security against its obligations to the principal is not.
  • If you are a contractor, expect to have to prove your cross-claims on their merits in the charge proceeding. You can’t shortcut that by pointing to a subcontractor-supplied security and saying the principal has called on it — s 133 strips that out.
  • When framing the charge proceeding, plead the underlying debt net of any genuine subcontract cross-claims. The court’s s 132 order will move on the “unsatisfied amount”, which is the real balance owing — not a notional figure ignoring legitimate set-offs.
  • If the principal has already drawn on a subcontractor-supplied security to cover the contractor’s default upstream, that drawing is “of no effect” against the subcontractor under s 133. Raise it in the proceeding; the money should be treated as still available to the charge.
  1. Section 200 BIF Act voids any provision of an agreement that purports to exclude, modify or restrict the operation of the Act, or that may reasonably be construed as an attempt to do so. A subcontract clause permitting the contractor to apply subcontractor-supplied security to the contractor’s own performance obligations engages both s 133 and s 200.
  2. Karam Group Pty Ltd v HCA Queensland Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 95 (McMurdo J) — charge attaches to the amount justly owing to the subcontractor under the subcontract, after legitimate cross-claims; decided under the Subcontractors’ Charges Act 1974 (Qld), the predecessor to Chapter 4 of the BIF Act.
