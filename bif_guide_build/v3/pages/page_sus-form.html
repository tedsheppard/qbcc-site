Statutory suspension of works

Form and content of the notice

The right to down tools under the BIF Act doesn’t arise on its own. It is unlocked by a written notice of intention to suspend — given under s 78 (where a respondent has failed to pay a scheduled amount) or s 92 (where a respondent has failed to pay an adjudicated amount) — followed by a 2 business day waiting period. Get the notice wrong, and any subsequent suspension is a breach of contract rather than a protected statutory step.

Section 98 BIF Act — Claimant’s right to suspend work

(1) A claimant may suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under a construction contract if at least 2 business days have passed since the claimant gave notice of intention to do so to the respondent under section 78 or 92.

(2) The right conferred under subsection (1) exists until the day on which the claimant receives payment from the respondent of the amount mentioned in section 78(1) or 92(1), and continues for another 3 business days immediately following that day.

(3) If, in exercising the right to suspend carrying out construction work or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract, the claimant incurs a loss or expense because the respondent removes any part of the work or supply from the contract, the respondent is liable to pay the claimant the amount of the loss or expense.

(4) A claimant who suspends carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract under subsection (1) is not liable for any loss or damage suffered by the respondent, or by any person claiming through the respondent, because of the claimant not carrying out that work or not supplying those goods and services, during the suspension.

Which notice you are giving

Section 98 (1) requires the notice to be given “under section 78 or 92”. Those are two different gateways and they correspond to two different unpaid amounts:

  • s 78 notice — given where the respondent has not paid the scheduled amount (a payment schedule was issued but the scheduled amount wasn’t paid by the due date, or the respondent failed to schedule and didn’t pay the claimed amount).
  • s 92 notice — given where the respondent has not paid an adjudicated amount by the due date for payment of that amount.

The notice itself should state which provision it is given under. Drafting a single “notice of intention to suspend” without identifying whether it relies on the s 78 or the s 92 pathway invites a respondent to argue the notice is non-compliant. If both gateways are open on the facts — for example a scheduled amount unpaid and later adjudicated — identify the operative one based on the amount you are actually pursuing.

What the notice must convey

The Act doesn’t prescribe a form. But s 98 (1) demands that the document be a “notice of intention” to suspend, given to the respondent, under s 78 or s 92. To do its work the notice should:

  • identify the construction contract and the parties;
  • identify the unpaid amount — the scheduled amount (s 78) or the adjudicated amount (s 92) — and the date by which it should have been paid;
  • state expressly that, if the amount is not paid, the claimant intends to suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under the contract;
  • state the statutory basis (s 78 or s 92, and s 98); and
  • be dated and given to the respondent in a way you can later prove (email with delivery confirmation, or service in accordance with any contractual notice clause, recorded on the file).

The 2 business day clock in s 98 (1) runs from when the notice is “given”, so the date of giving has to be capable of proof. If the notice is delivered late in the day, count from the next business day to be safe.

What “payment” ends the suspension

The notice triggers a window that closes only on receipt of the unpaid amount, plus 3 further business days under s 98 (2). The leading reading of “payment” in this regime is Beckhaus Civil Pty Ltd v Brewarrina Shire Council (No 2) [2004] NSWSC 1160, where Master Macready treated the touchstone as the actual transfer of value to the claimant:

the touchstone of the ordinary and natural meaning of the expressions ‘to pay’ and ‘receives payment’ in the present context is the act of a debtor transferring by way of satisfaction whatever is owed by him or her to a creditor.

His Honour added that a claimant will not have “received payment” where conditions placed on the funds — such as a secured bank guarantee — prevent the claimant from obtaining the benefit of them. So a notice is not spent simply because the respondent has put money somewhere; it is spent only when the claimant can actually use it.

The same point was reinforced in On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163, where Crowe AJ held that the right to suspend continues until either the underlying amount is set aside, or there is an unequivocal communication acknowledging discharge of the payment claim, adjudicated amount, or provisional judgment debt.1 The practical lesson for the notice drafter is that, once the notice has done its work, the claimant’s suspension right keeps running until something concrete — payment in fact, or a clear setting-aside — closes it out. You don’t need to issue a fresh notice every few days.

What this means on a project

  • Use a clean, dated, written notice. Identify the contract, the unpaid amount, and the statutory basis (s 78 or s 92, and s 98). Keep it short.
  • Send it in a way you can prove. Email with read receipt, or service per the contract’s notice clause. The 2 business day clock under s 98 (1) only starts when you can show the notice was given.
  • Don’t suspend before the 2 business days have actually passed. A premature suspension is unprotected by s 98 (4) and exposes you to a damages claim from the respondent.
  • Once you suspend, don’t stand the suspension down on a vague promise of payment. Under Beckhaus, “received payment” means money you can actually use; conditional or encumbered transfers don’t end the s 98 window.
  • If the respondent removes scope from the contract while you are lawfully suspended, log the loss or expense — s 98 (3) gives you a direct statutory entitlement to recover it.
  1. On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163 (Crowe AJ) was decided under s 26(2)(b) of the ACT security of payment Act, the equivalent of s 98 (1) of the BIF Act; Crowe AJ held that constructive receipt of the adjudicated amount being set aside occurred only after consent orders were made, and that the right to suspend then ended 3 business days after that date, mirroring s 98 (2).
