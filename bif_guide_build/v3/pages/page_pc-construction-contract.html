Requirements of a payment claim

A construction contract must exist

Chapter 3 of the BIF Act applies only where there is a “construction contract” between the claimant and the respondent. Without one, no payment claim is available and any adjudication that follows lacks jurisdictional basis.

Section 64 BIF Act — “construction contract”

construction contract means a contract, agreement or other arrangement under which 1 party undertakes to carry out construction work for, or to supply related goods and services to, another party.

Can include verbal agreements

An oral agreement may constitute a valid "construction contract", as with a partly-oral / partly-written exchange — for example, a series of emails confirming scope and price, or a sequence of agreed meeting minutes.1

The work being undertaken must be construction work, or the supply of related goods and services in connection with construction work. Those concepts are defined separately at s 65 and s 66 and are dealt with on the next two pages.

“Other arrangement”

The “other arrangement” words operate to capture dealings that do not satisfy the legal definition of a contract — for instance, a head contractor’s direction to a subcontractor to perform variation work where the parties are operating informally — without extending to every situation in which work has been performed by one party for another. The location of that line has generated the majority of case law under this part of the Act.

The current position is that an “other arrangement” must give rise to either:

  • a legally binding obligation (not necessarily a contract — an estoppel or other source of legal compulsion can suffice, provided some legal consequence flows from it); or
  • a “concluded state of affairs” involving reciprocity — that is, both parties have accepted mutual rights and obligations relating to payment or price for the works.

[insert re Lendlease vs Crown Green]

The most recent appellate case is Bettar Holdings Pty Ltd trading as Hunt Collaborative v RWC Brookvale Investment Pty Ltd as trustee for Brookvale Development Trust [2025] NSWCA 242. The pleading in Bettar alleged only a common law contract or, in the alternative, an estoppel preventing denial of a contract. The NSW Court of Appeal held that was insufficient: what was missing was a separate allegation that the claimant had “undertaken” to perform work on some basis other than a common law contract. McHugh JA (Bell CJ and Kirk JA agreeing) expressed the test for an “other arrangement” in these terms:

[139] a legally binding obligation… or a concluded state of affairs… involving some element of reciprocity or acceptance of mutual rights and obligations relating to payment or price for the works.

His Honour expressly declined to resolve the earlier first-instance conflict between Lendlease Engineering Pty Ltd v Timecon Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 685 (Ball J — required a legally binding obligation) and Crown Green Square Pty Ltd v Transport for NSW [2018] NSWSC 1080 (McDougall J — adopted a less stringent reciprocity-based test),2 because the pleading in Bettar failed under either test. The question of which test applies in close cases therefore remains open at appellate level.

A bare quantum-meruit claim — where work has been done but there is no agreement at all about price or payment terms — is unlikely to qualify: [insert].  The “arrangement” requires reciprocal payment obligations, not merely the fact that work was performed.

Practice tips

  • Where there is a signed contract, a construction contract exists, and the analysis proceeds to the next element.
  • An engagement evidenced by exchange of emails, purchase orders or agreed meeting minutes remains capable of being a construction contract. The terms agreed and the timing of agreement should be documented; that evidence may be required later.
  • For variation or out-of-scope work without a written variation order, the instruction and the response should be recorded in writing as soon as practicable. Many “other arrangement” disputes turn on whether there is evidence of agreement on price or rate.
  1. That a written instrument is not required follows from the words “contract, agreement or other arrangement” in s 64: see Okaroo Pty Ltd v Vos Construction & Joinery Pty Ltd [2005] NSWSC 45 (Nicholas J); Machkevitch v Andrew Building Constructions Pty Ltd [2012] NSWSC 546 (McDougall J).
  2. Lendlease Engineering Pty Ltd v Timecon Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 685, [29]–[33] (Ball J); Crown Green Square Pty Ltd v Transport for NSW [2018] NSWSC 1080 (McDougall J). For Queensland, Capricorn Quarries Pty Ltd v Devcon Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 28 (Daubney J) is the leading reading of the contract limb of the BIF Act definition.
