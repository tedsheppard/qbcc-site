Enforcing a subcontractor’s charge

Effect of insolvency of the contractor

If the contractor up the chain becomes insolvent, the subcontractor’s charge is often the only thing standing between you and the unsecured-creditor queue. The charge attaches to money owing from the principal to the contractor, not to the contractor’s general assets, so the money you have hit with a notice of claim under s 122 is not part of the contractor’s pool on liquidation. The practical question that then arises is: how far does the charge reach? Section 137 answers that question for the common case where, by the time the contractor falls over, all that is left in the principal’s hands is the retention.

Section 137 BIF Act — claims of charge relating to retention amount not limited

137 Claims of charge relating to retention amount not limited

To remove any doubt, it is declared that, for a subcontractor’s charge relating only to a retention amount—

(a) the charge to which the subcontractor is entitled is not limited to merely a charge on a retention amount; and

(b) the charge to which the subcontractor is entitled is not limited to merely a charge on a security that has been exchanged for, or is held instead of, a retention amount.

Why this section exists

By the time a contractor goes into administration or liquidation, progress payments have usually stopped flowing and the only money the principal is still holding under the head contract is the retention — either retained cash, or a bank guarantee or other security exchanged for retention. A natural reading of the older language might have been that a subcontractor whose notice of claim catches the contract at that point is limited to a charge on the retention itself, and nothing else. Section 137 is drafted as a declaratory provision (“to remove any doubt, it is declared”) precisely to shut that reading down.

The section says two things, and they need to be read together. Paragraph (a) confirms that the charge is not limited to merely a charge on the retention amount. Paragraph (b) confirms that the charge is not limited to merely a charge on a security that has been exchanged for, or is held instead of, the retention — typically a bank guarantee or insurance bond. So whether the principal is sitting on cash retention, or has swapped that retention for a guarantee, the subcontractor’s charge reaches the wider pool of money owing under the head contract, not just the slice the parties have labelled “retention”.

How the section interacts with insolvency

The contractor’s insolvency does not, of itself, defeat a subcontractor’s charge. The charge bites on the principal’s payment obligation to the contractor under the head contract, and a liquidator stands in the contractor’s shoes — the liquidator takes the receivable subject to the charge, not free of it. Section 137 matters in that setting because the receivable that survives the insolvency is most often the retention. Without s 137 a liquidator could plausibly argue that the surviving claim is just “the retention” and that the subcontractor’s charge is correspondingly capped. Section 137 forecloses that argument: a charge that ends up “relating only to a retention amount” (because that is all the principal still has to pay) is not for that reason confined to the retention.

Read with the rest of Chapter 4, this means the s 130 and s 131 machinery for the use of security pending a court order under s 132 still has full work to do after the contractor’s insolvency. The principal continues to retain the security or pay it into court; the subcontractor still has to commence proceedings to obtain a s 132 order; and the order, when made, can reach the retained funds and any security exchanged for them.

What it does not do

Section 137 expands the reach of the charge; it does not enlarge what is owing. The charge is still capped by the “unsatisfied amount” and the “difference amount” on the head contract under s 130 and s 131. If the principal genuinely owes the contractor nothing beyond the retention, the charge will, as a matter of arithmetic, fall on the retention; what s 137 prevents is the charge being legally confined to the retention by reason of its label. Nor does s 137 alter the time-limit, response or court-proceeding requirements elsewhere in Chapter 4 — the rest of the enforcement machinery applies in the ordinary way.

Practice tips

  • If the contractor above you is heading into administration or liquidation, get a notice of claim under s 122 served on the principal as soon as you can. The charge attaches to money owing under the head contract, which sits outside the contractor’s insolvent estate.
  • Don’t assume that, because retention is the only money still in the principal’s hands, your charge is capped at the retention figure. Section 137 is express that it is not.
  • The same point applies if the principal has swapped cash retention for a bank guarantee or other security — the charge isn’t limited to that substituted security either.
  • Section 137 does not change the rest of Chapter 4. You still need a valid notice under s 122, a copy to the contractor under s 123, attention to the contractor’s response under s 128, and a court proceeding seeking a s 132 order within the statutory time limit. The liquidator’s appointment doesn’t pause those steps.
  • When you frame the proceeding, plead the “unsatisfied amount” and “difference amount” on the head contract, not just the retention figure. Section 137 is the answer to any argument from a liquidator or principal that the charge is confined to the retention slice.
  1. Section 137 BIF Act is declaratory in form (“to remove any doubt, it is declared”) and operates on the scope of a subcontractor’s charge that relates only to a retention amount. It does not create a free-standing entitlement; it qualifies the way a charge under Chapter 4 is to be read where, on the facts, the surviving claim against the principal is the retention or a security exchanged for it.
  2. The s 130 and s 131 machinery for the use of security — retention, payment to the subcontractor where liability is accepted, or payment into court where it is disputed — continues to operate after the contractor’s insolvency. The court order under s 132 remains the mechanism by which the retained security or money paid into court is released to the subcontractor.
