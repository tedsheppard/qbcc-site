Requirements of an adjudicator’s decision

Consider only what is permitted

Section 88(2) does two jobs at once. It tells the adjudicator what they are allowed to look at — and, by saying “the following matters only”, it locks the door on everything else. Just as importantly, the courts read the subsection as mandating consideration of every item on that list. A failure to engage with one of those items, where the point is material, is jurisdictional error and will void the decision.1

Section 88(2) BIF Act — matters the adjudicator must consider

88 Adjudicator’s decision

(2) In deciding an adjudication application, the adjudicator is to consider the following matters only—

(a) the provisions of this chapter and, to the extent they are relevant, the provisions of the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991, part 4A;

(b) the provisions of the relevant construction contract;

(c) the payment claim to which the application relates, together with all submissions, including relevant documents, that have been properly made by the claimant in support of the claim;

(d) the payment schedule, if any, to which the application relates, together with all submissions, including relevant documents, that have been properly made by the respondent in support of the schedule;

(e) the results of any inspection carried out by the adjudicator of any matter to which the claim relates.

What “consider” actually means

The obligation to “consider” each matter in s 88(2) is not a tick-box exercise. It is a substantive duty to engage. The canonical formulation is McDougall J’s in Laing O’Rourke Australia Construction v H & M Engineering & Construction [2010] NSWSC 818, repeatedly quoted in Queensland:

As a matter of plain English, the obligation to “consider” something requires that it be given attention, or looked at on its merits… the obligation to consider various matters imposed by s 22(2) of the Act should be read… as requiring an active process of intellectual engagement. It may be thought that this imposes a substantial burden on adjudicators. That may be so; but… the parties to an adjudication are entitled to have the adjudicator’s consideration, in the sense that I have explained, of the case that each of them brings.

That standard has been picked up in Queensland in QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292, where Peter Lyons J held that a failure to consider submissions of real significance is a failure to comply with a basic and essential requirement of the Act, and the resulting decision is invalid.2 The duty is real, but it is not unlimited — an adjudicator does not have to deal with every contention explicitly, and a failure to mention a particular submission in the reasons is not, by itself, proof that it was not considered.3

The five matters — and how they fit together

Paragraphs (a) and (b) put the legal framework in front of the adjudicator: the BIF Act itself (and the relevant licensing provisions of the QBCC Act), and the terms of the construction contract between the parties. Paragraphs (c) and (d) put the parties’ cases in front of the adjudicator: the payment claim and the claimant’s properly-made submissions, the payment schedule and the respondent’s properly-made submissions. Paragraph (e) covers anything the adjudicator has seen on a site inspection. Together, these are the universe of permissible inputs.

The shape of paragraphs (a) and (b) versus (c) and (d) matters. The legal framework (a)–(b) is not gated by what the parties have argued: an adjudicator is entitled (and in some cases obliged) to take account of provisions of the Act or the contract that they consider of real relevance, even if a party did not raise them in submissions properly made. Hodgson JA explained the point in The Minister for Commerce v Contrax Plumbing (NSW) Pty Ltd & Ors [2005] NSWCA 142, refining it in John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads & Traffic Authority of New South Wales & Ors [2007] NSWCA 19: the threshold is awareness of the matter plus a belief that it is of real relevance to issues arising under paragraphs (a) or (b).4 Paragraphs (c) and (d), by contrast, are tied to what the parties put forward in their submissions properly made; an adjudicator cannot range outside the dispute the parties have presented.

What “properly made” means — and who decides

Paragraphs (c) and (d) only oblige the adjudicator to consider submissions that have been “properly made”. Whether a submission has been properly made — on time, in the form required, and not introducing reasons that should not be there — is a matter the legislature entrusts to the adjudicator to decide. A wrong call on whether a submission was properly made is generally an error within jurisdiction, not a jurisdictional error: see Civmec Electrical & Instrumental Pty Ltd v Southern Cross Electrical Engineering Limited & Ors [2019] QSC 300 (Mullins J).5 But there is a flip side: if the adjudicator concludes within jurisdiction that a submission was not properly made, that is the end of any natural-justice complaint about not considering it.6

Failing to consider a submission that was properly made

The clearest Queensland illustration of how s 88(2) bites is Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 16. The adjudicator decided that Descon was entitled to release of the retention and dismissed Iris’s submission under clause 5.7(a) of the contract. Wilson J held that the adjudicator’s decision was affected by jurisdictional error on two grounds. The first was procedural — the adjudicator had decided the case partly on clause 5.2, which neither party had relied on. The second is the more directly relevant for present purposes:

Wilson J held that, even putting natural justice to one side, the adjudicator had not “considered” Iris’s submissions about the effect of clause 5.7(a), as mandated by s 88(2)(d) of the BIF Act. Engaging with the submission required actually grappling with what it said, not merely noting that the respondent had “a claim” without further engagement. The decision was declared void in part. The case is a useful reminder that s 88(2) is not just a list of permitted inputs — it is a list of things the adjudicator has to do, and a failure to do them on a material point will not survive judicial review.7

What this means on a project

  • Build your application or response so the adjudicator cannot miss the key points. Identify each contractual provision and each submission you rely on, with cross-references back to the payment claim or schedule, so that engagement (or non-engagement) is visible on the face of any later determination.
  • If you are the respondent, get your reasons into the payment schedule. Anything you raise for the first time in the adjudication response is at risk of being treated as not properly made under s 88(2)(d) and excluded from consideration — and the adjudicator’s view on that point is generally not reviewable.
  • If you are reading a determination and a key submission is simply absent from the reasons, that is a flag — but not, on its own, a winning ground. You need to be able to show that the omission reflects a failure of intellectual engagement, not a permissible decision to give the submission no weight or to treat it as not properly made.
  • Don’t over-stretch a “failure to consider” argument. After Niclin Constructions and Martinus Rail, the courts will not infer a failure to consider from silence in the reasons unless the omission is significant and unexplained.3
  • Where a contractual provision or BIF Act provision is plainly engaged but neither party has run it, expect the adjudicator to be entitled to raise it — and watch for a request for further submissions. If you don’t get one before the adjudicator decides on a basis neither side ran, you are into natural-justice territory rather than s 88(2) proper.
