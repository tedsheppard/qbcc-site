Valid subcontractor’s charge

Service of the notice

A subcontractor’s charge under chapter 4 is a creature of statute, and it only comes into existence if two notices reach two different people the right way. Section 122 requires the notice of claim to go to the party above your contractor in the chain — the person obliged to pay the money — and s 123 requires a copy to go to your own contractor. Get either piece of service wrong and the consequence written into the Act is the same: the notice is “of no effect and the subcontractor’s charge does not attach”.

Sections 122 and 123 BIF Act — who is served, and what happens if you miss

122 Notice of claim

(1) To claim a subcontractor’s charge over money payable to the contractor under the contract, the subcontractor must give written notice (a notice of claim) to the person obliged to pay the money under the contract.

(9) If the notice of claim is not given in compliance with this section, the notice is of no effect and the subcontractor’s charge does not attach.

123 Copy of notice of claim to contractor

(1) This section applies if a subcontractor gives a notice of claim to a person obliged to pay money to a contractor under a contract.

(2) The subcontractor must—

(a) give the contractor a copy of the notice of claim; and

(b) advise the contractor of the name and address of the person given the notice of claim.

(3) If the subcontractor does not comply with subsection (2), the notice is of no effect and the subcontractor’s charge does not attach.

Two notices, two recipients

The chapter draws a careful distinction between the “notice of claim” itself, which is what creates the charge, and the copy that must go to the contractor under s 123. The notice of claim is served on “the person obliged to pay the money under the contract” — in practice the principal, employer or superior contractor sitting one step above your contractor in the chain. The copy goes to the contractor with whom you have your subcontract. Both must happen, and both must hit the right person.

Identifying the right payer matters because a notice given to the wrong entity will normally not save the charge. In Walsh & Donaghay v Queensland (Dist Ct Mackay, Wylie QCJ, 20 May 1987) a notice that named “The Manager, State Works Department” rather than the “State of Queensland” only survived because the notice in fact came into the possession of the employer through its agents; the inaccuracy was rescued by actual receipt, not by the form of the document. The lesson is to check the head contract before drafting: who is named as principal, what is their correct legal name, and what address have they given for service.1 A notice naming a wholly-owned subsidiary of the employer is not necessarily fatal where the subsidiary is in truth the contracting entity (Paks Contractors Pty Ltd v Maruko Inc [1993] 1 Qd R 119), but the safer course is to get the name right first time.

The s 123 copy — speed matters

Section 123 doesn’t set its own time limit, but the courts have read in a requirement of commercial urgency. In Re Austco Pty Ltd [1985] 2 Qd R 1, Thomas J described the obligation to give the contractor a notice of having made the claim as “something to be done with as little delay as possible” — a question of fact in each case, but one that “should commonly result in that notice being given to the contractor within a few days of the notice of claim of charge being given to the employer” (at p 4). The order of the two steps is not jurisdictional: the Court of Appeal in Ed Ahern Plumbing (Gold Coast) Pty Ltd v JM Kelly (Project Builders) Pty Ltd [2008] 2 Qd R 123; [2007] QCA 452 confirmed that a charge can attach where the copy is provided to the contractor before the notice has actually been transmitted to the employer, provided the subcontractor has at least taken the steps necessary to send it on (Holmes JA at [46]).2

Failure to serve the s 123 copy at all is a hard line. As Weld M put it in the unreported Re Austco decision, if the subcontractor fails to give notice of having made the claim to the contractor to whom the money is payable, “a charge does not attach”.3 Service on the contractor at somewhere other than its place of residence or registered office has been held to have the same effect (Re Peter Bushnell & Co Pty Ltd [1985] 2 Qd R 383).

How to actually serve — AIA s 39 and the corporate rules

Chapter 4 itself doesn’t prescribe a method of service, so the default rules in s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) do the work. For an individual, that means delivering the notice personally, or leaving it at, or sending it by post, fax or similar facility to, the address of the person’s last-known place of residence or business. For a body corporate, it means leaving it at, or sending it by post or similar facility to, the head office, a registered office or a principal office. Section 109X of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) is also available for companies — leaving or posting to the registered office, or personal delivery to a director resident in Australia — and is not an exclusive code (Howship Holdings Pty Ltd v Leslie (No 2) (1996) 41 NSWLR 542).

Two cautions follow from the cases decided under the cognate s 102 service regime in chapter 3. First, service on a post office box is not, by itself, valid service under s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act or s 109X of the Corporations Act: CMF Projects Pty Ltd v Masic Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 209, [23] (Daubney J). Unless the contract authorises PO box service, do not use one for either the s 122 notice or the s 123 copy. Second, electronic service is not safely assumed. In Conveyor & General Engineering Pty Ltd v Basetec Services Pty Ltd & Anor [2014] QSC 30, McMurdo J held that emailing a link to documents on Dropbox was not effective service under either s 39 or s 109X — the document itself had not been “sent” or “left” at the relevant office. If you are going to email the s 122 notice or the s 123 copy, send the document itself to an address that is plainly the recipient’s business or registered email, and keep evidence of delivery.4

If you are relying on post under s 39A, hold the receipts. Service is taken to occur when the letter would be delivered in the ordinary course of post, but only if the contrary is not proved — and the onus of proving that it was properly addressed, prepaid and posted sits with the subcontractor. Bare evidence of attending a post office on a particular date will not always be enough to make out the regularity of postage.

Practice tips

  • Map the chain before you draft. Identify the legal entity that is “the person obliged to pay the money” to your contractor — usually the principal or superior contractor — and find their correct corporate name and registered office. Get this from ASIC, not from the project directory.
  • Serve the s 122 notice on the payer at a recognised s 39 / s 109X address: head office, registered office, principal place of business, or to a director personally. Avoid PO boxes unless the head contract authorises them.
  • Serve the s 123 copy on your own contractor at its place of residence or registered office, and do it within days of the s 122 notice. “A few days” is the working benchmark from Re Austco; sitting on it because the section has no express deadline is not safe.
  • Don’t forget s 123(2)(b) — the copy must be accompanied by advice of the name and address of the person you served with the notice of claim. Putting the payer’s details in a covering letter to the contractor satisfies this in practice.
  • Use a service method you can prove. Personal delivery with a sign-off, courier with proof of delivery, registered post with tracking, or email to a clearly nominated business address all work. Keep the tracking numbers and delivery confirmations on file.
  • Avoid Dropbox links, OneDrive shares and similar “cloud” mechanisms for either notice. A link is not the document, and the chapter 4 service regime does not assume the recipient will open it.
  • If the head contract or your subcontract specifies a notice address or a method of service for the relevant party, follow it as well as the statutory route. Belt and braces is cheap; a charge that does not attach is not.
