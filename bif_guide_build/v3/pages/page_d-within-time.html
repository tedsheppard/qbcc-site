Requirements of an adjudicator’s decision

Decided within time

Once an adjudicator is appointed, the clock starts running. The Act fixes hard deadlines for the decision — 10 business days for a standard payment claim, 15 for a complex one — with a limited mechanism in s 86 to extend. In Queensland, unlike NSW or Victoria, missing those deadlines is fatal: the decision is void.

Section 85 BIF Act — time for deciding adjudication application

(1) Subject to section 86, an adjudicator must decide an adjudication application no later than—

(a) for a standard payment claim—10 business days after the response date; or

(b) for a complex payment claim—15 business days after the response date.

(2) The response date is—

(a) if the adjudicator is given an adjudication response under section 83—the day on which the adjudicator receives the response; or

(b) if the respondent is prevented from giving the adjudicator an adjudication response under section 82(2)—the last day on which the respondent could have given the adjudicator an adjudication response under section 83 had it not been prevented from doing so under section 82(2); or

(c) otherwise—the last day on which the respondent could give the adjudicator an adjudication response under section 83.

(3) An adjudicator must not decide an adjudication application before the end of the period that the respondent may give an adjudication response to the adjudicator under section 83, unless—

(a) the adjudicator decides he or she does not have jurisdiction to adjudicate the application; or

(b) the adjudicator decides the application is frivolous or vexatious.

Working out the response date

The deadline runs from the “response date”, not from the day the application was lodged. Section 85(2) gives three possibilities. If the respondent does file an adjudication response under s 83, the response date is the day the adjudicator receives it. If the respondent is locked out from responding (because no payment schedule was given on time, under s 82(2)), the response date is the last day the respondent could otherwise have responded. If no response is filed but the respondent could have, it’s the last day they could have done so. From whichever of those dates applies, count 10 business days for a standard claim or 15 for a complex one.

Extensions under s 86

Section 86 is the only lawful way to push past the s 85(1) deadline. The parties can agree in writing — either before or after the maximum period expires — that the adjudicator has additional time. For complex claims only, the adjudicator can take an extra 5 business days unilaterally if the parties have failed to agree. Any extension must be notified to the registrar within 4 business days. An adjudicator cannot manufacture an extension by, for example, imposing a tight deadline on the parties to object and treating silence as consent: in Allpro v Micos [2010] NSWSC 453, McDougall J held that s 86’s equivalent “requires agreement. That means real or actual agreement”, and an adjudicator is not entitled to purport to impose agreement of the parties.1

Queensland: out of time means void

The Queensland position diverges from the NSW and Victorian authorities. Interstate, courts have consistently held that the time limit is directory, not jurisdictional — an out-of-time decision remains valid.2 Queensland has gone the other way. In Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd t/a CCA Winslow & Ors [2020] QSC 51, Dalton J held that an adjudication decision handed down outside the s 85 timeframe is void. Her Honour reasoned that the legislature’s “particularity and precision” in drafting the Queensland time limits, combined with the existence of s 86 as a deliberate extension mechanism, signalled that compliance with s 85 was essential. The Court of Appeal affirmed that view in Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd & Ors; Jones v Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd & Ors [2021] QCA 10, distinguishing the NSW and Victorian authorities on the basis that the BIF Act’s wording is materially different.

the changes to the current Queensland legislation were made after all the extant interstate decisions on similar provisions.

That distinction matters in practice: if you are dealing with a BIF Act adjudication, do not rely on the NSW or Victorian line of cases. The decision must be made — and, after Karam Group, communicated to the parties — within the s 85 window or it is a nullity.

The decision must also be communicated within time

It is not enough for the adjudicator to have written the decision before the deadline. In Karam Group Pty Ltd as Trustee for Karam (No. 1) Family Trust v HCA Queensland Pty Ltd & Ors [2022] QSC 290, Williams J held that an adjudicator who purportedly made their decision in time, but did not notify the parties until after the s 85(1) period had expired, had failed to make the decision within time. The decision was invalid. The s 88 obligations — including notice to the parties — form part of what it means to “decide” the application within the statutory period.3

What this means on a project

  • From the day the adjudicator is given the response (or the day the response period closes), diary 10 business days for a standard claim or 15 for a complex claim. That’s your decision deadline absent an extension.
  • If the adjudicator asks for more time, your agreement must be in writing under s 86(1) — an email is fine, silence is not. Do not let an adjudicator assume consent from non-response.
  • For a complex claim, the adjudicator can take an extra 5 business days without your agreement. Build that into your cashflow planning.
  • Watch the notification step. Under Karam Group, the decision is not “made” until it has been communicated to the parties. If the adjudicator’s reasons land on day 11 of a standard claim, the decision is void in Queensland even if the document is dated earlier.
  • If the decision lands out of time and you are the respondent facing enforcement, take advice immediately about a declaration that the decision is void on Galaxy Developments. The Queensland position is settled at appellate level and is materially more favourable to respondents than the position interstate.
  • If you are the claimant and the adjudicator has run out of time, your remedy is to withdraw and re-lodge under s 87 within the statutory window — you cannot rely on a late decision.
