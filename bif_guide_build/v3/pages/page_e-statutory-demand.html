Enforcing an adjudicator’s decision

Statutory demand (insolvency pathway)

The court-judgment route under s 93 is not the only way to put pressure on an unpaid respondent that is a company. An adjudicated amount is a debt due and payable, and once it is unpaid you can use it as the foundation for a creditor’s statutory demand under s 459E of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth). If the company doesn’t pay or apply to set the demand aside within 21 days, it is presumed insolvent — which opens the door to a winding-up application. Used carefully, that pressure often produces payment faster than enforcement of a filed judgment.

Section 93 BIF Act — underlying enforcement provision

93 Filing of adjudication certificate as judgment debt

(1) An adjudication certificate may be filed as a judgment for a debt, and may be enforced, in a court of competent jurisdiction.

(2) An adjudication certificate can not be filed under this section unless it is accompanied by an affidavit by the claimant stating that the whole or a part of the adjudicated amount has not been paid to the claimant at the time the certificate is filed.

(3) If the affidavit states that part of the adjudicated amount has been paid, the judgment is for the unpaid part of the amount only.

How the security-of-payment debt fits the Corporations Act

Section 459H of the Corporations Act lets a company apply to set aside a statutory demand on the basis of a “genuine dispute” about the existence or amount of the debt, or because the company has an “offsetting claim”. The threshold for a genuine dispute is famously low — the company only needs to show that even one issue has “a sufficient degree of cogency to be arguable”.1 The question is how much of that machinery survives where the debt is one created by the security-of-payment regime.

The leading approach is the analysis of Brereton J in Re Douglas Aerospace Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 167. His Honour drew a sharp line between the two limbs of s 459H. A statutory debt under the security-of-payment legislation — for example, a debt that has crystallised because the respondent failed to give a payment schedule — is generally not capable of being the subject of a “genuine dispute”, because the underlying contractual arguments have been shut out by the Act itself. But the company can still raise an “offsetting claim”: a counterclaim, set-off or cross-demand that does not deny the debt but asserts a countervailing liability the claimant owes back. The position was put as follows:

some debts are not capable of genuine dispute — because they are judgment debts or conclusive debts — but may still be amenable to an offsetting claim. It is difficult to see how a debt arising under s 14(4) (“SOPA”), which creates a statutory liability upon the failure to provide a payment schedule, could be the subject of a genuine dispute, if the conditions in s 15(1) are satisfied, regardless of any underlying dispute. As it seems to me, the only way in which a “genuine dispute” could be raised in respect of such a debt would be by disputing whether the circumstances referred to in s 15(1) existed.

The practical effect for a Queensland project is that a claimant holding an unpaid adjudicated amount — or an unpaid scheduled amount — has a very strong base on which to issue a s 459E demand. The respondent cannot reopen the underlying construction contract dispute to manufacture a “genuine dispute”; it must either point to an offsetting claim, or argue that the threshold facts that make the debt a statutory debt are themselves not made out (for example, that the document relied on was not a valid payment claim, or that no contract existed in the first place).2

Drafting the demand — describe the debt accurately

A statutory demand can be defeated on its own face if it doesn’t make clear what the debt is and where it comes from. In Re Roberts Construction Group Pty Ltd [2024] VSC 679 a demand based on an unpaid scheduled amount under the Victorian SOP Act was set aside because it didn’t identify the “scheduled amount”, the document under which the amount was due, or the SOP Act itself. Steffensen AsJ treated that as a substantive defect: the recipient of a statutory demand is entitled to know unambiguously what kind of debt is being asserted and on what legal footing, otherwise it cannot meaningfully decide whether to pay, dispute or move to set aside within the 21-day window.

For an adjudicated amount under s 93 BIF Act, that means the demand should identify the payment claim, the adjudication application, the adjudicator’s decision (with reference number and date), the adjudicated amount and the due date for payment, and should expressly state that the debt arises under the BIF Act. If you have already filed the certificate as a judgment debt, the demand can rest on the judgment — which is conclusive and even harder to disturb.

Practice tips

  • If the company that owes you an adjudicated amount looks marginal financially, a s 459E statutory demand is often a faster lever than an enforcement warrant. The 21-day clock and the insolvency presumption focus the mind.
  • Build the demand on the cleanest possible debt. A filed judgment under s 93 is strongest; an adjudicated amount that is overdue but not yet filed is next; an unpaid scheduled amount or unscheduled payment claim is workable but more vulnerable to argument about whether the threshold conditions were met.
  • Describe the debt in the demand with precision — identify the payment claim, the adjudication, the adjudicated amount, the due date, and that the debt is owed under the BIF Act. After Roberts Construction Group, vague or mis-described demands get set aside.
  • Don’t expect to brush away every set-aside application. The respondent cannot rely on the underlying contract dispute as a “genuine dispute”, but it can still raise a credible offsetting claim — for liquidated damages, defective work, or backcharges that exist independently of the payment-claim dispute. Be ready to meet that on its merits.
  • If you are the respondent on the receiving end of a demand based on an adjudicated amount, focus your set-aside application on either (a) a quantified offsetting claim supported by evidence, or (b) the threshold facts — was there a construction contract, was the document a valid payment claim, was the debt-creating condition under the Act actually satisfied. A general protest that “we dispute the work” will not get you across the line.
