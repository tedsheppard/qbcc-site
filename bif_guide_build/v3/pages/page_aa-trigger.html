Requirements of an adjudication application

A trigger condition must exist

You can’t apply for adjudication just because you are unhappy with how your payment claim has been treated. Section 79(1) sets out a closed list of trigger events — one of them must have happened, otherwise the registrar has no application to receive and any adjudication that follows is built on sand.

Section 79(1) BIF Act — when a claimant may apply

(1) A claimant may apply to the registrar for adjudication of a payment claim (an adjudication application) if—

(a) the claimant is entitled to apply for adjudication under section 78(2)(b) because of a failure by the respondent to pay an amount owed to the claimant by the due date for the payment; or

(b) the amount stated in the payment schedule, given in response to the payment claim, is less than the amount stated in the payment claim.

The three triggers in practice

Although s 79(1) reads as two paragraphs, the cross-reference to s 78(2)(b) pulls in the s 78 definition of “amount owed” and produces three distinct factual triggers. Each one matches a different time-bar in s 79(2)(b), so identifying the correct trigger is the first thing to do before you start counting business days.

  • No payment schedule, no payment. The respondent didn’t respond with a payment schedule under s 76 and didn’t pay the full amount claimed by the due date. The “amount owed” defaults to the amount claimed (s 78(5)(a)). Time-bar: 30 business days under s 79(2)(b)(i).
  • Schedule given, but not paid in full. The respondent did issue a payment schedule under s 76, the scheduled amount was something less than nil-but-greater-than-the-claim (i.e. the schedule effectively accepted part of the claim), and then they didn’t pay the scheduled amount by the due date. The “amount owed” is the scheduled amount (s 78(5)(b)). Time-bar: 20 business days under s 79(2)(b)(ii).
  • Scheduled amount less than claimed. The respondent issued a payment schedule under s 76 and the amount in it is less than the amount in the payment claim — whether or not anything has actually been paid. This is the s 79(1)(b) trigger and it is independent of any failure to pay. Time-bar: 30 business days from receipt of the schedule under s 79(2)(b)(iii).

Triggers one and two share a feature that trigger three does not: they depend on the respondent failing to pay by the due date. Trigger three operates the moment the schedule is received, because the dispute is about the gap between the claim and the schedule, not about the cash.

The election point under s 78(2)

Section 78(2) gives a claimant who is owed an amount two options — sue for the unpaid portion as a debt, or apply for adjudication. They are framed as alternatives, and once an adjudication application has been validly “made” the claimant is locked out of the debt route for the same amount.1 The election only bites where there is a valid trigger in the first place: in Kyle Bay Removals Pty Ltd v Dynabuild Project Services Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 334, Meagher JA held that where no valid payment claim had been served, no liability to pay attached and there could be no binding election under the s 78 equivalent. So if the underlying claim was bad, suing in court doesn’t prevent a later adjudication when a valid claim is finally made.

Telling the triggers apart matters

Misidentifying the trigger is one of the easier ways to lose an application. The leading Queensland illustration is Gisley Investments Pty Ltd v Williams & Anor [2010] QSC 178, where the claimant treated a “relatively informal” email as not amounting to a payment schedule and applied on the no-schedule trigger long after the 30-day window from receipt of that email had closed. Douglas J approached the question as one of statutory construction — was it a purpose of the Act that an application made on the wrong trigger should be invalid? — and held that, on the facts, the real objects of the Act (notice of the claim, opportunity to respond, hearing before the adjudicator) had been met, so the adjudicator’s decision was not a nullity. Gisley is a saving decision rather than a licence: don’t rely on it. If there is any chance the respondent’s document is a payment schedule, treat it as one and apply on the s 79(1)(b) trigger within 30 business days of receipt.

One further point on s 79(1)(b). The trigger is that the scheduled amount is “less than the amount stated in the payment claim”. A nil schedule is less than the claim, so a nil response is enough — you don’t need any payment failure on top. The wording also doesn’t require the schedule to be a valid one in every respect; what matters is that something purporting to be a payment schedule has been given and the dollar figure in it falls short.

What this means on a project

  • Before you start counting time, identify which of the three triggers you are running on. The trigger fixes both your time-bar and the “amount owed” you can pursue.
  • If you receive anything from the respondent in the schedule window — even an informal email pushing back on the claim — assume it is a payment schedule. Apply within 30 business days of receiving it under s 79(1)(b) rather than waiting out the no-schedule clock.
  • A nil schedule is a trigger. You don’t need a separate failure to pay; the gap between “claimed” and “nil” is the dispute.
  • Don’t commence court proceedings and an adjudication for the same amount in parallel. Once you have validly made an adjudication application under s 78(2)(b), the s 78(2)(a) debt route is closed for that amount.
  • If the underlying payment claim is shaky — for example, served outside the s 75 reference-date window — fixing that is upstream work. No s 79(1) trigger will save an application based on an invalid claim.
  1. Seymour Whyte Constructions Pty Ltd v Ostwald Bros Pty Ltd (In liquidation) [2019] NSWCA 11 (NSW Court of Appeal) — election between the two limbs of s 78(2) only operates where an adjudication application has actually been “made”; an invalid application does not lock the claimant out of the debt route. Canterbury-Bankstown Council v Payce Communities Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 1419 (Henry J) — running parallel court proceedings while a fresh payment claim goes to adjudication is not, without more, an abuse of process; the threshold is “exceptional circumstances” and the onus is “a heavy one”. National Management Group Pty Ltd v Biriel Industries Pty Ltd trading as Master Steel & Ors [2019] QSC 219 (Wilson J) — minor errors in the application document don’t defeat the trigger so long as the project, reference date and amount due are identifiable.
