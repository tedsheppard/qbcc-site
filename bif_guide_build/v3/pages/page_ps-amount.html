Requirements of a payment schedule

State the scheduled amount

A payment schedule has to do more than dispute the claim. Under s 69(b) it must state the amount — if any — that you propose to pay in response to the payment claim. That figure is what the courts and adjudicators call the “scheduled amount”, and it is the number that fixes your downside if you fail to pay it on the due date.

Section 69 BIF Act — meaning of payment schedule (chapeau and (b))

A payment schedule, responding to a payment claim, is a written document that—

…

(b) states the amount of the payment, if any, that the respondent proposes to make…

What “scheduled amount” means

Parliament chose not to define the phrase “the amount of the payment, if any, that the respondent proposes to make” as the “scheduled amount”, but the label survives from the earlier case law and is still used by adjudicators. The scheduled amount is the single overall figure the respondent proposes to pay across the whole payment claim — not a line-by-line tally against each item.1 If the payment claim is one invoice or many bundled invoices, you state one number for the whole thing.

The figure can be nil. An amount of “nothing”, “nil” or “zero” satisfies s 69(b) just as readily as a positive number, including where the respondent says the payment claim itself is invalid and proposes to pay nothing on that basis.2

Stating the amount — expressly or by inference

You don’t have to put the figure in a box marked “scheduled amount”. The amount can be stated expressly, or it can be clearly inferred from the document read as a whole. The leading Queensland authority is Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA) Pty Ltd [2007] QSC 333, where Chesterman J held that an email which did not put a number on the page nonetheless implied a nil scheduled amount because, read fairly, it rejected the invoice in its entirety. The catch in Minimax was that the email only addressed one of three items in the payment claim:

The machinery for prompt payment and enforcement of payment would break down if a document, said to be a payment schedule, took issue with part only of a claim but was silent as to what it proposed to pay in respect of the balance.

So the inference of nil works, but only if the inference covers the whole of the payment claim. Silence on part of the claim is fatal — the document either has to address every item, or has to state a global figure that on its face responds to the lot.

Two practical consequences follow. First, “without prejudice” settlement-style letters that propose a deal rather than state what will be paid do not qualify: they aren’t stating a scheduled amount, they’re inviting a negotiation.3 Second, a response that merely asks for clarification or a reissued claim — “please adjust your claim accordingly and resubmit”, or “could you expand on the difference between these two quotes” — doesn’t state any amount at all and so falls outside s 69(b).4

Conditional and deemed amounts

Conditional figures are a trap. A document that says an amount will be paid once two conditions are met has been held not to state any amount at all for the purposes of the equivalent of s 69(b): it is neither a statement that nothing will be paid nor a statement that the stated figure will be paid.5 By contrast, a superintendent’s assessment combined with a contractual deeming clause — common in standard-form contracts — can satisfy s 69(b), because read with the deeming clause the assessed figure becomes the amount the respondent proposes to pay.6 The lesson is that the document, taken with anything it incorporates, has to identify a definite figure.

Practice tips

  • Put the scheduled amount on the front page of the schedule as a single dollar figure for the whole claim. If you propose to pay nothing, write “Scheduled amount: $0”. Don’t make the claimant infer it.
  • Make sure the amount you state covers every item in the payment claim. If you only address some of the items, you risk the whole document being held not to be a payment schedule.
  • Don’t dress the response up as a settlement offer or a “without prejudice” letter if you intend it to function as a payment schedule. State the figure you propose to pay, full stop.
  • Avoid conditional formulations (“we will pay $X once you do Y and Z”). State an amount now — nil if necessary — and deal with the conditions as reasons under s 69(c).
  • If the contract has a deeming clause that turns the superintendent’s assessment into the payment schedule, check that the assessment actually states a number, and that it’s issued and delivered within the s 76 timeframe.
  • Remember the downside. The scheduled amount is the figure the claimant can sue for as a debt under the Act if you fail to pay it on the due date. Get the number right.
