Requirements of an adjudicator’s decision

Bind valuation in later applications

If an earlier adjudicator has already decided the value of a particular item of construction work or related goods and services, a later adjudicator must adopt that same value — unless someone shows the value has actually changed since. The rule is in s 87 BIF Act, and it stops parties from re-running the same valuation argument again and again on rolling payment claims.

Section 87 BIF Act — Valuation of work etc. in later adjudication application

(1) This section applies if, in deciding an adjudication application, an adjudicator has decided the value of—

(a) any construction work carried out under a construction contract; or

(b) any related goods and services supplied under a construction contract.

Note—See section 72 for the valuation of construction work and related goods and services.

(2) Any adjudicator must, in any later adjudication application that involves the working out of the value of the construction work or of the related goods and services, give the work, or the goods and services, the same value as that previously decided by the adjudicator unless the claimant or respondent satisfies the adjudicator concerned that the value of the work, or the goods and services, has changed since the previous decision.

(3) However, if a decision or order of a court changes the value of the construction work or of the related goods and services, the adjudicator must give the work, or the goods and services, the same value as changed by the decision or order unless the claimant or respondent satisfies the adjudicator concerned that the value of the work, or the goods and services, has changed since the decision or order.

Valuation, not entitlement

The first thing to grasp is that s 87 only locks in valuation — it doesn’t lock in everything an earlier adjudicator decided. The leading distinction comes from McDougall J in Rothnere Pty Ltd v Quasar Constructions NSW Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 1151, refined in John Goss Projects Pty Ltd v Leighton Contractors Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 789; (2006) 66 NSWLR 707, between the calculation of the amount of a progress payment (what the adjudicator ultimately has to do) and the valuation of construction work (one input into that calculation). Only the second is captured by s 87. That distinction has been approved by the NSW Court of Appeal in Dualcorp Pty Ltd v Remo Constructions Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 69; (2009) 74 NSWLR 190 and by the Queensland Supreme Court in ACN 060 559 971 Pty Ltd v O’Brien [2007] QSC 91; [2008] 2 Qd R 396.1

The practical filter is this: if the dollar amount in dispute is not referable to a particular item of construction work or related goods and services, s 87 doesn’t bite. Liquidated damages are the classic example — in O’Brien, Mullins J held that liquidated damages under a construction contract were not referable to any particular item of construction work, so a later adjudicator who relied on s 87 (then s 27 BCIPA) to adopt the earlier adjudicator’s LD figure had erred in law.2

What “previously decided” actually covers

The bind only operates over items the earlier adjudicator actually valued. In Hitachi Ltd v O’Donnell Griffin Pty Ltd [2008] QSC 135, the first adjudicator had taken a selective approach to 113 variation claims, only assessing 12. The second adjudicator nonetheless treated the first decision as a valuation of the whole and assessed the later claim at $nil. Skoien AJ held that on the facts the first adjudicator had only valued six variation claims — none of which were in the later payment claim — so s 87 simply had nothing to grip on. Before you treat an earlier decision as binding, you need to identify which items it actually decided the value of.

The corresponding rule for the respondent is that you can’t hide an earlier adjudication and then complain the second adjudicator ignored it. In Bezzina Developers Pty Ltd v Deemah Stone (Qld) Pty Ltd [2008] QCA 213; [2008] 2 Qd R 495 the Court of Appeal held that where an adjudicator does not know there is, or probably is, a prior adjudication, no occasion arises to call for further submissions on it, and no obligation to seek them out should be implied. Tell the adjudicator about the earlier decision in your submissions, or live with the consequences.

Showing the value has changed

The exception in s 87(2) — that the value has changed since the previous decision — has to be actively engaged with by the adjudicator. Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 16 is a recent Queensland illustration. An earlier adjudication had valued an architect’s work at $200,000. In the later adjudication Iris produced an invoice, dated after the previous decision, suggesting only $183,780 was payable. The adjudicator simply applied the earlier $200,000 figure without engaging with Iris’s submissions. Wilson J held that was jurisdictional error: s 87(2) required the adjudicator to consider whether the claimant or respondent had satisfied her that the value had changed since the previous decision, and on the evidence Iris’s material was capable of doing exactly that.

The cross-border position is consistent. In Goyder Wind Farm 1 Pty Ltd v GE Renewable Energy Australia Pty Ltd [2024] SASC 108, Stein J accepted that delay costs claimed across multiple payment claims were not a single “same construction work” for the purposes of the equivalent SA provision, so the second adjudicator wasn’t bound to apply the earlier valuation. And in Harlech Enterprises Pty Ltd v Beno Excavations Pty Ltd [2022] ACTCA 42, the ACT Court of Appeal emphasised that an adjudicator’s valuation can only be disturbed in the limited circumstances the section allows — the section is not a back-door issue estoppel protecting every finding the earlier adjudicator made.3

Practice tips

  • If you’re the respondent and an earlier adjudication decided the value of items now back in play, put the earlier decision squarely in front of the second adjudicator. Identify the specific items valued and the values given. Under Bezzina, if you don’t raise it, the adjudicator isn’t obliged to chase it.
  • Don’t over-claim the bind. Section 87 only locks in the value of construction work or related goods and services. Liquidated damages, financing claims, set-offs not referable to a specific work item — none of those are caught.
  • If you’re the claimant trying to get out from under an earlier valuation, lead concrete evidence that the value has changed since — later invoices, defect rectification, scope changes, court orders. A bare assertion won’t do, but real evidence has to be engaged with: that’s the lesson of Iris Broadbeach.
  • Before you assume an item was “previously decided”, read the earlier decision carefully. As Hitachi shows, an adjudicator who took a selective approach may not have valued the items you think they did.
  • If a court has since changed the value of work (for instance, on a final-account dispute), s 87(3) makes that court figure the new baseline — subject again to the “changed since” exception.
