Requirements of an adjudicator’s decision

Procedural fairness / natural justice

An adjudicator must give each side a fair hearing on the issues that drive the outcome. A “substantial” denial of natural justice is jurisdictional error and renders the determination void. This is one of the most commonly run challenges in the Supreme Court — usually framed as “the adjudicator decided the case on a basis we never got to address”.

Section 84(2)(b) and s 88(2) BIF Act — the statutory hooks

84 Adjudication procedures

(2) For a proceeding conducted to decide an adjudication application, an adjudicator…

(b) may ask for further written submissions from either party and must give the other party an opportunity to comment on the submissions…

88 Adjudicator’s decision

(2) In deciding an adjudication application, the adjudicator is to consider the following matters only—

… (c) the payment claim to which the application relates, together with all submissions, including relevant documentation, that have been properly made by the claimant in support of the claim;

(d) the payment schedule, if any, to which the application relates, together with all submissions, including relevant documentation, that have been properly made by the respondent in support of the schedule…

Where natural justice comes from in this Act

There is no free-standing “fair hearing” rule grafted onto the BIF Act from the general law. The content of procedural fairness is fixed by the statutory scheme itself. The leading statement is Hodgson JA’s in Brodyn Pty Ltd t/as Time Cost and Quality v Davenport [2004] NSWCA 394 — the requirement to give notice to the respondent and an opportunity to make submissions (now reflected in s 84 and s 88(2) of the BIF Act) carries with it a legislative intention that “no substantial denial of the measure of natural justice that the Act requires to be given” is essential to validity.1 Basten JA put the same point a different way in Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v Climatech (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 229: the relevant content of procedural fairness is ascertained by reference to the specific statutory scheme; where the scheme is inconsistent with what general-law fairness might otherwise require, the general law gives way.

Two consequences follow. First, the timetable is short and the adjudicator is not obliged to chase further submissions on every loose end — that would defeat the speed-of-payment purpose of the Act. Second, the duty that does exist is real, and it bites hardest where the adjudicator strays beyond the issues actually joined between the parties.

The “substantial” threshold

Not every procedural slip vitiates a determination. The denial must be “substantial” — meaning material to the outcome. The canonical Queensland formulation is Applegarth J’s in John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302:

The adjective “substantial” has been used in the relevant authorities to capture the principle that the opportunity denied was material, namely that the matter about which the adjudicator did not provide an opportunity to be heard was a point upon which the adjudicator based his or her decision and was significant to the actual determination… even if the Court is satisfied that there has been a denial of natural justice, relief may be denied if it can be shown that compliance with the requirements of natural justice could have made no difference to the outcome. It is probably sufficient in this regard for the applicant for relief to show that there were substantial submissions that, as a matter of reality and not mere speculation, might have persuaded the adjudicator to change his or her mind.

The applicant therefore does not have to prove it would have won had it been heard. It only has to show “something to be put that might well persuade the adjudicator to change his or her mind”.2 The NSW Court of Appeal in Demex Pty Ltd v McNab Building Services Pty Ltd [2023] NSWCA 261 has more recently restated the same point as a “substantial practical injustice” test, and that has been picked up in Queensland.3

The classic trigger: a decision on a basis neither party contended

By far the most common scenario is the adjudicator deciding the dispute on a route neither side argued. In Hanson Construction Materials Pty Ltd v Brolton Group Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 1641 the adjudicator picked an alternative reference date that no one had contended for; the determination was set aside, and the Court of Appeal affirmed.4 The Queensland authorities are to the same effect — an adjudicator who is “minded” to decide a question on a basis not put by either party must put the proposed basis to the parties first.5

The most directly useful Queensland decision for project-level fact patterns is Ingeteam Australia Pty Ltd v Susan River Solar Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 30. There Applegarth J declared an adjudication determination void in part for jurisdictional error because the adjudicator had decided two limbs of a licensing point (whether the work was unlicensed building work, and whether the contractor had intended to comply with s 42 of the QBCC Act) without putting either limb to the contractor. His Honour found denial of procedural fairness in two respects: the adjudicator failed to give Ingeteam an opportunity to refute the evidence and submissions Susan River had put forward on unlicensed building work, and he reached a conclusion about Ingeteam’s intention to comply with s 42 without giving Ingeteam any opportunity to make submissions on it. The errors were also material — there was a real possibility the result would have been different if Ingeteam had been heard.

Other recurring trigger points

  • Material received from one party but not shown to the other. Direct correspondence with the adjudicator (a side email, a late attachment) that the other party never sees is a classic denial: J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Cada Formwork Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 63 (the “10 June email”); TQM Design & Construct Pty Ltd v Dasein Constructions Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 1216.
  • Unilaterally disregarding a document and not flagging it. If the adjudicator decides that material is inadmissible or non-compliant, the parties should be told, otherwise they lose the chance to argue the point: Syntech Resources Pty Ltd v Peter Campbell Earthmoving (Aust) Pty Ltd [2011] QSC 293.
  • Failure to engage with a fundamental part of a party’s case. Reasons that simply do not grapple with a central submission can amount to a denial: Suprima Bakeries Pty Ltd v Australian Weighing Equipment Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 998.
  • Going outside the requested scope of further submissions. Where an adjudicator asks for further submissions under s 84(2)(b) but then receives material going beyond the request, and does not tell the parties whether that material will be used, the procedure may not result in justice being seen to be done: Minesco Pty Ltd v Anderson Sunvast Hong Kong Ltd [2017] VSC 299.

Where the challenge usually fails

Adjudicators are not required to invite further submissions on every issue, and the courts will not “sift finely” through reasons looking for slips. Failing to give lengthy or elaborate reasons, on its own, is not a denial of natural justice: see Future Power Developments Pty Ltd v TJ & RF [2017] NSWSC 232, picking up Clyde Bergemann Senior Thermal Pty Ltd v Varley Power Services Pty Ltd [2011] NSWSC 1039. A challenge will also fail where the “new” basis the adjudicator adopted was implicit in what one of the parties had already put,6 or where the disputed point was simply not material to the outcome.7 And there is no denial in failing to consider a submission that the adjudicator has, within jurisdiction, decided was not duly made.8

What this means on a project

  • If you are reading the determination and find the adjudicator decided a key point on a route neither side ran, that is your first natural-justice flag. Identify the exact passage and check whether the basis appears anywhere in the application, response or any further submissions.
  • Be ready to articulate, with specificity, what you would have said had you been given the chance. Vague assertions of unfairness fail. Draft the “missing” submission you would have made and show it would have at least had a real prospect of changing the result.
  • If the adjudicator asks for further written submissions under s 84(2)(b), make sure both sides see whatever the other side puts in. Side-channel emails to the adjudicator that the other party doesn’t see are an obvious risk — copy everything.
  • If you suspect material was received from your opponent that you weren’t shown, raise it with the adjudicator before the decision issues. Silence weakens the later argument that you were denied a hearing.
  • Don’t over-pitch the challenge. Reasoning errors, misunderstandings of evidence and short reasons are not, on their own, denials of natural justice. The argument that travels is “the adjudicator decided the case on a point we never got to address, and here is what we would have said”.
