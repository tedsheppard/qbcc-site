Retention trust accounts

Opening the retention trust account

Once a retention trust is required under s 32, the contracting party has to open a dedicated bank account to hold the retention money. The Act controls three things about that account: when it has to be opened, the name it has to be held under, and the kind of financial institution that can hold it. Get any of those wrong and you are exposed to a penalty before a single dollar of retention has even been withheld.

Section 34 BIF Act — contracting party must open retention trust account

(2) The contracting party must open an account for the retention trust at a financial institution—

(a) if the retention trust is required under section 32(1)—before the retention amount is withheld; or

(b) if the retention trust is required under section 32(2)—within 20 business days after the contract becomes a withholding contract.

Maximum penalty—500 penalty units.

(3) However, the contracting party need only establish 1 retention trust account for all retention amounts withheld by the party under any number of contracts for which it is the contracting party.

When the account has to be opened

The timing rule splits according to which limb of s 32 brought the trust into existence. If the trust is required under s 32(1) — the ordinary case, where the contract is a withholding contract from the outset — the account must be open before the retention amount is withheld. You cannot withhold first and bank later. The first dollar of retention deducted from a progress payment has to land in an account that already exists.

If the trust is required under s 32(2) — the case where a contract becomes a withholding contract part-way through, typically because a later variation or further contract pushes the party over the threshold — you have 20 business days from the day the contract becomes a withholding contract to get the account open. That window only applies to the s 32(2) trigger; it does not extend the s 32(1) deadline.

The penalty for missing either deadline is 500 penalty units. That is the highest penalty attached to the opening obligations and reflects the structural importance of the account being in place before retention money starts flowing.

One account, many contracts

Section 34(3) makes clear that a contracting party only needs one retention trust account, not one per contract. Every retention amount the party withholds, across every contract for which it is the contracting party, can sit in the same account. That keeps the compliance burden manageable for principals and head contractors who run multiple jobs, and it fits with the way the rest of Part 4 is drafted — the trustee’s record-keeping and notification obligations are calibrated to a single pooled account.

The account name

Section 34A(2) requires the trustee to ensure the account is held under a name that includes both the trustee’s name and the word “trust”. Practically that means the account label your bank prints on statements has to identify the trustee entity (the contracting party) and contain the literal word “trust” — for example, “ABC Constructions Pty Ltd Retention Trust”. A generic project account, or an account in the trustee’s name without the word “trust”, will not satisfy the section. The maximum penalty is 200 penalty units.

The naming requirement matters for a reason that goes beyond compliance: the account name is the principal external signal that the funds are trust money rather than the contracting party’s general operating cash. If the contracting party becomes insolvent, the account name is part of how a liquidator, the contracted parties and the commissioner identify the trust pool.

The financial institution

Section 34A(1) requires the account to be held at an “approved financial institution”. The list of approved institutions is maintained administratively; it is not enough that a bank is willing to open the account, it has to be one that has been approved for the purpose of holding retention trust money. Holding the account anywhere else is an offence carrying up to 200 penalty units.

If you later want to move the account, s 34C controls the change. The receiving institution must also be an approved financial institution, every dollar in the existing account has to transfer with the account, and the trustee has to inform every contracted party from whom retention has been withheld about the transfer in the form prescribed by the regulation. Section 34B layers a separate notification obligation on top: the commissioner has to be notified, in an approved way, within 5 business days of opening, closing, renaming or transferring the account.

Practice tips

  • If your contract is a withholding contract from day one, open the retention trust account before you make the first deduction. Do not wait for the first progress claim and then scramble — the s 34(2)(a) deadline is “before the retention amount is withheld”, and the maximum penalty is 500 penalty units.
  • If a contract becomes a withholding contract mid-project (for example through a variation that pushes you over the s 32(2) threshold), diarise the 20-business-day window from that date to get the account open.
  • You only need one retention trust account across your business, not one per contract. Pool the retention amounts from every contract you administer as contracting party in the same account.
  • When you open the account, make sure the account name on the bank’s system includes your full trustee entity name and the word “trust”. “Project retention” or your trading name alone will not satisfy s 34A(2).
  • Confirm with your bank that the account is being opened at an approved financial institution for the purposes of s 34A(1). Do not rely on a generic business transaction account — ask the bank to confirm the product is suitable for a BIF Act retention trust.
  • Within 5 business days of opening the account, lodge the s 34B notice with the commissioner using the approved way. The same notice obligation applies if you later rename, close or transfer the account.
  • If you ever need to switch banks, work through s 34C: confirm the destination institution is approved, transfer every dollar across with the account, and notify all affected contracted parties as prescribed by regulation.
  1. Sections 34, 34A, 34B and 34C of the BIF Act, read with s 32, set out the opening, naming, location and notification regime for a retention trust account. The list of approved financial institutions is maintained administratively by the commission.
