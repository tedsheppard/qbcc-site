Requirements of a payment schedule

Served on the claimant

A payment schedule only does its job — locking in the amount you propose to pay and confining the dispute — if it is actually given to the claimant. Section 76(1) requires the respondent to “give” the schedule to the claimant; s 102 tells you how that giving may happen. Get service wrong and you are in the same position as a respondent that filed nothing: liable for the full claimed amount as a debt, with no right to put a defence at adjudication.

Sections 76(1) and 102 BIF Act — how to give the schedule

76 Responding to payment claim

(1) If given a payment claim, a respondent must respond to the payment claim by giving the claimant a payment schedule within whichever of the following periods ends first—

(a) the period, if any, within which the respondent must give the payment schedule under the relevant construction contract;

(b) 15 business days after the payment claim is given to the respondent.

102 Service of notices

(1) A notice or other document that, under this chapter, is authorised or required to be given to a person may be given to the person in the way, if any, provided under the relevant construction contract.

(2) Subsection (1) is in addition to, and does not limit or exclude, the Acts Interpretation Act 1954, section 39 or the provisions of any other law about the giving of notices.

(3) To remove any doubt, it is declared that nothing in this Act—

(a) excludes the proper service of notices or documents by a person’s agent; or

(b) requires a person’s acknowledgement of a notice or document properly given to the person.

“Giving” the schedule — a facultative regime

The Act uses “give”, not “serve” or “provide”. Nothing turns on that change of language: the courts treat “give”, “serve” and “provide” as equivalent for these purposes.1 Section 102 is “facultative rather than mandatory” — it gives you valid routes for giving the schedule, but it does not displace s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) or the general law on service.2 In practice that means three independent paths are open: the contract’s notice clause (s 102(1)), s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act, and actual receipt by someone within the claimant’s organisation who is authorised to deal with payment claims.

Section 102(3)(a) puts beyond doubt that giving by the respondent’s agent is effective. The standard arrangement — superintendent or contract administrator issuing the schedule on the principal’s behalf — is therefore valid where the contract makes the agent’s role clear. Service by a respondent’s solicitors is also generally effective, provided the retainer extends to that step; the Court of Appeal in Baulderstone Hornibrook Pty Ltd v Queensland Investment Corporation [2007] NSWCA 9 upheld solicitor service after looking at the retainer, prior conduct and the commercial sense of the conclusion.3

Service by email — when the schedule is “given”

Email is now the default. Section 102(1) lets the contract specify email expressly, and most do. Even where it does not, s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act reaches email as a “similar facility” in many circumstances, although that view is not free from doubt.4 The timing rule comes from s 24 of the Electronic Transactions (Queensland) Act 2001: an electronic communication is received when it becomes “capable of being retrieved” at a designated address. The email need not be opened or read, and a document caught by a spam filter is still capable of being retrieved.5

The most useful Queensland decision for project teams is CKP Constructions Pty Ltd v Gabba Holdings Pty Ltd [2016] QDC 356. The respondent emailed its payment schedule at 11:45pm on the fifth business day after the s 99 warning notice. The contract permitted service by email. Applying s 24 of the Electronic Transactions Act, McGill DCJ held the schedule had been given when it became capable of being retrieved — 11:45pm — and was therefore in time. Late at night is still in time, so long as it is the right night. The case is the answer to the question every contract administrator asks at 5pm on day 15: can I still get this away tonight?

The most recent appellate guidance on emailed service comes from Roberts Co (NSW) Pty Ltd v Sharvain Facades Pty Ltd (Administrators Appointed) [2025] NSWCA 161. The case is about a payment claim, but the reasoning applies equally to a payment schedule given by email under s 76. The Court held that service occurs when the document is emailed to an address specified by the recipient for service and becomes capable of retrieval — with no requirement that the recipient access or become aware of the communication. McHugh JA put the underlying point about service mechanics this way:

section 31 governs the mechanics of valid service under the Act, irrespective of awareness.

His Honour also held that a contractual “deeming” clause that purported to defer the time of service (treating an email arriving on Friday as served on Monday) had no effect on the statutory timeframe.6 A respondent cannot push back the s 76 clock by drafting a deeming clause; equally, a respondent cannot use one to claim its schedule was given earlier than it actually was. What you sent, when you sent it, and to where, is what matters.

Cloud platforms, post-office boxes and corporations

Cloud platforms are more fragile. Service by Dropbox link has been held to fall outside both s 39 and s 109X of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) because the email delivers only the means of access, not the document itself.7 The same logic has been applied to dedicated platforms such as Corrigo where the recipient was unaware of the upload.8 Where the contract designates a platform like Aconex or Payapps as a service channel, however, service occurs when the schedule becomes capable of retrieval at that designated address.9 If you must use a portal, attach the schedule to the email as well.

For corporate claimants, s 109X of the Corporations Act permits service by leaving the document at, or posting it to, the company’s registered office, or by delivering it personally to a director resident in Australia. Service to a post-office box is not valid service under s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act or s 109X unless the contract authorises that mode.10 Where service is by post to the registered office, the document is “received” when it arrives at the office during ordinary business hours; it does not have to come to the attention of any particular individual.11

What this means on a project

  • Identify the right service channel before you start drafting. Read the contract’s notice clause: if it nominates an email address or a platform (Aconex, Payapps), use that exact channel and send to that exact address.
  • Don’t leave it to the last hour. The schedule is “given” when it is capable of being retrieved at the recipient’s end, but the time of receipt — not the time of sending — is what counts. CKP Constructions says 11:45pm on the last day works; 12:01am does not.
  • Send to multiple addresses where possible — the claimant’s nominated address, the project manager, the contracts team. Keep the sent-mail record and any read receipts.
  • If the schedule is to be given by your solicitors or by the superintendent on your behalf, make sure their authority to do so is clear from the contract or a documented retainer. Section 102(3)(a) protects giving by an agent, but only if the agent is in fact authorised.
  • Don’t rely on a contractual deeming clause to extend the s 76 clock. After Roberts Co, those clauses do not alter when a document is given for the purposes of the Act.
  • Avoid Dropbox and bare cloud links. Attach the schedule itself to an email, even if you also upload it to a portal.
  • If the claimant is a company, send to the address nominated in the contract; failing that, the registered office during business hours. A post-office box only works if the contract authorises it.
