Statutory suspension of works

Trigger — failure to pay adjudicated amount

Once an adjudicator has decided what the respondent owes, the claimant has a self-help remedy under the BIF Act if the cash doesn’t turn up. Section 92 lets you suspend work or the supply of related goods and services after a respondent fails to pay an adjudicated amount, provided you give the right notice first. It is one of two statutory triggers that unlock the suspension regime in s 98 — the other (failure to pay a scheduled amount) is dealt with on the next page.

Section 92 BIF Act — consequence of not paying adjudicated amount

92 Consequence of not paying adjudicated amount

(1) If the respondent fails to pay the whole or any part of the adjudicated amount to the claimant as required under section 90, the claimant may give the respondent written notice of the claimant’s intention to suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under the construction contract under section 98.

(2) The notice about suspending work must state that it is made under this Act.

What has to have happened

The trigger in s 92(1) has three moving parts and all of them have to be in place before the notice can validly go out.

  • An adjudicated amount. The adjudicator must have decided an amount is payable to the claimant under Part 4. A bare s 88 decision in your favour is enough — you don’t need to have registered an adjudication certificate as a judgment first.
  • A failure to pay under s 90. Section 90 fixes when the adjudicated amount is due. The respondent must have failed to pay “the whole or any part” of it by that date. Partial payment is enough to keep the trigger live for whatever balance remains.
  • Written notice of intention to suspend. The claimant must give the respondent a written notice that it intends to suspend under s 98, and the notice must state on its face that it is made under the Act.

Each of those is a precondition. If the adjudicator hasn’t decided an amount, or the s 90 due date hasn’t passed, or the notice doesn’t identify the Act, the s 98 right to down tools hasn’t crystallised and any walk-off is a contractual breach rather than a statutory suspension.

The link to s 90 and s 98

Section 92 doesn’t suspend anything by itself. It is an enabling provision: it tells you that, if the respondent has missed the s 90 due date for the adjudicated amount, you may give a notice of intention to suspend and that — once the timing in s 98 is satisfied — you may then actually stop work. The architecture is deliberately staged so the respondent gets a written warning and a window to pay before the site goes quiet.

Two practical consequences flow from that staging. First, the s 92 notice is the gateway to s 98; without it, you have no right to suspend on this trigger no matter how overdue the payment is. Second, because s 92(1) speaks of “the whole or any part” of the adjudicated amount, you can issue the notice even where the respondent has paid some of the determination but is short on the rest. You don’t need to wait for a complete default.

Form and content of the notice

The Act prescribes very little about the form of the s 92 notice itself. It must be in writing, it must communicate an intention to suspend carrying out construction work (or supplying related goods and services) under the contract, and — under s 92(2) — it must state that it is made under the Act. The detail of what the notice should say, and the mechanics of how the suspension itself works, sit on the dedicated “Form of suspension notice” and “When suspension may begin” pages.

One drafting point worth emphasising at the trigger stage: don’t blend the s 92 notice with a generic contractual default notice or a demand letter. The statutory notice has a specific function — arming you with the s 98 right to suspend — and a respondent challenging a later suspension will go straight to whether the document on its face does what s 92 requires. Keeping the s 92 notice as a discrete, clearly-labelled document is the simplest way to put that issue beyond argument.

What this means on a project

  • Diary the s 90 due date for the adjudicated amount the moment the determination is published. The s 92 trigger only opens once that date has passed without full payment.
  • Don’t suspend on the back of an unpaid determination without first issuing the s 92 notice. The notice is a precondition to the s 98 right; suspending without it converts a statutory remedy into a repudiation risk.
  • Make sure the notice is in writing, identifies the contract and the adjudicated amount that is unpaid, and states expressly that it is given under the BIF Act. The s 92(2) requirement that the notice state it is made under the Act is short, specific and easy to overlook.
  • Partial payment doesn’t close the door. If the respondent has paid some but not all of the adjudicated amount by the s 90 due date, the trigger is still available for the unpaid balance.
  • Treat the s 92 notice as a standalone document, not a paragraph buried in a default letter. The cleaner the notice, the harder it is to attack at the s 98 stage.
  1. Section 90 BIF Act fixes the date by which the respondent must pay the adjudicated amount; the s 92 trigger turns on a failure to pay by that date. Section 98 then sets out the rights that flow from a notice given under s 92, including when suspension may begin.
  2. Section 92(2) BIF Act — the notice must state that it is made under the Act; this is a content requirement of the notice itself, not merely good practice.
