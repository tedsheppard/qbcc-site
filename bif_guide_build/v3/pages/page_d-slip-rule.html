Requirements of an adjudicator’s decision

Slip-rule corrections

Adjudicators occasionally make obvious mistakes — a typo, a transposed figure, the wrong party name, an arithmetic slip. Section 89 lets the adjudicator correct that kind of error after the determination has issued, either on their own initiative or on the application of either side. Used properly, it cleans up an honest slip without anyone having to run to the Supreme Court. Used as a back-door for a re-hearing, it doesn’t work.

Section 89 BIF Act — Adjudicator may correct clerical mistakes etc.

89 Adjudicator may correct clerical mistakes etc.

(1) This section applies if the adjudicator’s decision includes—

(a) a clerical mistake; or

(b) an error arising from an accidental slip or omission; or

(c) a material miscalculation of figures or a material mistake in the description of a person, thing or matter mentioned in the decision; or

(d) a defect of form.

(2) The adjudicator may, on the adjudicator’s own initiative or on the application of the claimant or respondent, correct the decision.

(3) The adjudicator may, if requested by the registrar, correct the decision.

What s 89 is for

Section 89 lists four discrete circumstances — a clerical mistake, an accidental slip or omission, a material miscalculation or material mis-description, or a defect of form — any one of which can found the adjudicator’s discretion to correct. The four limbs are disjunctive: the error only needs to fall within one. The unifying idea behind the section is that minor mistakes don’t need to bring down the whole determination. The legislature recognised that adjudication is fast and pressured, and that obvious slips should be cleaned up at the adjudicator level rather than treated as jurisdictional error.

That logic is reflected in McNab Developments (Qld) Pty Ltd v MAK Construction Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QCA 232, where Jackson J held that the presence of s 28 of BCIPA (the predecessor to s 89) reflects the legislature’s view that an accidental or erroneous omission to consider part of a claim does not invalidate the adjudicator’s decision for jurisdictional error. Applying the Project Blue Sky approach, his Honour reasoned that because s 28(2) preserves the operation of an adjudicator’s decision and provides a power of correction, there is no reason to treat the decision as invalid until that power is exercised — and the parties may decide a particular error isn’t important enough to bother correcting at all.1

The inadvertence principle

The boundary of the section is inadvertence. Section 89 is not a vehicle for the adjudicator to revisit reasoning, change methodology or repair a deliberate decision that turned out to be wrong. The leading Queensland statement is Daubney J’s in Uniting Church in Australia Property Trust (Qld) v Davenport & Anor [2009] QSC 134, which picks up Macrossan CJ’s observation in Cawood v Infraworth Pty Ltd [1990] 2 Qd R 114 at 122:

Inadvertence, as distinguished from an error or mistake resulting from deliberate decision, is the basis of the jurisdiction to correct under the slip rule.

Daubney J then made the key point about how that maps onto s 89: of the four limbs in s 89(1), only paragraph (b) — “an error arising from an accidental slip or omission” — actually imports the notion of inadvertence. The other three limbs (clerical mistake, material miscalculation or mis-description, defect of form) operate independently. So a material miscalculation can be corrected even where it wasn’t strictly “inadvertent”, provided it is genuinely a miscalculation rather than a change of mind.2

What a miscalculation is not is a complete re-working of the reasoning. Both Uniting Church and Thiess Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373 hold that a “correction” that would require the adjudicator to adopt a completely different method of calculation, or apply a different chain of reasoning to reach a different result, is not a correction of a miscalculation under s 89(1)(c). Lyons J in Thiess distinguished cases under rule 388 of the UCPR on the basis that something more than a different final figure has to be inserted before s 89 is engaged.3

The discretion to correct — and to decline

Even where an error falls within s 89(1), correction is permissive, not mandatory. As McDougall J said in Musico v Davenport [2003] NSWSC 977, a party has the right to request the adjudicator to consider exercising the power, not the right to have it exercised in a particular way. If the adjudicator declines — for example, because they don’t accept there is a mistake — that decision is an error within jurisdiction and is not reviewable for jurisdictional error.4 Section 89 is also not analogous to UCPR r 388: Daubney J in Uniting Church rejected that analogy as “inapt”, given r 388’s conjunctive structure compared to the four discrete disjunctive limbs in s 89(1).

Practice tips

  • If the determination contains an obvious error in your favour or against you — a typo, the wrong party name, a transposed figure, an arithmetic slip — write to the adjudicator promptly and ask for a s 89 correction. Identify the limb you say is engaged and the correction you want made.
  • Don’t use s 89 as a back-door re-hearing. If your “correction” would require the adjudicator to change methodology or chain of reasoning to reach a different result, it isn’t a slip-rule case. That argument belongs in the Supreme Court, not in a s 89 request.
  • Material miscalculations and mis-descriptions don’t need to be “inadvertent” in the strict sense — only the s 89(1)(b) accidental slip limb does. So a genuine arithmetic mistake or wrong figure description is correctable even if the adjudicator was concentrating when they made it.
  • If the adjudicator refuses to correct, that’s the end of the road on the slip rule. The refusal is within jurisdiction and won’t be set aside on review. Your only remaining options are the substantive jurisdictional-error grounds covered elsewhere in this section.
  • If a typographical error in the determination doesn’t actually affect the result — for example, a wrong word in the first line that doesn’t change the operative finding — the courts will not intervene on judicial review either: see the approach in CPB Contractors Pty Limited v Heyday5 Pty Limited [2020] NSWSC 1625.
