Project trust accounts

Penalties for non-compliance

Chapter 2 is enforced almost entirely through criminal offence provisions. Each substantive trust obligation — opening the account, depositing the right money in, paying subcontractors out of it, giving the right notices, reporting related entities — carries its own maximum penalty expressed in penalty units, and several of the core obligations also carry a term of imprisonment. The maxima are not symbolic. They reflect a deliberate policy choice that the trust regime is the protective backbone of the Act, and that breaching it is to be treated as an offence rather than a private contractual default.

Section 18(1) BIF Act — Contracted party must open project trust account

(1) If a project trust is required for a contract under section 12, the contracted party must open an account at a financial institution for the trust as required by this section.

Maximum penalty—500 penalty units.

Section 19 BIF Act — All payments from contracting party to be deposited in project trust account (penalties)

(2) The contracting party must deposit the amount into the project trust account for the contract (the deposit obligation) unless …

Maximum penalty—200 penalty units.

(4) If an amount is paid to the contracted party or its agent in contravention of the deposit obligation, the contracted party must deposit the amount into the project trust account as soon as practicable after receiving the amount.

Maximum penalty for subsection (4)—200 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment.

The headline offence: failing to open the account

The largest standalone maximum in the bundle of offences attached to setting up a project trust sits at s 18(1): if a project trust is required under s 12 and the contracted party does not open the account at a financial institution as required, the maximum penalty is 500 penalty units. There is no imprisonment limb on this provision, but the financial exposure dwarfs the next tier of trust offences. Section 18 also contains structural prohibitions that reinforce the offence — the account cannot be a virtual account or subordinate to another account (s 18(4)), there cannot be more than one account for the trust (s 18(5)), and any contractual term purporting to extend the 20-business-day opening window beyond what s 18(2) allows is “of no effect” (s 18(6)). A head contractor cannot drafting-out of the timing.

Deposit in, payment out: the imprisonment-tier offences

The next tier targets the flow of money through the trust, and this is where imprisonment first appears. Section 19(2) imposes a deposit obligation on the contracting party: any amount paid by the contracting party to the contracted party in connection with a project trust contract has to be paid into the project trust account, subject to the carve-outs in s 19(2)(a)–(e). Breach carries a maximum penalty of 200 penalty units. There is no imprisonment limb at the contracting-party level, reflecting that the contracting party is typically the principal at the top of the chain rather than the trustee.

Section 19(4) sits below it and is more serious. If money has reached the contracted party (or its agent) in contravention of the deposit obligation — for example, where the principal pays the head contractor directly into a general operating account — the contracted party must deposit that money into the project trust account as soon as practicable. Breach carries 200 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment. The same imprisonment-grade exposure attaches to the payment-out side. Section 20(1) requires the contracted party to pay subcontractor beneficiaries only from the project trust account, and only by depositing the amount into the financial-institution account nominated by the beneficiary. Maximum penalty: 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment. Together, s 19(4) and s 20(1) bracket the trust — money that is meant to be in must be in, and money that goes out has to leave through the right pipe.

Notices and reporting: lower-tier but easy to miss

The notice and reporting offences carry smaller maxima but track obligations that are easy to overlook on a busy project. Section 23(1) requires the contracted party to give each subcontractor a written notice of project trust before entering into the subcontract (or, where the trust is established later, within 10 business days of establishment): maximum 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment. Section 25 requires the contracted party to inform the commissioner within 5 business days when it enters into a subcontract with a related entity: maximum 200 penalty units. Section 24 imposes the mirror obligation on the contracting party where it knows, or ought reasonably to know, that a subcontractor beneficiary is a related entity for the contracted party: maximum 50 penalty units, the lowest in the suite.

Reading the maxima as a hierarchy

The maxima are not random. Read together, they sketch a hierarchy that maps onto the seriousness of the underlying breach:

  • 500 penalty units — failing to open the project trust account at all (s 18(1)). The largest standalone exposure in the establishment phase.
  • 200 penalty units or 2 years imprisonment — the contracted party failing to remediate a payment that has bypassed the trust (s 19(4)). The only two-year limb in this part of Chapter 2.
  • 200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment — paying subcontractor beneficiaries other than from the trust account (s 20(1)) and failing to give the required notice of project trust (s 23(1)).
  • 200 penalty units (no imprisonment) — the contracting party’s deposit obligation (s 19(2)) and the contracted party’s related-entity reporting (s 25).
  • 50 penalty units — the contracting party’s related-entity reporting under s 24.

Two patterns emerge. First, imprisonment limbs cluster on the contracted party — the trustee — rather than the principal. The Act treats misuse of trust funds at the trustee level as more culpable than a principal’s administrative failure to route a payment correctly. Second, omissions to report and notify carry materially lower maxima than misdirecting the money itself, but they are not trivial: s 23(1) sits at imprisonment grade despite being “just” a notice obligation, because absence of notice frustrates the protective purpose of the regime in s 7.

Common compliance traps

Most prosecutions and audit findings under this part come from a small set of recurring failures rather than from sophisticated avoidance. The patterns worth flagging are:

  • Missing the 20-business-day window in s 18(2). The clock runs from the first subcontract, not from the head contract. Project teams that diary the head-contract execution date instead of the first subcontract date routinely under-shoot the window.
  • Drafting around s 18(6). A head-contract clause that purports to give the contracted party more than 20 business days to open the account is “of no effect”. The s 18(1) offence is engaged on the statutory timeline regardless of what the contract says.
  • Treating direct payments outside the trust as a contractual issue. Where the principal pays straight into an operating account, both s 19(2) (principal’s deposit obligation) and s 19(4) (head contractor’s remediation obligation) are engaged. The remediation limb is the imprisonment-grade offence.
  • Paying subbies out of the wrong account. Section 20(1)(a) permits payment to a subcontractor beneficiary “only” from the project trust account. A payment made from a general account to a subbie — even one that clears the same day — is the s 20(1) offence on its face.
  • Forgetting the notice of project trust under s 23. The notice has to be given before the subcontract is entered into (or within 10 business days of trust establishment if the trust is established later). It is a written notice with prescribed content; a generic project induction document will not satisfy it.
  • Under-reporting related-entity subcontracts. Section 25 catches the contracted party; s 24 catches the contracting party where it knows or ought reasonably to know. Both are short-fuse 5-business-day obligations, and the “ought reasonably to know” limb in s 24 means a principal cannot rely on willed ignorance.
  • Assuming amendments turn off exposure. Section 12(4) keeps the trust requirement on regardless of variations, amendments or price changes. The matching offence provisions stay live until dissolution under s 21.

What this means on a project

  • Build the s 18(2) 20-business-day clock into your subcontracting workflow, not into your head-contract execution checklist. The trigger is the first subcontract.
  • Route every incoming payment from the principal through the trust account. If a payment lands elsewhere, treat s 19(4) remediation as a same-day priority — this is the only 2-year imprisonment exposure in the part.
  • Pay subcontractor beneficiaries only from the project trust account into a beneficiary-controlled account (s 20(1), s 20(3)). Do not net off, set off informally, or pay from a parent-company account.
  • Issue the s 23 notice of project trust before each subcontract is signed. Build the prescribed content into your standard subcontract pack so the notice goes out with every award letter.
  • Identify related-entity subcontractors at award stage and run the 5-business-day reporting clock under s 25 (contracted party) and s 24 (contracting party) from the date of subcontract execution, not the date someone first asks the question.
  • Treat s 18(6) as a hard limit when negotiating head-contract clauses about trust set-up. Do not accept extended-timing clauses; they will not protect the contracted party from the s 18(1) offence.
  • Keep the trust live until s 21 dissolution criteria are actually met (no remaining subcontractor beneficiaries, or maintenance-only work). Do not close the account on the strength of a variation that drops scope or price — s 12(4) keeps the requirement on.
