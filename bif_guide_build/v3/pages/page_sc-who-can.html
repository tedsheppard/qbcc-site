Valid subcontractor’s charge

Who can give a notice of charge

Chapter 4 reserves the subcontractor’s charge for a particular kind of person in a particular kind of relationship. Before you spend any time on form, content or timing, you need to be sure you are within the class the chapter protects — a “subcontractor” who has contracted to perform “work” under a contract with a “contractor”. If you are outside that class, the notice has nothing to bite on and any charge purportedly given under s 122 will not attach.

Section 104 BIF Act — Definitions (extract)

contractor, for a contract, means the party to the contract who is required to carry out work under the contract.

contract price, for a contract, means the amount the contractor is entitled to be paid under the contract or, if the amount can not be accurately calculated, the reasonable estimate of the amount the contractor is entitled to be paid under the contract.

land means any land within Queensland and includes land under water.

subcontractor’s charge see section 109(4).

work see section 105.

The contractor / subcontractor relationship

Section 104 defines “contractor” as the party to the contract who is required to carry out work under the contract. The chapter then operates downwards: a person who has contracted with that contractor (or with another subcontractor) to perform part of the work is the “subcontractor” entitled to give a notice of charge under s 122. The status is fixed by the contract, not by what happens on site. As the Court put it in Re Gradeline Contracting Pty Ltd [1998] 2 Qd R 251, “what characterises a subcontractor as defined is not what he does but that he contracts with a contractor for the performance of works”.1

Two points follow. First, you must identify the right counterparty. The chain matters: your charge attaches to money payable to your contractor by the person above your contractor (the employer or superior contractor), so you need a real, bilateral contract with the party immediately above you in the chain. A direct employee, a labour-hire worker placed on site by an agency, or someone supplying material to the head contractor on a sale-of-goods basis is not a subcontractor in the s 104 sense. Second, the inquiry is about what was contracted to be done, not what was actually done on the day. The court will read the agreement and ask whether what the claimant promised to do is “work” within s 105.2

The work has to be “work” under the chapter

Eligibility is not just about the label “subcontractor”; it is about the subject matter of the contract. Section 105 carries an inclusive definition of “work” that captures construction, alteration, repair, decoration, demolition, removal or relocation of a building or structure, plus the supply of materials used or brought on premises in connection with that work. Several recurring fact patterns sit awkwardly with that definition and routinely knock claimants out of the chapter:

  • Mere supply of goods. A contract that does no more than oblige a party to fabricate and deliver chattels, with no work to be done after delivery, is not a contract for work and will not support a charge — whether the head contract is for fabrication and delivery, or it is the subcontract that is so limited.3
  • Pure hire of plant or machinery. Hiring out compressors, plant or machinery, without the supplier doing work on the land, is excluded. Where the “hire” in truth involves the subcontractor providing plant and an operator who performs the work, the arrangement may be a contract for work despite the contractual label.4
  • Off-site work that is not integral to the construction. Work done off the land where the structure is being built is generally not work within the chapter; if a substantial part of what was contracted is not work, none of the contract may fall within the regime.5
  • Damages, interest and restitution. Damages for breach of contract, costs and interest on a judgment, and damages for conversion are not “work done” and cannot be the subject of a charge.6

The territorial limit — land in Queensland

The chapter only applies where the land on which the head contract work is being performed is in Queensland. That is the consequence of the s 104 definition of “land” (“any land within Queensland”) read with the operative provisions of the chapter. In Transfield Pty Ltd v Fondside Australia Pty Ltd [2000] QSC 480, Mullins J considered a notice of charge given by the receiver of a subcontractor for work on a gas pipeline running between Victoria and NSW. Her Honour held that the equivalent legislation applied only where the relevant land was within Queensland; the reference to “land” in a Queensland statute is prima facie a reference to land within the jurisdiction, and there was nothing in the Act to suggest a wider territorial reach. That construction is now reflected on the face of s 104. If your project is in another State, Chapter 4 simply does not give you a charge, no matter how clearly you fit the “subcontractor” description.

Strict construction of the entitlement

The courts have consistently treated the chapter as a remedial scheme conferring a special privilege on a restricted class, the price of that privilege being strict compliance with its terms.7 Eligibility is the first place that strictness bites: if you are not on the chapter’s side of the line — if you are a supplier rather than a subcontractor, or your “work” is really hire, or the project sits in another jurisdiction — the charge does not attach and a notice purporting to create one is, in the language of s 122(9), “of no effect”.

Practice tips

  • Map the contractual chain before you serve. Identify who your contract is with, who that party’s contract is with, and where the money you want to charge is sitting. The notice goes to the person above your contractor — you need to be sure that person is “the person obliged to pay the money under the contract” for the purposes of s 122(1).
  • Read your subcontract and ask the Gradeline question: what did you contract to do? If the agreement reduces to delivering goods, hiring out plant, or providing services that are not integral to construction on Queensland land, you may not be a “subcontractor” for chapter 4 purposes — even if everyone on site calls you one.
  • Treat “hire” labels with care. A contract called a “hire agreement” that in substance requires you to operate the plant and perform the work can still be a contract for work; conversely, dressing up a bare equipment hire as a “subcontract” will not bring it within the chapter.
  • Strip out the parts of your claim that are damages, interest or pure cost recovery. Those amounts cannot be charged under s 122 and including them risks rendering the notice non-compliant.
  • Confirm the work is on land within Queensland. Cross-border infrastructure, offshore fabrication and interstate projects need a separate analysis — do not assume the chapter follows the work.
  • If eligibility is borderline, take advice before serving. A notice given by a person outside the protected class produces no charge and may expose you to the costs and damages consequences in s 130 if it has been used to freeze payment.
