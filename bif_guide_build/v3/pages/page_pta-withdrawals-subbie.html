Project trust accounts

Withdrawals to pay subcontractor beneficiaries

Once a project trust is established, the project trust account is the only lawful conduit for paying a subcontractor beneficiary for work done under the head contract. Section 20 fixes both the source of the payment (the project trust account) and the destination (a financial-institution account nominated by, and controlled by, the beneficiary). Get either limb wrong and the contracted party commits an offence carrying up to 200 penalty units or one year’s imprisonment.

Section 20 BIF Act — All payments to subcontractor beneficiaries to be paid from project trust account

(1) The contracted party for a project trust contract may only pay an amount to a subcontractor beneficiary of the project trust—

(a) from the project trust account; and

(b) by depositing the amount into the account of a financial institution nominated by the beneficiary.

Maximum penalty—200 penalty units or 1 year’s imprisonment.

(2) To remove any doubt, it is declared that the obligation to pay an amount from the project trust account applies whether or not the amount is held in the account when it is to be paid.

Note—

See section 51 about covering shortfalls.

(3) The account nominated by the subcontractor beneficiary under subsection (1)(b) must be—

(a) controlled by the beneficiary; and

(b) if the beneficiary is also required to establish a project trust for its subcontract—the account for the project trust for the subcontract.

(4) This section does not apply to—

(a) a retention amount withheld from payment to a subcontractor beneficiary if the amount is deposited into a retention trust account of which the subcontractor is, or will be, a beneficiary; and

(b) a retention amount to be released to a subcontractor beneficiary from a retention trust account.

When a withdrawal is permitted

A withdrawal from the project trust account to a subcontractor beneficiary is only permitted to discharge an amount the contracted party owes that beneficiary for subcontracted work under the head contract. The trust is not a general operating account: s 11 makes the trust an account “over amounts payable in connection with” the project trust contract or subcontract, and “primarily for the benefit of subcontractors”. The corollary is that the contracted party (the head contractor as trustee) cannot withdraw funds from the trust account to pay its own overheads, its other projects, its tax, or anyone who is not a subcontractor beneficiary of that particular trust.

The permission to pay a subcontractor beneficiary is also a compulsion: under s 20(1) a payment to a subcontractor beneficiary “may only” be made from the project trust account and into the beneficiary’s nominated account. There is no lawful alternative route — the contracted party cannot pay the subbie out of its general business account and reimburse the trust later, and it cannot ask the subbie to take cash or a cheque drawn elsewhere. The form of payment is prescribed.

The payment-instruction process

Two pieces of information have to be in place before the contracted party instructs the financial institution to release funds. First, the trust account itself must be properly opened in the contracted party’s name as trustee at an approved financial institution under s 18 — not a virtual or sub-account, and not subordinate to any other account. Second, the beneficiary must have nominated a destination account that satisfies s 20(3): it must be an account at a financial institution that the beneficiary controls, and if the beneficiary is itself required to run a project trust for its own subcontract, the nominated account must be that downstream project trust account. The trustee is responsible for confirming both points before pushing the payment through.

The instruction itself is then a straightforward electronic transfer from the project trust account to the nominated account. The deposit is what discharges the head contractor’s payment obligation to the subcontractor beneficiary — mirroring the rule at s 19(3), where a contracting party’s payment is taken to be made when the amount is deposited into the project trust account. Cash withdrawals, transfers to the trustee’s own account first, or transfers to a third party at the subbie’s direction all fall outside what s 20 authorises.

Paying even when the account is short

Section 20(2) is the provision project teams most often misread. The obligation to pay a subcontractor beneficiary “from the project trust account” applies “whether or not the amount is held in the account when it is to be paid”. Put plainly: if the subbie is owed $200,000 today and only $50,000 is sitting in the trust account today, the trustee is still required to pay the full $200,000 from the trust account on the due date. The shortfall is not the subcontractor’s problem — it is the trustee’s. The note to s 20(2) cross-refers to s 51, which obliges the trustee to top the trust account up out of its own funds to cover the shortfall before the payment date.

That mechanism produces the order of payment the regime is built around. Funds come into the trust account from the contracting party under s 19. Funds go out of the trust account to subcontractor beneficiaries under s 20. If incoming funds are insufficient to meet the next outgoing payment in full, the trustee makes up the gap from its own resources under s 51 so that the s 20 payment still goes out on time and in full. The trust never lawfully runs an unfunded payment to a subbie — the trustee funds it.

Retention amounts sit outside section 20

Section 20(4) carves out two specific kinds of retention movement from the s 20 process. The first is a retention amount withheld by the head contractor from a payment otherwise due to a subcontractor beneficiary, where that retention is deposited into a retention trust account of which the subcontractor is (or will be) a beneficiary. The second is the release of retention from a retention trust account back to the subcontractor when retention is due to be released. Both of those movements run through the retention trust regime in Part 3, not through the project trust account. The practical point is that a single progress claim from a subbie can produce two separate flows: the cash component is paid out under s 20 from the project trust account into the subbie’s nominated account, and the retention component (if any) is dealt with under the retention trust rules.

What this means on a project

  • Every dollar paid to a subcontractor beneficiary for subcontracted work under the head contract must leave the project trust account and land in the beneficiary’s nominated account. There is no alternative route — not the head contractor’s general account, not cash, not a third-party direction.
  • Before instructing payment, confirm two things: the account being debited is the project trust account opened under s 18, and the destination account is one the beneficiary controls (and is the beneficiary’s own project trust account if a downstream trust is required of them under s 20(3)(b)).
  • Pay the full amount due on the due date even if incoming funds from the contracting party haven’t arrived in time. Section 20(2) puts the timing risk on the trustee, and s 51 requires the trustee to cover any shortfall from its own funds before the payment goes out.
  • Do not withdraw trust funds for any non-beneficiary purpose. The trust is for the beneficiary class only; using trust funds for the trustee’s overheads, other projects, or other creditors is outside s 20 and triggers the offence in s 20(1).
  • Run the cash and retention components of a progress claim through different pipes: cash through s 20 from the project trust account; retention into or out of a retention trust account under Part 3. Section 20(4) keeps retention movements outside the project trust process.
  • Keep the bank-account paperwork tidy. A subbie’s nominated-account form should record that the account is controlled by the subbie and, where the subbie is itself a head contractor on its own eligible contract, that the account is its project trust account. That is the documentation that demonstrates s 20(3) compliance if a withdrawal is later questioned.

Where the beneficiary is itself running a project trust under s 12 for its own subcontract, s 20(3)(b) requires the nominated account to be that project trust account, so the funds remain inside the trust regime as they pass down the chain.
