Statutory suspension of works

Protection from liability while suspended

Where a claimant has validly suspended work under s 98, the Act confers two entitlements that are not available at general law: an immunity from liability for loss the respondent suffers during the suspension, and a right to be reimbursed any loss or expense the claimant incurs if the respondent uses the suspension as the occasion to remove work from the contract. The right is time-limited — it runs only until the respondent makes payment, plus three further business days — so the start and end dates require careful attention.

Section 98 BIF Act — claimant’s right to suspend work

98 Claimant’s right to suspend work

(1) A claimant may suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under a construction contract if at least 2 business days have passed since the claimant gave notice of intention to do so to the respondent under section 78 or 92.

(2) The right conferred under subsection (1) exists until the day on which the claimant receives payment from the respondent of the amount mentioned in section 78(1) or 92(1), and continues for another 3 business days immediately following that day.

(3) If, in exercising the right to suspend carrying out construction work or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract, the claimant incurs a loss or expense because the respondent removes any part of the work or supply from the contract, the respondent is liable to pay the claimant the amount of the loss or expense.

(4) A claimant who suspends carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract under subsection (1) is not liable for any loss or damage suffered by the respondent, or by any person claiming through the respondent, because of the claimant not carrying out that work or not supplying those goods and services, during the suspension.

The immunity in s 98(4)

Section 98(4) is the central operative provision of the protection. While the suspension is on foot, the claimant is “not liable for any loss or damage” the respondent (or anyone claiming through the respondent) suffers because the work is not being performed or the goods are not being supplied. That covers the claims a respondent would otherwise be expected to bring — delay damages, liquidated damages for the period of the stoppage, the cost of engaging another contractor to maintain the program, and any flow-on losses to head contractors or principals up the chain. The immunity attaches to the act of suspending, provided the s 98(1) preconditions are satisfied: a payment claim under s 78 or an adjudicated amount under s 92 is unpaid, the claimant has given the respondent the required notice of intention to suspend, and at least two business days have passed since that notice.

Two practical points follow. First, the immunity only protects a suspension validly engaged under s 98 — it does not protect a cessation of work characterised as a s 98 suspension where the statutory preconditions are absent. If the s 78 or s 92 trigger is not present, or the notice has not been given, the suspension constitutes a repudiatory breach and the claimant bears the consequences. Second, the protection is bounded in time by s 98(2): the claimant is immune during “the suspension”, and the suspension only legitimately runs while the right in s 98(1) subsists.

Removal of work during suspension — s 98(3)

Section 98(3) is the corollary. If, while the claimant is validly suspended, the respondent removes part of the work or the supply from the contract — descoping, terminating for convenience over the suspended portion, or re-letting the package to another contractor — and the claimant incurs a loss or expense as a result, the respondent is liable to pay that loss or expense. That entitlement operates alongside any contractual rights available on descoping or termination, and does not require the claimant to elect between contractual and statutory remedies. The trigger is causal: the loss or expense must be incurred “because” the respondent removed the work, while the claimant was exercising the s 98(1) right.

When does the right end? Receipt of payment and three business days

Section 98(2) ties the duration of the right (and so the immunity) to the day the claimant “receives payment” of the s 78(1) or s 92(1) amount, plus another three business days. What counts as “receives payment” was considered in Beckhaus Civil Pty Ltd v Brewarrina Shire Council (No 2) [2004] NSWSC 1160, where Master Macready treated the touchstone of “to pay” and “receives payment” as the act of the debtor transferring, by way of satisfaction, what is owed to the creditor — with the qualification that a claimant has not “received payment” if it cannot obtain the benefit of the money because of conditions placed on its receipt:

a claimant will not have ‘received payment’… in circumstances where the claimant is unable to obtain the benefit of that payment due to conditions, such as the provision of a secured bank guarantee, placed on its receipt.

The point is significant because respondents sometimes seek to end the suspension by tendering payment in a form the claimant cannot freely use — payment into a controlled account, payment conditional on the claimant signing a release, or payment by way of a bank guarantee held back against unrelated claims. On the Beckhaus approach, none of these starts the three-business-day tail in s 98(2) running, because the claimant has not yet received payment in the relevant sense.

The other end of the question — when the right ends if the legal status of the underlying debt later changes — was considered in On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163. The claimant had suspended after the respondent failed to pay an adjudicated amount. While the proceedings were on foot, an expert determination under the contract concluded that, on net, the claimant in fact owed the respondent more than the adjudicated amount, and the respondent argued that the right to suspend ended at the date of the expert determination. Crowe AJ rejected that argument. The right to suspend continued until either the outstanding amount was set aside, or there was an unequivocal communication to the claimant acknowledging discharge of the payment claim, the adjudicated amount or, on those facts, the provisional judgment debt. Constructive receipt was held to occur only when consent orders were made setting aside the adjudication amount, and the right then ended three business days later.

Practice tips

  • The s 98(4) immunity only attaches if the s 98(1) preconditions are met — a s 78 or s 92 trigger, the notice of intention to suspend, and the two business day wait. Omitting any of those forfeits the protection and exposes the claimant to delay, LD and step-in claims.
  • Record the start of the suspension and retain a clean record of it. If the respondent later removes work from the contract during the suspension, s 98(3) confers a statutory right to the loss or expense — but the timing and the causal link must be proven.
  • A conditional or controlled-account payment should not be treated as the trigger for the three business day tail. If the funds are not freely available, on Beckhaus the claimant has not yet “received payment” for the purposes of s 98(2).
  • If the respondent contends that some later event (an expert determination, a contractual set-off, an interim accounting) has retrospectively ended the right to suspend, the position in On Forbes is that the right continues until the outstanding amount is actually set aside, or until the respondent unequivocally communicates discharge of the relevant amount.
  • Lift the suspension promptly once payment is received in a usable form. The immunity expires three business days after receipt; remaining off site beyond that point is no longer a s 98 suspension and is no longer protected.
  1. Beckhaus Civil Pty Ltd v Brewarrina Shire Council (No 2) [2004] NSWSC 1160 (Master Macready) — meaning of “pay” and “receives payment” under the equivalent NSW provisions; a claimant has not “received payment” where conditions on receipt prevent it from obtaining the benefit of the money.
  2. On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163 (Crowe AJ) — on s 26(2)(b) of the ACT Act (equivalent to s 98(1)): the right to suspend continues until the outstanding amount is set aside or the respondent unequivocally communicates discharge of the payment claim, adjudicated amount or provisional judgment debt; the s 98(2)-equivalent three business day tail then runs from constructive receipt.
