Valid subcontractor’s charge

Timing of the notice

A subcontractor’s charge only attaches to money that is still payable up the chain when the notice is given. Get the timing wrong and there is either nothing for the charge to bite on, or the notice is “of no effect” under s 122(9). The chapter sets two windows — one for a notice that covers progress money (and possibly retention as well), and a longer one for a notice that targets retention only — and it tells you what money the notice can reach once it is given.

Section 122 BIF Act — Notice of claim (timing extracts)

(4) The notice of claim may be given even if the work is not completed, or payment of the money relating to the charge is not yet due.

(5) However, if the work has been completed, the notice of claim must be given within 3 months after practical completion for the work.

(6) The claim may relate only to—

(a) money payable to the subcontractor by the date the notice is given; and

(b) money to become payable to the subcontractor after the date the notice is given if the money is for work done by the subcontractor before that date.

(8) If the notice of claim relates only to a retention amount for the contract, the notice—

(a) may be given at any time while work under the contract is being performed; and

(b) must be given within 3 months after the expiration of the defects liability period for the contract.

(9) If the notice of claim is not given in compliance with this section, the notice is of no effect and the subcontractor’s charge does not attach.

The two windows

Section 122 contemplates two distinct timing regimes depending on what the notice is aimed at. A notice that claims money payable under the contract generally — progress payments, or progress payments together with retention — can be given before the work is finished and before payment is due (s 122(4)), but once the work has been completed the notice must be given within three months after practical completion for the work (s 122(5)). A notice that is expressed to claim against retention only travels on a different track: it may be given at any time while the contract work is being performed, and at the back end it must be given within three months after the expiration of the defects liability period (s 122(8)).

The choice between the two is a matter of substance, not labelling. Reading s 122 as a whole, a charge may be claimed either on retention money, or on moneys otherwise payable under the contract, or on both. The only material consequence of choosing the third category is that the notice has to be given within three months of completion of the work, and not within the extended period that is available where the charge is sought against retention only.1 A subcontractor who wants the longer window must restrict the notice in terms to retention money; a notice expressed simply in relation to “money payable under the contract” will charge retention as well, but it has to be given inside the three-month-from-completion window or it fails.2

What “completion” means for the three-month clock

The three months in s 122(5) runs from completion of the subcontract works, not from completion of any particular package of work that the claim happens to cover. That was decided in Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd [2004] QSC 198, where Chesterman J held that “the work” in the equivalent provision had to mean the whole of the subcontract work in order for the related subsections to be reconciled. Reading the section that way meant the time limit did not start running until all of the work required by the subcontract had been done; the alternative would produce a separate time limit for each item of work as it was completed, which would defeat the protective purpose of the chapter and load an unworkable burden on subcontractors who were still on site. The Court of Appeal upheld that construction in Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd [2005] 1 Qd R 610; [2005] QCA 61, Jerrard JA endorsing the reasoning at [21]–[22].

The chapter also looks the other way. Section 122(4) makes clear that the notice can be given even though the work is not finished and even though payment is not yet due — you do not have to wait for an invoice to mature, or for practical completion, before you act. In practice, that means the safe course on a troubled job is to serve early rather than late: there is no “too soon” problem under s 122, only a “too late” one.3

What money the notice can reach

Timing is not just about when you serve. Section 122(6) limits what a validly-timed notice can attach to. The claim may relate only to money that is payable to the subcontractor by the date the notice is given, or money that becomes payable after that date if it is for work done by the subcontractor before the notice. You cannot use a single notice to capture future work you have not yet performed. If further work is done after the notice and you want to charge the money for it, a fresh notice is required — subject to the same s 122(5) and s 122(8) windows, and to the rule that successive notices can be given where the earlier ones were defective but not where a charge has been extinguished by the time-bar in the chapter.4

Withdrawal does not reset the clock

A subcontractor can withdraw a notice in whole or in part at any time, in the approved form, by giving notice of the withdrawal to the person to whom the original notice of claim was given, and by giving a copy to each person who received a copy of the original notice (s 125). Withdrawing a notice does not enlarge the time for giving a fresh one: any new notice still has to satisfy s 122(5) or s 122(8) as the case may be, measured from the original completion or defects liability period.

Practice tips

  • Diarise practical completion of the subcontract works the day it occurs. The s 122(5) clock starts from completion of the whole of your subcontract, not from completion of a particular item or trade package — but it is still only three months.
  • If you want to claim progress money (with or without retention), serve before practical completion if you can, and in any event within three months after it. Section 122(4) lets you serve before payment is due; there is no advantage to waiting.
  • If only retention is left and the three-month-from-completion window has passed, you can still serve under s 122(8) — but the notice must be expressed to be limited to retention, and it has to be given before three months after the defects liability period expires.
  • Do not bundle future work into a single notice. The charge only reaches money for work done before the notice is given (s 122(6)(b)). Plan to serve a further notice if more work is performed after the first.
  • Track the defects liability period as carefully as you track practical completion. Many retention-only charges are lost because the DLP expired weeks earlier and nobody had it on the register.
  • If a notice has gone out with a problem — wrong addressee, missing certification, ambiguous description — consider whether there is still time to serve a corrected notice. A second, compliant notice within time is available where the first was defective; it is not available once a charge has been extinguished by operation of the chapter’s time-bars.
