Requirements of an adjudication response

Copy to the claimant within 2 BD

Lodging the adjudication response with the adjudicator isn’t the end of the job. Section 83(6) imposes a separate, free-standing obligation: you must give the claimant a copy of the response within 2 business days of giving it to the adjudicator. It’s a short, mechanical step at the back end of the response process — but missing it leaves you exposed.

Section 83(6) BIF Act — copy to the claimant

(6) If the respondent gives the adjudicator an adjudication response under this section, the respondent must give a copy of the response to the claimant not more than 2 business days after giving the response to the adjudicator.

What the obligation actually requires

Three things follow from the way s 83(6) is drafted. First, the trigger is the act of giving the adjudication response to the adjudicator — not the date the adjudicator acknowledges receipt, and not the date the application was received. Diary the 2 business day deadline from the moment your response goes out the door. Second, what you give the claimant is a copy of the response — the same document, with the same submissions and the same annexures. You can’t hand over a stripped-down version or hold back attachments you’d prefer the claimant didn’t see. Third, the window is measured in business days under the BIF Act definition, so weekends and public holidays don’t count.

The obligation only bites if you actually give an adjudication response. If you elect not to respond — which is sometimes a tactical call where the claim is hopeless on its face or where the schedule already covers the field — s 83(6) is never enlivened.

Service and receipt are different concepts under the Act

Section 83(6) speaks of “giving” a copy. The Act distinguishes between service of a document and the addressee’s receipt of it, and the distinction has been the subject of judicial comment in adjacent contexts. In CMF Projects Pty Ltd v Masic Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 209, an adjudicator’s notice of acceptance had been sent not to the respondent’s registered office but to a post office box. Daubney J drew a clear line between service and receipt, and held that the adjudicator had erred in equating deposit of the notice into the post office box with receipt of it.1 The takeaway for s 83(6) is straightforward: don’t cut the timing fine by relying on a method of delivery that puts the document somewhere in the postal system but not actually in the claimant’s hands. Email to a known address used in the claim, or hand delivery against signature, is the safer course.

What happens if you miss it

Section 83(6) is mandatory in form (“must give”), but the Act doesn’t spell out the consequence of breach. The closest analogue in the case law is Rise Constructions v El-Hajj [2019] VSC 818, where Digby J considered the equivalent Victorian timing provision. The challenge there was the other way around — the adjudicator had refused to consider an out-of-time response — but Digby J’s reasoning confirmed that the response timing rules in the Victorian Act (the equivalent of s 83(1)(b) here) are taken seriously, and that a response given outside the prescribed period can validly be disregarded.2 The point for s 83(6) is more limited: the claimant deprived of a timely copy may have natural justice arguments downstream, particularly if the adjudicator proceeds to determination without the claimant having had a fair opportunity to know what was put against it. Treat the 2 business day window as hard.

What this means on a project

  • The moment your response goes to the adjudicator, diary the 2 business day deadline for the claimant copy. Don’t wait for the adjudicator’s acknowledgement — the clock runs from when you give the response, not from any reply.
  • Send the claimant the same response and the same annexures you sent the adjudicator. A pared-back version is not a “copy”.
  • Use a method of service that puts the document in the claimant’s actual possession — email to the address used on the payment claim, or hand delivery against receipt. Don’t rely on a post office box or generic registered office address if you can avoid it.
  • Keep proof of what you sent and when. A sent-mail record with the response and annexures attached is usually enough; if you hand-deliver, get the package signed for.
  • If you decide not to lodge an adjudication response at all, s 83(6) doesn’t apply — but make sure that decision is recorded internally so no one second-guesses it later.
  • Count in business days under the Act, not calendar days and not your contract’s definition of working days. Public holidays in the project’s jurisdiction matter.
  1. CMF Projects Pty Ltd v Masic Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 209 (Daubney J — distinction between service and receipt under the predecessor Act; deposit into a post office box is not receipt). See also s 102 of the BIF Act on the meaning of receipt.
  2. Rise Constructions v El-Hajj [2019] VSC 818 (Digby J — adjudicator validly declined to consider an adjudication response made outside the time required by s 21(1)(b) of the Victorian Act, the equivalent of s 83(1)(b) of the BIF Act).
