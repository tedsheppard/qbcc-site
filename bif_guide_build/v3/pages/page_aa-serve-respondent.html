Requirements of an adjudication application

Serve a copy on the respondent

Lodging the adjudication application with the registrar is only half the job. Within four business days of making the application, the claimant must give the respondent a copy of the application and a copy of any submissions that accompanied it. Skip this step, get the timing wrong, or hand over a document that isn’t a true copy, and the adjudicator can be left without jurisdiction — even if everything else has been done properly.

Section 79(4) BIF Act — service on the respondent

(4) The claimant must give the following documents to the respondent within 4 business days after making the adjudication application—

(a) a copy of the adjudication application;

(b) a copy of the submissions, if any, accompanying the application under subsection (3).

“Give” means actually deliver, and the claimant has to prove it

The obligation in s 79(4) is to “give” the documents to the respondent. That word does real work. The Queensland Court of Appeal in Niclin Constructions Pty Ltd v SHA Premier Constructions Pty Ltd & Anor [2019] QCA 177 upheld Ryan J at first instance and confirmed that an adjudication application must be served on the respondent “as soon as possible” after lodgement with the registrar. The reason is structural: the BIF Act runs on what Ryan J called “brutally fast timeframes”, and the respondent’s response clock and the adjudicator’s decision clock both depend on knowing that the application is on foot and what it contains.1

Because s 79(4) is a precondition to a valid adjudication, the claimant carries the onus of proving service. In Equinox Construction Pty Ltd v Henning & Anor [2021] QSC 223, Ryan J declared an adjudication determination void where the claimant relied on having posted the application in a “postage paid” envelope under s 39 / s 39A of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) but could not explain how postage had been calculated or paid, and the respondent denied receipt. Her Honour held that strict proof is required — evidence of attending a post office on a particular date is not enough to establish how posting was effected.2 The practical lesson: if you are going to rely on post, keep the receipts and the tracking; if you are going to rely on email, make sure the address is one the respondent has nominated for service and that you have a record of delivery.

The same theme runs through the cloud-storage cases. In McCarthy v TKM Builders Pty Ltd & Anor [2020] QSC 301, Martin J held that emailing the respondent a Dropbox link to the submissions was not “giving” the submissions for s 79(4) purposes — the respondent does not become aware of the contents of a document merely by being referred to a hosted file. The court required a positive action on the part of the receiver before the document can be said to have been given. The earlier reasoning of McMurdo J in Conveyor & General Engineering Pty Ltd v Basetec Services Pty Ltd & Anor [2014] QSC 30 on Dropbox service points the same way.3

What counts as “a copy”

The other recurring fight is over what the claimant has actually handed over. Section 79(4)(a) requires “a copy of the adjudication application”. In Equa Building Services Pty Ltd v KLG Trading Pty Ltd [2021] NSWSC 1674, Stevenson J held that the equivalent NSW provision tolerates degrees of non-compliance, provided the differences between what went to the adjudicator and what went to the respondent do not prejudice the substantive operation of the Act. The claimant in that case served the adjudication application electronically through an online “lockbox” and provided a hard copy to the respondent. The respondent complained that the hard copy was missing a video on a USB, three documents, contained illegible photographs and had mislabelled documents. Stevenson J found those differences trivial:

it would not be consistent with that legislative object for s 17(5) of the NSW SOP Act… to be read as having the effect that the slightest difference between the adjudication application as submitted to the adjudicator and the copy served on the respondent rendered invalid the purported service of the copy adjudication application, let alone an adjudication determination based on it.

The Queensland courts have taken a stricter view where the differences are not trivial. In Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2023] QSC 290, Williams J held that the “QBCC PDF Form” auto-generated as a record after lodgement was not “a copy” of the Electronic Form actually lodged — the differences were “not trivial”, the claimant could have screen-captured each page of the platform when lodging, and there was no evidence that doing so was onerous or inconsistent with the statutory timeframes. The June 2024 amendments to the BIF Act have rendered the Iris Broadbeach approved-form principle nugatory, but the underlying point about giving the respondent a true copy of what was lodged still holds.4

The four-business-day window and how s 102 fits in

The window is four business days after the application is “made” — that is, after the application is lodged with the registrar under s 79(1). The mechanics of service sit in s 102: a notice required to be given under Chapter 3 may be given in any way provided in the construction contract (for example, service by email, where the contract allows it), and that is in addition to, not in substitution for, s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) and any other law about giving notices. Service on an agent is preserved (s 102(3)(a)) and the respondent’s acknowledgement is not required (s 102(3)(b)). For corporations, s 109X of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) is also available — leaving or posting to the registered office, or personal delivery to a director resident in Australia.

Two practical points fall out of the s 102 framework. First, service on a post office box is not, by itself, valid service under s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act or s 109X of the Corporations Act, so unless the contract expressly provides for PO box service, do not rely on it. Second, when serving by email, the claimant should be able to show that the address was specified by the respondent for service under the Act — a generic project email or a quote inquiry address may not be enough.

Practice tips

  • Diarise the four-business-day deadline the moment the application is lodged. The clock runs from lodgement, not from the registrar’s referral to an adjudicator under s 79(5).
  • Give the respondent the same document you lodged with the registrar — not a system-generated receipt or a PDF cover sheet. If you lodged through the QBCC online portal, screen-capture each page of the form before submitting and serve those.
  • Include the submissions. Section 79(4)(b) requires a copy of any submissions that accompanied the application; serving the form alone is not enough where submissions were lodged.
  • Use a service method you can prove. Personal delivery, courier with proof of delivery, or email to an address the respondent has nominated for service under the contract or under the Act all work; bare “postage paid” envelopes without supporting evidence may not.
  • Avoid Dropbox or cloud-link service for the documents themselves. A link is not the document; the respondent has to actually receive the contents within the four-business-day window.
  • If the contract specifies an email address or a method of service, follow it. Section 102(1) lets you use the contractual mechanism, and doing so makes proof of service straightforward.
  • If something goes wrong with service, fix it inside the four-business-day window if you can. Outside it, the only safe path is usually to lodge again — the courts will not rescue an application that was never properly given to the respondent.
