Requirements of a payment claim

Served on the right person, the right way

A payment claim only triggers the BIF Act regime if it is given by the right entity, to the right entity, in a way that the Act recognises as valid service. Pick the wrong respondent and the claim falls outside s 75(1); pick a method of service the Act doesn’t accept and the clock for a payment schedule may never start running. Both points are jurisdictional — an adjudicator who proceeds on a misdirected or misserved claim is acting without authority.

Section 75(1) and section 102 BIF Act — who, and how

75 Making payment claim

(1) A person (the claimant) who is, or who claims to be, entitled to a progress payment may give a payment claim to the person (the respondent) who, under the relevant construction contract, is or may be liable to make the payment.

102 Service of notices

(1) A notice or other document that, under this chapter, is authorised or required to be given to a person may be given to the person in the way, if any, provided under the relevant construction contract.

(2) Subsection (1) is in addition to, and does not limit or exclude, the Acts Interpretation Act 1954, section 39 or the provisions of any other law about the giving of notices.

(3) To remove any doubt, it is declared that nothing in this Act—

(a) excludes the proper service of notices or documents by a person’s agent; or

(b) requires a person’s acknowledgement of a notice or document properly given to the person.

Who you can serve: the “is or may be liable” question

Section 75(1) lets a claimant give a payment claim to the person who, under the relevant construction contract, “is or may be liable” to pay. The Queensland Court of Appeal nailed down what those words mean in Mansouri & Anor v Aquamist Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 209. The expression “may be liable” does not loosen the requirement that the respondent be a party to the construction contract for whom the claimant has actually undertaken to perform work or supply related goods and services. It picks up the fact that progress-payment liability is provisional — it may or may not ultimately be established — not the identity of who can be served. The Court put it this way:

The expression “may be liable” does not qualify that requirement. It serves a different purpose. Progress payments under BCIPA are made on account of a liability which may or may not ultimately be established… Neither expression suggests any liberalisation of the requirement that “the respondent” must be a person for whom the claimant undertook to carry out construction work or to whom the claimant undertook to supply related goods and services.

The respondent must therefore be a party to the construction contract, not merely someone who “may” be a party. NSW authority on the equivalent provision is to the same effect: in Grave v Blazevic Holdings Pty Ltd [2010] NSWCA 324, McDougall J (Allsop P and Macfarlan JA agreeing) held that the proposed recipient must not be a stranger to the contract, although primary or secondary liability (e.g. as guarantor) and disputes about quantification or whether anything is owed at all are all caught by “is or may be liable”.1

Service on agents, superintendents and project personnel

Section 102(1) is “facultative”: it lets you serve in any manner the contract provides, but it does not displace s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) or the general law on service.2 Section 102(3)(a) preserves service by a person’s agent. Whether a superintendent, quantity surveyor, project manager or other intermediary is the principal’s agent for receiving payment claims turns on the contract and on the parties’ conduct — not on the title.3 Where the contract expressly states that the superintendent has no authority to receive claims under the Act, service on the superintendent will fail.4

The most useful Queensland decision for project teams is Canadian Solar Construction Pty Ltd v Re Oakey Pty Ltd [2023] QSC 288. The claimant emailed its payment claim to the respondent’s sole director and to five individuals working on the project. The director never received it; five of the six recipients did, four of whom were superintendent/project-management representatives with authority to assess payment claims. Freeburn J held the claim had been validly given to the respondent, and described the function of contractual service clauses as being “to facilitate service, and not to restrict the means by which service might be effected”. His Honour endorsed the approach of the NSW Supreme Court in BCFK Holdings Pty Ltd v Rork Projects Pty Ltd [2022] NSWSC 1706 — a party who actually receives a payment claim should not be heard to say service didn’t happen because of some technical shortcoming in how it was effected.5

Service by email and through cloud platforms

Email is now the practical default. Section 102(1) lets the contract specify email; even where it doesn’t, s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act reaches email as a “similar facility” in many circumstances, although the position is not free from doubt.6 Under s 24 of the Electronic Transactions (Queensland) Act 2001, the time of receipt of an electronic communication is when it becomes “capable of being retrieved” at a designated address — the email need not be opened or read, and a payment claim caught by a spam filter is still capable of being retrieved.7

Cloud-based delivery is more fragile. Service by Dropbox link has been held to fall outside both s 39 and s 109X of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) because the email delivers only the means of access, not the document itself.8 The same logic has been applied to dedicated platforms such as Corrigo: the document is “received” only when the recipient becomes aware of it.9 In contrast, where the contract designates a platform like Aconex or Payapps as a service channel, service occurs when the document becomes capable of retrieval at that designated address, regardless of whether the recipient has actually opened it.10

Service on a corporation, and the post-office-box trap

For corporate respondents, s 109X of the Corporations Act permits service by leaving the document at, or posting it to, the company’s registered office, or by delivering it personally to a director resident in Australia. Service on a post-office box is not valid service under either s 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act or s 109X — unless the contract itself authorises that mode.11 Where service is by post to the registered office, the document is “received” when it arrives at the office during ordinary business hours; it does not have to come to the attention of any particular individual.12

What this means on a project

  • Identify the respondent before drafting the claim. The respondent must be a party to the construction contract for whom you have actually undertaken to perform the work or supply the goods and services. Mansouri shuts the door on serving someone who “may” be a party.
  • Read the contract’s notice clause and follow it. If the contract nominates an email address, an electronic platform (Aconex, Payapps, Corrigo) or a postal address for service, use that channel. Section 102(1) makes contractual service routes valid by default.
  • If you are serving on a superintendent, project manager or quantity surveyor as agent, check the contract first. If the contract says the agent has authority to receive claims under the Act — or there is a clear pattern of dealing — service is effective. If the contract excludes that authority, serve the principal directly.
  • For email service, send to addresses the recipient has nominated for that purpose, copy multiple project personnel where possible, and keep the delivery and read receipts. After Canadian Solar, multi-addressee delivery to people who can act on the claim is a strong protection against a technical service challenge.
  • Avoid Dropbox or bare cloud links. The document must actually arrive in the recipient’s system, not sit behind a hyperlink. If you must use a cloud platform, attach the document to the email as well.
  • Don’t serve on a post-office box unless the contract authorises it — serve at the registered office or principal place of business. The clock for a payment schedule does not start running on a misserved claim.
  • If you are the respondent and you actually receive the claim, don’t assume technical service failures will save you. The courts have consistently held that actual receipt by someone with authority to deal with the claim cures most service irregularities.
