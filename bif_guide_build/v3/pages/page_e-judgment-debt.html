Enforcing an adjudicator’s decision

Filing the certificate as a judgment debt

If the respondent doesn’t pay the adjudicated amount, you don’t need to sue on the contract. You take the adjudication certificate from the registrar, file it in a court of competent jurisdiction, and from that moment you hold a judgment for the unpaid portion that you can enforce like any other money judgment.

Section 93 BIF Act — Filing of adjudication certificate as judgment debt

93 Filing of adjudication certificate as judgment debt

(1) An adjudication certificate may be filed as a judgment for a debt, and may be enforced, in a court of competent jurisdiction.

(2) An adjudication certificate can not be filed under this section unless it is accompanied by an affidavit by the claimant stating that the whole or a part of the adjudicated amount has not been paid to the claimant at the time the certificate is filed.

(3) If the affidavit states that part of the adjudicated amount has been paid, the judgment is for the unpaid part of the amount only.

Filing mechanics

The mechanics under s 93 are deliberately simple. Once you have the certificate (issued by the registrar after the adjudicated amount falls due and remains unpaid), you file it in a court that has jurisdiction over the dollar value — the Magistrates Court, District Court or Supreme Court depending on the amount. The certificate itself is what becomes the judgment; there is no fresh hearing on the merits.

Two procedural points matter. First, s 93(2) requires a supporting affidavit from the claimant stating that the whole or part of the adjudicated amount remains unpaid at the time of filing. Without that affidavit the certificate cannot be filed. Second, s 93(3) means the judgment tracks reality — if the respondent has already paid part of the adjudicated amount, the judgment is only for the unpaid balance.

Once filed, the judgment can be enforced by the usual means — garnishee orders, enforcement warrants over property, statutory demands. There is no obligation to give the respondent advance notice that the certificate has been filed or that enforcement is on foot.1

The Neller policy — cash flow trumps repayment risk

If the respondent applies to set aside or stay the judgment, the courts approach the application against the background of what the Act is for. The leading Queensland authority is R J Neller Building Pty Ltd v Ainsworth [2008] QCA 397; [2009] 1 Qd R 390. Keane JA (Fraser JA and Fryberg J agreeing) framed the policy this way:

It is evidently the intention of the BCIP Act, and, in particular, s 31 and s 100 to which reference has been made, that the process of adjudication established under that Act should provide a speedy and effective means of ensuring cash flow to builders from the parties with whom they contract… the risk that a builder might not be able to refund moneys ultimately found to be due to a non-residential owner after a successful action by the owner must, I think, be regarded as a risk which, as a matter of policy in the commercial context in which the BCIP Act applies, the legislature has, prima facie at least, assigned to the owner.

The practical effect is that a respondent who simply argues “we might not get our money back if we lose at final hearing” is unlikely to obtain a stay. The Act has already weighed that risk and assigned it to the respondent. Neller was treated as turning on a valid adjudication; where the respondent has a prima facie case that the determination is void for jurisdictional error, the analysis shifts — courts then weigh the policy as a factor going to the nature of the right in question, rather than as a near-conclusive answer.2 A common compromise is to grant interlocutory relief on condition that the disputed amount is paid into court, or secured by bank guarantee, pending final determination.3

What the respondent cannot do under s 93(4)

If the respondent commences proceedings to set the judgment aside, s 93(4) closes off the obvious lines of attack. The respondent cannot bring a counterclaim, raise a defence about matters arising under the construction contract, or challenge the adjudicator’s decision; and the respondent must pay the unpaid portion of the adjudicated amount into court as security pending the outcome.

The recent appellate decision in Hynash Constructions Pty Ltd v BRP Industries Pty Ltd [2025] NSWCA 14 illustrates how strictly that bar is applied. The respondent had paid an up-front 10% establishment and mobilisation fee under the contract and tried to argue, in proceedings to recover the “unpaid portion” of a payment claim, that the up-front payment ought to be credited against the adjudicated amount. The Court of Appeal rejected that — the “unpaid portion” refers to amounts unpaid in response to the payment claim, not to unrelated prior payments. If the respondent wanted credit for an earlier payment, it had to raise it in a payment schedule. By contrast, where a payment was made in direct part-satisfaction of the payment claim itself (for example, a direct payment to a third-party supplier whose invoices formed part of the claim), that is a factual question about how much remains unpaid, not an impermissible cross-claim.4

The s 93(4)(a)(ii) bar on raising defences “in relation to matters arising under the construction contract” has also been read to cover equitable set-off and contractual set-off arguments,5 but not arguments that sit outside the contract — for example, a misleading and deceptive conduct claim under the Australian Consumer Law.6 And where the underlying determination has been quashed for jurisdictional error, the resulting judgment can still be set aside — on the footing that there is in truth no adjudicator’s determination at all, rather than a challenge to the determination’s correctness.7

Practice tips

  • Once the adjudicated amount is overdue, get the certificate from the registrar and file it. You do not need to issue fresh proceedings or prove the underlying claim again.
  • Prepare the s 93(2) affidavit before you file. It must state that the whole or part of the adjudicated amount remains unpaid at the time of filing. If the respondent has paid part, file for the unpaid balance only.
  • Choose the right court for the dollar value. The certificate is filed as a judgment in a court of competent jurisdiction — once filed, the usual enforcement tools (garnishee, enforcement warrant) are available.
  • If you are the respondent and want to challenge enforcement, recognise the steep climb. You will need to pay the unpaid portion into court as security, and you cannot rely on counterclaims, contract-based defences or a frontal attack on the adjudicator’s reasoning. A jurisdictional-error case is the realistic route, often coupled with payment in or a bank guarantee.
  • If you are the respondent and you have already made a payment that should reduce the claim, make sure the basis is clear — a direct payment against the payment claim is a factual reduction of the unpaid portion; a payment under some other contractual obligation is not, and after Hynash you cannot smuggle it in at the enforcement stage if it wasn’t in your payment schedule.
