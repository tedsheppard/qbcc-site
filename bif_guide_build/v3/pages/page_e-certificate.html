Enforcing an adjudicator’s decision

Adjudication certificate

Once an adjudicator has decided in favour of the claimant and the respondent has not paid by the due date, the next step is to obtain an adjudication certificate from the registrar. The certificate is the document filed in court — it can be filed as a judgment for the adjudicated amount, which is the principal mechanism by which the BIF Act is enforced.

Section 91 BIF Act — Adjudication certificate

(1) As soon as practicable after being given a copy of a decision by an adjudicator, but no later than 5 business days after being given the decision, the registrar must give the claimant a certificate (an adjudication certificate) of the decision stating the following matters—

(a) the name of the claimant;

(b) the name of the respondent who is liable to pay the adjudicated amount;

(c) the adjudicated amount;

(d) the date on which payment of the adjudicated amount was required to be paid to the claimant;

(e) the rate of interest payable on the adjudicated amount;

(f) the fees, identified in the decision, that the respondent is to pay;

(g) that the certificate is made under this Act.

(2) However, the registrar does not have to give the claimant an adjudication certificate if—

(a) the respondent is not required to pay an amount under the decision; or

(b) the adjudicator decided he or she does not have jurisdiction to decide the application; or

(c) the adjudicator decided the adjudication application is frivolous or vexatious.

What the certificate is for

An adjudicator’s decision, by itself, is not a court order and cannot be enforced directly. The adjudication certificate is the bridge between the adjudicator’s decision and the courts: once obtained, the claimant may file it as a judgment debt and then invoke the usual enforcement mechanisms — statutory demands, garnishee orders, warrants of execution and the like.

The certificate is issued by the registrar of the Queensland Building and Construction Commission, not by the adjudicator. The adjudicator’s function ends when the decision is given; the registrar’s function is administrative — to take the decision and convert it into a certificate that can be filed in court.

Timing and the registrar’s obligation

Section 91(1) imposes a clear deadline on the registrar: the certificate must be given to the claimant “as soon as practicable” after the registrar receives a copy of the decision, and in any event no later than 5 business days afterwards. The 5-business-day cap is an outer limit, not a target. In practice, certificates are often issued within 1–2 business days of the registrar receiving the decision.

The claimant does not need to apply for the certificate — the obligation runs the other way. The registrar must give it to the claimant. If 5 business days have passed and the certificate has not arrived, the claimant should contact the registry: decisions occasionally go astray between the adjudicator and the registrar, and a follow-up is generally faster than waiting.

What the certificate must contain

The seven matters listed in s 91(1)(a)–(g) are mandatory. The certificate must identify the parties, state the adjudicated amount, give the date by which payment was required, set out the interest rate, identify the fees the respondent must pay, and recite that the certificate is made under the Act. Each of these matters then carries through into the judgment when the certificate is filed in court — so the certificate, on its face, is what fixes the amount of the judgment debt and the rate at which interest accrues.

The “date on which payment… was required to be paid” in (d) is the due date fixed by the adjudicator’s decision under s 88, not the date of the decision itself. That distinction matters for interest calculations and for determining when the claimant became entitled to enforce.

When a certificate will not issue

Section 91(2) sets out three situations in which the registrar does not have to issue a certificate:

  • where the respondent is not required to pay any amount under the decision (for example, the adjudicator decided the adjudicated amount was nil or in the respondent’s favour);
  • where the adjudicator decided he or she did not have jurisdiction to decide the application; or
  • where the adjudicator decided the application was frivolous or vexatious.

In each of those cases there is nothing to enforce, so the certificate provisions do not apply. If the adjudicator declined jurisdiction, the claimant’s remedy is to address the jurisdictional defect (if able to do so) and re-apply, not to seek a certificate over a decision that found there was no jurisdiction in the first place.

Practice tips

  • On receipt of an adjudicator’s decision in the claimant’s favour, diary the due date for payment under s 88 and the 5-business-day window in s 91(1). If payment is not made by the due date, the certificate will usually issue within days.
  • Check the certificate carefully when it arrives. The seven matters in s 91(1)(a)–(g) carry through into the judgment, so any error — wrong party name, wrong amount, wrong due date — should be raised with the registrar before filing.
  • The certificate is not the judgment. To enforce, the claimant must file the certificate as a judgment in a court of competent jurisdiction. That is a separate step under the enforcement provisions later in Chapter 3.
  • If the adjudicated amount is nil, or the adjudicator declined jurisdiction, or the application was held to be frivolous or vexatious, no certificate will issue. The claimant should reassess its options rather than seek a certificate.
  • Retain the original certificate. It will be required on filing in court, and replacement takes time that may not be available if the respondent is approaching insolvency.
  1. The 5-business-day cap in s 91(1) runs from when the registrar is given a copy of the decision, not from when the decision is given to the parties. There is typically a short administrative gap between the two.
  2. The matters listed in s 91(1)(a)–(g) mirror what is needed to enter a money judgment: identification of debtor and creditor, principal sum, due date, interest rate and ancillary fees. The certificate is, in substance, a packaged judgment debt awaiting filing.
