Requirements of a payment claim

The claim must request payment

Section 68(1)(c) requires that the written document actually “request payment of the claimed amount”. This is a requirement BCIPA never had, and it has become a live battleground — the Queensland Court of Appeal has now confirmed that a document which merely identifies and totals work done, without asking to be paid for it, is not a payment claim. The safe harbour is to use the word “invoice”: s 68(3) deems any written document bearing that word to satisfy s 68(1)(c).

Section 68 BIF Act — Meaning of payment claim (extract)

(1) A payment claim, for a progress payment, is a written document that—

…

(c) requests payment of the claimed amount; and

…

(3) A written document bearing the word ‘invoice’ is taken to satisfy subsection (1)(c).

Why s 68(1)(c) exists

Section 68(1)(c) is new to the BIF Act — the BCIPA equivalent (s 17) had no analogous requirement. The explanatory note tells us only that “[a] request for payment may be, for example, in the form of an invoice that contains a due date.” There is no extrinsic guidance on what other forms of words will do, and no other Australian security of payment statute imposes a comparable requirement.

Read with s 68(3), the structure of the section is straightforward. If the document bears the word “invoice”, the request element is taken to be satisfied and the inquiry stops. If it does not, the document still has to request payment in substance — and what that means is now the subject of binding Queensland authority.

The leading Queensland position: MWB Everton Park

The leading decision is MWB Everton Park Pty Ltd as trustee for MWB Everton Park Unit Trust v Devcon Building Co Pty Ltd [2024] QCA 94. The builder had emailed the principal a covering message with two spreadsheets attached, totalling the value of work done to date. The documents did not bear the word “invoice” and did not in terms ask the principal to pay. Dalton JA (Brown J and Kelly J agreeing) gave three independent reasons why the documents were not a payment claim, the third being that they did not request payment of the claimed amount as required by s 68(1)(c). Her Honour explicitly disagreed with Wilson J’s contrary analysis at first instance in Iris Broadbeach:

[36] … In [Iris Broadbeach] the judge was prepared to find that the documentation impliedly included a request for payment … While I cannot see any relevant distinction between the documents in this case and the documents in Iris Broadbeach Business, I am afraid that I am not persuaded by the analysis in that case. I do not think it can be said that the documents comprising the email and attachments of 30 June 2023 made a request for payment, independently of identifying the amount the subject of the claim.

The practical takeaway from MWB Everton Park is that you cannot infer a request for payment from the bare fact that a document identifies work and states a total. Something on the face of the document must ask for the money. Use the word “invoice” and you are home; rely on context and inference and you are exposed.

The earlier first-instance view in Iris Broadbeach

The position the Court of Appeal disapproved was Wilson J’s decision in Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 16. Her Honour found that a document headed “progress claim”, stating that it was submitted under the BIF Act and showing a “Total Progress Claim Value for the Month”, objectively requested payment even though the words “please pay” or “invoice” were absent. A supporting statutory declaration referring to “the payment of Progress Claim No. 7” was treated as part of the cumulative picture.1 After MWB Everton Park, that reasoning is unsafe. To the extent Iris Broadbeach survives, it does so on its facts and only because it was not appealed on this point.

Practical drafting

The cheapest and safest course is to put the word “invoice” on the face of the document. Section 68(3) is unconditional: a written document bearing that word is taken to satisfy s 68(1)(c), and there is no judicial gloss requiring the invoice to take any particular form. If “invoice” is for some reason not appropriate — for example, where you are issuing a single “progress claim” document under a head contract that uses the language of certificates rather than invoices — the document needs an express request for payment. “Please pay the claimed amount of $X by [date]” on the face of the claim is enough; a covering email saying the same will do, provided it is part of the same payment claim.

The courts continue to read s 68 as a whole “not from an unduly critical viewpoint” — the test articulated by Henry J in Camporeale Holdings Pty Ltd v Mortimer Construction Pty Ltd [2015] QSC 211 under BCIPA, approved by the Court of Appeal in Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Traspunt No 5 Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 119; [2011] 2 Qd R 114 — but that approach goes to construction of the document as a whole, not to filling in missing elements.2 Section 68(1)(c) is one of those elements; if it is missing, no amount of common-sense reading will rescue the claim.

Practice tips

  • If you are the claimant, the easiest path to validity is to put the word “invoice” on the face of the document. Section 68(3) deems that to satisfy s 68(1)(c), full stop.
  • If your standard form is a “progress claim” rather than an invoice, add an express request for payment — for example, “Please pay the claimed amount of $X by [date]”. Don’t leave it to inference from a total.
  • Don’t rely on the title (“progress claim”), a reference to the BIF Act, or a covering statutory declaration to do the work of s 68(1)(c). After MWB Everton Park, those things are not enough on their own.
  • If you are the respondent and the document received does not bear the word “invoice” and does not contain any request for payment, that is now a clean jurisdictional point — raise it in your payment schedule and reserve it for any adjudication or court application.
  • For variation claims and one-off claims served outside the regular cycle, take particular care. These are the documents most likely to lack a clear request for payment because they are often drafted as letters, notices or spreadsheets rather than invoices.
