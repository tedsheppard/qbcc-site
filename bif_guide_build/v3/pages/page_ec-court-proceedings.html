Enforcing a subcontractor’s charge

Court proceedings to recover

A notice of claim of charge under Chapter 4 doesn’t pay you on its own. Once the contractor has responded under s 128, the next step is a court action: it is the court — not the principal, the contractor or the security holder — that ultimately decides whether the charge attaches and orders the money out. Sections 130 and 131 set up what the holder of the security must do in the meantime, and both routes funnel the dispute into the same place: “the court in which the subcontractor’s claim is heard”.

Sections 130 and 131 BIF Act — use of security pending court order

130 Use of security for benefit of subcontractor if contractor accepts liability for all claims

(1) This section applies if—

(a) a subcontractor gives a notice of claim to a person under section 122 because the person is obliged to pay money to a contractor under a contract; and

(b) the subcontractor gives a copy of the notice of claim to the contractor under section 123; and

(c) in the contractor’s response to the claim under section 128, the contractor accepts liability to pay the amount claimed; and

(d) the unsatisfied amount for the contract is more than the retained amount for the contract.

(2) The holder of a security for the contract must—

(a) retain the security until the court in which the subcontractor’s claim is heard makes an order under section 132 about enforcing the subcontractor’s charge over the security; or

(b) instead of retaining the security—

(i) if the security is held as an amount of money—pay the amount, up to the difference amount for the contract, to the subcontractor or subcontractors to whom the acceptance of liability relates; or

(ii) if the security is not held as an amount of money but may be converted into an amount of money—convert the security, wholly or partly, into an amount of money and pay the amount, up to the difference amount for the contract, to the subcontractor or subcontractors to whom the acceptance of liability relates.

131 Use of security for benefit of subcontractor if contractor does not accept liability for all claims

(2) The holder of a security for the contract must—

(a) retain the security until the court in which the subcontractor’s claim is heard makes an order under section 132 about enforcing the subcontractor’s charge over the security; or

(b) instead of retaining the security—

(i) if the security is held as an amount of money—pay the amount, up to the difference amount for the contract, into court; or

(ii) if the security is not held as an amount of money but may be converted into an amount of money—convert the security, wholly or partly, into an amount of money and pay the amount, up to the difference amount for the contract, into court.

(7) An amount paid into court under subsection(2)(b) may be paid out only under an order of the court.

Why you have to start a proceeding

The notice of claim mechanism creates a charge over money owed up the contractual chain and freezes that money in the principal’s hands. But the freeze is temporary. Sections 130 and 131 both contemplate the security being “retained” only until “the court in which the subcontractor’s claim is heard” makes an order under s 132. If you don’t start a proceeding, there is no court, no order under s 132, and ultimately nothing to keep the money sitting where the notice put it. The proceeding is the engine that turns the charge into actual recovery.

The proceeding is also the only way that disputed money paid into court under s 131 comes back out. Section 131(7) is express: an amount paid into court under s 131(2)(b) “may be paid out only under an order of the court”. No agreement between the contractor and the subcontractor, no instruction from the principal, and no withdrawal by the security holder can release that money. It moves only on a court order.

The two pathways into court

The s 128 response sorts your claim into one of two channels, and that determines the urgency and shape of the proceeding.

If the contractor accepts liability under s 128, s 130 governs. The security holder must either retain the security against a s 132 order, or pay it (or its converted value) directly to the subcontractor up to the “difference amount” for the contract. In an accepted-liability case the proceeding tends to be more administrative — you are confirming entitlement and getting the s 132 order so the security holder can release the retained amount cleanly. The personal-liability backstop in s 130(3) gives the security holder strong incentive to do the right thing without litigation, but a court order remains the safest route to a clean release.

If the contractor disputes the claim under s 128, s 131 governs. The security holder either retains the security or pays its converted value into court — not to the subcontractor. The dispute is now genuinely contested, and you need a proceeding to obtain a s 132 order that resolves whether the charge attaches and, if so, in what amount. This is the typical subcontractor’s charge proceeding.

What the proceeding has to achieve

The relief you are ultimately asking for is an order under s 132 enforcing the subcontractor’s charge over the security. Practically, that means the proceeding has to:

  • identify the underlying debt for which the charge is claimed (the work done, money owing, and the contractor against whom the charge is asserted);
  • establish that the notice of claim was validly given under s 122 and copied to the contractor under s 123;
  • establish the contractor’s s 128 response (acceptance or dispute), which fixes whether you are in s 130 or s 131 territory;
  • quantify the “unsatisfied amount” and the “retained amount” for the contract, because the s 130/131 machinery only engages where the unsatisfied amount exceeds the retained amount — the “difference amount” is the headroom available to the subcontractor; and
  • name the holder of the security (typically the principal or head contractor up the chain) so that any s 132 order is binding on the entity actually holding the money.

You will normally join the contractor (as the party against whom the underlying debt is owed) and the security holder (as the party who must hand over the money on a s 132 order). The court’s order under s 132 then unlocks the retained security or releases the funds paid into court under s 131(2)(b).

Don’t miss the time bar

A subcontractor’s charge has a tight statutory life. The proceeding to enforce the charge has to be commenced within the period the Act allows after the notice of claim is given — otherwise the charge lapses, the security holder can deal with the security free of the charge, and any money sitting in court can be paid out without the subcontractor seeing any of it. The time-limit element is dealt with on its own page; the practical point here is that starting the proceeding is not optional and not something to defer once you know the contractor has either accepted under s 128 or disputed.

Practice tips

  • The notice of claim freezes the money; the court proceeding is what actually recovers it. Plan the proceeding from the moment you serve the notice, not after the s 128 response arrives.
  • Read the s 128 response carefully. Acceptance puts you in s 130 (security holder may pay you directly); dispute puts you in s 131 (money goes into court and only comes out on a court order).
  • Frame the proceeding around s 132 relief. That means joining the contractor and the security holder, pleading the notice and copy under s 122 and s 123, and quantifying both the unsatisfied amount and the retained amount so the difference amount is on the face of the pleading.
  • If the contractor disputes and money has been paid into court under s 131(2)(b), don’t expect any informal release. Section 131(7) means only a court order gets it out. Build that order into the relief sought.
  • Watch the time bar. Once the statutory window closes, the charge lapses and the s 130/131 machinery falls away — the security holder is then free to surrender the security back up the chain.
