Requirements of an adjudicator’s decision

Give written reasons

The adjudicator’s decision must be in writing and must include the reasons for the decision — unless both parties have agreed otherwise. This is not a formality. Reasons that don’t actually disclose how the adjudicator got to the result, or that side-step a critical issue altogether, can amount to jurisdictional error and bring the determination down. There is also a discretion in s 88(4) to disregard parts of an application or response that breach the page-limit and word-limit rules in the Regulation.

Section 88(4)–(5) BIF Act — the adjudicator’s decision: form and reasons

(4) Also, the adjudicator may disregard an adjudication application or adjudication response to the extent that the submissions or accompanying documents contravene any limitations relating to submissions or accompanying documents prescribed by regulation.

(5) The adjudicator’s decision must—

(a) be in writing; and

(b) include the reasons for the decision, unless the claimant and the respondent have both asked the adjudicator not to include the reasons in the decision.

Section 88(4) — the page-limit discretion

Section 88(4) lets the adjudicator disregard parts of the application or response that go beyond the limits prescribed by the Regulation on the length of submissions or accompanying documents. It is a discretion, not a duty, and it operates only to the extent of the contravention. The practical message is to keep your application or response within the prescribed envelope — if you don’t, the adjudicator may simply ignore the offending pages, which is rarely the result you want.

Section 88(5) — writing and reasons

The decision has to be written, and it has to include the adjudicator’s reasons for the decision unless both parties expressly opt out. The reasons must address the matters the adjudicator was actually required to decide under s 88(1) — the adjudicated amount, the date for payment and the rate of interest — and the issues the parties joined under s 88(2). They don’t need to be lengthy or elaborate. As Sackar J put it in Avopiling (NSW) Pty Ltd v Menard Bachy Pty Ltd [2012] NSWSC 1466, picked up with approval by Bond J in the Queensland decision JAG Projects Qld Pty Ltd v Total Cool Pty Ltd [2015] QSC 229:

There is clearly no requirement for the reasons to be lengthy elaborate or detailed. The reasons should be sufficient to show that the adjudicator has engaged actively with the dispute and dealt with it in a way that is reasoned, not perverse, arbitrary or capricious… A statement of reasons should be looked at as a whole.

The other side of that coin is the warning that a “mere indication” of having read the material, or a bare statement of satisfaction, “may not, subject to viewing the determination as a whole, survive as adequate reasons”.1 Reasons given under tight statutory timetables, often by non-lawyers, will be read fairly and as a whole — not parsed with a fine-tooth comb.2

What “include the reasons” really requires

The leading appellate authority on s 88(5)(b) is Cockram Construction Ltd v Fulton Hogan Construction Pty Ltd [2018] NSWCA 107. The NSW Court of Appeal held that asking whether an adjudicator’s reasons were “adequate” or “sufficient” raises a false issue: the statutory question is simply whether the adjudicator gave their reasons for their determination. In Cockram the adjudicator had treated a contractual clause as unworkable; the Court of Appeal held that was reason enough, and the adjudicator was not required to explain why the clause was unworkable. The Queensland courts read s 88(5) the same way.3

That doesn’t collapse the obligation into nothing. The point at which reasons become so thin that the determination falls is the point at which they are “opaque” in the sense that no reasons for decision are revealed at all. That formulation comes from Jackson J in Sierra Property Qld Pty Ltd v National Construction Management Pty Ltd [2016] QSC 108 and was clarified in Annie Street JV Pty Ltd v MCC Pty Ltd [2016] QSC 268, where Flanagan J explained that “opaque” means no reasons revealed — “a recognised basis for finding jurisdictional error”.4

When inadequate reasons become jurisdictional error

The Queensland authority closest to the line is Bond J’s decision in Acciona Agua Australia Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 133. The adjudicator there had considered new reasons in the response (a s 82(4) breach), and the reasoning that flowed from that improper consideration was held to be “nothing more than a vote one way” rather than the “active process of intellectual engagement with the issues and the submissions before him” that the BIF Act requires. The result was a finding of jurisdictional error under s 88(5) — partial declaration of voidness, with the balance of the determination left to stand.

The pattern across the cases is fairly settled. Reasons that show the adjudicator turning their mind to the disputed issues, weighing the competing positions and reaching a conclusion will satisfy s 88(5)(b), even if the reasoning is brief or in places clearly wrong. Reasons that simply parrot one side’s case, or that fail to engage with a critical item at all, will not.5 A useful check is whether the determination explains why each significant disputed item was allowed or rejected. A blanket statement that all submissions have been “considered”, without more, is the textbook example of what won’t do.6

Practice tips

  • If you are reviewing an unfavourable determination, separate two questions. Did the adjudicator make an error in the reasoning? That is usually within jurisdiction and won’t get the determination set aside. Did the adjudicator fail to give any intelligible reasons on a significant disputed item? That can be jurisdictional error.
  • Look for items where the adjudicator says little more than “I am satisfied” or “I have considered the submissions” on a contested point. Bare conclusions on items that were genuinely in dispute are the strongest candidates for an “opaque reasons” challenge.
  • Don’t pitch the challenge as a complaint about brevity. Cockram closes that argument off — reasons can be very short and still comply with s 88(5)(b). The argument that travels is “there are no reasons revealed at all on this issue”, not “the reasons are too short”.
  • If you are running an adjudication application or response, keep within the page and word limits in the Regulation. Section 88(4) gives the adjudicator a discretion to disregard the excess, and you don’t want a critical exhibit cut for non-compliance.
  • Don’t agree to an adjudicator deciding without reasons. The opt-out in s 88(5)(b) is rarely worth taking — reasons are what give you a basis to test the determination later or, more commonly, to understand and absorb the result.
