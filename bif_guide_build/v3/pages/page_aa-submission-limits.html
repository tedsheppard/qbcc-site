Requirements of an adjudication application

Submission limits and the no-new-reasons rule

An adjudication application may be supported by submissions, but Chapter 3 of the BIF Act places substantive limits on what those submissions may do. They can argue the claimant’s case; they cannot rebuild the payment claim from scratch or open up issues that should have been raised earlier.

Section 79(3) BIF Act — submissions accompanying the application

79 Application for adjudication

(3) The adjudication application may be accompanied by submissions relevant to the application.

What “submissions” means

The word is read broadly. It is not confined to legal argument or theory: it covers anything put forward for the adjudicator to consider, including supporting documentation, statutory declarations and evidence verifying the claim. The leading authority is McDougall J’s judgment in Austruc Constructions Ltd v ACA Developments Pty Ltd; ACA Developments Pty Ltd v Sarlos [2004] NSWSC 131, where his Honour rejected the argument that “submissions” should be limited to advocacy and held that the dictionary meaning of “that which is submitted for decision or consideration” was the better construction. The same approach has been adopted in Queensland.1

Including submissions is also optional. The word in s 79(3) is “may”, and Dalton J in Kangaroo Point Developments MP Property Pty Ltd v RHG Construction Fitout and Maintenance Pty Ltd [2021] QSC 30 contrasted that discretionary language with the mandatory “must” in s 79(2)(a) and s 79(2)(c).2 The application may therefore be run on the face of the payment claim and payment schedule alone, but in practice submissions are filed in almost every case because they provide the only written opportunity to argue the case.

The substantive limit — no new claim, no new reasons

The ceiling on s 79(3) submissions is that they must be “relevant to the application”, and the adjudicator’s jurisdiction is anchored to the payment claim and payment schedule that preceded it. Submissions that go beyond that, by placing the claim on a basis the payment claim never raised, are not protected by s 79(3) and may invalidate the determination.

The leading judgment is Einstein J in John Holland v Cardno MBK (NSW) Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 258. The point his Honour drew out is structural: under the NSW equivalent of s 82(4), the respondent is confined to the reasons given in its payment schedule, so if the claimant uses its adjudication submissions to advance a new basis for the claim, the respondent has no way to answer it. His Honour put it this way:

The deploying for the first time in the adjudication application, of supporting documentation will require careful attention and becomes a matter of degree and detail. However in the main I do not see that a respondent which, by reason of insufficient information supplied with the payment claim, is unable to verify that claim, and says as much in the payment schedule… will be otherwise than barred… from including in its adjudication response reasons for withholding payment arising by reference to the later supporting documentation.

In short: a claimant who supplies thin detail in the payment claim, draws a “cannot verify” payment schedule, and then introduces the missing detail in the adjudication submissions is likely to lose the determination. The adjudicator either has no jurisdiction to deal with the new material or is obliged to set it aside, because acting on it would be a denial of natural justice to the respondent.3

What submissions can do

The distinction is between adding to the claim and answering the schedule. Submissions can engage with whatever the respondent raised in its payment schedule, develop the legal and factual arguments supporting the existing claim, attach evidence verifying figures already in the claim, and respond to defences raised by the respondent. McDougall J in Minister for Commerce v Contrax Plumbing (NSW) Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 823 confirmed that nothing in John Holland prevents a claimant from dealing with issues raised in the payment schedule. The contrary view would require claimants to anticipate and pre-answer every conceivable defence in the payment claim itself, which would defeat the speed and simplicity on which the Act is built.

What submissions cannot do is introduce a new claim, substitute a fresh contractual basis, or fill a gap that should have been addressed in the payment claim. The detail may be expanded; the foundations may not be re-laid.

Practice tips

  • Treat the payment claim as the place where the case is set. Itemise the work, identify the contractual basis, and include enough supporting detail that the respondent can sensibly verify or reject each item. If the claim is thin, submissions cannot rescue it.
  • Use s 79(3) submissions to argue your case — not to rebuild it. Address the reasons given in the payment schedule, expand on the legal and factual basis already pleaded, and attach the verifying evidence.
  • If the respondent’s payment schedule raises new defences or new factual contentions, you can answer those head-on in submissions. That is squarely within what s 79(3) is for.
  • Be careful with documents you produce for the first time in submissions. Going from “here are summary calculations” in the claim to “here are the full timesheets and rate cards” in submissions is usually fine. Going from “variation under cl 14” to “quantum meruit because there was no contract” is not.
  • Submissions are optional but in practice essential. Treat them as your one written run at the adjudicator and prepare them with the same care as the payment claim itself.
