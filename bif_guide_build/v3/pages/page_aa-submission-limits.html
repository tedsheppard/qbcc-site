Requirements of an adjudication application

Submission limits and the no-new-reasons rule

An adjudication application can be supported by submissions, but the BIF Act regime places real limits on what those submissions are allowed to do. They can argue your case — they cannot rebuild your payment claim from scratch or open up issues you should have flagged earlier.

Section 79(3) BIF Act — submissions accompanying the application

79 Application for adjudication

(3) The adjudication application may be accompanied by submissions relevant to the application.

What “submissions” means

The word is read broadly. It is not confined to legal argument or theory: it covers anything you put forward for the adjudicator to consider, including supporting documentation, statutory declarations and evidence verifying the claim. The leading reading is McDougall J’s judgment in Austruc Constructions Ltd v ACA Developments Pty Ltd; ACA Developments Pty Ltd v Sarlos [2004] NSWSC 131, where his Honour rejected the argument that “submissions” should be limited to advocacy and held that the dictionary meaning of “that which is submitted for decision or consideration” was the better fit. The same approach has been adopted in Queensland.1

Including submissions is also optional. The word in s 79(3) is “may”, and Dalton J in Kangaroo Point Developments MP Property Pty Ltd v RHG Construction Fitout and Maintenance Pty Ltd [2021] QSC 30 contrasted that discretionary language with the mandatory “must” in s 79(2)(a) and s 79(2)(c).2 So you can run the application on the face of the payment claim and payment schedule alone, but in practice almost everyone files submissions because they are the only opportunity to argue the case in writing.

The real limit — no new claim, no new reasons

The ceiling on s 79(3) submissions is that they must be “relevant to the application”, and the adjudicator’s jurisdiction is anchored to the payment claim and payment schedule that came before. Submissions that go beyond that — that put the claim on a basis the payment claim never raised — are not protected by s 79(3) and risk taking the determination down with them.

The leading judgment is Einstein J in John Holland v Cardno MBK (NSW) Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 258. The point his Honour drew out is structural: under the Act’s NSW equivalent of s 82(4), the respondent is locked in to the reasons given in its payment schedule, so if the claimant uses its adjudication submissions to spring a new basis for the claim, the respondent has no way to answer it. His Honour put it this way:

The deploying for the first time in the adjudication application, of supporting documentation will require careful attention and becomes a matter of degree and detail. However in the main I do not see that a respondent which, by reason of insufficient information supplied with the payment claim, is unable to verify that claim, and says as much in the payment schedule… will be otherwise than barred… from including in its adjudication response reasons for withholding payment arising by reference to the later supporting documentation.

Put plainly: a claimant who supplies thin detail in the payment claim, draws a “cannot verify” payment schedule, and then dumps the missing detail into the adjudication submissions will likely lose the determination. The adjudicator either has no jurisdiction to deal with the new material or is obliged to set it aside, because acting on it would be a denial of natural justice to the respondent.3

What submissions can do

The line is between adding to the claim and answering the schedule. Submissions can engage with whatever the respondent raised in its payment schedule, develop the legal and factual arguments supporting the existing claim, attach evidence verifying figures already in the claim, and respond to defences flagged by the respondent. McDougall J in Minister for Commerce v Contrax Plumbing (NSW) Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 823 confirmed that nothing in John Holland stops a claimant from dealing with issues raised in the payment schedule — the contrary view would force claimants to anticipate and pre-answer every conceivable defence in the payment claim itself, which would defeat the speed and simplicity the Act is built on.

What submissions cannot do is invent a new claim, swap in a fresh contractual basis, or fill a gap that should have been closed in the payment claim. The detail can be expanded; the foundations cannot be re-poured.

What this means on a project

  • Treat the payment claim as the place where the case is set. Itemise the work, identify the contractual basis, and include enough supporting detail that the respondent can sensibly verify or reject each item. If the claim is thin, submissions cannot rescue it.
  • Use s 79(3) submissions to argue your case — not to rebuild it. Address the reasons given in the payment schedule, expand on the legal and factual basis already pleaded, and attach the verifying evidence.
  • If the respondent’s payment schedule raises new defences or new factual contentions, you can answer those head-on in submissions. That is squarely within what s 79(3) is for.
  • Be careful with documents you produce for the first time in submissions. Going from “here are summary calculations” in the claim to “here are the full timesheets and rate cards” in submissions is usually fine. Going from “variation under cl 14” to “quantum meruit because there was no contract” is not.
  • Submissions are optional but in practice essential. Treat them as your one written run at the adjudicator and prepare them with the same care as the payment claim itself.
