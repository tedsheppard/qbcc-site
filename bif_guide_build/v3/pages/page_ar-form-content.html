Requirements of an adjudication response

Form and content

An adjudication response is the respondent’s one written run at the adjudicator. The Act keeps the form simple but locks the content tightly to what the respondent already said in its payment schedule. Get either part wrong and the response is either inadmissible or, if the adjudicator acts on it, the determination is exposed.

Section 82 BIF Act — adjudication response

82 Adjudication response

(1) After being given notice of an adjudicator’s acceptance of an adjudication application under section 81, the respondent may give the adjudicator a response to the adjudication application (the adjudication response).

(2) However, the respondent must not give an adjudication response if the respondent failed to give the claimant a payment schedule as required under section 76.

(3) The adjudication response—

(a) must be in writing; and

(b) must identify the adjudication application to which it relates; and

(c) may include the submissions relevant to the response the respondent chooses to include.

(4) However, the adjudication response must not include any reasons (new reasons) for withholding payment that were not included in the payment schedule when given to the claimant.

(5) The adjudicator may require the respondent to resubmit the adjudication response without the new reasons.

Form — the easy bit

The form requirements in s 82(3)(a)–(b) are minimal. The response must be in writing and it must identify the adjudication application it answers. There is no prescribed form: an emailed PDF identifying the application reference, the parties, and the payment claim it relates to is enough. The harder rule is the threshold one in s 82(2) — if the respondent failed to give a payment schedule under s 76, it is barred from filing any response at all. There is nothing the respondent can do at the adjudication stage to recover from a missed payment schedule.

Submissions can amplify, not parrot, the schedule

Section 82(3)(c) lets the response include “submissions relevant to the response”. Read together with s 82(4), that means submissions can develop, explain and evidence the reasons already in the payment schedule — but they cannot introduce reasons that were not. The leading reading is McDougall J in TQM v Dasein [2004] NSWSC 1216, considering the NSW equivalent:

[30] If it were intended that an adjudication response should do no more than mirror, word for word, a payment schedule, then there is little point to the ability… for a respondent to lodge an adjudication response… the adjudication response may do more than parrot the terms of the payment schedule.

The same point runs through the documents cases. An expert report or spreadsheet that supports a reason already given in the payment schedule is properly part of the response, even if the document itself was not attached to the schedule: Syntech Resources Pty Ltd v Peter Campbell Earthmoving (Aust) Pty Ltd [2011] QSC 293; Trysams Pty Ltd v Club Constructions (NSW) Pty Ltd [2007] NSWSC 941. In Syntech, Daubney J held that “submissions” in the equivalent provision was not to be construed narrowly and could include relevant supporting documentation. In Trysams, Hammerschlag J went further: where the report was foreshadowed in the payment schedule and provided in support of the position already taken, the adjudicator’s refusal to consider it was a substantial denial of natural justice that voided the determination.1

The no-new-reasons rule in s 82(4)

The hard limit is s 82(4). Any reason for withholding payment that was not in the payment schedule is a “new reason” and cannot be raised in the response. “Reasons for withholding payment” is read broadly: it captures everything from non-performance and bad work to set-offs, cross-claims and contractual or statutory bars on payment.2 If the respondent does not propose to pay an amount in the claim, whatever justifies that decision is a reason for withholding payment and has to be in the schedule.

The Queensland authorities are unforgiving. In Acciona Agua Australia Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 133, Bond J declared part of an adjudication void where the adjudicator accepted set-off bases and an implied-term breach argument that had no “mention” in the payment schedule. In Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 92, Martin J held that s 69(c) requires the respondent to include all of its reasons in the schedule — not just the ones prompted by the claim — and that once the adjudicator considers a matter prohibited by s 82, s 88(3)(b) is breached “whether or not the consideration leads to a particular decision”. The drafting consequence is straightforward: every basis the respondent might want to run at adjudication has to be visible on the face of the schedule.

Quantum versus “other” reasons — no distinction

It is sometimes argued that reasons going only to quantum (why the scheduled amount is less) sit outside s 82(4) because the section talks about “reasons for withholding payment”. The appellate answer is no. Hodgson JA in John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads & Traffic Authority of New South Wales [2007] NSWCA 19 rejected any narrow reading: if the respondent is not paying an amount for any reason, those are reasons for withholding payment, and any other view would do violence to the section’s purpose of preventing late submissions in a tight statutory timetable. Philip McMurdo J followed the same approach for Queensland in Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd [2015] QSC 307.

Jurisdictional points are different

The s 82(4) bar applies to reasons for withholding payment, not to challenges to the adjudicator’s jurisdiction. A respondent can raise jurisdictional points in the response — for example, that there was no reference date, or that the application was out of time — even if those points were not in the schedule, because they could not have been taken there. In Watkins Contracting Pty Ltd v Hyatt Ground Engineering Pty Ltd [2018] QSC 65, Brown J also accepted that detailed submissions on the grounds of contract termination were a permissible elaboration of the schedule’s “nil — no reference date because the contract has been terminated” reasoning, and that even if they had been new, an adjudicator must consider jurisdictional points regardless of s 82(4).3

What this means on a project

  • If you missed the payment schedule, you cannot file a response. There is no fix at the adjudication stage. Make sure the schedule is in — even a thin one — before you worry about anything else.
  • Treat the payment schedule as the gateway. Every reason you might want to argue at adjudication has to be visible there: set-offs, cross-claims, defects, contractual bars, statutory bars, quantum points and termination grounds.
  • You can amplify in the response. Adding an expert report, a spreadsheet of calculations, or a detailed legal argument that supports a reason already given in the schedule is fine and often essential.
  • You cannot smuggle. Swapping in a fresh contractual clause, a new defect category, or a different termination basis will be struck down as a “new reason”, and if the adjudicator runs with it the determination is exposed under s 88(3)(b).
  • Quantum-only points are still reasons for withholding payment. A new rate, a new measurement, a new deduction calculation — if it was not in the schedule, it cannot come in via the response.
  • Jurisdictional objections are different. You can run them in the response even if the schedule did not foreshadow them, because they go to whether the adjudicator should be there at all.
