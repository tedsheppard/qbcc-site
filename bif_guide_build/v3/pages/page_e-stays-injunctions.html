Enforcing an adjudicator’s decision

Stays and injunctions

A respondent who wants to stop the claimant filing or enforcing an adjudication certificate has to ask the court for an interlocutory injunction or a stay. The Act doesn’t hand that out for the asking. The application is decided on ordinary equitable principles — prima facie case and balance of convenience — but read against a statutory policy that has already weighed the risk and assigned most of it to the respondent.

Section 93 BIF Act — the enforcement provision the stay would interrupt

93 Filing of adjudication certificate as judgment debt

(1) An adjudication certificate may be filed as a judgment for a debt, and may be enforced, in a court of competent jurisdiction.

(4) If the respondent commences proceedings to have the judgment set aside—

(b) the respondent is required to pay into the court, as security, the unpaid portion of the adjudicated amount pending the final decision in those proceedings.

The test — prima facie case and balance of convenience

The starting point is Australian Broadcasting Corporation v O’Neill [2006] HCA 46; (2006) 227 CLR 57. To obtain an interlocutory injunction restraining the filing or enforcement of an adjudication certificate, the applicant has to show a prima facie case and that the balance of convenience favours the grant of relief. “Prima facie case” here doesn’t mean it is more probable than not that the applicant will win at trial — it is enough to show a sufficient likelihood of success to justify preserving the status quo pending the trial.1

That part of the test is conventional. What makes stays and injunctions in the BIF Act context distinctive is the way the second limb — balance of convenience — gets read in light of the legislation’s purpose.

The Neller policy — cash flow, and where the risk sits

The leading Queensland authority is R J Neller Building Pty Ltd v Ainsworth [2008] QCA 397; [2009] 1 Qd R 390. The Court of Appeal decided that the legislative policy — an assured cash flow to the contractor, with the risk of later repayment failure assigned to the respondent — is itself a relevant factor going into the balance of convenience. Keane JA (Fraser JA and Fryberg J agreeing) put it this way:

The BCIP Act proceeds on the assumption that the interruption of a builder’s cash flow may cause the financial failure of the builder before the rights and wrongs of claim and counterclaim between builder and owner can be finally determined by the courts… the risk that a builder might not be able to refund moneys ultimately found to be due to a non-residential owner after a successful action by the owner must, I think, be regarded as a risk which, as a matter of policy in the commercial context in which the BCIP Act applies, the legislature has, prima facie at least, assigned to the owner.

The practical effect on a stay application is significant. A respondent who simply argues “we will not get our money back if we lose at final hearing” is unlikely to succeed — that is the very risk Parliament has already allocated to the respondent. The respondent has to point to something more, and the obvious candidate is a prima facie case that the underlying determination is void for jurisdictional error.2

The jurisdictional-error qualification

Where the applicant’s case is that the determination is affected by jurisdictional error, the Neller policy doesn’t apply with the same force. In BRB Modular Pty Ltd v AWX Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 222 Bond J drew the distinction expressly: the policy of the Act, in its full force, applies to valid adjudication decisions made within jurisdiction; it could not have been intended to allocate the same risk in relation to decisions which are void and liable to be quashed. Where the applicant has established a prima facie case of jurisdictional error, the Act’s policy still characterises the nature of the right an injunction would interfere with — it just doesn’t answer the balance-of-convenience question on its own.3

The common landing point in those cases is conditional relief: the court will restrain enforcement, but only on terms that the disputed amount is paid into court (or secured by bank guarantee, or covered by an undertaking to pay in) pending final determination. That mirrors the s 93(4)(b) regime that would otherwise apply if the judgment were already on foot.4

Recent appellate caution

The recent New South Wales authorities have hardened against soft stay applications. In Martinus Rail Pty Ltd v Qube RE Services (No 2) Pty Ltd [2025] NSWCA 49 the Court of Appeal confirmed that entry of judgment is not precluded by a pending arbitration or by mere repayment risk, and that a stay running to the end of an arbitration would be contrary to the statutory purpose. Applying that line in Kuatro Build Pty Ltd v Elite Formwork Group Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 372 Hmelnitsky J reiterated that “considerable caution should attend the grant of such an injunction or a stay”, and refused to continue an interim stay where the claimant’s financial evidence was unchallenged and the respondent’s insolvency concerns were not made out.5 The same general framework has been applied to refuse stays in NSW where a stay would convert what the Act intends as an interim payment into a final one without the statutory criteria being met.6

Practice tips

  • If you are the respondent and you want to stop the certificate being filed or enforced, plan the application around the two limbs in O’Neill — a prima facie case (realistically, a jurisdictional-error case) and a balance of convenience that overcomes the Neller policy.
  • Don’t rely on “we might not get the money back”. After Neller, that risk has been pre-assigned to you by the legislature. You need a sharper case — usually a serious question that the determination is void.
  • Be ready to put the disputed amount into court, or to offer a bank guarantee or undertaking to do so. That is the standard condition of conditional relief, and it is also the substance of s 93(4)(b) once a judgment is in place.
  • Move quickly. Stays are interlocutory and discretionary — the more the underlying issue can be readied for an early final hearing, the more likely the balance of convenience tips your way.
  • If you are the claimant, resist on the policy and the recent appellate guidance. Martinus and Kuatro Build make clear that “considerable caution” attends the grant of a stay and that arbitration timetables and bare repayment risk are not enough to displace the Act’s cash-flow purpose.
