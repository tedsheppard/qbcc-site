[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 94 – Claimant may make new application in certain circumstances
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] A    Legislation
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 94    Claimant may make new application in certain circumstances 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] Subsection (2) applies if an adjudicator, who accepts a referral to decide an adjudication application (the original application), does not decide the application within the period required under section 85.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] The claimant may do either of the following within 5 business days after the period mentioned in subsection (1)— 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] request the registrar refer the original application to another adjudicator; or 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] make a new adjudication application (the new application) under section 79. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] If the claimant requests the registrar refer the original application to another adjudicator— 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the registrar must refer the application to another adjudicator within 4 business days after the request is made; and
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] no fee is payable for referring the original application to another adjudicator.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] Also, if another adjudicator accepts the referral, this division applies for the original application with the following changes—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the claimant must give the adjudicator a copy of his or her submissions included in the original application within 5 business days after the adjudicator accepts the referral;
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the respondent must give the adjudicator a copy of his or her submissions included in an adjudication response, if any, for the original application within 5 business days after the adjudicator accepts the referral;
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the response date for the application under section 85(2) is the day—
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] the adjudicator receives the copy of the submission from the respondent; or 
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] if the respondent does not give the adjudicator the copy of the submission within the period mentioned in paragraph (b)—immediately after the end of the period;
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] in deciding the adjudication application, the adjudicator must not consider any submissions other than—
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] the submissions mentioned in paragraph (a) or (b); or 
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] further written submissions asked for by the adjudicator under section 84(2)(b).
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] This division applies to the new application in the same way it applies to any other adjudication application made under section 79.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] However, the claimant may, despite section 79(2)(b), make the new application within 5 business days after the claimant becomes entitled to act under subsection (2).
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] B    Commentary
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 94.1    An ‘exhaustive’ definition
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] It has been held that section 32 of BCIPA ‘exhaustively defines’ a claimant’s rights in respect of a new adjudication application based on an earlier payment claim: Spankie v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 355, [29] (Fraser JA, Holmes and Chesterman JJA agreeing); Spankie & Ors v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2010] QSC 336.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] Following the Amendment Act, regard should be had to the effect of section 35B of BCIPA (equivalent to section 97 of the Act).
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 94.2    New application where the previous decision is set aside
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] There are, arguably, differing views on whether a claimant may make a new application where the previous adjudication decision was set aside for jurisdictional error or a breach of natural justice: see Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd v Ian James Ericson t/as Flea’s Concreting & Anor [2011] QSC 327, [139]-[144] (McMurdo J); Spankie v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 355, [29] (Fraser JA, Holmes and Chesterman JJA agreeing); Cardinal Project Services Pty Ltd v Hanave Pty Ltd [2010] NSWSC 1367.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 94.3    New application where previous decision is void
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd t/a CCA Winslow & Ors [2020] QSC 51, Dalton J held that the delivery of an adjudication decision outside the time limits prescribed by section 85 and 86 of BIFSOPA will give rise to the right of a claimant to immediately institute a second adjudication application pursuant to section 94 of BIFSOPA. The Court reasoned that this was because the purpose of BIFSOPA is to provide the claimant with “a prompt extra-curial decision on its progress claim; not a guarantee that its progress claim will be paid.
[Normal] In Sun Engineering (Qld) Pty Ltd v Registrar Appointed under section 150 of the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 [2023] QSC 168, Kelly J held that s 94(1) of the BIFSOPA applies in situations where the adjudicator has failed to make a decision within the allocated time. In coming to the decision, Kelly J considered the critical language in s 94(1) which states “if an adjudicator… does not decide the application within the period required under” s 85 of the Act, then the claimant can request to be referred to another adjudicator or make a new adjudication application under s 79 of the Act.

[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 95 – Adjudicator’s fee 
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] A    Legislation
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 95    Adjudicators fees 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] An adjudicator is entitled to be paid for adjudicating an adjudication application—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the amount, by way of fees and expenses, agreed between the adjudicator and the parties to the adjudication; or
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] if no amount is agreed, the amount, for fees and expenses, that is reasonable having regard to the work done and expenses incurred by the adjudicator. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] A regulation may prescribe the maximum amount for fees and expenses (the prescribed maximum) an adjudicator may be paid for adjudicating an adjudication application. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] Despite subsection (1), an adjudicator is not entitled to be paid any amount that is more than the prescribed maximum.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] The claimant and respondent are jointly and severally liable to pay the adjudicator’s fees and expenses.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] The claimant and respondent are each liable to contribute to the payment of the adjudicator’s fees and expenses in equal proportions unless the adjudicator decides otherwise.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] An adjudicator is not entitled to be paid any fees or expenses for adjudicating an adjudication application if the adjudicator fails to make a decision on the application.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] An adjudicator does not fail to make a decision only because— 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the adjudication application is withdrawn; or 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the adjudicator decided he or she did not have jurisdiction to adjudicate the application; or
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the adjudicator decided the application was frivolous or vexatious; or 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the adjudicator refuses to communicate the adjudicator’s decision on an adjudication application until the adjudicator’s fees and expenses are paid. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] Also, if a court finds that the adjudicator’s decision is void and unenforceable, the adjudicator is still entitled to be paid any fees or expenses for adjudicating the application if the adjudicator acted in good faith in adjudicating the application. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] In this section—
[1.1 BIFSOPA Body Text] adjudicating, an adjudication application, includes accepting, considering and deciding the application.
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] B    Commentary
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 95.1    Statutory right to payment
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Alucity Architectural Produce Supply Pty Ltd v Australian Solutions Centre; Alucity Architectural Produce Supply Pty Ltd v Paul J Hick [2016] NSWSC 608, Hammerschlag J dismissed a claim for restitution of all fees paid due to a total failure of consideration. In considering the Adjudicator’s right to payment under section 28 of the NSW Act, His Honour said: 
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] The relationship between the [applicant] on the one hand, and the authority and the adjudicator on the other, is statutory. The entitlement of the authority and the adjudicator and the authority to payment arises as a consequence of the [applicant] having invoked the procedures under the Act and because ss 28 and 29 give the authority and the adjudicator a statutory right to payment. 
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] However, in Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd t/a CCA Winslow & Ors [2020] QSC 51, the Court held that an adjudicator who handed down a decision outside the timeframe of section 85 and 86 of BIFSOPA was not entitled to be paid any fees or expenses in relation to the adjudication. In that case, Dalton J held that the adjudicator failed to act in good faith under section 95(8) of the Act in circumstances where, through his agent, the adjudicator represented that the decision had been made within time when this was not the case.
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd & Ors; Jones v Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd & Ors [2021] QCA 10, the Queensland Court of Appeal determined that the primary judge had erred in finding that the adjudicator had acted in bad faith and had made misrepresentations on the basis that the adjudicator did not have an opportunity to be heard on those subjects. Notwithstanding this error, the Queensland Court of Appeal upheld the primary judge’s decision that an adjudicator was not entitled to any fees because the adjudicator did not make his decision within the timeframe provided for by the BIF Act. The Court of Appeal confirmed that even if the adjudicator’s determination was valid or if the parties were still able and willing to grant the adjudicator an extension, the operation of section 95(6) would still have effect.

[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 96    Deciding fees payable by claimant and respondent 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] When making a decision about an adjudication application, an adjudicator may also decide the following— 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the proportion of the adjudicator’s fees and expenses to be paid by the claimant and respondent; 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] the proportion of the fee, payable for the adjudication application under section 79(2)(d), for which the respondent is to reimburse the claimant.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] In making the decision, the adjudicator—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] must consider the conduct of the claimant and respondent; and 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] may consider any of the following matters—
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] the relative success of the claimant or respondent in the adjudication;
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] whether the claimant or respondent commenced or participated in the adjudication for an improper purpose; 
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] whether the claimant or respondent commenced or participated in the adjudication without reasonable prospects of success;
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] the reasons given by the respondent for not making the progress payment the subject of the adjudication application;
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] whether an adjudication application is withdrawn;
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] the services provided by the adjudicator in adjudicating the adjudication application, including the amount of time taken to consider discrete aspects of the amount claimed;
[1.5 BIFSOPA level 3 (CDI)] another matter the adjudicator considers relevant in making the decision.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] In this section—
[1.1 BIFSOPA Body Text] adjudicating, an adjudication application, includes accepting, considering and deciding the application. 
[1.1 BIFSOPA Body Text] conduct, of the claimant and respondent, includes—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] whether the claimant or respondent has acted unreasonably leading up to, or during, the adjudication; or
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] whether the respondent attempted to include new reasons in an adjudication response in contravention of section 82(4). new reasons see section 82(4).

[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 97 – Withdrawing from adjudication 
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 97    Withdrawing from adjudication 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] An adjudication application—
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] is withdrawn if the claimant has given a written notice of discontinuation to the adjudicator and respondent; or 
[1.4 BIFSOPA level 2 (CDI)] is taken to have been withdrawn if the respondent has, before an adjudicator has decided the application, paid the claimant the amount stated in the payment claim the subject of the adjudication application.
[1.6 BIFSPOA example/note] Note—
[1.7 BIFSOPA example/note body] Despite the withdrawal of an adjudication application an adjudicator is still entitled to be paid fees for considering the application, see section 95. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)]  If subsection (1)(b) applies, the claimant must as soon as practicable inform the adjudicator that the adjudication application has been withdrawn because of payment.

[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 97.1    Refusal to communicate adjudicator’s decision
[1.1 BIFSOPA Body Text] In Karam Group Pty Ltd as Trustee for Karam (No. 1) Family Trust v HCA Queensland Pty Ltd & Ors [2022] QSC 290, Williams J held that an Adjudicator will have failed to make their determination within the statutory timeframe if they fail to attend to all aspects specified in s 88 BIFSOPA, including notifying the parties that the decision had been made. In this case, the adjudicator made their decision but did not notify the parties until after the time prescribed in s 85(1) BIFSOPA surpassed. The Court held that the adjudicator’s decision was invalid as it was not made within the time prescribed by the Act.  
[1.1 BIFSOPA Body Text] In Downer EDI Rail Pty Ltd v DT Infrastructure Pty Ltd [2025] QSC 212, Treston J declared that an adjudication application was taken to be withdrawn under section 97(1)(b) of the BIF Act (Qld) on the basis that a respondent paid the sum for which the adjudicator’s decision was sought but not the total payment claim. In that case, Treston J emphasised that the process under the Act is intended to be fast and efficient. The addition of the words “the subject of the adjudication application” therefore directs attention towards the genuine issues in the payment claim. Treston J distinguished the reasoning of Hodgson JA in Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v JM Hargreaves (NSW) Pty Ltd (2005) 63 NSWLR 385, finding that it would be an onerous construction of the legislation if the adjudicator was required to consider matters which are not in dispute on the face of the application (in this case, to some $8.7 million worth of claims) where the submissions on both sides had been confined to $681,445.37 worth.

[2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 1] SECTION 98 – Claimaint’s right to suspend work 
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] A    Legislation
[1 BIFSOPA Heading] 98    Claimant’s right to suspend work 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] A claimant may suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under a construction contract if at least 2 business days have passed since the claimant gave notice of intention to do so to the respondent under section 78 or 92. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] The right conferred under subsection (1) exists until the day on which the claimant receives payment from the respondent of the amount mentioned in section 78(1) or 92(1), and continues for another 3 business days immediately following that day. 
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] If, in exercising the right to suspend carrying out construction work or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract, the claimant incurs a loss or expense because the respondent removes any part of the work or supply from the contract, the respondent is liable to pay the claimant the amount of the loss or expense.
[1.3 BIFSOPA level 1 (CDI)] (4) A claimant who suspends carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract under subsection (1) is not liable for any loss or damage suffered by the respondent, or by any person claiming through the respondent, because of the claimant not carrying out that work or not supplying those goods and services, during the suspension.
[2.1 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 2] B    Commentary
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 98.1    Section 33(2) – payment
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In Beckhaus Civil Pty Ltd v Brewarrina Shire Council No. 2 [2004] NSWSC 1160, Master Macready defined the verb ‘pay’ to mean:
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] To give (a person) what is due in discharge of a debt, or as a return for services done, or goods received, or in compensation for injury done; to remunerate, recompense.” In turn, ‘payment’ is defined as “[t]he action, or an act, of paying.” From this it can readily be concluded that the touchstone of the ordinary and natural meaning of the expressions ‘to pay’ and ‘receives payment’ in the present context is the act of a debtor transferring by way of satisfaction whatever is owed by him or her to a creditor.
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] …
[2.5 Com-BIFSOPA Normal Body Quote] Thus an analysis of both the general and specific purposes of the Act permits the court to identify an alternative construction to the ordinary meaning of ‘pay’ and ‘payment.’ That is that a claimant will not have ‘received payment’ pursuant to s 27(2), and that a respondent will still have failed ‘to pay’ pursuant to s 15(1)(b), in circumstances where the claimant is unable to obtain the benefit of that payment due to conditions, such as the provision of a secured bank guarantee, placed on its receipt. The adoption of such a construction does not constitute a ‘re-writing’ of ss 15 and 27 as proscribed by the dictum of Dawson J above, but is rather a departure from the ordinary meaning of ‘payment’ in circumstances where the context and purpose of its use demands such a modification.
[2.2 Com-BIFSOPA Heading 3] 98.2    Claimant’s right to suspend work
[2.4 Com-BIFSOPA CDI Normal Body Text] In On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163, Crowe AJ held, following an adjudication determination made in favour of a claimant, that a claimant’s right to suspend work continues until a respondent gives an unequivocal acknowledgement of the discharge of the adjudication amount. In that case, the claimant exercised its right to suspended work pursuant to section 26(2)(b) of the ACT Act (equivalent to 98(1) of BIFSOPA) following the respondent’s failure to pay an adjudicated amount. A subsequent expert determination under the construction contract while the proceedings were on foot concluded that the claimant owed the respondent more than the adjudicated amount. The respondent argued the claimant’s right to suspend work existed only until the date of expert determination. Crowe AJ held that the claimant’s right to suspend work continued until either the outstanding amount was set aside, or there was an unequivocal communication to the claimant which acknowledged discharge of the payment claim, adjudicated amount, or in this case, provisional judgment debt. On the facts, constructive receipt of the adjudication amount being set aside was held to have been after consent orders were made by Crowe AJ on the date of the hearing, after which the claimant’s right to suspend work ended 3 business days after that date.
