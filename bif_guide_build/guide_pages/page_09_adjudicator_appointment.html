Chapter 3 · Sections 80 & 81

Appointment of adjudicator

Once the Registrar refers your adjudication application to an adjudicator, two short provisions govern who can take the job and how they take it. Section 80 sets the eligibility floor — an adjudicator who is a party to the contract, or who has a prescribed conflict, cannot adjudicate. Section 81 sets the mechanics — the adjudicator has 4 business days to accept or reject, and acceptance happens by serving a written notice on the claimant, the respondent and the Registrar.

Eligibility (s80)

Section 80 BIF Act – When adjudicator ineligible to adjudicate
80 When adjudicator ineligible to adjudicate

An adjudicator is not eligible to adjudicate an adjudication application if the adjudicator—

(a)  is a party to the construction contract to which the application relates; or

(b)  has a conflict of interest as prescribed by regulation.

Acceptance and notice (s81)

Section 81 BIF Act – Appointment of adjudicator
81 Appointment of adjudicator

(1)  If the registrar refers an adjudication application to an adjudicator for a decision, the adjudicator must, unless the adjudicator has a reasonable excuse, accept or reject the referral within 4 business days after the referral is made.

(2)  The adjudicator may accept the referral by serving written notice of the acceptance on the claimant, the respondent and the registrar.

(3)  The adjudicator may reject the referral by notifying the registrar of the refusal.

(4)  If the adjudicator rejects the referral or does not accept it within the time required under subsection (1)—

(a)  the registrar must refer the adjudication application to another adjudicator within 4 business days after becoming aware of the refusal or failure; and

(b)  no fee is payable for referring the adjudication application to another adjudicator.

(5)  The other adjudicator may accept the referral by serving written notice of the acceptance on the claimant, the respondent and the registrar.

(6)  The other adjudicator may reject the referral by notifying the registrar of the refusal.

(7)  On accepting a referral of an adjudication application under subsection (1) or (5), an adjudicator is taken to have been appointed to decide the application.

The mechanism is simple. The Registrar refers; the adjudicator has 4 business days to accept or reject; acceptance is by written notice served on all three (claimant, respondent and Registrar); rejection is by notifying the Registrar. If the adjudicator rejects or simply does not respond in time, the Registrar must re-refer to another adjudicator within 4 business days, and no further fee is payable. Appointment is taken to occur on acceptance — that is the moment the adjudicator’s clock starts.

Acceptance through an agent

An adjudicator does not have to sign and serve the notice of acceptance personally. An agent can do it. In Peekhrst Pty Ltd v Wallace & Anor [2007] QSC 159, Douglas J considered the equivalent of section 81 (then s23) and held:

There is nothing in the section to suggest that an adjudicator may not authorise an agent to accept on his behalf. Given the speed with which these claims are to be dealt with, it may well be the case from time to time that a nominated adjudicator may not be available immediately to sign and serve a notice of acceptance personally. That does not mean that those steps may not be taken by an agent.

…

Even if the acceptance of the application through an agent constituted a breach of the Act, which I do not accept, there is nothing to suggest that the use of an agent in circumstances like this should lead to invalidity; cf. Project Blue Sky v Australian Broadcasting Authority (1998) 194 CLR 355, 390-391 at [93].

The practical takeaway is that a notice of acceptance signed and served by the ANA (or another agent) on the adjudicator’s behalf is good enough. Don’t waste time challenging an appointment on that basis alone.

Service of notice of acceptance

Once the adjudicator (or their agent) serves the notice of acceptance, the Act’s service rules do the rest — including deeming receipt where the notice has been properly posted. In Randhawa v Serrato [2009] NSWSC 170, the adjudicator’s notice of acceptance was served by post. The plaintiff led evidence that it had not received the notice and used that to attack the adjudicator’s determination. The Court held that ‘if delivery is not disproved the fact of non-receipt does not displace the result that the delivery is deemed to be effected at the time at which it would have taken place in the ordinary course of the post’ (citing Fancourt v Mercantile Credits (1983) 154 CLR 87 at 97). Evidence of non-receipt, on its own, was not enough to disprove delivery, and the challenge to the determination failed.

Practice tips

  • When the notice of acceptance arrives, diary it — that is the moment the adjudicator is appointed under s81(7) and the decision-making clock starts running.
  • Don’t object to the notice of acceptance just because it was signed by an ANA staff member or other agent rather than the adjudicator personally. Peekhrst says that is fine.
  • If you genuinely have not received the notice of acceptance within the expected window, contact the Registrar promptly and ask for confirmation of when and how it was served — under Randhawa, mere non-receipt will not undo a properly posted notice.
  • Keep an eye on conflicts. If you know the appointed adjudicator has a connection to a party or to the contract, raise it with the adjudicator and the Registrar straight away under s80, before the adjudicator gets too far into the determination.
  • If the first adjudicator rejects (or doesn’t accept in time), expect a re-referral to another adjudicator within 4 business days at no extra fee — you do not need to lodge again or pay another fee.
