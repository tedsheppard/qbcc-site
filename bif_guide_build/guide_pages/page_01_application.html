Chapter 3 · Sections 61–63

Application of Chapter 3

Chapter 3 of the BIF Act is the security of payment regime — payment claims, payment schedules, adjudication, suspension and the rest. Sections 61 to 63 set the outer fence: which contracts the Chapter catches, what happens if you also chase a subcontractor's charge under Chapter 4, and how the statutory rights sit alongside whatever the contract says.

Section 61 — Application of chapter

Section 61 is the gateway. It applies Chapter 3 broadly to construction contracts in Queensland, then carves out a small set of exceptions. If you can't fit a contract into one of the exceptions, assume Chapter 3 applies.

Section 61 BIF Act – Application of chapter
61 Application of chapter

(1)  Subject to subsections (2) to (4), this chapter applies to construction contracts—

(a)  whether written or oral, or partly written and partly oral; and

(b)  whether expressed to be governed by the law of Queensland or a jurisdiction other than Queensland; and

(c)  whether entered into before or after the commencement of this section, other than to the extent the repealed Building and Construction Industry Payments Act 2004 continues to apply to unfinished matters under section 205.

(2)  This chapter does not apply to—

(a)  a construction contract to the extent that it forms part of a loan agreement, a contract of guarantee or a contract of insurance under which a recognised financial institution undertakes—

(i)  to lend an amount or to repay an amount lent; or

(ii)  to guarantee payment of an amount owing or repayment of an amount lent; or

(iii)  to provide an indemnity relating to construction work carried out, or related goods and services supplied, under the construction contract; or

(b)  a construction contract for the carrying out of domestic building work if a resident owner is a party to the contract, to the extent the contract relates to a building or part of a building where the resident owner resides or intends to reside; or

(c)  a construction contract under which it is agreed that the consideration payable for construction work carried out under the contract, or for related goods and services supplied under the contract, is to be calculated other than by reference to the value of the work carried out or the value of the goods and services supplied.

(3)  This chapter does not apply to a construction contract to the extent it includes—

(a)  provisions under which a party undertakes to carry out construction work, or supply related goods and services in relation to construction work, as an employee of the party for whom the work is to be carried out or the related goods and services are to be supplied; or

(b)  provisions under which a party undertakes to carry out construction work, or to supply related goods and services in relation to construction work, as a condition of a loan agreement with a recognised financial institution; or

(c)  provisions under which a party undertakes—

(i)  to lend an amount or to repay an amount lent; or

(ii)  to guarantee payment of an amount owing or repayment of an amount lent; or

(iii)  to provide an indemnity relating to construction work carried out, or related goods and services supplied, under the construction contract.

(4)  This chapter does not apply to a construction contract to the extent it deals with construction work carried out outside Queensland or related goods and services supplied for construction work carried out outside Queensland.

(5)  In this section—

resident owner, in relation to a construction contract for carrying out domestic building work, means a resident owner under the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991, schedule 1B, section 1, but does not include a person—

(a)  who holds, or should hold, an owner-builder permit under the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991 relating to the work; or

(b)  who is a building contractor within the meaning of the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991.

Read this as a default-on switch. The Chapter applies to construction contracts whether they are written, oral or a mix; whether they are governed by Queensland law or some other law; and whether they were signed before or after Chapter 3 commenced. The choice-of-law clause your lawyers fought over does not get you out of the Queensland security of payment regime if the work is in Queensland.

The exceptions in s61(2)–(4) are narrow. The most relevant for a typical PM are: (a) parts of a contract that are really a financing arrangement with a bank; (b) genuine domestic building work where a resident owner is on the other side of the contract; (c) contracts where the price is not calculated by reference to the value of the work (for example, lump-sum profit-share or success-fee arrangements); (d) employment-style provisions; and (e) work physically located outside Queensland.

The onus sits with the party trying to escape the Act. If you are the respondent and you want to argue the contract is excluded, you have to prove it. Drafting the contract to say it is excluded does not make it so — the question is one of substance.

A "rough and ready" regime that demands strict compliance

The courts have repeatedly described the security of payment regime as a fast, informal, interim process — designed to keep cash moving down the chain while the parties' final legal rights are sorted out later. In Musico v Davenport [2003] NSWSC 977, McDougall J described the equivalent NSW Act as:

[107] a somewhat rough and ready way of assessing a builder's entitlement to progress claims. It may also be accepted that the procedure is intended not only to be swift, but also to be carried out with the minimum amount of formality and expense.

That informality is balanced by an unforgiving attitude to compliance. The Queensland authorities treat the statute as creating a parallel regime — statutory rights that sit alongside the contract (see Beckhaus v Brewarrina Council [2002] NSWSC 960, [60] (Macready AJ); Falgat Constructions Pty Ltd v Equity Australia Corp Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 49; (2005) 62 NSWLR 385) — but only for those who follow the statutory steps.

In F.K. Gardner & Sons Pty Ltd v Dimin Pty Ltd [2007] 1 Qd R 10; [2006] QSC 243, Lyons J put the point bluntly:

[24] The Act sets up a statutory regime for the recovery of progress claims and it is dependent on a series of steps being completed. There must be a valid statutory entitlement to a progress payment before a payment claim can be made and then if a payment schedule does not issue within time the unpaid portion of the claim becomes a debt. Such a statutory regime depends on strict compliance with the provisions in the Act.

The same theme runs through De Neefe Signs Pty Ltd v Build1 (Qld) Pty Ltd; Traffic Technologies Traffic Hire Pty Ltd v Build1 (Qld) Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 279 (Fryberg J), Tailored Projects Pty Ltd v Jedfire Pty Ltd [2009] 2 Qd R 171; [2009] QSC 32 (Douglas J at 176, [18]), and Simcorp Development and Constructions Pty Ltd v Gold Coast Titans Property Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 162, where Douglas J said at [26] that:

[26] Failure to adhere strictly to the statutory regime for the recovery of claims has been held to preclude reliance on the special statutory rights available under the Act.

The interstate position is the same. In Walter Construction Group Ltd v CPL (Surry Hills) Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 266 at [59], Nicholas J said the Act:

[59] has been said that the Act offers to a claimant special statutory rights which override general contractual rights and place the claimant in a privileged position (Jemzone Pty Ltd v Trytan Pty Ltd (2002) NSWSC 395 at para 41 per Austin J). In order to exercise these rights compliance with the requirements which establish them are necessary.

And in Taylor Projects Group Pty Ltd v Brick Dept Pty Ltd [2005] NSWSC 439, Einstein J said at [49]:

[49] In my view it is simply critical for a rigid approach to be taken to compliance with the terms of the Act, particularly for the reason that the legislation provides for a fast dual-track interim determination, reserving the parties' final legal entitlements for subsequent determination.

The Victorian Court of Appeal struck the same note in Saville v Hallmarc Construction Pty Ltd [2015] VSCA 318 (Warren CJ and Tate JA), drawing on Chase Oyster Bar Pty Ltd v Hamo Industries Pty Ltd [2010] NSWCA 190; (2010) 78 NSWLR 393:

The considerations identified by the New South Wales Court of Appeal, as described above, indicate that the strict observance of the procedural steps in the statutory scheme, including time limits, was mandated by the legislature as the price by which a claimant can take the benefit of the regime. The observance of certain procedural steps are essential to the assumption of jurisdiction by an adjudicator. An assessment of whether those procedural steps have been observed may require matters of evaluation that travel beyond a mechanical process.

Set against the strict-compliance line is a softer thread that says — especially for the content of payment claims — "reasonable" compliance can be enough: see T&M Buckley Pty Ltd v 57 Moss Rd Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 381, [33] (Philippides J; Fraser and White JJA agreeing); Nepean Engineering Pty Ltd v Total Process Services Pty Ltd (in liq) [2005] NSWCA 409 at [34] (Hodgson JA), [76] (Ipp JA). And in Hawkins Construction (Australia) Pty Ltd v Mac's Industrial Pipework Pty Ltd [2002] NSWCA 136, Davies AJA warned that the equivalent provisions:

should not be approached in an unduly technical manner… The terms used by subs (2) of s 13 are well understood words of the English language. They should be given their normal and natural meaning. As the words are used in relation to events occurring in the construction industry, they should be applied in a common sense practical manner.

See also Amasya Enterprises Pty Ltd & Anor v Asta Developments (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor (No 2) [2015] VSC 500, [87] (Vickery J), citing Protectavale Pty Ltd v K2K Pty Ltd [2008] FCA 1248, [10]–[11] (Finkelstein J). The practical takeaway: timing rules and jurisdictional steps are unforgiving; the form and content of payment claims are read commercially. Treat the procedural deadlines (when to serve, when to respond, when to apply for adjudication) as bright lines.

One recent illustration of the commercial reading: in Hawkesbury City Council v The Civil Experts Pty Ltd trading as TCE Contracting [2023] NSWSC 962, Ball J held that where a contract said "Cost + 25%" without defining "cost", the adjudicator was entitled to read "cost" as covering all costs incurred by the claimant in carrying out its construction business, including off-site overheads.

The s61(2) exceptions — onus is on the party trying to escape the Act

Section 61(1) is a statement of general application; s61(2) carves out exceptions. The party that wants to argue an exception applies bears the onus of proof. As Nicholas J explained in Walter Construction Group Limited v CPL (Surry Hills) Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 266 in relation to the analogous NSW provision:

It seems clear to me that the intention of the legislature is that the Act applies to any construction contract, s 7(1) being a statement of general application. It is also clear that its intention is that the classes of contract described in s 7(2) are exceptions to the general rule so that if a party wishes to avoid its application it must demonstrate that the contract is within an excepted class. Obviously, it is probable that the features of a contract which might bring it within an excepted class will be within the knowledge of the party seeking to avoid the Act. Consistent with the principles referred to, that circumstance is further indication of the intention that the onus of proof concerning the exception is on the party claiming that the contract is within it.

"Forms part of" a loan agreement — s61(2)(a)

The exception only bites where the construction contract is genuinely included in the financing arrangement, not merely associated with it. In Consolidated Constructions Pty Ltd v Ettamogah Pub [2004] NSWSC 110, McDougall J considered the words "forms part of":

As a matter of ordinary English usage, something may be said to "form part of" another if the first thing is included or incorporated within the second. The first thing may form part of the second as a result of some natural process or as a result of some artificial process (for example, a process of manufacture). In general terms, the words "forms part of" seem to me to connote something akin to inclusion, as opposed to association. In a particular case, however, it may be difficult to discern the point at which association changes to inclusion: that is to say, the point at which one thing may be said to form part of, rather than merely to be associated with, another.

…

In both ordinary English usage and legal usage, the words "forms part of" therefore seem to indicate a relationship that is more than ancillary or associative. It is not enough to say only that the two things in question are in some way connected, for example because the one bears in some way on the other. The point at which connection becomes inclusion – at which the ancillary becomes integral – may not be easy to discern, and will in any event depend upon the facts of the particular case and the terms of the particular documents.

His Honour held at [21] that a "construction contract will not form part of a loan agreement unless, in some way, the former is included in, or incorporated into, the latter". A separately negotiated head contract that simply sits next to a project finance facility will not be excluded just because the bank's cash funds the build.

"Indemnity relating to construction work" — s61(2)(a)(iii)

Watch this exclusion carefully if you are the claimant and your claim is dressed up as an indemnity rather than as a claim for work done. In Forte Sydney Carlingford Pty Ltd v Li [2022] FCA 1499, Stewart J granted an injunction restraining the claimant from re-submitting an adjudication application after two earlier attempts. The claimant's payment claim was based entirely on an indemnity under the construction contract rather than on construction works carried out. Stewart J held that s 7(3)(c)(iii) of the NSW SOP Act — the equivalent of s61(3)(c)(iii) of the BIF Act — operates to exclude any part of a payment claim that falls under such an indemnity from adjudication, with the result that the payment claim itself was invalid.

"Domestic building work" and resident owners — s61(2)(b)

Chapter 3 does not apply to a construction contract for domestic building work where a "resident owner" is on the other side — but the definition of resident owner in s61(5) excludes anyone who holds (or should hold) an owner-builder permit under the QBCC Act, or who is a building contractor under that Act. So a tradie building their own home in their own name is not necessarily out of reach.

The Court of Appeal considered "resident owner" under the BCIPA equivalent in R J Neller Building Pty Ltd v Ainsworth [2008] QCA 397; [2009] 1 Qd R 390. Keane JA rejected the argument that an owner could only have one residence, holding:

In my respectful opinion, it is not possible to detect in the provisions of the BCIP Act an intention on the part of the legislature that the concept of "resident owner" should be confined in the way urged by Neller. The evident concern of the legislature in excluding contracts between builders and resident owners from the scope of the BCIP Act is to ensure that the ultimate consumers of the goods and services provided by the building and construction industry should not be subject to a regime which, among other things, imposes certain financial risks upon building owners who are participants in that industry to the advantage of the builders who contract with them. I shall discuss the effect of the BCIP Act upon the allocation of these financial risks in due course, but for the moment it is sufficient to say that, this being the concern which can be seen to inform the exclusion of "resident owners" from the scope of the legislation, there is little reason to think that it was any part of the legislative intention to alter the usual understanding that a person may be a resident of several locations at the same time.

The "building contractor" carve-out from the resident-owner definition was tested in Vis Constructions Ltd & Anor v Cockburn & Anor [2006] QSC 416. A husband and wife jointly owned land and engaged a builder to construct their home; the husband held a plastering licence but the wife did not. The adjudicator concluded that they were still a "resident owner". On review, Jones J said the decision "might well be wrong in law" but held that it was a non-jurisdictional error of law and so not reviewable.

For completeness, in BWAY Group Pty v Pasiopoulos [2019] VCC 691, Marks J held that whether a party to a domestic building contract is "in the business of building residences" for the purposes of the equivalent Victorian provision turns on objective indicia such as commercial history — not the parties' subjective intentions.

Consideration "calculated other than by reference to" the value of the work — s61(2)(c)

This exception targets contracts where the consideration is fixed by something other than the value of the work done or goods supplied — for example, contracts that pay a percentage tied to a downstream sale, or fixed fees that are not calibrated to work value. Einstein J in Brambles Australia Limited v Philip Davenport & Ors [2004] NSWSC 120 explained the purpose of the equivalent NSW exclusion:

"Clearly enough, this sub-section serves to exclude certain construction contracts from the reach of the Act. The construction contracts so excluded are identified by reference to whether or not the consideration payable for construction work carried out under the contract or for related goods and services supplied under the contract is to be calculated."

His Honour adopted Nicholas J's approach in Walter Construction Group Limited v CPL (Surry Hills) Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 266. In Sheppard Homes Pty Ltd v FADL Industrial Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 228, the amounts payable under the contract were determined by reference to what the owner paid the builder, not the value of the consultant's services. Fryberg J held that the equivalent BCIPA exclusion (s 3(2)(c)) applied — so the contract between the builder and the consultant was outside the Act.

"To guarantee payment" — s61(3)(c)

An undertaking to pay money owed will fall within the guarantee exclusion. In Walton Construction (Qld) Pty Ltd v Robert Salce & Ors [2008] QSC 235, an undertaking to pay money owed was held to fall clearly within the exclusion under the equivalent BCIPA provision (s 3(3)(c)(ii)).

Work or goods outside Queensland — s61(4)

Section 61(4) gives the Act a territorial limit. It excludes the Act to the extent the contract deals with construction work carried out outside Queensland (or related goods and services supplied for such work). Philip McMurdo J explained the rationale in Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 307:

"The evident purpose of s 3(4) is to confine the operation of the Act to circumstances which have a relevant association with Queensland. It is of the essence of construction work, as defined, that it involves work in relation to certain land. It is therefore logical that the Act would be given a territorial limitation which is defined according to whether the relevant land is in Queensland."

The location of the ultimate works matters more than where each fabrication step happens. In the same case, a shiploader and "tripper" were fabricated in Malaysia for installation at a coal terminal near Gladstone. The respondent argued that a payment claim made about four weeks before the shiploader arrived in Queensland was outside the Act. Philip McMurdo J disagreed:

The work of the fabrication of this equipment was carried out outside Queensland. But that work was construction work only because it was an integral part of construction work undertaken inside Queensland. In my view, that fabrication could not be relevantly characterised both as construction work carried out outside Queensland and an integral part of construction work carried out within Queensland.

In requiring the fabrications of this equipment, the contract was providing for part of the construction of a structure in Queensland and was, in the sense of s 3(4), "dealing with" construction work carried out in Queensland. It follows that the application of the Act was not displaced by s 3(4).

The Victorian County Court took a similar line in South City Plaster Pty Ltd v Modscape Pty Ltd [2018] VCC 1576, where Macnamara J held that the VIC Act applied even though some work was carried out in Tasmania, because the contract did not propose work outside Victoria and the Tasmanian work was "not an integral part of construction work."

Cross-border projects were addressed directly in Lendlease Building Pty Ltd v BCS Airport Systems Pty Ltd & Ors [2024] QSC 164. The Gold Coast Airport site straddles the Queensland–NSW border, and some of the works were carried out in NSW. Lendlease tried to quash an adjudication on the basis that the payment claim was invalid because part of the work was in NSW. Sullivan J rejected the argument: s61(4) only displaces the Act where construction work is wholly outside Queensland; what matters is the location of the ultimate building, structure or works, not where each individual activity takes place; and a payment claim is not invalidated merely because it includes a component of "excluded construction work". The cross-border claim was valid.

Final payments

Final payments fall within Chapter 3. The definitions of "final payment" (s75(3)) and "progress payment" (s64) confirm that the Chapter is not limited to interim claims — the final claim under a contract is also a payment claim for the purposes of the Act.

Section 62 — Effect of giving notice of claim for subcontractors' charges

Section 62 governs what happens when a claimant tries to run two parallel processes — a Chapter 3 payment claim and a Chapter 4 subcontractors' charge — for the same work. The short version: pick one. Lodging a Chapter 4 notice of claim shuts down Chapter 3 for the same work.

Section 62 BIF Act – Effect of giving notice of claim for subcontractors' charges
62 Effect of giving notice of claim for subcontractors' charges

(1)  This section applies if a person gives a notice of claim under chapter 4 in relation to construction work or related goods and services the subject of a construction contract.

(2)  Proceedings or other action may not be started or continued by the person under part 3 for all or part of the construction work or related goods and services.

(3)  Without limiting subsection (2), if the person gave a respondent a payment claim for all or part of the construction work or related goods and services before or at the same time as giving the notice of claim—

(a)  the respondent is not required to pay an amount to the person under section 77(2) in relation to the claim; and

(b)  amounts may not be recovered by the person as a debt owing to the person in any court of competent jurisdiction in relation to the claim; and

(c)  if the person made an adjudication application in relation to the claim and the application has not been decided by an adjudicator before the notice of claim is given, the person is taken to have withdrawn the application; and

(d)  if the person made an adjudication application in relation to the claim and the application has been decided by an adjudicator before the notice of claim was given—

(i)  the respondent to the application is not required to pay the adjudicated amount under section 90; and

(ii)  the registrar must not give the person an adjudication certificate under section 91 relating to the adjudication; and

(iii)  any adjudication certificate provided in relation to the adjudication can not be enforced by the person under section 93 as a judgment of a court; and

(e)  the person may not suspend, or continue to suspend, carrying out all or part of the construction work or the supply of the related goods and services under section 98.

(4)  This section does not affect the operation of section 95 and an adjudication application taken to have been withdrawn by the person under subsection (3)(c) is taken to have been withdrawn for the purpose of section 95.

(5)  This section does not stop the person serving, under this chapter, a payment claim in relation to all or part of the construction work or related goods and services and taking other action under this chapter in relation to that claim, if the notice of claim in so far as it relates to the construction work or related goods and services, or part, is withdrawn.

(6)  In this section—

notice of claim see section 122(1).

If you (or a subcontractor you are dealing with) issue a Chapter 4 notice of claim of charge over the same work, the Chapter 3 process for that work freezes. You can't start or continue court proceedings under Part 3 for it. If you had already served a payment claim, the respondent doesn't have to pay under s77(2), you can't recover the unpaid amount as a debt, any pending adjudication is treated as withdrawn, and any decided adjudication can't be enforced. You also lose the right to suspend work under s98 for that scope.

The freeze isn't permanent. Section 62(5) lets you come back to Chapter 3 if the notice of claim is withdrawn — but the withdrawal needs to be effective. In Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386, the Court of Appeal observed (under the predecessor BCIPA provision) that it is implicit that the notice of claim must be valid in the first place, and that a "withdrawn" notice must be withdrawn in a way that is effective in respect of all relevant parties — regardless of whether the withdrawal is in the approved form.

Practically: don't run both regimes for the same scope at the same time. Choose your weapon early. If you fire off a notice of claim of charge while an adjudication is on foot, you will be taken to have withdrawn the adjudication application — which has knock-on consequences under s95 for any later application.

Section 63 — Act does not limit claimant's other rights

Section 63 makes clear that Chapter 3 layers on top of the contract; it doesn't replace it. The statutory entitlements are additional, not substitutional.

Section 63 BIF Act – Act does not limit claimant's other rights
63 Act does not limit claimant other rights

A claimant's entitlements and remedies under this chapter do not limit—

(a)  another entitlement a claimant may have under a construction contract; or

(b)  any remedy a claimant may have for recovering the other entitlement.

Whatever rights a claimant has under the contract — to claim payment, to charge interest, to call on security, to pursue damages, to refer disputes to arbitration — remain available. Chapter 3 sits alongside them. As Austin J observed in Jemzone Pty Ltd v Trytan Pty Ltd [2002] NSWSC 395 in relation to the structure of the equivalent NSW Act:

generally leaves it to the construction contract to define the rights of the parties but makes "default provisions" to fill in the contractual gaps.

For PMs this matters in two practical ways. First, an adjudicator's decision is interim — it determines who holds the cash now, not who is ultimately entitled to it. The contract's final-account, dispute-resolution and damages mechanisms still run their course. Second, electing to use Chapter 3 does not waive your contractual rights; you can run an adjudication and still pursue the contractual claim later for any shortfall.

Practice tips

  • Assume Chapter 3 applies to any construction contract you are running in Queensland — whether written, oral, pre-existing or governed by another state's law — until you have specifically ruled out an exception in s61(2), (3) or (4).
  • If you are the respondent and you want to argue a contract is excluded, remember the onus is on you. Build the evidence (financing structure, residency, pricing mechanism, work location) early — don't rely on the contract simply saying it is excluded.
  • For cross-border or partly off-shore work, the question is the location of the ultimate works, not where each fabrication or activity happens. After Lendlease v BCS Airport Systems, a payment claim that includes some out-of-state work is not automatically invalid — s61(4) only bites where the work is wholly outside Queensland.
  • Treat the procedural steps and timeframes as bright lines. The form and content of payment claims are read commercially, but timing and jurisdictional steps are unforgiving — miss a deadline and you forfeit the statutory right.
  • Don't run a Chapter 3 payment claim and a Chapter 4 notice of claim of charge for the same scope at the same time. Lodging the Chapter 4 notice freezes Chapter 3 for that work, withdraws any pending adjudication, and stops you enforcing any adjudicated amount or suspending under s98.
  • Remember that an adjudication outcome is interim. Section 63 preserves your contractual rights — final account, damages, security, dispute resolution — so plan the litigation and contract administration strategy together, not separately.
