Chapter 3 · Sections 65–66

Construction work & related goods and services

Whether the BIF Act covers your contract turns on two definitions: is the work “construction work” (s 65) or, if not, is what is being supplied “related goods and services” (s 66)? A single contract can mix both, and it can also include scope that is neither — in which case only the construction-work and related-goods-and-services portions attract the Act’s payment regime.

Section 65 – Meaning of construction work

Section 65 BIF Act – Meaning of construction work
65 Meaning of construction work

(1)  Construction work means any of the following work—

(a)  the construction, alteration, repair, restoration, maintenance, extension, demolition or dismantling of buildings or structures, whether permanent or not, forming, or to form, part of land;

(b)  the construction, alteration, repair, restoration, maintenance, extension, demolition or dismantling of any works forming, or to form, part of land, including walls, roadworks, powerlines, telecommunication apparatus, aircraft runways, docks and harbours, railways, inland waterways, pipelines, reservoirs, water mains, wells, sewers, industrial plant and installations for land drainage or coast protection;

(c)  the installation in any building, structure or works of fittings forming, or to form, part of land, including heating, lighting, air-conditioning, ventilation, power supply, drainage, sanitation, water supply, fire protection, security and communications systems;

(d)  the external or internal cleaning of buildings, structures and works, so far as it is carried out in the course of their construction, alteration, repair, restoration, maintenance or extension;

(e)  any operation that forms an integral part of, or is preparatory to or is for completing, work of the kind referred to in paragraph (a), (b) or (c), including—

(i)  site clearance, earthmoving, excavation, tunnelling and boring; and

(ii)  the laying of foundations; and

(iii)  the erection, maintenance or dismantling of scaffolding; and

(iv)  the prefabrication of components to form part of any building, structure or works, whether carried out on-site or off-site; and

(v)  site restoration, landscaping and the provision of roadways and other access works;

(f)  the painting or decorating of the internal or external surfaces of any building, structure or works;

(g)  carrying out the testing of soils and road making materials during the construction and maintenance of roads;

(h)  any other work of a kind prescribed by regulation.

(2)  To remove doubt, it is declared that construction work includes building work within the meaning of the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991.

(3)  However, construction work does not include any of the following work—

(a)  the drilling for, or extraction of, oil or natural gas;

(b)  the extraction, whether by underground or surface working, of minerals, including tunnelling or boring, or constructing underground works, for that purpose.

Section 65 is the gateway provision. If your scope sits in one of the limbs (a)–(h), the BIF Act’s progress-payment machinery is engaged. A single contract often contains both construction work and other scope; only the construction-work component attracts the Act, so you need to identify it carefully (see also the discussion of “construction contract” in s 64).

The courts read s 65 in its natural sense — not stretched in either direction to capture or exclude marginal cases. In Capricorn Quarries Pty Ltd v Inline Communication Construction Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 388; [2013] 2 Qd R 1, Jackson J rejected an argument that BCIPA (the predecessor regime) should be given a “remedial” construction:

Having regard to the context as discussed, in my view, a “natural” construction of the relevant definitions of BCIPA in this case is to be preferred to an approach which seeks to extend the operation of BCIPA by a “liberal interpretation” to be engaged in with the purpose of increasing the width of the class of persons who are entitled to the benefit of a payment claim and correspondingly increasing the width of the class of persons who are subject to BCIPA’s restriction and obligations.

The language chosen by Parliament to define “construction contract”, “construction work” and “related goods and services” has no purpose other than to draw the line between who is in and who is out of those classes.  There seems to be little logic in seeking to stretch that language either way.  In saying this, I take a “natural” construction to be that arrived at by the usual process of the application of the common law of statutory interpretation, as affected by statute, but without a presumptive approach.

Two further early-authority points are worth noting. In Walter Construction Group Ltd v CPL (Surry Hills) Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 266, Nicholas J rejected the argument that an extension-of-time claim did not relate to construction work — EOT claims are within the regime. In Patrick Stevedores Operations (No 2) Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2014] NSWSC 1413, Ball J held that the costs of plant and materials the claimant ordered just to put itself in a position to comply with its contract, and the costs of demobilising equipment from site, were neither construction work under the contract nor related goods or services.

“Forming, or to form, part of land”

The phrase “works to form part of land” in s 65(1)(b) is, as Fryberg J put it in Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2011] QSC 345 at [28], “evidently a phrase of wide meaning”. His Honour rejected the suggestion that the works must be additions to the land, and said the section “should be given a practical interpretation which will minimise potential areas of dispute” (at [32]).

On mining leases and the meaning of “land”, the Court of Appeal in J & D Rigging Pty Ltd v Agripower Australia Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 406 confirmed that the word takes its ordinary meaning. Applegarth J (Holmes JA and Boddice J agreeing) said:

BCIPA reallocates financial risk between the parties to a construction contract to which it applies. Section 10 should be interpreted so that its ordinary words are able to be applied by parties to determine whether or not a contract is subject to BCIPA. If possible, the statute should be interpreted so that it is capable of being applied in a practical way by parties to a construction contract or a proposed construction contract. The ordinary meaning of the words in s 10 anticipates a practical inquiry into the physical relationship between an item and land, by asking in the case of s 10(1)(a) whether the building or structure forms, or is to form, part of land. In the case of the demolition or dismantling of a building or structure, the inquiry is whether the building or structure forms part of land. The inquiry is into the physical state of things, not the intention of parties at the time the building or structure was constructed, possibly many years earlier.

…

The word “land” in s 10 has the extended meaning contained in the Acts Interpretation Act as well as its ordinary meaning. The expression “forming, or to form part of land” uses the word “form” in its ordinary sense.

The words of s 10 do not call for an inquiry into whether the plant formed part of land according to common law rules about the ownership of fixtures. They do not import a requirement that the plant be owned by the owner of the land upon which it was constructed as a result of this having been the objective intention of certain parties. Requirements of the law of real property about ownership of things affixed to land are not imported into s 10. Instead, the degree of annexation will be relevant to the issue of whether or not a thing forms part of land.

Section 65(1)(b) – permanence not required

In Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2011] QSC 345, Fryberg J rejected an argument that works under paragraph (b) must be intended to be permanent (at [31]).

The Victorian decision in BCS Infrastructure Support Pty Ltd v Jones Lang Lasalle (NSW) Pty Ltd [2020] VSC 739 illustrates the breadth of the “works” limb. Stynes J held that the maintenance of baggage conveyor systems, aerobridges, check-in kiosks and baggage return carousels at an airport fell within construction work under s 5(1)(a) of the NSW SOP Act (the equivalent of s 65(1)(b) of the BIF Act), because those structures were permanently attached to the terminal building. The contract was therefore a construction contract for SOP-Act purposes.

Section 65(1)(e) – integral, preparatory and completing operations

Paragraph (e) only catches operations that have a sufficient nexus with construction work in paragraphs (a), (b) or (c). In Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 307, Philip McMurdo J explained the connection point this way:

the close connection between an operation within paragraph (e) and construction work of a kind within paragraphs (a), (b) or (c).  An operation falls within paragraph (e) only because of the part it plays in construction work which, according to the terms of paragraphs (a), (b) and (c), involves a connection with certain land.

Section 65(1)(e)(i) — site clearance, earthmoving, excavation, tunnelling and boring. In Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2011] QSC 345, Fryberg J held that clearing and grubbing work fell within this paragraph — see also Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2012] QCA 276; [2013] 2 Qd R 75.

The mining boundary was tested in Cadia Holdings Pty Ltd v Downer EDI Mining Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 1588. Stevenson J held that preparatory work which is not for the actual purpose of extracting minerals falls within construction work and is not knocked out by the “mining exception” in s 5(2) of the NSW SOP Act (the equivalent of s 65(1)(e) of BIFSOPA). Cadia had engaged Downer to perform tunnelling, boring and underground works to construct access to the proposed undercut and extraction levels of a mine. Cadia argued the scope was expressly excluded under the “Mining Exception” in s 5(2)(b) of the SOP Act. The Court read the exception narrowly: the tunnelling, boring or constructing of underground works must be for the actual purpose of extracting minerals. Where the object and purpose of the contract is not extraction itself, the exception does not apply.

Section 65(1)(e)(iv) — prefabrication of components. In Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 307, Philip McMurdo J held that a prefabricated shiploader and “tripper” to be installed on a wharf at a coal export terminal were properly characterised as components to form part of that wharf and within s 10(1)(e) of the predecessor Act.

Section 65(3) – the mining and oil/gas exclusion

Coal is a “mineral”. In the context of the predecessor s 10(3) of BCIPA, coal has been treated as a mineral for the purposes of what is now s 65(3): Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2011] QSC 345 at [39] (Fryberg J).

“Extraction” is read narrowly. Fryberg J in Theiss [2011] QSC 345 at [42] read the exclusion tightly:

I accept that the work performed by Warren was a necessary part of opening the coal mines. But that is not the issue. The exemption given by s 10(3)(b) is not expressed to apply to work done for the purpose of opening or as preparatory to operating a mine. The words used are much more limited than that. They focus purely on the process of extraction. In my judgment the ordinary meaning of the word, considered in isolation, does not apply to the work done by Warren.

Douglas J adopted the same approach in HM Mire Pty Ltd v National Plant and Equipment Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 4 at [13]:

I agree with his Honour’s reasons dealing with the proper construction of the section. The facts of this case are also similar to the facts then being considered by his Honour. It also seems to me to be necessary to construe s 10(3) in the context set by s 10(1) which describes a relatively broad set of circumstances amounting to construction work. It would detract unnecessarily from the apparent purpose of the legislation and the normal understanding of s 10(1) and its hierarchy in the section to extend the meaning of “extraction of minerals” to cover work associated with such extraction where the legislature, as Mr Ambrose submitted, could readily have made such a purpose clear by the use of familiar language of wider meaning than this phrase. There is no reason why s 10(3) should be read so as to displace or render nugatory the meaning of s 10(1).

The narrow approach was approved by the Court of Appeal in Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2012] QCA 276; [2013] 2 Qd R 75, 91 [62]–[63] (Philippides J, Holmes and White JJA agreeing). The practical message: work that merely enables a mine to operate, as opposed to the actual extraction of minerals, will usually still be construction work.

Section 66 – Meaning of related goods and services

Section 66 BIF Act – Meaning of related goods and services
66 Meaning of related goods and services

(1)  Related goods and services, in relation to construction work, means any of the following—

(a)  goods of the following kind—

(i)  materials and components to form part of any building, structure or work arising from construction work;

(ii)  plant or materials (whether supplied by sale, hire or otherwise) for use in connection with the carrying out of construction work;

(b)  services of the following kind—

(i)  the provision of labour to carry out construction work;

(ii)  architectural, design, surveying or quantity surveying services relating to construction work;

(iii)  building, engineering, interior or exterior decoration or landscape advisory services relating to construction work;

(iv)  soil testing services relating to construction work;

(c)  goods and services, relating to construction work, of a kind prescribed by regulation.

(2)  In this chapter, a reference to related goods and services includes a reference to related goods or services.

Section 66 picks up supplies that are not themselves construction work but feed into it — materials, plant hire, labour-only crews, design and engineering consultancies, surveyors, soil testers and the like. If a supply does not fall within s 65, this is the second gateway to test before assuming the BIF Act does not apply.

A “natural” construction

The same interpretive starting point applies. In Capricorn Quarries Pty Ltd v Inline Communication Construction Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 388; [2013] 2 Qd R 1, Jackson J held that a “natural” construction should be given to “construction contract”, “construction work” and “related goods and services”, rather than a remedial or liberal one:

Having regard to the context as discussed, in my view, a “natural” construction of the relevant definitions of BCIPA in this case is to be preferred to an approach which seeks to extend the operation of BCIPA by a “liberal interpretation” to be engaged in with the purpose of increasing the width of the class of persons who are entitled to the benefit of a payment claim and correspondingly increasing the width of the class of persons who are subject to BCIPA’s restriction and obligations.

The language chosen by Parliament to define “construction contract”, “construction work” and “related goods and services” has no purpose other than to draw the line between who is in and who is out of those classes.  There seems to be little logic in seeking to stretch that language either way.  In saying this, I take a “natural” construction to be that arrived at by the usual process of the application of the common law of statutory interpretation, as affected by statute, but without a presumptive approach.

“Supply”

In Patrick Stevedores Operations (No 2) Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2014] NSWSC 1413, Ball J held that plant and materials the claimant ordered, and the cost of removing equipment from site, were not the supply of related goods or services. The plant and materials had been ordered to put the claimant “in a position where it could comply with its contractual obligations” — that internal preparation was not, by itself, a supply of related goods or services to the other party.

Section 66(1)(a) – “for use in connection with”

The connector phrase “for use in connection with” was construed in Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2011] QSC 345. Fryberg J held:

In my judgment, “for use in connection with” is not satisfied simply by proving that the plant or materials supplied were used in connection with the carrying out of construction work.  Warren’s submission to that effect should be rejected. “For” is a word of wide denotation.  In the present context it carries a purposive meaning.  It suggests that the phrase must be satisfied at the outset of the transaction, before the plant or materials are used.  Evidence of the use to which plant or materials were put may support an inference as to the purpose for which they were at that time to be used, but such evidence could not satisfy the phrase without such an inference.

That conclusion is supported by the manner in which “related goods and services” is used in the Act. The most important use is in the definition of construction contract, a definition which relevantly refers to a “contract, agreement or other arrangement”. As noted above, in most other contexts in which the expression is used it is used to refer to related goods and services supplied under a construction contract. That suggests that the contract or arrangement will supply an important part of the evidence by which the proposed use of the plant or materials is to be identified. The use must be able to be identified at the time of the contract or arrangement or possibly, if the supply takes place at an earlier time, at the time of the supply.

That construction was adopted by Jackson J in Capricorn Quarries Pty Ltd v Inline Communication Construction Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 388; [2013] 2 Qd R 1, 11 [49]. The practical takeaway: to bring a plant-or-materials supply within s 66(1)(a)(ii), the construction-related purpose must be identifiable at the time of contract (or, where supply is earlier, at the time of supply) — not back-justified by how the goods were eventually used.

“Services”

In Sheppard Homes Pty Ltd v FADL Industrial Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 228, Fryberg J held that the supply of a licence for drawings does not fall within the definition of services. The agreement in question was between a consultant and a builder, under which the consultant licensed the builder to use certain plans for building work. His Honour held that the agreement was not the provision of related services under the Act.

Architectural services have been held to fall within “related goods and services”: Peter’s of Kensington v Seeksucker Pty Ltd [2008] NSWSC 897.

Project management services have likewise been held to fall within “related goods and services”: Biseja Pty Ltd v NSI Group Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 835.

Practice tips

  • Run the s 65 test scope-by-scope. A single contract often mixes construction work with other supplies; only the construction-work portion (and related goods and services for it) attracts the BIF Act.
  • If the work is not in s 65, do not stop — check s 66. Plant hire, labour hire, design, surveying, soil testing and similar inputs are caught even though they are not construction work themselves.
  • For s 65(1)(b), do not assume temporary or non-permanent works are out. Theiss and BCS Infrastructure confirm permanence is not required, and ongoing maintenance of structures attached to a building can qualify.
  • For mining and resources scope, identify whether your work is “for the actual purpose of extracting minerals” or merely enables the mine to operate. Following Cadia and Theiss, preparatory and access works are usually still construction work despite s 65(3).
  • For supply-only contracts, document the construction purpose at the time of contract (or supply, if earlier). Per Theiss, “for use in connection with” is judged at the outset, so a clear scope and project reference on the order makes the s 66(1)(a)(ii) test much easier.
  • Be cautious with IP-only or licence-style consultancy deals. Following Sheppard Homes, a bare licence to use plans is not a related service — if you want BIF coverage, structure the engagement as the provision of architectural, design or project management services connected to the construction work.
