Chapter 3 · Sections 82 & 83

Adjudication response

Once the adjudicator notifies the respondent that the application has been accepted, a short window opens for the respondent to file an adjudication response. Sections 82 and 83 of the BIF Act control what can go in that document and how long the respondent has to lodge it. Both rules are unforgiving: the response is locked to the reasons already given in the payment schedule, and the clock is measured in business days from receipt of the application papers.

Section 82 BIF Act – Adjudication response
82 Adjudication Response

(1)  After being given notice of an adjudicator’s acceptance of an adjudication application under section 81, the respondent may give the adjudicator a response to the adjudication application (the adjudication response).

(2)  However, the respondent must not give an adjudication response if the respondent failed to give the claimant a payment schedule as required under section 76.

(3)  The adjudication response—

(a)  must be in writing; and

(b)  must identify the adjudication application to which it relates; and

(c)  may include the submissions relevant to the response the respondent chooses to include.

(4)  However, the adjudication response must not include any reasons (new reasons) for withholding payment that were not included in the payment schedule when given to the claimant.

(5)  The adjudicator may require the respondent to resubmit the adjudication response without the new reasons.

Form and content (s82)

The structural rules are simple: the response must be in writing, must identify the application it answers, and may include whatever submissions the respondent chooses — subject always to s82(4). A respondent that did not serve a payment schedule under s76 is shut out of the response process entirely.

Although the response cannot expand the reasons given in the schedule, it is not limited to a word-for-word repeat of the schedule. In TQM v Dasein [2004] NSWSC 1216 at [30], McDougall J (commenting on the equivalent NSW provision) explained that the response can amplify reasons already given, with more detail or supporting evidence, even though it cannot raise new ones:

It was submitted for Dasein that Mr Davenport had in any event considered the payment schedule provided by TQM, so that (because of s 20(2B) of the Act) he had in substance considered its case. I do not accept that submission. It is correct to that, by s 20(2B), a respondent cannot include in its adjudication response any reason for withholding payment that has not been included in its payment schedule. However, it would be open to a respondent to argue, either in greater detail or with perhaps more supporting evidence, reasons that had been advanced. If it were intended that an adjudication response should do no more than mirror, word for word, a payment schedule, then there is little point to the ability under s 20(1) for a respondent to lodge an adjudication response. In that context, it may be noted that an adjudication response by s 20(2)(c) “may contain such submissions relevant to the response as the respondent chooses to include”. That must be qualified by subs (2B) so that the submission is not only relevant to the response but also in amplification of reasons earlier given; but it is a clear indication that the adjudication response may do more than parrot the terms of the payment schedule.

Submissions and supporting documents

“Submissions” in s82(3)(c) is read broadly. In Syntech Resources Pty Ltd v Peter Campbell Earthmoving (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 293, Daubney J observed:

The word “submissions” in s 26(2)(d) is not to be construed narrowly; indeed, the words of the section show specifically that the submissions may include relevant documentation in support.

That principle drove the result in Trysams Pty Ltd v Club Constructions (NSW) Pty Ltd [2007] NSWSC 941, where an expert report appeared in the adjudication response but had not been attached to the payment schedule. Hammerschlag J held that the report was either part of a submission or relevant documentation in support of a submission “duly made” by the plaintiff (at [49]). The report was ‘foreshadowed’ in the schedule and ‘[t]he report did not represent any shift from that position. It was provided in support of it’ (at [56]). His Honour continued:

Despite the interim nature of an adjudication, natural justice nevertheless clearly required the adjudicator to consider the report unless (even if erroneously) he determined that it was, or was part of, a submission not duly made. In this case, he made no such determination. This amounted to a substantial failure to afford natural justice which worked practical injustice on the plaintiff and rendered the whole adjudication void.

Section 22(2)(d) required the adjudicator, in the circumstances, to consider the report because it was part of submissions duly made. In so far as a failure to do so was jurisdictional error in the sense that a legislative requirement essential to the existence of a determination was not met, he made such an error.

The line between “explanatory” material and a new reason can be very fine. In Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 307, the respondent attached an EOT expert report to its response that had not been served with the payment schedule. Philip McMurdo J was unable to find that the expert report did not raise a reason for withholding payment that was not in the schedule (at [65]). In Syntech Daubney J reached the opposite view on the documents in front of him, treating the supporting material as “explanatory” reasons only (at [25]). And in Owners Strata Plan 61172 v Stratabuild Ltd [2011] NSWSC 1000, Macready AJ held that the schedule was “appropriately worded and specific enough to ‘sufficiently indicate’ the reasons for withholding payment”:

However, even if the adjudicator was incorrect, had he correctly considered section 20(2B) of the Act at paragraphs 31 to 35 of his reasons? I think the defendant is correct in submitting that one must focus on the substance rather than the form of the adjudicator’s statements. The adjudicator expressed his understanding of ss 14(3) and 20(2B) of the Act. The adjudicator’s view was that, consistent with the policies of fairness and promptness, the full reports should be included with the payment schedule to allow a claimant to properly articulate an adjudication application and prevent a fresh case being brought about in reply after that application was made. The defendant says that the adjudicator did not misconstrue the Act in a way that led to a misconception of his functions.

Unfortunately, the adjudicator did not appreciate how the courts have interpreted the use of the word “indicate” in s 14(3) and the difference between “reasons” and submissions” when used in the legislation.

A practical example: in Syntech, the schedule stated that there should be a $142,134.25 deduction for wet hire and that a review of past payments showed the claimant had been overpaid by $127,618.40. The response then attached supporting spreadsheets. The adjudicator refused to look at the spreadsheets on natural-justice grounds (treating them as material that should have been served with the schedule). The Court held that the failure to consider the spreadsheets was a breach of an essential requirement and amounted to jurisdictional error.

The “no new reasons” rule (s82(4))

Section 82(4) is the heart of the regime: the response cannot smuggle in fresh reasons for withholding payment. In John Holland Pty Ltd v Walz Marine Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 39, Margaret Wilson J (considering s24 of BCIPA, the equivalent of s88 of the Act) put it this way:

Thus, the issues to be decided by the adjudicator are defined in the payment claim and the payment schedule. The respondent must state its reasons for wholly or partially withholding payment in the payment schedule. While the parties may make submissions in support of their respective positions in the adjudication application and the adjudication response respectively, the respondent cannot use the adjudication response as a vehicle to advance reasons for withholding payment which it failed to include in its payment schedule.

The rule has been applied repeatedly. In Thiess Pty Ltd & John Holland Pty Ltd v Civil Works Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 187; [2011] 2 Qd R 276, Daubney J held that an adjudicator did not commit jurisdictional error by refusing to consider clauses raised in the response but not in the schedule. In GW Enterprises Pty Ltd v Xentex Industries Pty Ltd [2006] QSC 399, Lyons J approved the adjudicator’s decision ‘he could not take into account any matters raised by the current applicant which they had not raised in the payment schedule’ (at [36]) and held:

it is imperative that any reasons for withholding payment must be raised in the payment schedule or they cannot be raised at all.

The policy reason was put squarely by Sackar J in State Water Corporation v Civil Team Engineering Pty Ltd [2013] NSWSC 1879 at [63]:

… Once a payment claim and payment schedule have been exchanged, the claimant’s decision of whether or not to take the further step of lodging an adjudication application will depend on its assessment of the respondent’s reasons for non-payment as disclosed in its payment schedule. There are obvious and powerful policy considerations in ensuring that the respondent discloses all of its reasons for non-payment in its payment schedule. The difficulties a claimant would experience if faced with an adjudication response which raised reasons for non-payment which were not included in the payment schedule, are amplified by the fact that the Act does not grant to the claimant a right to reply to the respondent’s adjudication response.

In Brambles Australia Ltd v Davenport [2004] NSWSC 120, Einstein J held that the adjudicator was right to disregard a response that relied on a contract clause (cl 24.1) that had never been mentioned in the schedule:

In my own view, the proper approach, bearing in mind the time constraints built into the scheme, is to be reasonably strict where, as here for example, enforcing the injunction to be found in section 20(2B). Precision and particularity was required in the payment schedule to a degree reasonably sufficient to apprise the parties of the real issues in the dispute. If clause 24.1 was to have been relied upon it had to be expressly identified. Insofar as the adjudication response here went outside the reasons for withholding payment which were included in the payment schedule, it was appropriate for the adjudicator to disregard the adjudication response.

In BuiltCom Construction Pty Ltd v VSD Investments Pty Ltd atf the VSD Investments Trust; VSD Investments Pty Ltd atf The VSD Investments Trust v Builtcom Construction Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 250, Peden J upheld the adjudicator’s refusal to consider VSD’s additional site inspection documents that were not referenced in its payment schedule. Under s 20(2B) (equivalent to section 82(4) BIFSOPA) respondents are prohibited from introducing new reason for withholding payment that were not included in the payment schedule. The Court followed EnerMech Pty Ltd v Acciona Infrastructure Projects Australia Pty Ltd (2024) 115 NSWLR 56, where Basten AJA confirmed that claims for damages or restitution are generally outside an adjudicator’s jurisdiction. Peden J agreed that VSD’s defect setoffs were not particularised and amounted to global claims for damages, which the adjudicator had no power to assess.

Quantum reasons vs “other” reasons

An attempt is sometimes made to draw a line between reasons that explain “why the scheduled amount is less” and the broader category of “reasons for withholding payment”. In John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads & Traffic Authority of New South Wales & Ors [2007] NSWCA 19, Hodgson JA rejected that distinction:

In my opinion, this distinction does not justify a narrow view as to what amounts to reasons for withholding payment. If a respondent does not propose to pay any amount included in the payment claim for any reasons said to justify non-payment of that amount, then in my opinion that is withholding payment and the reasons are reasons for withholding payment. It does not matter whether the reasons relate to non-performance of work, bad work, set-offs or cross-claims of any kind, contractual provisions limiting the claimant’s right to payment or statutory provisions limiting the claimant’s right to payment, or indeed any other suggested justification. Any other view would do violence to the language “withholding payment for any reason”, and be contrary to the plain purpose of s.20(2B) to avoid new submissions being introduced late in a process going ahead on a brief and strict timetable. I agree with what Palmer J said on this matter in Mulitiplex Constructions Pty. Limited v. Luikens [2003] NSWSC 1140 at [65]-[68].

Philip McMurdo J adopted this passage in Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 307, [52]:

The evident intent of s 24 is to prevent the unfairness to a claimant which could follow from a respondent being allowed to contest its alleged liability for a reason which it had not advanced ahead of the adjudication application. If WICET’s argument was accepted, that beneficial operation of s 24(4) would be confined to one kind of reason for non-payment which would have no logical distinction from another.

In Baguley Build Pty Ltd v Olcon Concrete & Construction Pty Ltd [2025] QSC 126, Copley J held that the adjudicator did not fall into jurisdictional error by concluding that the applicant’s adjudication response included “new reasons” for withholding payment that had not been stated in the payment schedule, thereby not contravening s 82(4) of the BIF Act. The adjudicator found that the response attempted to rely on reasons derived from earlier payment schedules without specifying them in relation to the new consolidated claim, and thus determined that they could not be considered. His Honour affirmed that it is a matter “for the adjudicator to determine” whether reasons are new or properly included, and relied on Civmec Electrical & Instrumental Pty Ltd v Southern Cross Electrical Engineering Ltd [2019] QSC 300, where Mullins J drew a distinction between failing to consider properly made submissions (which may amount to jurisdictional error) and mischaracterising submissions (which is an error within jurisdiction). This approach was also supported by Applegarth J in Niclin Constructions Pty Ltd v Robotic Steel Fab Pty Ltd (2023) 16 QR 336.

Reasons going to the adjudicator’s jurisdiction

The s82(4) bar does not extend to arguments about the adjudicator’s jurisdiction. Those arguments can be raised in the response even if they were not in the schedule, because they could not have been raised in the schedule. McDougall J explained the point in Rail Corporation (NSW) v Nebax Constructions [2012] NSWSC 6:

More significantly, it appears to me, s 20(2B) talks of “reasons for withholding payment”. That is not what is at issue here. The question that was raised for the adjudicator’s consideration was whether he had any jurisdiction to hear the multiple applications that were lodged.

In Olympia Group (NSW) Pty Ltd v Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd [2011] NSWSC 165, Ball J said at [11], of a similar argument put to him for consideration, that s 20(2B) prevented the respondent:

“from raising in its adjudication response a reason for not making a payment that was not raised in its payment schedule. It did not prevent it from raising grounds on which it was asserted that the adjudicator did not have jurisdiction to make a determination”.

I agree. The point could not have been taken in the payment schedule. Thus, the alternative submission referred to at [8] above and the submission referred to at [10] above must fail.

In Watkins Contracting Pty Ltd v Hyatt Ground Engineering Pty Ltd [2018] QSC 65, Brown J held the respondent had not raised ‘new reasons’ where:

  • the payment schedule stated that Watkins proposed to pay ‘nil’, because there was no reference date to support the payment claim, as the contract had been terminated or frustrated; and
  • the adjudication response included detailed submissions as to the grounds of termination, including that the contract contained implied terms.

Her Honour held the response merely elaborated matters already raised, and added in obiter that even if the reasons had been new, because they went to jurisdiction the adjudicator was bound to consider them and s24(4) BCIPA would not apply.

In Bega Valley Shire Council v Kenpass Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 399, Nixon J refused to set aside an adjudication determination, including on the ground that the adjudicator had wrongly characterised reasons in the response as “new” under s20(2B). At [49] his Honour held that “any error by the Adjudicator in interpreting the Payment Schedule, or in understanding the reasons given in it, is not a reviewable error”. Whether new reasons have been raised is a matter for the adjudicator.

The other side of that coin is that, where the adjudicator does consider a new reason, the determination is exposed. In Acciona Agua Australia Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 133, Bond J declared part of a determination void where the adjudicator had accepted two grounds raised in the contractor’s response — that the subcontractor should be prevented from taking advantage of its breach, and that an alleged breach of an implied term entitled the contractor to set off — that had no counterpart in the payment schedule. Considering those new reasons offended s82(4) and breached s88(3)(b); the affected part of the determination was void with the balance left binding.

In Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 92, Martin J declared part of a determination void where the adjudicator had applied a contract clause that the respondent first relied on in the response. The court held that:

  • section 69(c) of the BIF Act requires a respondent to include all of its reasons for withholding payment in its payment schedule and not just those that are specifically raised or prompted by the payment claim;
  • section 82(3)(c) of the BIF Act must be read subject to section 82(4) which provides that an adjudication response must not include any reasons for withholding payment that were not included in the payment schedule when given to the claimant; and
  • once an adjudicator considers a matter that it is not permitted to pursuant to section 82 of the BIF Act, then section 88(3)(b) has been breached, whether or not the consideration leads to a particular decision.

The same dispute returned in Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd (No. 2) [2021] QSC 231. There, the claimant had suspended works after non-payment; without telling the claimant, the respondent engaged a replacement contractor. In its response (but not in the schedule), the respondent argued it had only engaged the new contractor after the suspension period and so had not removed any work for s98(3) purposes. The adjudicator accepted that argument. Freeburn J held the adjudicator had considered a new defence not included in the schedule, in breach of s88(3), and declared the affected parts of the revised determination void.

The reverse situation is also a trap. In Castle Constructions Pty Ltd v Napoli Excavations and Civil Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 348, the NSW Supreme Court quashed a determination because the adjudicator failed to engage with a submission in the response. Both parties accepted the contract was terminated; the dispute was about the date. The adjudicator proceeded as if the works had been suspended rather than terminated and so never grappled with the respondent’s termination submission. Because the submission had been duly made, that failure was material and amounted to jurisdictional error.

Section 24(5) BCIPA – complex claims

Section 24(5) provided that where an adjudication application is about a complex payment claim, a respondent may include reasons for withholding payment not included in the payment schedule. This subsection was unique to Queensland under BCIPA, introduced in December 2014 by the Amendment Act. Cases from other jurisdictions, and Queensland cases under BCIPA before 15 December 2014 and after the commencement of the BIF Act, should be read with that history in mind.

Section 83 BIF Act – Time for making adjudication response
83 Time for making adjudication response

(1)  If responding to a standard payment claim, the respondent must give the adjudicator the adjudication response within the later of the following periods to end—

(a)  10 business days after receiving the documents mentioned in section 79(4);

(b)  7 business days after receiving notice of the adjudicator’s acceptance of the adjudication application.

(2)  If responding to a complex payment claim, the respondent must give the adjudicator the adjudication response within the later of the following to end—

(a)  15 business days after receiving the documents mentioned in section 79(4);

(b)  12 business days after receiving notice of the adjudicator’s acceptance of the adjudication application.

(3)  However, if responding to a complex payment claim, the respondent may apply to the adjudicator for an extension of time, of up to 15 additional business days, to give the adjudication response to the adjudicator.

(4)  The application must—

(a)  be in writing; and

(b)  be made within the later of the following periods to end—

(i)  5 business days after receiving the documents mentioned in section 79(4);

(ii)  2 business days after receiving notice of the adjudicator’s acceptance of the adjudication application; and

(c)  include the reasons for requiring the extension of time.

(5)  If the application is granted, the respondent may give the adjudicator the adjudication response no later than the end of the extension of time granted by the adjudicator.

(6)  If the respondent gives the adjudicator an adjudication response under this section, the respondent must give a copy of the response to the claimant not more than 2 business days after giving the response to the adjudicator.

Timing (s83): standard vs complex deadlines

For a standard payment claim, the respondent must lodge the response by the later of 10 business days after receiving the s79(4) application papers and 7 business days after receiving notice of the adjudicator’s acceptance. For a complex claim, those windows extend to 15 business days from the application papers and 12 business days from the notice of acceptance.

For complex claims only, the respondent can apply to the adjudicator for up to 15 extra business days. The extension request itself has to be in writing, has to set out the reasons, and must be made within the later of 5 business days from the application papers or 2 business days from the notice of acceptance. Miss those windows and the extension is unavailable. Once the response is filed, the respondent has 2 business days to serve a copy on the claimant.

“Receiving” the application

Each of the s83 windows runs from the date the respondent “received” either the application or the notice of acceptance. The Act draws a deliberate distinction between “service” and “receipt”: the further commentary on s102 deals with what counts as receipt.

The point matters because miscalculating receipt can cost the respondent its entire response. In CMF Projects Pty Ltd v Masic Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 209, an adjudicator’s notice of acceptance was sent not to the respondent’s registered office but to a post office box. Daubney J held the adjudicator had erred by treating delivery to the post office box as receipt of the notice. In Rise Constructions v El-Hajj [2019] VSC 818, Digby J refused to set aside a determination for jurisdictional error or want of procedural fairness where the adjudicator had decided the notice of acceptance was served on a particular date and, on that basis, declined to consider an out-of-time response (the response was treated as filed outside the s21(1)(b) Vic Act window equivalent to s83(1)(b) of the BIF Act).

Practice tips

  • Treat your s69 payment schedule as the script for the adjudication response. If a reason is not in the schedule, s82(4) will lock you out of running it before the adjudicator — and on the authorities (Acciona Agua, Total Lifestyle Windows) any determination that does consider the new reason is exposed to being set aside.
  • Use the response to amplify, evidence and develop reasons already in the schedule (TQM v Dasein, Trysams) — but don’t mistake ‘more detail’ for ‘new ground’. If the supporting documents (expert reports, spreadsheets, calculations) exist, attach them to the schedule rather than holding them back for the response.
  • Diary the s83 deadline as soon as the s79(4) papers and notice of acceptance arrive. Calculate both the ‘papers’ clock and the ‘notice of acceptance’ clock and use the later date — do not assume the longer one applies without checking.
  • If the claim is complex and you need more time, lodge the s83(3) extension request inside the very tight 5/2 business day window. Miss it and you cannot get more than the standard 15/12 days; the request must be in writing and must give reasons.
  • Watch the ‘receipt’ trap. The clock runs from receipt, not service (CMF Projects v Masic, Rise Constructions v El-Hajj). Confirm where the application and notice of acceptance were actually delivered and when they were actually received before counting business days.
  • Do not forget the 2 business day copy-to-claimant obligation in s83(6); and remember that if you failed to serve a payment schedule under s76, s82(2) means you cannot file a response at all.
