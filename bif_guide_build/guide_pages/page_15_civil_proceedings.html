Chapter 3 · Sections 99–102

Civil proceedings & service

Sections 99 to 102 deal with the back end of the security-of-payment process: the warning notice that has to be given before suing for an unpaid claim, the limited grounds on which a respondent can defend recovery proceedings, the way Part 3 sits alongside the parties’ ordinary contractual rights, and how notices and documents are validly served. For a project manager, these are the rules that decide whether you can convert a payment claim or adjudicated amount into money in the bank, and whether the “pay now, argue later” outcome is final or just an interim allocation of cash flow.

Section 99 – Notice required before starting particular proceedings

Section 99 is the “second-chance” provision. Before a claimant can sue in court to recover an unpaid payment claim, it must give the respondent a warning notice in the approved form. The respondent then has a short window in which it can still pay before proceedings start.

Section 99 BIF Act – Notice required before starting particular proceedings
99 Notice requires before starting particular proceedings

(1)  This section applies if—

(a)  after being given a payment claim, the respondent fails to pay the amount stated in the claim on or before the due date for the progress payment to which the claim relates; and

(b)  because of the failure to pay, the claimant intends to start proceedings in a court to recover the unpaid portion of the amount owed to the claimant.

Note—

See section 78 for the claimants right to recover from a respondent an amount owed to the claimant.

(2)  Before taking the intended action, the claimant must give the respondent written notice (a warning notice), in the approved form, of the claimant’s intention to start the proceedings.

(3)  The claimant must not give the respondent the warning notice later than 20 business days after the due date for the progress payment.

(4)  The claimant must not take the intended action before the end of 5 business days after giving the respondent the warning notice.

(5)  The giving of a warning notice does not—

(a)  require the claimant to complete the action stated in the notice; or

(b)  prevent the claimant from taking different action to that stated in the notice.

Section 99(2) – the warning notice

The warning notice is more than a polite reminder — it is a jurisdictional precondition to the court action it foreshadows. The Victorian Supreme Court considered an analogous provision in Vinson v Neerim Properties Developments Pty Ltd [2016] VSC 321, where Vickery J held that an email which merely “reserved” the claimant’s rights under the Act did not satisfy the statutory notice requirement:

Neerim made its purported Adjudication Application under s 18(1)(b) of the Act. However, s 18(2) of the Act provides a bar to the making of an adjudication application to which sub-section 1(b) applies unless both of the mandatory requirements of paragraphs 18(2)(a) have been satisfied.

As to the requirements under s 18(2)(a) of the Act, Neerim’s email of 9 February 2016, whether or not it was provided to Ms Vinson within the time prescribed, manifestly failed to notify her of Neerim’s intention to apply for adjudication of the Payment Claim, as is required. The notice, such that it was, merely reserved the exercise of the company’s rights under the Act. This is insufficient, for the purposes of the Act, to amount to a notice that Neerim intended to apply for adjudication of its Payment Claim.

The object and purpose of a s 18(2)(a) notice is to enable the respondent to the payment claim to be given an opportunity under s 18(2)(b) to provide a payment schedule to the claimant within 2 business days after receiving the claimant’s notice provided under s 18(2)(a). This opportunity was denied to Ms Vinson by Neerim simply stating that it ‘reserved’ its rights.

For these reasons, Neerim failed to comply with an essential and obligatory requirement of s 18(2) of the Act,[6] and the purported notice was invalid for this purpose.

The warning notice also operates as the trigger for any “second-chance” payment schedule the respondent might want to put on. In Forte Sydney Construction v Lin Betty Building Group [2018] NSWSC 1429, McDougall J held that a payment schedule served out of time under section 14 of the NSW Act, but before the section 17(2)(a) notice (the equivalent of section 20A BCIPA, and section 99 of the Act), is not a valid response to the section 17(2)(a) notice — in other words, the second-chance payment schedule must be served after the warning notice is received. In that case:

  • Forte Sydney provided its payment schedule outside the required 10 business day timeframe and as such, Lin Betty was required to provide Forte Sydney a further opportunity to provide a valid payment schedule, under section 17(2)(a) of the NSW Act, prior to applying for adjudication of the payment claim.
  • Forte Sydney did not respond to the section 17(2)(a) notice and, at adjudication, the adjudicator awarded Lin Betty the majority of its claim.

Counsel for Forte Sydney argued that its earlier (out-of-time) payment schedule should be treated as the response to the section 17(2)(a) notice. McDougall J rejected that submission, focusing on the wording in section 17(2)(b) of the NSW Act that the payment schedule must be provided “within 5 business days after receiving the claimant’s notice”. Because Forte Sydney’s purported payment schedule was issued before the notice was received, it could not have been provided “within 5 business days after receiving the claimant’s notice”.

Section 99(5) – giving a warning notice and election

Section 99(5) preserves the claimant’s freedom of action: giving a warning notice does not lock the claimant into court proceedings, and does not stop it from taking some different action instead. The election point was considered under the NSW analogue in Rojo Building Pty Ltd v Jillcris Pty Ltd [2007] NSWSC 880, where McDougall J held that simply serving a notice of intention to apply for adjudication did not amount to an election:

For present purposes, I am prepared to assume that s 15(2)(a) does provide for inconsistent alternatives, and that it does raise a question of election. That I think would be an election between remedies, not an election between rights. The right is to be paid the progress payment, or the statutory liability created by s 14(4). Section 15(2)(a) provides alternative remedies whereby that right can be enforced.

But, assuming that s 15(2)(a) does entail the notion of election, it is an election between bringing proceedings in a court and making an adjudication application. As I have said, a notice of intention to apply for adjudication does not amount to making an adjudication application. It is a procedural, although necessary, precondition to the making of such an application.

In those circumstances, I do not accept that the mere giving of a notice under s 17(2)(a) is of itself sufficient to constitute, or to trigger, the making of any election for which s 15(2)(a) provides.

…

The question in those cases arose in the context of election at law between alternative and inconsistent rights. For the reasons that I have given, I think that what is involved here is at most a question of election between alternative and inconsistent statutory remedies. The application by analogy of legal doctrines relating to election between inconsistent rights to statutory election between inconsistent remedies requires careful consideration. Specifically, it requires consideration of the extent to which the common law doctrine can sit with the statutory scheme. Since I have concluded that there was no election, it is unnecessary to pursue that topic further.

See also Rojo Building Pty Ltd v Jillcris Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 309. The result in Rojo sits comfortably with section 99(5), which expressly says that giving a warning notice neither commits the claimant to the action stated in the notice nor stops it from taking different action.

Section 100 – Proceedings to recover unpaid amount as debt

Where a claimant sues under section 78(2)(a) to recover an unpaid claim as a debt, section 100 turns the proceeding into a near-summary recovery: the respondent cannot run a counterclaim and cannot raise any defence in relation to matters arising under the construction contract.

Section 100 BIF Act – Proceedings to recover unpaid amount as debt
100 Proceedings to recover unpaid amount as debt

(1)  This section applies if a claimant starts proceedings in a court under section 78(2)(a) to recover an unpaid amount from a respondent as a debt owing to the claimant.

(2)  Judgment in favour of the claimant is not to be given by a court unless the court is satisfied that—

(a)  the respondent did not pay the amount to the claimant on or before the due date for the progress payment to which the payment claim relates; and

(b)  if the respondent’s liability to pay the amount arises because of a failure to give a payment schedule—the respondent did not give the claimant a payment schedule within the time required to do so under this Act.

(3)  The respondent is not, in those proceedings, entitled—

(a)  to bring any counterclaim against the claimant; or

(b)  to raise any defence in relation to matters arising under the construction contract.

Section 100(3) – the scope of the prohibition

The leading Queensland authority is Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Traspunt No 5 Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 119; [2011] 2 Qd R 114, where the Court of Appeal considered the equivalent BCIPA provision (then section 19(4), later 19(6)). Muir JA emphasised that the prohibition is targeted, not universal:

[i]t is essential, in my respectful opinion, to have regard to the words of s 19(4) in determining whether or not it prevents a defence being raised. It prohibits a respondent to a payment claim, who has failed to duly serve a payment schedule, from bringing a counterclaim against the claimant, or from raising “any defence in relation to matters arising under the construction contract”. The prohibition is not against the raising of any defence whatsoever.

…

In my view, s 19(4)(b)(ii) prohibits the raising of a defence only if it can fairly be described as one which relates to matters arising under the relevant construction contract. Counsel for Neumann argue, partly in reliance on observations in Brookhollow, that having regard to the width of the expression “in relation to”, there is a sufficient connection between Traspunt’s defence and matters arising under the contract. To my mind the connection is tenuous. I do not consider that a defence that the payment claim relied on by the claimant is virtually identical in all relevant respects to a previous one made by the claimant meets the description in s 19(4)(b)(ii), at least, where, as is the case here, the merits of the defence stand to be determined essentially by the comparison of one document with another.

Had the provision been intended to catch all defences having a connection with the construction contract, however remote, all words in s 19(4)(b)(ii) after “to raise any defence” would have been otiose: all payment claims are necessarily concerned with “matters arising under the construction contract”. The aim of the provision would appear to be to prevent respondents to claims relying on allegations that the moneys claimed are not owing for reasons referable to the terms of the construction contract and/or to the parties’ performance or failure to perform thereunder. If such matters are to be relied on as a defence to a payment claim, they must be included in a payment schedule. This approach to the construction of s 19(4)(b)(ii) is consistent with that taken by Basten JA in Bitannia Pty Ltd v Parkline Constructions Pty Ltd.

Applying that targeted reading, the courts have allowed certain non-contractual arguments to survive section 100(3). In Aalborg CSP A/S v Ottoway Engineering Pty Ltd [2017] SASFC 158, the Full Court of South Australia held that the equivalent provision did not preclude a respondent from running misleading or deceptive conduct or estoppel arguments to challenge whether a payment claim had been appropriately served — on the facts, Ottoway had served 22 successive payment claims by email and hard copy to Aalborg’s head office in Denmark, but its 23rd was served only by hard copy to the registered office in Australia.

By contrast, in Somerset Civil Pty Ltd v Eaglerise Developments Pty Ltd; Eaglerise Developments Pty Ltd v Somerset Civil Pty Ltd [2021] QDC 070, the Queensland District Court held that a respondent to a section 100 summary judgment proceeding cannot commence its own proceedings on the same issues and rely on contractual matters. The respondent had tried to start a separate proceeding alleging that the work was unlicensed and then asked to consolidate. The court refused, on the basis that joining the proceedings would defeat the purpose of section 100(3), which expressly bars a counterclaim or contract-based defence in the recovery proceeding.

The reach of section 100 was further confirmed in EnerMech Pty Ltd v Acciona Infrastructure Projects Australia Pty Ltd [2024] NSWCA 162, where Basten AJA (Meagher JA and Griffiths AJA agreeing) held at [79] that, in proceedings to recover an adjudicated amount as a judgment debt, the respondent cannot challenge an adjudicator’s interpretation of the contract (even if legally erroneous) or any “error of law on the face of the record”.

In 1559 High Street Pty Ltd v Camillo Builders Pty Ltd, Stynes J held that section 28R of the Building and Construction Industry Security of Payment Act 2002 (Vic) (equivalent to section 100(3) BIFSOPA), which limits the ability to raise defences and cross-claims in enforcement proceedings, does not create a constitutional inconsistency with the Australian Consumer Law (ACL) under s 109 of the Constitution. Her Honour found that the SOP Act establishes a distinct and interim statutory entitlement to progress payments that operates without prejudice to the parties’ final legal rights. Her Honour applied Birdon Pty Ltd v Houben Marine Pty Ltd (2011) 197 FCR 25 (which confirmed that SOP schemes provide administrative mechanisms for provisional payments that do not interfere with federal consumer protection rights), and distinguished Bitannia Pty Ltd v Parkline Constructions Pty Ltd [2006] NSWCA 238 on the basis that the alleged misleading conduct in 1559 High Street concerned final legal entitlements, not the SOP payment process itself.

Section 42 of the QBCC Act

Section 42(3) of the QBCC Act provides that a person who carries out, or undertakes to carry out, building work without holding the appropriate class of contractor’s licence is not entitled to monetary or other consideration for that work, subject to the limited recovery permitted by section 42(4).

In Cant Contracting Pty Ltd v Casella [2006] QCA 538; [2007] 2 Qd R 13, the Court of Appeal held that the (then) section 19(6)(b)(ii) does not prevent a respondent from relying on section 42(3) of the QBCC Act. Williams JA explained:

Because s 42(3) of the Building Act provides that an unlicensed contractor “is not entitled to any monetary or other consideration” for doing work pursuant to the contract, such a contractor cannot be said to have an entitlement to progress payments pursuant to ss 7, 12 and 17 of the Payment Act.

Where section 42(1) is breached the contract cannot simply be severed — the whole agreement is affected: see Dart Holdings Pty Ltd v Total Concept Group Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 158 at [39] where McMurdo J said:

…Where s 42(1) is engaged, it is not simply the promise by which the builder undertakes to carry out unlicensed work which is affected. Rather it is the contract by which that promise is made. As McPherson JA said in Sutton, in such a case, s 42(1) “prohibits the very process of formation of a contract”. The consequence of this statutory prohibition is that a court will not enforce the contract, at least in favour of the party which contravened the prohibition.

Examples:

  • In JAG Projects Qld Pty Ltd v Total Cool Pty Ltd & Anor [2015] QSC 229, the respondent argued that section 42(3) restricted recovery because the claimant was not licensed when the payment claim was made, even though it had held the relevant licence when the work was performed. The Court rejected that submission.
  • In St Hilliers Property Pty Ltd v Pronto Solar Innovations Pty Ltd [2018] QSC 164, Daubney J, having determined that the respondent had carried out building work without a licence, confirmed the position in Cant Contracting that an unlicensed contractor is not entitled to progress payments pursuant to ss 7, 12 and 17 of BCIPA (Qld).
  • Walton Construction (Qld) Pty Ltd v Plumber by Trade Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 264. An adjudicator made a decision in favour of a claimant for plumbing and drainage services which was ‘building work’ under the then QBSA Act. The claimant was a partnership of two companies as trustees of two respective family trusts (at [47]). Neither company was licensed under the then QBSA Act. Following Cant Contracting Pty Ltd v Casella [2007] 2 Qd R 13, the Court held both the payment claim and the adjudication decision to be void.

Section 55 of the DBCA

Similar reasoning was applied to section 55 of the (now repealed) Domestic Building Contracts Act 2000 (Qld) in Gemini Nominees Pty Ltd v Queensland Property Partners Pty Ltd [2008] 1 Qd R 139; [2007] QSC 20, where Mullins J said:

It is difficult to reconcile how the scheme under the BCIPA which seeks to facilitate progress payments under a construction contract could apply to facilitate a progress payment in respect of an amount that may be awarded under s 55(4) of the DBCA where the domestic building contract has been made unenforceable pursuant to s 55(3) of the DBCA. As a matter of construction of the BCIPA, the BCIPA does not apply to the plaintiff’s claim under the contract, as the plaintiff is prohibited by s 55(3) from enforcing the contract and therefore cannot claim to be entitled to a progress payment under the contract. This conclusion is supported by the reasoning of the Court of Appeal in Cant and the dicta of Hodgson JA in Brodyn at paragraph[82]. The construction of s 55 of the DBCA shows that it is analogous to s 94 of the Home Building Act.

Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth)

See Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Traspunt No 5 Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 119; [2011] 2 Qd R 114 where the Court of Appeal held that the circumstances gave rise to an arguable case that the claimant had acted in breach of section 52 of the former Trade Practices Act 1974 (Cth) by changing the basis of its claims without alerting the respondent.

Professional Engineers Act 2002 (Qld); Electrical Safety Act 2002 (Qld)

In Agripower Australia Ltd v Queensland Engineering & Electrical Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 268, Douglas J followed Cant Contracting Pty Ltd v Casella [2007] 2 Qd R 13; [2006] QCA 538 in the context of the Professional Engineers Act 2002 (Qld) and the Electrical Safety Act 2002 (Qld). His Honour found that the claimant had breached section 56 of the Electrical Safety Act 2002 (Qld) by not holding the appropriate licence and section 115(1) of the Professional Engineers Act 2002 (Qld) by carrying out professional engineering services without its agents being practising professional engineers. The contract was held to be illegal and unenforceable, and the adjudicator’s decision was set aside.

Not the party liable ‘under the construction contract’

A defence that a party is not the party liable to a payment claim ‘under the construction contract’ is not precluded by the operation of section 19(6): see Grave v Blazevic Holdings Pty Ltd (2010) 79 NSWLR 132; [2010] NSWCA 324, [36] (McDougall J, Allsop P and Macfarlan JA agreeing). See also Mansouri & Anor v Aquamist Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 209, where the Court of Appeal set aside summary judgment on the ground that there was an insufficiently high degree of certainty that the applicants ‘were or might be liable’ under a construction contract.

Section 101 – Effect of Pt 3 on civil proceedings

Section 101 makes the central design of the BIF Act explicit: Part 3 sits alongside the parties’ contractual rights, it does not replace them. An adjudicator’s decision is interim, and a court can later make orders for restitution or otherwise to bring the position into line with the parties’ final legal entitlements. Section 101(4) also gives the court a power to sever the part of an adjudicator’s decision affected by jurisdictional error.

Section 101 BIF Act – Effect of pt. 3 on civil proceedings
101 Effect of pt. 3 on civil proceedings

(1)  Subject to section 200, nothing in this chapter affects any right that a party to a construction contract—

(a)  may have under the contract; or

(b)  may have under part 2 in relation to the contract; or

(c)  may have apart from this chapter in relation to anything done or omitted to be done under the contract.

(2)  Nothing done under or for this chapter affects any civil proceedings arising under a construction contract, whether under this chapter or otherwise, except as provided by subsection (3).

(3)  In any proceedings before a court or tribunal in relation to any matter arising under a construction contract, the court or tribunal—

(a)  must allow for any amount paid to a party to the contract under or for this chapter in any order or award it makes in those proceedings; and

(b)  may make the orders it considers appropriate for the restitution of any amount so paid, and any other orders it considers appropriate, having regard to its decision in the proceedings.

(4)  If, in any proceedings before a court in relation to any matter arising under a construction contract, the court finds that only a part of an adjudicator’s decision under this chapter is affected by jurisdictional error, the court may—

(a)  identify the part affected by the error; and

(b)  allow the part of the decision not affected by the error to remain binding on the parties to the proceeding.

An adjudication is not a final determination

Section 101 makes plain that Part 3 does not affect the parties’ rights under the construction contract (section 101(1)(a)). In Abel Point Marina (Whitsundays) Pty Ltd v Uher & Anor [2006] QSC 295, Wilson J explained the consequence:

The Act provides for progress payments to contractors whether or not the relevant contract makes provision for progress payments and establishes a procedure for the making and recovery of such claims and their speedy adjudication where they are disputed. However, an adjudication does not finally determine the rights of the parties in the sense that a party may “claw back” progress payments which it is forced to make through the adjudication process in subsequent civil proceedings.

A party is not limited to the conclusion of the contract before it can claw back: see Gambaro Pty Ltd v Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd [2015] QSC 170, discussed below.

In Beno Excavations Pty Ltd v Harlech Enterprises Pty Ltd (No 4) [2022] ACTSC 275, the Court considered whether section 38(3)(b) of the ACT SOP Act (the equivalent of section 101(3)(b) BIFSOPA) precluded a restitution claim for an adjudicated amount paid where the adjudication decision had subsequently been overturned. In rejecting that argument and ordering payment, the Court said:

The difficulty I have however is that there are no final proceedings awaiting resolution. The only proceedings in this matter are constituted by the adjudication, which has now been set aside, and the decisions of Mossop J and the Court of Appeal respectively, both of which have been completed…It is possible that there may be future proceedings, but that is speculative and I do not think any such proceedings, whatever they may be and by whom they may be brought, can be influential upon a restitution claim presently before the Court…

In CBEM Holdings Pty Ltd v Sunshine East Pty Ltd [2025] NSWCA 250, Adamson JA held that s 32 of the NSW Act, (equivalent to s 101 of the Act) preserves a Respondent’s ability to later challenge contractual entitlement even after paying an adjudicated amount. Her Honour emphasised that payments made under the SOP Act are “prima facie… provisional” and adopted Dixon J’s statement in York Air Conditioning v The Commonwealth that such payments do not finally determine the ultimate liability between the parties. Section 32(3) acts as the statutory vehicle that obliges a court to allow for prior Act payments and permits orders for restitution of any excess once contractual entitlement is judicially determined. Her Honour confirmed that the Act creates no finality: paying a claim does not prevent recovery via the common money count.

Dual contractual and statutory regimes

Section 101 creates a dual system: the statutory progress-payment regime sits alongside, but does not displace, the contractual one: see Falgat Constructions Pty Ltd v Equity Australia Corporation Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 49. As Daubney J observed in Eco Steel Homes Pty Ltd v Hippo’s Concreting Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 135:

The contractual rights and statutory rights conferred by BCIPA co-exist in a dual system which is expressly contemplated by the legislation. As has been said: “The framework of the Act is to create a statutory system alongside any contractual regime.”

The same point was made under the NSW Act in Paynter Dickson Constructions Pty Ltd v JF & CG Tilson Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 869, where Bergin J said (at [26]):

The claims, responses and adjudication process under the Act do not affect any rights that a party to a construction contract may have under the contract and do not affect any civil proceedings arising under such a contract; except that a court or tribunal must allow for any amount paid pursuant to a claim or an adjudication determination under the Act and may make orders for restitution of any amount so paid or any other orders considered to be appropriate (s. 32).

The right to use the statutory process and the right to pursue curial remedies are not inconsistent: Eco Steel Homes Pty Ltd v Hippo’s Concreting Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 135, [14] (Daubney J). Restitution may be sought of amounts paid under the Act while the contract is still on foot: Gambaro Pty Ltd v Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd [2015] QSC 170. It has been accepted in principle that a court might order restitution of an adjudicated amount on the basis of a final determination that the adjudicated amount exceeds a party’s contractual entitlements: Gambaro Pty Ltd as Trustee for the Gambaro Holdings Trust v Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd; Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd v Gambaro Pty Ltd [2015] QCA 288, [29] (Fraser JA, Morrison JA and Boddice J agreeing).

In Gambaro Pty Ltd v Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd [2015] QSC 170, the respondent had paid an adjudicated amount and then sought to litigate the contractual entitlement. The claimant argued that, under the dual regime, contractual rights inconsistent with the Act were “suspended” and could only be reconciled at the end of the project. Atkinson J rejected that as a gloss not supported by the words of the Act:

Having complied with the requirements of that regime by payment of the adjudicated amount under BCIPA, Gambaro wishes to exercise its right to litigate in civil proceedings the entitlements of the parties under the contract. Under s 100(2) of BCIPA, the payment of an adjudicated amount does not affect the right of a party to take civil proceedings to enforce rights and obligations arising out of a construction contract. The enforcement of contractual rights by civil litigation may give rise to a requirement on one party under s 100(3)(a) to make an additional payment to that paid under Part 3, or it may give rise to a requirement under s 100(3)(b) for the other party to repay some or all of the payments made under Part 3. There is no other express limitation upon the rights recognised by s 100, nor is there any justification for implying any limitation on the rights so recognised.

There is no reason why a purposive rather than a literal reading of s 100 would lead to any different result. The purpose of Part 3 is to provide a quick method for the amount of disputed payment claim being determined and then paid. This ensures cash-flow to the builder. However, it is not intended to exclude the parties’ rights to litigate in a Court to determine their rights inter se, so long as amounts paid under Part 3 are taken into account. Nothing in s 100 or in the objects of BCIPA mandates that this may only happen on completion of the construction contract. The defendant’s argument as to striking out the whole of the plaintiff’s Claim and Statement of Claim must fail.

The decision was upheld on appeal in Gambaro Pty Ltd as Trustee for the Gambaro Holdings Trust v Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd; Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd v Gambaro Pty Ltd [2015] QCA 288. The Court of Appeal rejected the argument that it would be unjust to allow the claimant to retain so much of the adjudicated amount as exceeded the superintendent’s progress certificates, and rejected the submission that a claimant’s right to recover a progress payment is ‘exhausted’ on payment of the adjudicated amount: at [39] (Fraser JA, Morrison JA and Boddice J agreeing).

In Gambaro Holdings Trust v Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd [2018] QSC 149, Holmes CJ confirmed (consistently with John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads & Traffic Authority (NSW)) that a party cannot rely on section 100 BCIPA to create a freestanding restitutionary right.

In Vannella Pty Ltd Atf Capitalist Family Trust v TFM Epping Land Pty Ltd and Katoomba Residence Investment Pty Ltd; Decon Australia Pty Ltd v TFM Epping Land Pty Ltd and Katoomba Residence Investment Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 659, Stevenson J refused to stay a judgment to pay an adjudicated amount despite a pending appeal and the prospect that the developers would be pushed into liquidation. His Honour reasoned that the scheme of the NSW Act is to transfer the risk of a contractor’s insolvency to the developer, and it would be inconsistent with that scheme to allow the developers to use their own potentially insolvent position to defer payment.

The appeal in TFM Epping Land Pty Ltd v Decon Australia Pty Ltd [2020] NSWCA 118 was dismissed. The Court of Appeal held that section 32 (the NSW equivalent of section 101) “speaks only” to the legal rights of the parties and not to the practical effect of those rights, so the question of solvency is “not relevant” to its operation. A countervailing cross-claim did not entitle the judgment debtor to avoid paying, given that “the basal purpose of the Act is to create a speedy determination pro tem to ensure that progress claims are dealt with and paid promptly”. Grosvenor Constructions (NSW) Pty Ltd (in administration) v Musico [2004] NSWSC 344 was distinguished; the relevant exception remained where there would be “a likelihood of irreparable prejudice” and the “practical effect is to make permanent that which the legislature intended to be merely interim”.

Examples:

  • Eco Steel Homes Pty Ltd v Hippo’s Concreting Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 135. The Court rejected a submission that the claimant, by pursuing its claim in the Magistrates Court, had made an ‘irrevocable election’ inconsistent with the statutory procedure under the Act.

In Lindvest DM Pty Ltd v CPDM Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 1290, Stevenson J declared that it was not an abuse of process for a developer to commence section 32 proceedings against its judgment creditor, despite an unsatisfied adjudication judgment against the developer. The contractor had obtained an adjudication determination and District Court judgment; the developer commenced separate proceedings seeking a declaration that the contractor was not entitled to any payment. The contractor argued abuse of process, but Stevenson J held that section 32 expressly reserves that right to the developer.

In McDonald v MAK Constructions and Building Services Pty Ltd [2024] NSWCA 63, the Court of Appeal granted leave and held that the primary judge had erred in failing to find that the owner’s right to bring a claim for damages is expressly preserved by s 32(2) of the NSW Act (the Queensland equivalent of section 101(2) BIFSOPA). Agreeing with Ball J in Samadi Developments Pty Ltd v SX Projects Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 1576 at [6], Griffiths AJA (Payne and Adamson JJA agreeing) held that the policy of the SOP Act (NSW) “is to provide quick and efficient means by which contractors are entitled to recover progress payment, without interfering with the parties’ rights in relation to the relevant construction contract”. The Court concluded that s 32(3) endorses a right to bring a claim at common law only after the judgment debt is satisfied.

In Harlech Enterprises Pty Ltd v Beno Excavations Pty Ltd [2025] NSWCA 5, the New South Wales Court of Appeal (Bell CJ, with Ward P and Payne JA agreeing) confirmed that section 38(3)(b) of the NSW Act (equivalent to section 101(3)(b) BIFSOPA) does not prevent a restitution claim where an adjudication determination has been quashed. The Court of Appeal upheld the District Court decision of Montgomery DCJ and clarified that section 38(3)(b) is facilitative, not prohibitive — it allows for restitution where appropriate, particularly where payment has been made on the “pay now, argue later” principle and it is later determined that all or part of that payment was not actually owed.

Election

In Eco Steel Homes Pty Ltd v Hippo’s Concreting Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 135, Daubney J rejected a submission that a party was ‘held to its election’ by instituting proceedings in the Magistrates Court, and so had given up an ‘inconsistent right’ to use the statutory remedies under BCIPA (at [12]–[14]).

Release and waiver

The interaction between section 100 of the Act (the predecessor) and a contractual release and waiver clause was considered in Leighton Contractors v Vision Energy [2010] QSC 353. Under the contract, the claimant was to provide a release and waiver in the contract’s form in exchange for the practical completion payment, subject to its right to qualify the release in writing. The claimant gave notice in writing that ‘it is willing to execute the release excluding all matters the subject of adjudication’. The Court held that section 100 of the Act was subject to the operation of the release and waiver, which was in turn subject to the extent to which the claimant had stated its disagreement.

Section 101(3) – ‘any matter arising under a construction contract’

In Ceerose Pty Ltd v Building Products Australia Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 1886, Beech-Jones J held that a ‘matter arising under a construction contract’ included a claim for restitution of money paid under two judgment debts based on adjudication certificates issued under section 24 of the NSW Act. Similarly, in Gambaro Pty Ltd as Trustee for the Gambaro Holdings Trust v Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd; Rohrig (Qld) Pty Ltd v Gambaro Pty Ltd [2015] QCA 288, the Queensland Court of Appeal confirmed that proceedings for restitution of an adjudicated amount under a construction contract concern a ‘matter arising under a construction contract’.

In Pico Play Pty Ltd v Coast Entertainment Operations Limited [2025] QSC 227, Kelly J held in relation to section 101(4) of the QLD Act that:

the mischief to which the section is directed is the perceived injustice where the identification of jurisdictional error results in the invalidity of an entire decision. … In my consideration, it would be a pedantic and less than beneficial interpretation of s 101(4) to confine its operation to circumstances where the party seeking severance does not contend that a construction contract is unenforceable. Section 101(4) may be availed of by the court of its own volition in the exercise of its supervisory jurisdiction. The words “in relation to any matter arising under a construction contract” should be interpreted beneficially and so as to include a situation, such as here, where one party, albeit it not the party seeking severance, contends for the existence of an enforceable construction contract.

Section 101(4) – severance

Section 101(4), introduced under section 100(4) BCIPA by the Amendment Act, gives the court a power to sever the part of an adjudication decision that is affected by jurisdictional error. Before its introduction, the Queensland position was that there was no mechanism to sever an infected part. The need for legislative reform had been raised by Applegarth J in BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 346:

Consideration might be given to the need for legislative reform in this area. One of the purposes of the legislation is to facilitate the cash flow of contractors. Legislative reforms might permit a severable, invalid part of a decision to be declared invalid. One can imagine a case in which a jurisdictional error is found in respect of a relatively minor part of a claim. Without provision to declare part of the decision invalid, a contractor will be deprived of the benefit of the entire decision and ordinarily be required to repay the entire amount awarded under the decision, not just the small amount that has been successfully challenged. Such an outcome undermines the purposes of the Act.

The provision was considered in Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 168, where Byrne SJA said (at [73]):

The evident purpose of s.100(4) is not difficult to discern. It had been judicially determined that “jurisdictional error which affects one part of a decision will render the whole of [the decision] void”. That rule produced inconvenient consequences. More than one judge urged that the Act be amended to permit so much of an adjudicator’s decision as was not affected by jurisdictional error to stand.

(footnotes omitted)

His Honour also held that section 100(4) BCIPA applies to a proceeding challenging an adjudication decision for jurisdictional error that started before the commencement of the Amendment Act.

Examples:

  • In Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 168, the Court found that an extension of time claim in an adjudication involved the re-agitation of issues determined in an earlier adjudication decision. The adjudicator in the second adjudication had therefore exceeded jurisdiction in allowing the extension of time claim, and the Court used section 100(4) to sever that part of the decision.
  • CKP Constructions Pty Ltd v Evolution Piling Pty Ltd & Ors (Unreported, Supreme Court of Queensland, Dalton J, 24 July 2015). Under a foundation works subcontract, the claimant was entitled to progress payments for the proportion of the subcontract sum representing the part of the works satisfactorily completed at the time of the claim. The claimant made a payment claim covering both construction work (CW1 to CW6) and variations. Claims CW2, 3 and 6 were for materials and preparation work; claims CW1, 4 and 5 were held by Dalton J not to represent the proportion of the work actually completed. Dalton J applied section 100(4) to sever claims CW1 to CW6, and also severed the costs decision, holding that it could not be said that the costs decision was ‘“not affected by the error” to use the words of s 100(4)(b)’ (at 6).
  • In Sierra Property Qld Pty Ltd v National Construction Management Pty Ltd [2016] QSC 108, Jackson J held that part of an adjudication decision was affected by jurisdictional error because the adjudicator had not given reasons. His Honour severed the affected part and ordered that the remainder remain binding under section 100(4).

In Ceerose Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 239, Darke J considered the scope of the discretionary power under s 32A of the NSW SOP Act (the equivalent of s 101(4) BIFSOPA) to sever the part of an adjudication decision affected by jurisdictional error. The Court was required to consider whether that power extended to two adjudication decisions in which the adjudicator had failed to afford procedural fairness (and other grounds of jurisdictional error were also made out).

  • In finding that severance was available, and directing the parties to agree on appropriate orders, the Court explained at [186]:

…the adjudicated amount is in reality the sum of a number of distinct parts only some of which are, according to my conclusions, affected by jurisdictional error. In each case…numerous parts of the Determination, that together reflect substantial sums of money and a significant portion of the adjudicated amount, are not so affected. It seems to me that it may be appropriate to not set aside those parts, and indeed confirm those parts, as envisaged by s 32A(2), whilst setting aside the balance of the Determinations.

In Ceerose Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd (No 2) [2023] NSWSC 401, the NSW Supreme Court considered the correct application of the severance power in s 32A for two adjudication determinations partly affected by jurisdictional error. The respondent argued that the right approach was to set aside only the amount awarded for the affected part. The Court disagreed and held that the correct approach was to set aside the amount in dispute at adjudication. Darke J reasoned that this removed the effect of the adjudicator’s errors in respect of every item affected by jurisdictional error, because the disputed amount represents the ambit of the dispute the adjudicator was called on to determine.

That approach was confirmed by the Court of Appeal in Ceerose Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd [2023] NSWCA 215. The payment claim included a line item for demolition works (the Line Item) in which the amount claimed was for 85% of the works, whereas the respondent had certified only 65%. The adjudicator awarded the full claimed amount, $116,632.40 more than the certified amount. The primary judge held that the adjudicator had fallen into jurisdictional error on the Line Item, and applied the severance power to set aside the $116,632.40 as the “disputed amount” affected by the error. On appeal, the respondent argued that jurisdictional error on the Line Item meant the entire payment claim must be set aside. The Court of Appeal rejected that argument and held that the primary judge had correctly applied the severance power by removing the amount in dispute from the adjudicator’s determination.

In Civmec Electrical & Instrumental Pty Ltd v Southern Cross Electrical Engineering Limited & Ors [2019] QSC 300, the applicant sought a declaration that the adjudication decision was void for jurisdictional error, or alternatively that the court exercise its power under section 101(4). The power was not engaged because the Court upheld the adjudicator’s decision as being within jurisdiction.

In Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2023] QSC 290, Williams J held that s 101(4) of the BIF Act applies where an adjudicator makes a jurisdictional error as part of the process of undertaking the adjudication. Her Honour adopted the reasoning in Ehome Construction Pty Ltd v GCB Construction Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 291, where Bond J commented (at page 7) that:

The proposition was that if there was error, only a part of the adjudicator’s decision was affected by error. But logically, if – and this is a proposition I reject –the inclusion of a claim for something that cannot be the subject of a payment claim did mean that the payment claim could not be validly regarded as a payment claim, then that error would infect the entirety of the adjudicator’s decision and no part of it could be resurrected by operation of s 101(4).

In that case, the finding that the disputed payment claim was invalid meant that there was no payment claim to enliven the adjudicator’s jurisdiction; the entire adjudication decision was infected, and there was no scope for severance.

In Ingeteam Australia Pty Ltd v Susan River Solar Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 30, Applegarth J considered whether to sever the part of an adjudication determination affected by jurisdictional error. Susan River argued that s 101(4) does not apply to decisions under s 84 and only applies to decisions under s 88 where an adjudicator has already decided that he or she has jurisdiction and proceeds to assess the amounts owed. The Court rejected that construction, noting that s 101(4) refers to “an adjudicator’s decision under this chapter”. Having regard to the text and statutory context, his Honour held that there was “no principle why a jurisdictional error made in respect of the existence or non-existence of a jurisdictional fact should not be subject to s 101(4)”.

In Karam Group Pty Ltd ATF The Karam (No. 1) Family Trust v HCA Queensland Pty Ltd & Ors [2024] QSC 34, the Queensland Supreme Court considered whether an adjudication decision should be set aside in full or in part. Bradley J considered how the discretion in s 101(4) should be exercised, holding at [9] that “if only part of the decision is affected by the relevant error, then the Court may consider whether to save the balance”, and finding that there would be utility in allowing the part of the decision dealing with delay costs to remain binding because that “would avoid the parties incurring the costs of a further adjudication process in respect of that claim” (at [52]).

Section 102 – Service of notices

Section 102 sets the rules for serving notices and documents under Chapter 3. The starting point is the contract: if the construction contract provides a method of service, that method works. Section 102(1) sits on top of, but does not displace, section 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 and any other law about the giving of notices, and the Act expressly preserves service through agents.

Section 102 BIF Act – Services of notices
102 Services of notices

(1)  A notice or other document that, under this chapter, is authorised or required to be given to a person may be given to the person in the way, if any, provided under the relevant construction contract.

Example—

A contract may allow for the service of notices by email.

(2)  Subsection (1) is in addition to, and does not limit or exclude, the Acts Interpretation Act 1954, section 39 or the provisions of any other law about the giving of notices.

(3)  To remove any doubt, it is declared that nothing in this Act—

(a)  excludes the proper service of notices or documents by a person’s agent; or

(b)  requires a person’s acknowledgement of a notice or document properly given to the person.

A ‘facultative’ provision

In Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Traspunt No 5 Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 119, Muir JA (Holmes and Chesterman JJA agreeing) considered that section 103 of the Act “appears to be facultative rather than mandatory in nature”.

Time limitations under the Act

Service is the trigger for almost every clock that runs under the Act, and the courts have repeatedly emphasised how harsh the consequences of late service can be. In Emag Constructions Pty Ltd v Highrise Concrete Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 903, Einstein J said:

Plainly enough the whole of the rationale underpinning the procedures laid down by the Act is directed at providing a quick and efficient set of procedures permitting recovery of progress payments and the quick resolution of disputes in that regard. Time limits under the Act are strict. The consequences of not complying with the stipulated time limits can be significant…

…

Service being effected in accordance with the Act is critical as it governs the commencement of the time limitations following such service. The consequence of non-compliance with the time limitation periods is harsh. As was submitted to the court by counsel for the plaintiff, the Act exhibits “zero tolerance” for delay. To borrow a phrase from the world of contract, and in particular conveyancing, in a real sense time is of the essence.

Service under the Acts Interpretation Act 1954

In Penfold Projects Pty Ltd v Securcorp Limited [2011] QDC 77, Irwin DCJ held that email was a ‘similar facility’ within the meaning of section 39(1) of the Act. That view was doubted in Conveyor & General Engineering Pty Ltd v Basetec Services Pty Ltd & Anor [2014] QSC 30.

Service on a corporation

Section 109X of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) provides that a document may be served on a company by:

  • leaving it at, or posting it to, the company’s registered office (section 109X(1)(a)); or
  • delivering a copy of the document personally to a director of the company who resides in Australia or in an external Territory (section 109X(1)(b)).

Section 109X also makes further provision for service where a liquidator or administrator has been appointed. The provision is facultative and is not an exclusive code: Howship Holdings Pty Ltd v Leslie (No 2) (1996) 41 NSWLR 542. Section 109X(6)(a) of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) confirms that section 109X does not affect any other provision of the Corporations Act, or any provision of another law, that allows a document to be served in a different way.

Service on a post office box

Service on a post office box is not valid service under either section 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) or section 109X of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth): CMF Projects Pty Ltd v Masic Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 209, [23] (Daubney J). Accordingly, unless service to a post office box is provided for under the construction contract, service of a notice to a post office box will not on its own amount to valid service: CMF Projects Pty Ltd v Masic Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 209, [16], [23] (Daubney J).

Service by email

Section 24(1) of the Electronic Transactions (Queensland) Act 2001 (Qld) provides that, unless otherwise agreed, the time of receipt of an electronic communication is:

  • ‘the time the electronic communication becomes capable of being retrieved by the addressee at an electronic address designated by the addressee’ (section 24(1)(a)); or
  • at another electronic address of the addressee, at the time when both ‘the electronic communication has become capable of being retrieved by the addressee at that address’ and ‘the addressee has become aware that the electronic communication has been sent to that address’ (section 24(1)(b)).

The phrase ‘capable of being retrieved’ was considered, in the context of service of an adjudication response under the NSW Act, in Bauen Constructions Pty Ltd v Sky General Services Pty Ltd [2012] NSWSC 1123. Sackar J held (at [77]):

The words “capable of being retrieved” are ample in their reach. They certainly do not require an email to be opened, let alone read. Again the Oxford dictionary defines “retrieve” in its primary sense as “to get or bring back from somewhere”. In its secondary sense it is said to mean “to find or extract (information stored in a computer)”.

A document will be ‘capable of being retrieved’ even if it has been caught by the spam filter on the recipient’s system: Bauen Constructions Pty Ltd v Sky General Services Pty Ltd [2012] NSWSC 1123, [77] (Sackar J).

In MN Builders Pty Ltd v MMM Cement Rendering Pty Ltd [2019] NSWDC 734, Abadee DCJ held that once it is known that a payment claim was actually received by the respondent, compliance with section 31 of the NSW Act (the equivalent of section 102 of the Act) regarding service drops out of the picture. The subcontractor had given its claim to the builder’s foreman, who was not a person authorised to be served under section 31; the subcontract required claims to be emailed to the builder’s accounts team. The builder denied liability on that basis. The District Court held that, because the claim was actually received by the builder, it had been validly served, and granted summary judgment for the subcontractor.

In Pelligra Build Pty Ltd v Australian Crane & Machinery Pty Ltd [2020] VCC 545, Archer J held that service of a payment claim by email was valid even though service of “notices” by email was expressly prohibited by the contract. The payment claim had come to the respondent’s attention. Following Vickery J in Amasya Enterprises Pty Ltd v Asta Developments (Aust) Pty Ltd (No 2) [2015] VSC 500, Archer J reasoned that there is no mandatory requirement in section 14 of the SOP Act (Vic) (equivalent to section 75 BIFSOPA) for payment claims to be served exclusively by the methods set out in section 50 (equivalent to section 102 BIFSOPA). A standardised contractual clause cannot frustrate a claimant’s statutory right to serve a payment claim.

In Spirito Development Pty Ltd v Sinjen Group Pty Ltd [2020] VCC 1368, Woodward J declared a payment claim invalid because it had been served before the relevant reference date: the email was sent at 10:17 pm on a Friday, with the next reference date due to accrue on the following Monday, and the contract said nothing about service after hours. His Honour observed that section 50 of the Vic Act (equivalent to section 102 BIFSOPA) “says nothing about service by emails”, while it says that a facsimile received after 4.00 pm on any day “must be taken to have been received on the next business day”. Applying MKA Bowen v Carelli Constructions [2019] VSC 436, his Honour held that “if a reference date is not available on the date that a payment claim is taken to have been served, the payment claim is invalid regardless of when it was received”.

In Piety Constructions Pty Ltd v Hville FCP Pty Ltd [2022] NSWSC 1318, Stevenson J held that an electronically served document is taken to have been “provided” for the purposes of s 14 of the NSW SOP Act when it is accessed and viewed. The contract contained a clause deeming any document delivered after 4:30 pm on a business day to be given at 9:30 am on the next business day. Stevenson J held that, to the extent the clause purported to amend the “provided” requirement, it was invalid. Accordingly, an electronically delivered document required to be provided under the security-of-payment regime will arguably be “provided” on the day it is accessed and viewed irrespective of any deeming clause.

In Fredon Infrastructure Pty Ltd v Hitachi Rail GTS Australia Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 1244, Stevenson J considered when two payment claims delivered electronically had been served. The two claims arose because the parties were dealing under two contracts on the same project. Fredon delivered the payment claims through Aconex on 21 February 2024. Hitachi did not issue payment schedules until 7 March 2024 — outside the 10 business day window if service was on 21 February. Hitachi argued it did not become aware of the claims until 22 February.

Stevenson J was satisfied as a matter of fact that Hitachi had “become aware” of the Aconex messages on 21 February 2024 because:

  • the Hitachi representatives whom the Aconex messages were delivered to were present at work at the time the 21 February 2024 Payment Claims were served;
  • one of the Hitachi representatives whom the Aconex messages were delivered left a voice message at 8:33am on 22 February 2024 suggesting receipt occurred on the previous day; and
  • one of the Hitachi representatives whom the Aconex messages were delivered managed a register which recorded when payment claims were received, but failed to depose as to the contents of that register under cross examination.

Although that finding of fact disposed of the issue, his Honour also applied section 13A(1) of the Electronic Transactions Act 2000 (NSW). Because the Aconex correspondence had been sent to an electronic address other than one designated under the contract, section 13A(1) of the ETA was engaged, and the “time of receipt” was the time Fredon became aware of the communications and when the communication had reached the electronic address.

In Claire Rewais and Osama Rewais t/as McVitty Grove v BPB Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 1271, McGrath J restated the principles on service under the NSW Act. The onus is on the claimant, who must show either:

  • the payment claim was served in accordance with the facultative provisions in s 31 of the NSW Act (in which case the time of service will be the time at which the s 31 requirements were satisfied); or
  • if they were not served in accordance with s 31, that they were in fact brought to the respondent’s attention and the time of service will be the time at which the respondent became aware of the payment claim

On the facts, McGrath J held that the claimant failed to prove the payment claim had been served in accordance with s 31(1)(d) of the NSW Act. That section requires the claimant to prove that the respondent ‘specified’ its email address for the purpose of ‘service of documents of that kind’ — that is, documents required to be served under the Act. The respondent had only provided his email address for the purpose of receiving a quote or indication of costs, which was not a document required to be served under the Act. The claimant also failed to prove the payment claim was brought to the respondent’s attention. At [143] his Honour held:

I consider that BPB has also failed to prove that the payment claim was brought to the Rewaises’ attention prior to 11 June 2024. While the evidence shows that Dr Rewais would, from time-to-time, send emails from and receive emails to his account, there is no evidence of any email communications from Dr Rewais to BPB between 24 April 2024 and 11 June 2024, no evidence that BPB notified the Rewaises over text message or in person of the payment claim that had been emailed, no evidence that he opened the 24 April emails, no evidence that he was aware the 24 April emails were in his inbox and no evidence that Dr Rewais read the 24 April emails prior to 11 June 2024. Moreover, Dr Rewais was not challenged in cross-examination to the contrary. More is required for BPB to meet its onus of proof.

In Roberts Co (NSW) Pty Ltd v Sharvain Facades Pty Ltd (Administrators Appointed) [2025] NSWCA 161, McHugh JA held that service under section 31(1)(d) of the NSW SOP Act (equivalent to section 102(1) of the QLD Act) occurs when a document is emailed to an address specified by the recipient for service and becomes capable of being retrieved. The Court found that Sharvain’s payment claim was served on 28 February 2025 when it was uploaded to Payapps and sent to Robert’s nominated email address. His Honour confirmed that there is no requirement under section 31 for the recipient to actually access or become aware of the communication. In coming to this conclusion, McHugh JA followed followed Claire Rewais and Osama Rewais t/as McVitty Grove v BPB Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2025] NSWCA 103 and distinguished Parkview Constructions v Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd [2017] NSWSC 194. The Court reaffirmed that section 31 governs the mechanics of valid service under the Act, irrespective of awareness.

In Kumar v Frankies Cranes [2025] NSWSC 1264, Peden J held that handing over a business card, “without more”, does not amount to specification of an email address for service under s 31(1)(d) of the NSW Act (equivalent to s 102 of the Act). Applying Rewais v BPB Earthmoving Pty Ltd, her Honour confirmed that “specification” may be express or inferred from conduct, and quoted McHugh JA’s guidance that given “the jurisdictional significance of service”, parties should obtain express confirmation before using email addresses for service under the Act. Her Honour also applied QC Communications NSW Pty Ltd v CivComm Pty Ltd and BCFK Holdings Pty Ltd v Rork Projects Pty Ltd, confirming that in the absence of specification the claimant must prove actual receipt, which was not established.

Dropbox and cloud storage

In Conveyor & General Engineering Pty Ltd v Basetec Services Pty Ltd & Anor [2014] QSC 30, Philip McMurdo J held that service of an adjudication application by ‘Dropbox’ was not effective service under either section 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) or 109X of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) because:

  • the electronic communication (an email) did not contain the adjudication application, but rather the means to access the adjudication application such that the whole adjudication application had not been ‘sent’ to the relevant office (at [29]–[30]); and
  • the adjudication application was not ‘left’ or ‘sent’ to the relevant office at least until it had been opened on the Dropbox site ‘and probably not until its contents had been downloaded to a computer’ at the relevant office (at [32]).

His Honour also held that actual service had not occurred within the statutory time period as ‘it was insufficient for the document and its whereabouts to be identified absent something in the nature of its receipt’. The same reasoning is likely to apply to other forms of cloud-storage document hosting.

In BCS Infrastructure Support Pty Ltd v Jones Lang Lasalle (NSW) Pty Ltd [2020] VSC 739, Stynes J found that service of a payment claim by a cloud-based information system was not effective until the payment claim was identified and read by the respondent. The claimant had uploaded the claim to Corrigo, where it was available to retrieve from the time of upload, and Corrigo sent an email notifying the respondent of its existence; but the respondent did not become aware of the claim until later. Because Corrigo was an electronic address for the purposes of the Electronic Transactions Act 2000 (Vic), the time for receipt was when the respondent became aware that the payment claim had been sent to Corrigo.

‘Service’ and ‘receipt’ of documents under the Act

The Act no longer draws an explicit distinction between ‘service’ and ‘receipt’, because of the move to plain English drafting. The earlier authorities remain relevant for context, particularly in other jurisdictions.

In Falgat Constructions Pty Ltd v Equity Australia Corporation Pty Ltd [2006] NSWCA 259, Hodgson JA said:

There remains the question of whether the time at which service or provision has taken effect is also the time at which a document is received, for the purposes of s.17(3)(b) (and see also s.17(2)(b) and s.21). I note that the word “receive” is also used in s.31(2), but used in the context of “received at that place”. In my opinion, mail delivered to a registered office or place of business is received at that place when it is put into the mail box of that registered office or place of business, without the necessity of anyone actually seeing it.

In my opinion, the word “receive” in s.17(3)(c) does not necessarily require that the document come to the notice of a person authorised to deal with the document on behalf of the claimant. In general, in my opinion, it would be satisfied once the document has arrived at the claimant’s registered office or place of business and is there during normal office hours. There may be circumstances in which service or provision has been effected within s.109X of the Corporations Act or s.31 of the Act, but the document has not been received, but I find it difficult to identify any such circumstances.

Falgat Constructions was a departure from the earlier view of McDougall J in Pacific General Securities Ltd v Soliman & Sons Pty Ltd [2005] NSWSC 378:

It will be seen that there is some distinction drawn between the concept of service and the concept of receipt, although for some purposes at least, the two will coincide. Thus, in s 19, the operative concept is that of service, whilst in s 20, the operative concept is that of receipt. Again, where s 31(1)(c) is availed of, the operative concept, by virtue of subs (2), is receipt. It is receipt that both sets the clock running under s 20 and following and that provides both the fact and the time of service, at least where s 31(1)(c) is relied upon.

…

The verb “receive” in its ordinary meaning denotes the taking of something into one’s hand or possession, of something given or delivered, or having something delivered or brought to one. I see no reason why the word “receive”, and its cognate forms in the Act, should not be given that ordinary English meaning. This does not mean, in the case of a corporation (at least absent any contractual stipulation to the contrary) a document must come to a particular person within a corporation before it can be received. It means that the document must come into the hand or possession of, or be delivered or brought to, someone on behalf of the corporation; or, perhaps, that otherwise somehow it comes into the hand or possession of, or is delivered or brought to, the corporation.

In CMF Projects Pty Ltd v Masic Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 209, Daubney J summarised the current authorities:

It seems now to be clear enough on the authorities that the word “receive” connotes that, whilst the document in question need not come to the attention of a particular person within the relevant office, it nevertheless does actually need to have arrived at, and thereby been received’, at the recipient’s registered office, or place of business, and be there during normal office hours.

Ball J applied that approach in QC Communications NSW Pty Ltd v CivComm Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 1095, stating that:

A document will be served in accordance with the requirements of the [Building and Construction Industry Security of Payment Act 1999 (NSW)] if it actually comes to the attention of the person to be served. It is not necessary that it be served in accordance with s 31 [of the Building and Construction Industry Security of Payment Act 1999 (NSW)].

His Honour cited Hodgson JA in Falgat Constructions Pty Ltd v Equity Australia Corporation Ltd [2006] NSWCA 259 at [58]:

In the first place, in my opinion it is clear that if a document has actually been received and come to the attention of a person to be served or provided with the document, or of a person with authority to deal with such a document on behalf of a person or corporation to be served or provided with the document, it does not matter whether or not any facultative regime has been complied with: see Howship Holdings Pty Limited v Leslie (1996) 41 NSWLR 542; Mohamed v Farah [2004] NSWSC 482 at [42]–[44]. In such a case, there has been service, provision and receipt.

In MGW engineering Pty Ltd (t/as forefront services) v CMOC mining Pty Ltd [2021] NSWSC 514, Stevenson J held that service of a payment claim on an employee of the respondent did not constitute effective service until the document came to the attention of the relevant representative. The payment claims had been addressed to the respondent’s representative and the superintendent, neither of whom was on site on the day of service. The Court considered section 31(1)(a) and (b) of the NSW SOP Act (no equivalent in the BIF Act), dealing with personal service and lodgement. His Honour held that for a document to be served “personally” and “lodged” under those provisions “more is required than simply leaving the document with an employee, no matter what that employee’s functions were nor how junior that employee was, at any location within the corporation’s business premises. Some further step, the effect to which would likely be bringing the Payment Claim to the attention of the relevantly responsible person, is necessary”.

Examples:

  • CMF Projects Pty Ltd v Masic Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 209. An adjudicator’s notice of acceptance was sent not to the respondent’s registered office, but to a post office box of the respondent. Daubney J noted the distinction between ‘service’ and ‘receipt’ of documents under the Act and held that the adjudicator had erred in equating deposit of the notice into the post office box with receipt.

Service on or by an agent of the principal

In Bucklands Convalescent Hospital v Taylor Projects Group [2007] NSWSC 1514, Hammerschlag J followed the Court of Appeal in Baulderstone Hornibrook Pty Ltd v Queensland Investment Corporation [2007] NSWCA 9, holding:

…As a matter of law it does not seem to me that a person who is a Superintendent under a contract and who has certifying functions under it is incapable of being appointed as agent to respond to a payment claim under the Act.

In Lucas Stuart v Hemmes Hermitage [2009] NSWSC 477, McDougall J held that an assessment by a quantity surveyor did not constitute a payment schedule:

Under the Act, a claimant is entitled to have a written reply from (or binding on) the respondent, so that the claimant can decide whether to accept the scheduled amount or to proceed to adjudication. A claimant should not be required to act upon the basis of informal indications by those who advise the respondent, even if (in a particular case) that adviser is trusted, and its advice has always been followed in the past.

In Director of Housing v Structx Pty Ltd t/as Bzibuilders & Anor [2011] VSC 410, an architect issued what was purported to be a payment schedule under the VIC Act. There was no contractual provision conferring such a right, but the architect had been appointed by letter as a representative of the superintendent with authority to issue progress certificates. The Court held that the architect did possess the relevant authority. In Lewance Construction Pty Ltd v Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd [2014] NSWSC 1726, Nicholas AJ held that the superintendent’s representative had authority to receive progress claims and payment claims, and to respond by providing both a progress certificate and a payment schedule.

Refer also to the commentary under section 75.3 in relation to service of a payment claim by an agent of the principal.

Practice tips

  • Treat the section 99 warning notice as a hard prerequisite. If you intend to sue to recover an unpaid claim as a debt, issue the warning notice in the approved form within 20 business days of the due date and wait at least 5 business days before filing — an email that just “reserves rights” will not do (Vinson v Neerim).
  • Use the warning-notice window to your advantage on the respondent side: if the deadline for your payment schedule has slipped, your opportunity to put on a “second-chance” payment schedule only opens once you actually receive the warning notice (Forte Sydney v Lin Betty) — do not assume an earlier out-of-time schedule will save you.
  • Remember that section 100 strips the respondent of contract-based defences and counterclaims in the recovery proceeding, but section 101 keeps the contractual fight alive: a developer can still sue later for restitution or contractual entitlement once the adjudicated amount has been paid (Gambaro; McDonald v MAK Constructions).
  • Service is the trigger for almost every clock under the Act, and the courts apply “zero tolerance” for delay (Emag Constructions). If the contract specifies a method of service, follow it; if it does not, fall back on section 39 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 and section 109X of the Corporations Act — not a PO box (CMF Projects v Masic).
  • For email and electronic platforms (Aconex, Payapps, Corrigo, Dropbox), confirm the recipient’s designated email address in writing, send to that address, and keep evidence of delivery and access. Assume the document is “capable of being retrieved” even if it lands in spam (Bauen Constructions); but cloud-link emails are not service of the document itself (Conveyor & General Engineering; BCS Infrastructure).
  • Where you are dealing with a superintendent or principal’s representative, check the appointment documentation. The right person can validly serve and receive payment claims and payment schedules (Bucklands; Lewance), but a quantity surveyor or junior employee on site, without more, is unlikely to bind the principal (Lucas Stuart; MGW Engineering).
