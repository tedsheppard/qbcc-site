Chapter 3 · Sections 84–86

Adjudication procedures

Once the adjudicator has the application and any response, sections 84 to 86 of the BIF Act govern how the adjudication actually runs — what powers the adjudicator can use to gather information, how quickly the decision must come out, and when the deadline can be pushed back. For a PM, these sections set the tempo of the dispute and define what you can and cannot expect from the process.

Section 84 BIF Act – Adjudication procedures
84 Adjudication Procedures

(1)  Subject to the time requirements under section 85, an adjudicator must decide the following as quickly as possible—

(a)  an adjudication application;

(b)  applications for extensions of time under section 83.

(2)  For a proceeding conducted to decide an adjudication application, an adjudicator—

(a)  must decide—

(i)  whether he or she has jurisdiction to adjudicate the application; and

(ii)  whether the application is frivolous or vexatious; and

(b)  may ask for further written submissions from either party and must give the other party an opportunity to comment on the submissions; and

(c)  may set deadlines for further submissions and comments by the parties; and

(d)  may call a conference of the parties; and

(e)  may carry out an inspection of any matter to which the claim relates.

(3)  If a conference is called, it must be conducted informally, and the parties are not entitled to any legal representation unless allowed by the adjudicator.

(4)  The adjudicator’s power to decide an adjudication application is not affected by the failure of either or both of the parties to make a submission or comment within time or to comply with the adjudicator’s call for a conference of the parties.

The adjudicator’s procedural powers (s84)

Section 84 gives the adjudicator a tight set of tools: decide jurisdiction and whether the application is frivolous or vexatious, request further written submissions, set deadlines, call a conference, and inspect the work. Conferences are informal and lawyers do not appear unless the adjudicator allows it. Crucially, if a party fails to respond to a request, comply with a deadline, or attend a conference, the adjudicator can still proceed and decide the application.

The role of the adjudicator

The starting point for what an adjudicator must actually do is that the adjudicator must “come to a view as to what is properly payable”: see Pacific General Securities Ltd & Anor v Soliman & Sons Pty Ltd & Ors [2006] NSWSC 13; Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v J M Hargreaves (NSW) Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 228; (2005) 63 NSWLR 385; Thiess Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373.

In Pacific General Securities Ltd & Anor v Soliman & Sons Pty Ltd & Ors [2006] NSWSC 13, Brereton J described the role of the adjudicator in this way:

I therefore respectfully agree with the view tentatively expressed by Hodgson JA in Hargreaves: the adjudicator’s duty is to come to a view as to what is properly payable, on what the adjudicator considers to be the true construction of the contract and the Act and the true merits of the claim, and while the adjudicator may very readily find in favour of the claimant on the merits of the claim in the absence of a payment schedule or adjudication response, or if no relevant material is advanced by the respondent, the absence of such material does not entitle the adjudicator simply to award the amount of the claim without addressing its merits, which as a minimum will involve determining whether the construction work identified in the payment claim has been carried out, and what is its value.

Brereton J was picking up on the earlier judgment of Hodgson JA in Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v J M Hargreaves (NSW) Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 228; (2005) 63 NSWLR 385:

The adjudicator’s duty is to come to a view as to what is properly payable, on what the adjudicator considers to be the true construction of the contract and the Act and the true merits of the claim.

The same idea was applied in Queensland by Ann Lyons J in Thiess Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373:

The adjudicator must also then make a genuine attempt to exercise his power in accordance with the BCIPA. An adjudicator is required to come to a determination based on the factors set out in s 26(2) of the BCIPA and is required to “turn their minds to, grapple with and form a view on all matters that they are required to ‘consider’.”

That settled position has more recently been qualified. In Ceerose Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd [2023] NSWCA 215, Payne JA (Ward ACJ and Basten AJA agreeing) declined to follow the “true merits” framing in Hargreaves and Pacific General Securities as a test for jurisdictional error where the respondent has not advanced material. Their Honours held at [75]:

The essence of [52] of Hargreaves is that the ‘adjudicator’s duty is to come to a view as to what is properly payable, on what the adjudicator considers to be the true construction of the contract and the Act and the true merits of the claim’ (emphasis added). There is a potential difficulty in this reasoning, at least in so far as it is being used as a test to identify jurisdictional error. The ‘true construction of the contract’ and the ‘true merits’ of the payment claim are, by reason of the structure of the Security of Payment Act I have set out above, matters where an error by an adjudicator will not be jurisdictional. As Basten JA explained at [66] of Hargreaves itself, in the light of the express restriction in s 20(2B) of the Act, there is merit in the conclusion that the adjudicator is not entitled to go beyond the terms of the adjudication response in rejecting part or all of the payment claim. Certainly, it is not a jurisdictional error for an adjudicator, having decided all the reasons advanced by the respondent were invalid, to then and without more, determine the amount of the progress payment in favour of the claimant based on the payment claim.”

That reasoning was applied in Bega Valley Shire Council v Kenpass Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 399, where Nixon J refused to set aside an adjudication determination, holding at [85]:

… Having decided that the Council had not advanced any valid objections to the quantification of delay damages in the Payment Schedule, or any objections in the Adjudication Response which he was permitted to consider, he proceeded “then and without more” to “determine the amount of the progress payment in favour of the claimant based on the payment claim”. It was not a jurisdictional error to do so.

His Honour at [86] also linked the result to the legislative purpose described in Probuild Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd v Shade Systems Pty Ltd (2018) 264 CLR 1:

In Probuild at [47], the High Court identified the “clear legislative intention” of the statutory being regime as being “to ensure that the statutory entitlement can be determined and enforced with minimal delay”. It is consistent with this legislative intention that if a payment claim is made, and the adjudicator finds that the respondent does not advance in the payment schedule any valid reason for withholding payment, the adjudicator can “then and without more” determine the amount of the progress payment based on the payment claim, without independently examining and being satisfied as to the contractual basis for the claim, the merits of the claim, the proof of loss, or the quantification of damage. To the extent that there is an issue about any of those matters, the Security of Payment Act “defers the final determination of contractual rights to a different forum, in which the consequences of any erroneous determination can and must be taken into account”: Probuild at [47].

The contrasting (and earlier) line is illustrated by Laing O’Rourke Australia Construction Pty Ltd v Monford Group Pty Ltd [2018] NSWSC 491, where Stevenson J declared an adjudication void for jurisdictional error because the adjudicator awarded amounts to the claimant simply on the basis that the respondent had not disputed them in its response, without engaging with the merits. His Honour adopted Brereton J’s approach in Pacific General Securities Ltd v Soliman & Sons [2006] NSWSC 13: at a minimum the adjudicator must decide whether the construction work was carried out and what it is worth.

The Victorian summary by Vickery J in SSC Plenty Road Pty Ltd v Construction Engineering (Aust) Pty Ltd [2015] VSC 631 at [101] also remains a useful overview of the adjudicator’s task in valuing a claim.

Importantly for Queensland practice, the adjudicator’s consideration is not boxed in by the parties’ submissions. In BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 346, Applegarth J said:

In carrying out the functions entrusted to him or her, an adjudicator must consider the submissions that have been made. That does not mean that the consideration of the merits of the payment claim is limited to issues actually raised by the submissions. An adjudicator’s task is to come to a view as to what is properly payable, but the submissions do not set the parameters of the application or its determination. It may be that there are no submissions or only partial submissions.

Where the adjudicator is “minded” to decide on a basis the parties have not raised, natural justice requires that the parties be given a chance to address it: BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 346, [52] (Applegarth J).

Adjudication examples

  • SSC Plenty Road Pty Ltd v Construction Engineering (Aust) Pty Ltd [2015] VSC 631. The adjudicator dealt with 37 disputed variation claims. For 33 of them, Vickery J found that the adjudicator applied a “formula” that effectively put the onus on the respondent to justify withholding payment for each item, then adopted the payment claim figure once that onus was not met. The same approach was applied to three of five disputed deductions. Vickery J held that the adjudicator showed no real reasoning beyond adopting the claimant’s value, and fell into jurisdictional error in respect of all 33 variations and three deductions.

In Ingeteam Australia Pty Ltd v Susan River Solar Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 30, Applegarth J said at [86] that “a jurisidictional error will occur where a decision-maker finds a fact that is a precondition to the conferral of jurisdictional exists when it does not exist, or finds that such a fact does not exist when it does”. At [87] his Honour also confirmed that the principle summarised in Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] 239 CLR 531 applies to tribunals and other decision-makers:

An inferior court falls into a jurisdictional error ‘if it mistankenly asserts or denies the existence of jurisdiction.’

The adjudication determination in Ingeteam was declared void in part for jurisdictional error on three grounds:

  • Procedural fairness was denied because Ingeteam was not given an opportunity to make submissions on the jurisdictional point Susan River had raised in its adjudication response — that Ingeteam had breached section 42 of the QBCC Act when entering into the contract. Ingeteam was not given the chance to refute the alleged unlicensed building work, and was not given a chance to address the adjudicator’s conclusion that Ingeteam did not intend to comply with section 42.
  • The adjudicator was wrong to find that Ingeteam was carrying out unlicensed building work (floor repair). The work in question was the laying of a floating floor, and section 30 of the QBCC Regulation lists the laying of “carpets, floating floors or vinyl” as work that is not building work.
  • The adjudicator’s conclusion that Ingeteam intended not to comply with section 42 of the QBCC Act was not one any reasonable decision-maker could have reached on the evidence and submissions before him.

Susan River argued that, even on those findings, the errors were not material and so the determination should not be set aside. A jurisdictional error is material if the ultimate decision “could have been different” without the error. Applegarth J held all three errors were material because, had the adjudicator allowed submissions on the floor repair and on Ingeteam’s intention at the time of contract, there was a real possibility he would not have erred on either point and a real possibility procedural fairness would have led to a different conclusion on jurisdiction under both limbs of section 42.

Section 84(2)(a) – ‘must decide’ jurisdiction and frivolous/vexatious

Section 84(2)(a) requires the adjudicator to “decide whether he or she has jurisdiction to adjudicate the application”. That obligation was introduced by the Amendment Act and is a departure from the equivalent section 21(4) of the NSW Act.

The Act also requires the adjudicator to decide whether the application is frivolous or vexatious, but does not define those terms. Section 14A of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) directs that, in interpreting sections 84, 85 and 91, the construction that best achieves the purpose of the BIF Act — helping people in the building and construction industry get paid for the work they do — is to be preferred.

What “frivolous” or “vexatious” means in this context has not yet been judicially considered, and the explanatory notes do not explain the purpose of the amendment. The likely intention is to give the adjudicator a discretion to refuse a claimant any progress payment on that basis.

Section 84(2)(b), (c), (d) and (3) – discretionary tools

The equivalent NSW provisions were considered in Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Luikens & Anor [2003] NSWSC 1140, where Palmer J said:

s.21(4) makes it clear that it is within the discretion of the adjudicator whether or not to take any of the steps set out in paragraphs (a) to (d). Exercise of that discretion one way or another will depend upon the adjudicator’s judgment as to whether or not he or she will be assisted in reaching a decision within the constraints, particularly the time constraints, imposed by the Act.

In Parliament Square Hobart Landowner Pty Ltd v Tonkin [2020] TASSC 30, Estcourt J held that an adjudicator’s jurisdiction extends to deciding whether contractual terms have been affected by waiver or estoppel, by reference to the powers under section 25(2) of the SOP Act (Tas) (equivalent to section 88(2) of the BIF Act). Parliament Square argued that the adjudicator could decide common law waiver but not equitable estoppel, because the latter would require an equitable remedy outside the contract. The court rejected that distinction and applied Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Luikens [2003] NSWSC 1140: an adjudicator can consider acts that may found an estoppel in deciding whether a party can insist on strict compliance with the contract.

Section 84(3)(b) – the power to ask for further written submissions

The equivalent NSW power was considered in John Holland Pty Ltd v Cardno MBK (NSW) Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 258, where Einstein J said:

Whether or not any of these problems may be addressed by the adjudicator requesting further written submissions from either party may become the subject of curial examination on another occasion. However it would seem unlikely that the legislature would have intended the provisions of section 21 (4) (a) and (b) to permit a radical departure from the statutory scheme described above. Rather it seems likely that these sub-sections are to be read as permitting no more than additional submissions which clarify earlier submissions: those earlier submissions being constrained in the manner above described.

That passage was applied by Sackar J in State Water Corporation v Civil Team Engineering Pty Ltd [2013] NSWSC 1879. In that case the argument was that section 20(2B) of the NSW Act (equivalent to section 24(4) BCIPA) was not breached because the further submissions had been given in answer to the adjudicator’s specific request:

In my view, State Water's first submission should not be accepted. The provision permitting an adjudicator to request further submissions from the parties (s 21(4)(a)) cannot simply be exercised by an adjudicator to transform a party's submissions which were not duly made (by reason of s 20(2B)) to submissions duly made and capable of being considered by the adjudicator. That would empower adjudicators to defeat the underlying purpose of s 20(2B). There is authority supporting this view (John Holland Pty Ltd v Cardno MBK (NSW) Pty Limited & Ors [2004] NSWSC 258 at [26] per Einstein J).

In Minesco Pty Ltd v Anderson Sunvast Hong Kong Ltd [2017] VSC 299, Vickery J quashed an adjudication determination for denial of natural justice. The defendant had served documents that went beyond the adjudicator’s request for further information. The plaintiff wrote asking the adjudicator not to read or consider that material, but received no response and was left not knowing whether the additional information had been taken into account. His Honour held that the procedure adopted by the adjudicator was not such as to result in justice being seen to be done.

Failing to request further submissions

Adjudicators are not always required to chase further information. In Ku-Ring-Gai Council v Ichor Constructions Pty Ltd [2014] NSWSC 1534, Stevenson J considered that, given the “object of the Act is to provide a speedy resolution of progress claims”, the adjudicator could not be criticised “for failing to seek further information from the parties than that already placed before him”. That comment was made in the context of the adjudicator having concluded he could not decide the claimant’s delay damages claim on the material before him.

In Ausenco Operations Pty Ltd & Anor v Ferretti International Ottoway Pty Ltd & Anor [2020] SASC 46, Doyle J held that an adjudicator failed to afford the respondent procedural fairness by rejecting its claim for liquidated damages on a basis the claimant had not put forward in its adjudication application. The denial of procedural fairness was “plainly” substantial because the amount was material (over $200,000) and submissions the contractor was denied had a real prospect of influencing the result. The decision was infected by jurisdictional error and set aside. The court said that, if the adjudicator had intended to reject the liquidated damages on that basis, the adjudicator should have requested further submissions.

In 12 Bridge Street Epping Pty Ltd atf 12 Bridge Street Epping Unit Trust v D.R. Design (NSW) Pty Ltd [2022] NSWSC 866, Stevenson J refused to set aside an adjudication determination, holding that the adjudicator had power under section 21(4)(a) of the NSW SOP Act (the equivalent of section 84(2)(a) of the BIF Act) to request “further written submissions” even before an adjudication response was given, because the respondent’s email saying it had not received the adjudication application itself constituted a written “submission”. The respondent argued it had been denied natural justice because that submission was treated as its adjudication response, preventing it from filing one. His Honour rejected that contention: section 21(4)(a) does not stop an adjudicator from requesting further submissions and setting a deadline before an adjudication response is served, and there was no lack of procedural fairness because the submission was itself in answer to the adjudicator’s request and did not exclude the respondent from giving an adjudication response. Natural justice is dealt with in the commentary on section 88.

The adjudicator’s role in an uncontested adjudication

Palmer J in Brookhollow Pty Ltd v R&R Consultants Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 1 said that the adjudicator’s role and duties differ depending on whether the respondent participates:

In a contested adjudication, the adjudicator need consider only those provisions of the Act and of the contract which are relevant to the issues formulated by the parties in their submissions. In an adjudication in which the respondent does not participate the position of the adjudicator is, in my opinion, analogous to that of the Court when a plaintiff seeks the entry of judgment in default of an appearance by the defendant or where the defendant has failed to file a defence. In such a case, the Court still has a duty to decide the case according to truth and fact and if the plaintiff’s case appears on the face of the pleading or on the plaintiff’s evidence (or lack of it) to be fatally flawed, then the Court will refuse to enter judgment…

…

In my opinion, where the respondent has not participated in the adjudication process so that the payment claim is undefended, s.22(2) requires the adjudicator to address in good faith such issues arising from the need to conform with the provisions of the Act and of the contract as manifestly appear on the face of the payment claim, the adjudication application and any supporting material.

…

If a fatal flaw in compliance with the Act or the contract is manifestly apparent from a consideration of these matters, the adjudicator will refuse to make a determination in favour of the claimant.

Palmer J’s approach in Brookhollow Pty Ltd v R&R Consultants Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 1 was approved by Lyons J in G W Enterprises Pty Ltd v Xentex Industries Pty Ltd & Ors [2006] QSC 399.

The adjudicator’s role where the respondent makes no valid submission on a claim

A related question is what the adjudicator must do where there is no submission from the respondent on a particular claim made by the claimant. In Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v J M Hargreaves (NSW) Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 228, Hodgson JA gave a “tentative” view:

…The adjudicator may very readily find in favour of the claimant on the merits of the claim if no relevant material is put by the respondent; but the absence of such material does not mean that the adjudicator can simply award the amount of the claim without any addressing of its merits.

Indeed, my tentative view is that, if an adjudicator determined the progress payment at the amount claimed simply because he or she rejected the relevance of the respondent’s material, this could be such a failure to address the task set by the Act as to render the determination void.

Brereton J agreed in Pacific General Securities Ltd & Anor v Soliman & Sons Pty Ltd & Ors [2006] NSWSC 13:

I therefore respectfully agree with the view tentatively expressed by Hodgson JA in Hargreaves: the adjudicator’s duty is to come to a view as to what is properly payable, on what the adjudicator considers to be the true construction of the contract and the Act and the true merits of the claim, and while the adjudicator may very readily find in favour of the claimant on the merits of the claim in the absence of a payment schedule or adjudication response, or if no relevant material is advanced by the respondent, the absence of such material does not entitle the adjudicator simply to award the amount of the claim without addressing its merits, which as a minimum will involve determining whether the construction work identified in the payment claim has been carried out, and what is its value.

As to whether allowing a claim in full without examination of its merits in the absence of valid submissions duly made by a respondent is such a failure to perform the task of adjudication as to result in invalidity, as Hodgson JA also tentatively thought, the question is whether that amounts to a failure in a basic and fundamental requirement of validity of a determination, or whether it means that there has not been a good faith attempt at performance of the function, in the sense referred to in Brodyn.

…I have endeavoured to explain above the minimum content of an adjudication: the absence of relevant material from a respondent does not entitle the adjudicator simply to award the amount of the claim without addressing its merits, which as a minimum will involve determining whether the construction work identified in the payment claim has been carried out, and what is its value. Adoption of the other approach by an adjudicator – by allowing a claim in full just because a respondent’s submissions are rejected, without determining whether the construction work the subject of the claim has been performed and without valuing it – would bespeak a misconception of what is required of an adjudicator. …Accordingly, I respectfully agree with Hodgson JA’s tentative view that for an adjudicator to determine a progress payment at the amount claimed simply because he or she rejects the relevance of the respondent’s material is such a failure to address the task set by the Act as to render the determination void.

The statements of Hodgson JA in Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v J M Hargreaves (NSW) Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 228 and Brereton J in Pacific General Securities Ltd & Anor v Soliman & Sons Pty Ltd & Ors [2006] NSWSC 13 were accepted as the law in Queensland by Peter Lyons J in J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Cada Formwork Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 63, [72]. See also Bezzina Developers Pty Ltd v Deemah Stone (Qld) Pty Ltd [2008] QCA 213; [2008] 2 Qd R 495.

It has also been held in Queensland that the adjudicator must not ignore a point “where it is apparent on the face of the material which the adjudicator is obliged to consider under s 26(2)”, even where the respondent has made no submission on it: South East Civil and Drainage Contractors Pty Ltd v AMGW Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 45; [2013] 2 Qd R 189, 198 [45] (Jackson J).

Reasoning from implicit facts, admissions or absence of material

In John Holland Pty Ltd v Walz Marine Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 39, Margaret Wilson J held that the adjudicator was entitled to reach a conclusion on facts impliedly admitted by the respondent or assumed for the purposes of submissions made — an approach approved by the Court of Appeal in Bezzina Developers Pty Ltd v Deemah Stone (Qld) Pty Ltd [2008] QCA 213; [2008] 2 Qd R 495.

In SSC Plenty Road Pty Ltd v Construction Engineering (Aust) Pty Ltd [2015] VSC 631, Vickery J accepted that an adjudicator may draw inferences from the absence of controverting material:

if the claimant has put on material as to the value of a claim, but the respondent has not, the adjudicator in assessing the value is entitled to draw any necessary inference from the absence of controverting material from the respondent, including an inference that no credible challenge can be made to the value of the claim advanced by the claimant. Such an inference may not be conclusive, but it may be taken into account in assessing the evidence of value overall.

His Honour qualified this by saying that “if all the adjudicator does is reject the respondent’s contentions and adopt those of the claimant”, there will not be a valid adjudication of a payment claim under the VIC Act.

Section 85 BIF Act – Time for deciding adjudication application
85 Time for deciding adjudication application

(1)  Subject to section 86, an adjudicator must decide an adjudication application no later than—

(a)  for a standard payment claim—10 business days after the response date; or

(b)  for a complex payment claim—15 business days after the response date.

(2)  The response date is—

(a)  if the adjudicator is given an adjudication response under section 83—the day on which the adjudicator receives the response; or

(b)  otherwise—the last day on which the respondent could give the adjudicator an adjudication response under section 83.

(3)  An adjudicator must not decide an adjudication application before the end of the period that the respondent may give an adjudication response to the adjudicator under section 83, unless—

(a)  the adjudicator decides he or she does not have jurisdiction to adjudicate the application; or

(b)  the adjudicator decides the application is frivolous or vexatious.

Time for decision (s85)

Section 85 sets the deadline: 10 business days after the response date for a standard payment claim, 15 for a complex payment claim. The clock runs from the day the adjudicator receives the response, or, if no response is given, from the last day on which the respondent could have given one. The adjudicator cannot decide before the response window closes, except where the application is rejected for lack of jurisdiction or as frivolous or vexatious.

Section 85(1) – ‘Subject to section 86’ (extensions of time)

An adjudicator cannot manufacture an extension of time by setting a short deadline for the parties to object. In Allpro v Micos [2010] NSWSC 453, McDougall J held that the adjudicator erred in:

it was open to him firstly to impose a very short time limit on the parties to indicate their attitude to the request for an extension of time and secondly to assume that consent was given if the parties did not respond within the time so limited. There may conceivably be circumstances where a party to an adjudication application should be taken to have agreed to an extension of time under s 21 (3)(b) without expressly having so indicated. But that could not arise in circumstances such as those shown on the evidence in this case where, as I have said, the time limited by the adjudicator was extremely short. Section 21 (3)(b) requires agreement. That means real or actual agreement. It does not in my view entitle an adjudicator to purport to impose agreement of parties in the way that I have outlined.

Section 86 of the BIF Act allows a further five business days for a complex payment claim, or any other period agreed between the parties, where the section 86(1) requirements are met.

Section 85(1) – deciding within time

Under the previous section 25(3) BCIPA (before the Amendment Act), the Court of Appeal in Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386 said:

Unless the mechanism of s 32 is engaged, there is nothing in the Act which directly or indirectly relieves the adjudicator of the obligation to decide an adjudication within the times allowed by s 25(3).

The effect of an adjudication decision delivered out of time was considered in MPM Constructions v Trepcha Constructions [2004] NSWSC 103. McDougall J said:

In accordance with what the majority said in Project Blue Sky at 397 [97], I start from the proposition that it is unlikely in this case that the legislature intended that an act done in breach of the time limit set out s 21(3)(a) would be invalid. I say that because the consequences of invalidity seem to me to be susceptible of undermining the purpose of the legislation both as set out in s 3 and as expanded upon by the Minister in the Second Reading Speech to which Bergin J referred in Paynter Dixon and to which I referred in Musico. That is to my view confirmed because where a determination is not made within the relevant time limit, the only relief that a claimant seems to have under the Act is that for which s 26 applies, namely, to withdraw the application and make a new application. However, by s 26(3) that course may only be taken within five business days after the claimant becomes entitled to withdraw the previous adjudication application. Relevantly for present purposes, that means that the application must have been withdrawn within five days of the expiry of the s 21(3) time period. It would be, to put it mildly, anomalous if the effect of non-compliance with the time period allowed by s 21(3) were to render any subsequent adjudication a nullity, but if because of non-compliance of s 26(3) the claimants were unable to seek adjudication of the dispute. That would not seem to me an outcome consistent with the evident objects of the legislation and, therefore, something to be avoided unless no other view is available.

His Honour held the challenge failed and that the requirement to decide within time was not jurisdictional (at [24]). McDougall J followed his own decision in Cranbrook School v JA Bradshaw Civil Contracting [2013] NSWSC 430:

I dealt with this in MPM Constructions Pty Ltd v Trepcha Constructions Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 103. I concluded that the requirements of the section were not jurisdictional. I noted, among other things, that the Act contained its own provision for non-compliance on the part of the adjudicator, with the time requirements of s 21(3).

To my mind, it would be quite extraordinary if the legislature intended that a builder or subcontractor who had got through the various hurdles that the Act imposes, in the path of obtaining a successful determination, up until the point of receipt of the adjudicator's reasons, should be disqualified from the benefit of a determination in its favour simply because the adjudicator did not comply with the statutory time limit.

In MT Lewis Estate Pty Ltd v Metricon Homes Pty Ltd [2017] NSWSC 1121, Hammerschlag J applied Cranbrook School and held that an out-of-time decision was not invalid. At [61] his Honour added:

[U]nder s 31 [of the NSW Act], an adjudicator is exempt from liability for anything omitted to be done in good faith. This would undoubtedly extend to a failure to deliver on time. If the time limit in s 29(4) is a guillotine, the obligation on an adjudicator to deliver a determination, whilst it may have been breached, would come to an end with no redress against her or him unless the failure was not in good faith. The adjudicator would be relieved of the burden of producing, albeit that she or he could not charge for work done. If a later adjudication is nevertheless valid, the adjudicator’s duty would continue and the sanction of not being paid is more real. This position is more conducive to the prompt delivery by adjudicators and fulfilment of the overall objects of the Act.

The same line was followed in Ian Street Developer Pty Ltd v Arrow International Pty Ltd [2018] VSC 14, where Riordan J held that an adjudication delivered outside the VIC Act time limit was still valid. His Honour’s reasons included:

  • invalidating an adjudicator’s determination would be inconsistent with the objects of the Victorian Act;
  • section 23(2B) of the Victorian Act provides that the adjudicator’s determination is void in certain circumstances, arising out of post-appointment conduct, which do not include non-compliance with s 22(4);
  • If an out of time determination was a nullity there would be no purpose in the Act providing that the claimant may withdraw the application in the circumstances;
  • to deprive the parties of the benefit of an adjudication determination after they have completed their submissions would result in an inconvenience and the likely costs of a further adjudication; and
  • if sub-s 22(4) of the Victorian Act was intended to impose a jurisdictional requirement, then an adjudication determination, which was given within the 10 day period, could still be invalid if it was not given as ‘expeditiously as possible’. A requirement to do an act as ‘expeditiously as possible’ is less likely to be jurisdictional because it does not have a ‘rule-like quality which can be easily identified and applied’ (in the equivalent Queensland section an adjudicator must decide an adjudication application ‘as quickly as possible’).

In Ian Street Developer Pty Ltd v Arrow International Pty Ltd [2018] VSCA 294, the Victorian Court of Appeal upheld Riordan J. Maxwell P (McLeish and Niall JJA agreeing) found that “to hold that non-compliance with the time limit invalidated the adjudication decision would be inconsistent both with the express provisions of the [VIC] Act governing the adjudication process and with the objects of the statutory scheme as a whole”. The court applied Cranbrook School v JA Bradshaw Civil Contracting [2013] NSWSC 430 and MT Lewis Estate Pty Ltd v Metricon Homes Pty Ltd [2017] NSWSC 1121 and added at [95]:

I agree with McDougall J and Hammerschlag J that a failure to comply with the time limits for determining an adjudication application does not invalidate the adjudication determination for the reasons given by them, referred to above. In my opinion, I can have regard to this reasoning in interpreting the Victorian Act because the relevant provisions are substantially identical. I would also add the following considerations:

(a) Section 22(4) of the Act regulates ‘the exercise of functions already conferred on [the adjudicator], rather than impos[ing] essential preliminaries to the exercise of those functions’. This is a strong indicator of an intention that non-compliance does not deprive the adjudicator of his or her jurisdiction.

(b) Section 23(2B) of the Act provides that the adjudicator’s determination is void in certain circumstances, arising out of post-appointment conduct, which do not include non-compliance with s 22.

Queensland has gone the other way. In Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd t/a CCA Winslow & Ors [2020] QSC 51, Dalton J held that an adjudication delivered outside the timeframe in section 85 of BIFSOPA is void. Her Honour reasoned that the legislature’s drafting choices showed “particularity and precision” about time, and that section 86’s express extension mechanism reinforced the importance of the section 85 limits. Dalton J distinguished the appellate decision in Ian Street Developer Pty Ltd v Arrow International Pty Ltd & Anor [2018] VSCA 294, observing that “the changes to the current Queensland legislation were made after all the extant interstate decisions on similar provisions”.

The Queensland Court of Appeal upheld that conclusion in Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd & Ors; Jones v Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd & Ors [2021] QCA 10, distinguishing the NSW and Victorian authorities on the basis that the BIF Act is worded differently.

In Demex Pty Ltd v Marine Civil Contractors Pty Ltd & Anor [2022] QSC 141, Bowskill CJ refused an interlocutory injunction restraining enforcement of an adjudication determination. The applicant said the determination was void for jurisdictional error because, although the adjudicator had decided within the statutory timeframe under section 21(3) of the NSW SOP Act (similar to section 85(1) of the BIF Act), the determination was not delivered to the parties until the next business day — outside that timeframe. Her Honour held there was a question to be tried but a weak one: under the NSW SOP Act the timing is not jurisdictional and a late delivery will not invalidate the determination, although in Queensland the position may be different on the basis of Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd t/a CCA Winslow & Ors [2020] QSC 51, given the differences between the NSW SOP Act and the BIF Act.

In Karam Group Pty Ltd as Trustee for Karam (No. 1) Family Trust v HCA Queensland Pty Ltd & Ors [2022] QSC 290, Williams J held that an adjudicator will have failed to decide within time if they do not also do everything required by section 88 of the BIF Act, including notifying the parties that the decision had been made. The adjudicator there made the decision in time but did not notify the parties until after the section 85(1) deadline. The court held the failure to notify meant the decision was not made within time and was invalid.

‘Gives’

The deadline in section 85(2) is keyed to the day the adjudicator “gives” the response or the reply. See section 102 for further commentary on that distinction.

Section 25A(3) – ‘minimum consideration period’

Under BCIPA, section 25A(1) provided that an adjudicator must not decide an application before the end of the “minimum consideration period”. Under section 25A(3), that period is, at a minimum, the period in which the respondent may give an adjudication response under section 24A.

Before the Amendment Act, the equivalent restriction in the former section 25(1) read:

An adjudicator must not decide an adjudication application until after the end of the period within which the respondent may give an adjudication response to the adjudicator.

That restriction was considered in Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA Pty Ltd) [2007] QSC 333, where Chesterman J held that, where the respondent was not entitled to give an adjudication response (because no payment schedule was served on the claimant within time), the adjudicator did not have to wait five business days before delivering a decision:

The applicant therefore could not give an adjudication response. Was the second respondent nevertheless obliged to wait for the expiration of the five days? The answer must be negative. Such a construction of the Act would achieve no purpose. There was no period within which the applicant could give an adjudication response because of s 24(3). Section 25(1) is not dealing with abstractions. It provides the procedure that an adjudicator must follow in particular cases. The time within which an adjudicator may or may not decide an application will depend upon the particular facts on which the Act operates. Where a respondent may not deliver an adjudication response the adjudicator does not have to pretend that he can, and wait for a period during which nothing can happen. The adjudication was not premature.

The former section 25(1) restriction has now been replaced by the “minimum consideration period” under section 25A(3). Given the parallels, it is an open question whether Chesterman J’s reasoning in Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA Pty Ltd) [2007] QSC 333 will apply where the respondent has no entitlement to deliver an adjudication response.

Under the NSW Act, in Emag Constructions Pty Ltd v Highrise Concrete Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 903, Einstein J held that an adjudication determination delivered on the last day on which an adjudication response might have been lodged was invalid.

Section 86 BIF Act – Extending time for deciding adjudication application
86 Extending time for deciding adjudication application

(1)  The claimant and respondent for an adjudication application may, before or after the end of the maximum period for deciding the application under section 85(1), agree in writing that the adjudicator has additional time to decide the application.

(2)  Despite section 85(1), an adjudicator may decide an adjudication application within a longer period if—

(a)  the claimant and respondent have informed the adjudicator that they have agreed under subsection (1) that the adjudicator has additional time to decide the application; or

(b)  the application relates to a complex payment claim and, in the opinion of the adjudicator, the claimant and respondent have failed to reach an agreement mentioned in subsection (1).

(3)  The longer period is—

(a)  if subsection (2)(a) applies—the additional time agreed to by the claimant and respondent under subsection (1); or

(b)  if subsection (2)(b) applies—5 business days after the time the adjudicator would otherwise have to decide the application under section 85(1).

Extending time (s86)

Section 86 is the only valid pathway to a longer decision period. The claimant and respondent can agree in writing — before or after the section 85(1) deadline — that the adjudicator has more time. For a complex payment claim, where the parties have failed to reach an agreement, the adjudicator can take an extra five business days. Where the parties agree, the longer period is whatever they agreed; where the adjudicator extends for a complex claim, it is five business days on top of the section 85(1) deadline.

Additional time to decide adjudication

In Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd t/a CCA Winslow & Ors [2020] QSC 51, Dalton J said in obiter that the parties’ power under section 86(1) to extend the adjudicator’s time must be regarded as the legislature expressly giving the parties to an adjudication the “power to confer additional jurisdiction on the adjudicator in circumstances where he or she would not otherwise have jurisdiction, i.e. in circumstances where the original jurisdiction conferred had expired because no decision had been delivered within the statutory time.”

Practice tips

  • Treat any request from the adjudicator for further submissions as time-critical; respond on the deadline set, and if the other side is given a chance to comment, expect to be given the same opportunity in return.
  • If the adjudicator appears to be heading towards a result on a basis neither party put forward, write in immediately and ask to be heard on it — BM Alliance and Ausenco show that’s where natural justice cases are won or lost.
  • Do not assume silence equals consent to an extension of time. Under section 86 you need a written agreement; tactically, claimants should consider agreeing to a short extension rather than risking a void Queensland determination after Galaxy.
  • Diary the section 85 deadline carefully — 10 business days for standard claims, 15 for complex, measured from the response date — and remember that in Queensland, late delivery (or late notification under Karam) can void the determination.
  • If you are the respondent and have lost the right to give a response, do not assume the adjudicator must wait out the five days; Minimax suggests they can decide sooner.
  • If the adjudicator calls a conference, prepare it as an informal meeting, not a hearing — you have no automatic right to legal representation, and missing the conference will not stop the adjudicator deciding without you.
