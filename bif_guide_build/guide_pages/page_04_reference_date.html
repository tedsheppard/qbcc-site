Chapter 3 · Sections 67 & 70

Reference date

The reference date is the engine that drives the BIF Act’s payment regime. Section 67 tells you when a reference date arises — either by the contract or by a statutory default — and section 70 tells you that, from each reference date, a contractor or supplier is entitled to a progress payment. If there is no available reference date, there is no valid payment claim, and any adjudication that follows is at risk of being set aside.

Section 67 – Meaning of reference date

Section 67 BIF Act – Meaning of reference date
67 Meaning of reference date

(1)  A reference date, for a construction contract, means—

(a)  a date stated in, or worked out under, the contract as the date on which a claim for a progress payment may be made for construction work carried out, or related goods and services supplied, under the contract; or

(b)  if the contract does not provide for the matter—

(i)  the last day of the month in which the construction work was first carried out, or the related goods and services were first supplied, under the contract; and

(ii)  the last day of each later month.

(2)  However, if a construction contract is terminated and the contract does not provide for, or purports to prevent, a reference date surviving beyond termination, the final reference date for the contract is the date the contract is terminated.

Section 67 broadly mirrors the previous definition under Schedule 2 of BCIPA, with two textual tweaks: it drops the words “undertaken to be carried out” and reduces “named month” to “month”. The earlier case law on the BCIPA wording remains a reliable guide.

The real change is sub-s (2). It creates a statutory final reference date on the day of termination where the contract is silent, or where the contract tries to stop reference dates surviving termination. The explanatory notes describe this as closing a loophole exposed by the High Court’s decision in Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd (in liq) v Lewence Construction Pty Ltd:

Clause 67 is a new provision defining the term ‘reference date’ for the purposes of this chapter. The definition of reference date also provides for when a construction contract is terminated and does not provide for a reference date surviving beyond termination. In such cases, the final reference date for when payment claim may be made is the day the contract is terminated.

“Stated in, or worked out under, the contract”

Paragraph (a) of the definition is the contract limb. The reference date is whatever date the contract states, or whatever date can be calculated by applying facts to a formula in the contract. In State of Queensland v T & M Buckley Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 265 at [17]–[19], Margaret Wilson J explained the difference between the two:

By paragraph (a) of the statutory definition, “reference date” means a date “stated in” or “worked out under” the contract. Whether a date is one “stated in” the contract depends on the proper construction of the words used in the contract without reference to extrinsic evidence (with the possible exception of evidence of the matrix of facts).

According to the Oxford English Dictionary Online, one of the meanings of “work out” is —

“To go through a process of calculation or consideration so as to arrive at the solution of (a problem or question), to solve; also, to reckon out, calculate.”

Just as a mathematical problem may be solved or “worked out” by applying values to a given formula, so may a “reference date” be “worked out under the contract” by applying facts to a formula found within the contract. In my view counsel for the first respondent’s submission ignores the true import of the expression “worked out under”. If it were correct, there would arguably be no distinction between a date “stated in” the contract and one “worked out under” it, because in each case the date would be one ascertained by construction of the contract without reference to extrinsic evidence.

That “worked out” idea was taken further in Lean Field Developments Pty Ltd v E & I Global Solutions (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor [2014] QSC 293, where Applegarth J (at [30]) accepted that a reference date can be calculated by reference to facts or post-contract conduct — including conduct that is within the power of one party to bring about:

[30] The term “worked out” is not defined in the Act. The ordinary meaning of “worked out” in this context connotes a process of calculation. Absent some contextual basis to not apply that ordinary meaning, the statutory definition of “reference date” would seem to allow a reference date to be worked out by applying a formula to facts that are capable of being ascertained. For example, it might provide that a claim for a progress payment may be made 14 days after event X occurs. There is no compelling reason why the fact in question should not be something within the power of a party to bring about. For instance, a contract might provide that a claim for a progress payment may be made 60 days after the contractor commences the works and every 60 days thereafter. The first and subsequent reference dates are worked out by reference to the conduct of the contractor after the formation of the contract in commencing work. In such a case the existence of a reference date is conditioned by the conduct of one of the parties. There is nothing absurd about such an outcome, and it might be inconvenient if the parties could not agree such a provision. It would be odd if the parties could not negotiate, subject to limits imposed by s 99, a formula to work out a reference date which applied to the conduct of one of the parties, rather than rely on the default position set by sub-paragraph (b) of the statutory definition. Subject to limits imposed by s 99 and the assumption in s 12 that there will be a reference date under a construction contract, the parties should be free to agree provisions for the timing of claims for progress payments by reference to post-formation events, including events that depend on a party’s conduct.

Applegarth J in Lean Field (at [31]) went further again, holding that a reference date can even be worked out by reference to an event which is not certain to occur (such as a milestone or the submission of a particular document). The freedom to draft creative reference-date triggers is, however, limited by section 200 of the Act (the equivalent of section 99 of BCIPA), which can invalidate provisions that “effectively prevent or inordinately delay a reference date arising”:

[34] Section 99 may operate to invalidate certain contractual conditions which effectively prevent or inordinately delay a reference date arising. For example, it has been said that if a contract provided for yearly reference dates the provision would be so inimical to the statutory entitlement as to be avoided by a provision like s 99. However, the possibility that certain contractual provisions, which effectively prevent the accrual of a reference date, may be invalidated by s 99 of the Act does not require “worked out” to be given the narrow meaning for which the first respondent contends. It simply means that s 99 may have work to do in the context of an Act which grants an entitlement to progress payments and which, in the section defining the entitlement, assumes that there will be a reference date.

Two NSW decisions confirm that contractual sleight of hand cannot rescue an early payment claim. In Regal Consulting Services Pty Ltd v All Seasons Air Pty Ltd [2017] NSWSC 613, McDougall J held that a deeming clause — saying that a payment claim made before the reference date is taken to have been made on the reference date — does not validate a claim served too early. The NSW Court of Appeal upheld that view in All Seasons Air Pty Ltd v Regal Consulting Services Pty Ltd [2017] NSWCA 289. The same point was followed in Castle Constructions Pty Ltd v N & R Younis Plumbing Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 225, where Parker J set aside an adjudicator’s determination because the claim was served before the contractual reference date of the 28th day of the month had arrived.

The contract drafting really matters. In Waco Kwikform Ltd v Complete Access Scaffolding (NSW) Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 1702, Justice Stevenson held that a clause attracts the benefit of the equivalent of section 67(1)(a) only if it expressly nominates the date by which a claim for a progress payment may be made. A clause that simply gave the claimant a discretion to “choose if and when to make a claim” was not enough — the contract failed to fix a reference date, and the default in paragraph (b) had to do the work instead.

By contrast, in Whitehorse Box Hill Pty Ltd v Alliance CG Pty Ltd and Anor [2022] VSC 22, Stynes J upheld an adjudicator’s determination on a final payment claim where a deed of amendment created an additional reference date eight days after execution “for either party to make a claim under the contract”. The respondent argued the deed only covered monthly progress claims; Stynes J disagreed, finding that the word “claim” on its plain reading captured both monthly progress claims and the final payment claim, and that the parties could have used narrower language if that was their intent.

In Argyle Building Services Pty Ltd v Dalanex Pty Ltd (No 2) [2022] VSC 452, Delany J held that if “surgical intervention” (rewriting the wording of the contract) is required for an adjudicator to determine the reference date, then there is no “express provision” for a reference date and the statutory default applies (the last day of the named month).

“Named month” and the statutory default

If the contract does not deal with reference dates, paragraph (b) supplies a default: the last day of the month in which work was first carried out, and the last day of each later month. In the NSW context (which uses “named month”), Veer Build Pty Ltd v TCA Electrical and Communication Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 864 held that “named month” takes its statutory meaning under the Interpretation Act 1987 (NSW), section 21 — January, February, March, April, May, June, July, August, September, October, November or December. That view was approved in Broadview Windows Pty Ltd v Architectural Project Specialists Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 955 at [34] (McDougall J).

Some construction work needs to have been carried out in the relevant month for a reference date to arise on the last day of that month: Veer Build Pty Ltd v TCA Electrical and Communication Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 864 at [45] (Darke J), citing Grid Projects NSW Pty Ltd v Proyalbi Organic Set Plaster Pty Ltd [2012] NSWSC 1571 at [23]–[39].

The default is also limited by the “one claim per reference date” rule. In Dickson Developments Precinct 5 Pty Ltd v Core Building Group Pty Ltd [2024] FCA 86, Jackman J held an adjudication determination void because the only available reference date had already been used for an earlier payment claim. Section 75(3) of BIF (the Queensland equivalent of s 15(5) of the ACT Act) prevented the second claim. Drawing on Dualcorp Pty Ltd v Remo Constructions Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 69 at [65] (cited with approval in Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd (in liq) v Lewence Construction Pty Ltd [2016] HCA 52), his Honour emphasised that a document purporting to be a payment claim that is in respect of the same reference date as a previous claim is not a payment claim under the Act and does not attract the recovery regime under the Act.

Inferring or varying a reference date

An adjudicator cannot infer a reference date from “probability” or commercial habit. In Burrell v JGE Machinery [2014] NSWSC 32, McDougall J found a serious question to be tried that an adjudicator had acted without jurisdiction by determining the reference date on the basis of an inference from business practice.

Conversely, the parties’ post-contract conduct can vary when reference dates fall. In T & T Building Pty Ltd v GMW Group Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 211, Martin J found that the parties had agreed to vary the times for submitting progress claims, drawing the implication using the test in BP Refinery (Westernport) Pty Ltd v Shire of Hastings [1977] HCA 40; (1977) 180 CLR 266.

Reference dates after termination

Once a contract is terminated, only one payment claim can be made under the new statutory rule. In BCFK Holdings Pty Ltd v Rork Projects Pty Ltd [2022] NSWSC 1706, Stevenson J confirmed that the equivalent NSW provision (s 13(1C) of the NSW SOP Act, similar to s 67(2) BIFSOPA) limits the claimant to a single post-termination payment claim. The reasoning drew heavily on the Second Reading Speech, where the minister explained that the new subsection was aimed at closing the loophole identified by the High Court in Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd (in liq) v Lewence Construction Pty Ltd.

The same point was applied in Taylor Construction Group Pty Ltd v Adcon Structural Group Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 723, where Rees J granted a permanent injunction restraining a subcontractor from making further adjudication applications based on second post-termination payment claims. Rees J confirmed that the equivalent provision applies strictly “on its terms” without discretion, unless the previous post-termination claim is withdrawn.

Section 70 – Right to progress payments

Section 70 BIF Act – Right to progress payments
70 Right to progress payments

From each reference date under a construction contract, a person is entitled to a progress payment if the person has carried out construction work, or supplied related goods and services, under the contract.

Section 70 simplifies the previous BCIPA wording: the entitlement now sits with “a person who has carried out construction work” rather than “a person [who] has undertaken to carry out construction work”. The explanatory notes say the new section “essentially replicates” section 12 of BCIPA, so the older authorities continue to apply.

The section gives effect to the entitlement that flows from each valid reference date. Without an available reference date there is no progress-payment entitlement to enforce, and a payment claim made when no reference date is available is liable to be set aside.

“From” each reference date

Queensland authorities have split on whether a payment claim must be made “on” the reference date or may be made “from” (in respect of, but after) it. In Reed Constructions (Qld) Pty Ltd v Martinek Holdings Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 345, Daubney J took the “on” approach (at [26]):

[26] Accordingly, I consider that the correct interpretation is that of the respondent; that the contract contemplates that the progress claim be made on the first of the month. On this interpretation, there can only be one opportunity per month to make a progress claim. However, as was noted by Fryberg J in the Rubikcon case, where a progress claim is not made on the first of the month, the right to claim has not been lost entirely, but merely postponed until the first day of the following month.

Fryberg J in Tenix Alliance Pty Ltd v Magaldi Power Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 7 expressly declined to follow Daubney J:

On the face of s 12, the opening words from each reference date would seem to suggest the existence of an entitlement on and after each date, which was a reference date. Magaldi submitted however, that that was not the correct approach.

In support of that decision, it relied upon the decision of Daubney J, in Reed Constructions (Qld) Pty Ltd v Martinek Holdings Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 345. As far as I am aware, that is the only case which could give any support to the submission.

…

His Honour reached the conclusion that the Act allowed a claim to be served only on each reference date, not on and after the date. He did so because he thought that acceptance of the applicant’s contention would mean that more than one progress claim could be served in relation to each reference date.

…

With great respect, I am not able to agree with his Honour’s interpretation. It is true that the Act does not permit two claims to be made in respect of the one reference date. It is, however a considerable jump from that proposition to conclude that the Act does not permit one claim to be made after the relevant reference date but in respect of that date. It does not seem to me that the words of the Act require such a conclusion.

Douglas J noted the divergence in Simcorp Developments and Constructions Pty Ltd v Gold Coast Titans Property Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 162 but did not need to resolve it. Mullins J then sided with the broader view in McNab Developments (Qld) Pty Ltd v more Construction Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 293, holding that the “from each reference date” wording in section 12 of BCIPA gave the claimant the right to make a statutory claim at any time after the relevant date, subject to the timing limit then in section 17(4) of BCIPA (now section 75 of the Act).

The NSW Act uses the phrase “[o]n and from” each reference date, and the NSW courts have read it that way: see Hill (as Trustee for Ashmore Superannuation Benefit Fund) v Halo Architectural Design Services Pty Ltd [2013] NSWSC 865; Walter Construction Group Ltd v CPL (Surry Hills) Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 266; All Seasons Air Pty Ltd v Regal Consulting Services Pty Ltd [2017] NSWCA 289.

Two Victorian decisions illustrate the consequence of getting it wrong. In Rapid Concrete Developments Pty Ltd v Lorem Constructions Pty Ltd [2020] VCC 858, Judicial Registrar Burchell held that nine separate payment claims were invalid because no valid reference date had arisen at the time of service. The contract said the date for payment was “in accordance with our payment terms being on or before 30 days from invoice date”, while the reference date would only accrue on completion — and completion had not yet occurred. The statutory default did not assist because the contract was not silent. And in Roar Fire Systems Pty Ltd v The Construction Studio [2020] VCC 1576, the court read the words “by the 25th day of each month” in their ordinary commercial sense — meaning “on or before” or “not later than” — and held that a reference date could properly arise on a date up to and including the 25th, so a claim issued in that window was valid.

In MPA Construction Pty Ltd v Profine Construction Pty Ltd (No 2) [2020] VCC 1254, Woodward J upheld a payment claim that included an element of advance payment, finding that work “on” or “from” the reference date can include work to be undertaken where the contract provides for advance payment, applying the principles in Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd (in liq) v Lewence Construction Pty Ltd [2016] HCA 52 at [67] and Seymour Whyte Constructions Pty Ltd v Ostwald Bros Pty Ltd (in liq) [2019] NSWCA 11 at [228].

Concurrent right to claim under the contract

A construction contract usually gives a separate, contractual right to make a progress claim, alongside the statutory right under the Act. The two can have different timing rules, and how strictly “on” the contractual date a claim must be served depends entirely on the wording of the clause.

The Queensland Court of Appeal took a narrow view of the contractual right in John Holland Pty Ltd v Costal Dredging and Construction Pty Ltd [2012] 2 Qd R 435. The clause in that contract was distinctively restrictive, and the Court held a claim could be submitted “only on each reference date.” That decision remains authoritative for contracts that adopt similarly restrictive language.

A different result followed in Reed Constructions (Qld) Pty Ltd v Martinek Holdings Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 345. Clause 37.1 and Item 28 of the contract provided:

“The Contractor shall claim payment progressively in accordance with Item 28. An early progress claim shall be deemed to have been made on the date for making that claim. Each progress claim shall be given in writing to the Superintendent and shall include details of the WUC [Work Under Contract] done and may include details of other moneys then due to the Contractor pursuant to the provisions of the Contract.”

…

“Time for progress claims: first day of each month for Work Under Contract to the last day of that month to which the claim relates.”

Daubney J read those words as requiring the progress claim to be served “on” the first day of the month, importing the word “on” into the clause:

[23] … The critical words found in Item 28 of the Schedule provide the time for making progress claims is the “first day of each month for Work Under Contract to the last day of that month to which the claim relates” (emphasis added). There is no evidence to suggest that this contractual provision was altered by agreement between the parties.

…

[26] Accordingly, I consider that the correct interpretation is that of the respondent; that the contract contemplates that the progress claim be made on the first of the month. On this interpretation, there can only be one opportunity per month to make a progress claim. However, as was noted by Fryberg J in the Rubikcon case, where a progress claim is not made on the first of the month, the right to claim has not been lost entirely, but merely postponed until the first day of the following month.

That part of Reed Constructions was distinguished a year later in Tenix Alliance P/L v Magaldi Power P/L [2010] QSC 7 (where the contract supplied no reference date at all, and the focus was on the statutory entitlement rather than the contractual one). The Queensland District Court expressly rejected the Reed Constructions approach to contractual entitlement in CKP Constructions Pty Ltd v Gabba Holdings Pty Ltd [2016] QDC 356. McGill SC DCJ remarked, in obiter, that the clause before him did not properly address what happens when a progress claim is made late, and he held the entitlement arose on the day stipulated under the contract (at [35]):

“My conclusion is that the correct interpretation of the contract is that a right to make a claim arose on the 25th of each month, but that there was nothing in the contract which provided that that right had to be exercised, if at all, only on that day, or that if a claim in respect of that month was submitted after that day, it stood as an early claim for the following month.  In effect, I am being asked to imply such a restriction into the contract, and I consider there are no grounds for doing so.  In my opinion, the right to make a claim under cl. 37.1 does not have to be exercised on the day specified, so long as it is clear that what is being claimed involves an exercise of the right in relation to the particular specified day for a month.”

Preconditions to progress payments and to reference dates

The case law draws an important distinction between two situations:

(1) preconditions imposed on the making of a payment claim where a reference date has arisen; and

(2) where a reference date is conditional and does not arise until certain conditions are fulfilled.

Applegarth J explained the distinction in Lean Field Developments Pty Ltd v E & I Global Solutions (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor [2014] QSC 293 at [66]:

[66] There is a distinction between two categories of case. The first is where a reference date has arisen and the contract purports to require a payment claim to meet certain conditions in making a payment claim or provides that if certain conditions are not met, the payment claim is ineffective and the reference date is deferred. In such a case, the statutory reference date is not conditional upon compliance with the condition, such as the delivery of a statutory declaration. Also the contract is ineffective to defer what would have otherwise been the contractor’s statutory entitlement to a progress payment from a reference date. The second category of case, and the one with which I am concerned, is where a reference date has not arisen because the contract purports to provide that no reference date will arise until certain conditions are fulfilled. Authorities about the first category of case do not determine what the position should be in the second category.

Preconditions to the making of a payment claim

A contractual provision that defers what would otherwise be a claimant’s statutory entitlement — for example, by saying no payment claim is effective without a statutory declaration — is void. The Queensland Court of Appeal in John Holland Pty Ltd v Coastal Dredging & Construction Pty Ltd [2012] QCA 150; [2012] 2 Qd R 435 at 443 [21] (Fraser JA, White JA and Peter Lyons J agreeing) reached that conclusion under section 99(2)(b) of BCIPA, now section 200 of the Act. Applying John Holland, Applegarth J in McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 269 at [26] put the principle this way:

[26] If a “reference date” arises under a construction contract and a party has a statutory entitlement to a progress payment under the contract in relation to that reference date, then a contractual provision which would operate to defer what otherwise would have been the party’s statutory entitlement will be ineffective to do so since it would change the effect of a provision of the Act.

The same approach was followed in BHW Solutions Pty Ltd v Altitude Constructions Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 214, where Mullins J held that summary judgment was available even though the respondent argued the claimant had not provided a statutory declaration required by the contract.

Example:

  • John Holland Pty Ltd v Coastal Dredging & Construction Pty Ltd [2012] QCA 150; [2012] 2 Qd R 435. Clause 12.6 of the construction contract contained a number of warranties from the claimant on payment claims, including a warranty that payment claims would be in the format the respondent required and would include a statutory declaration. Failure to comply rendered the payment claim void and pushed the reference date to the same day in the following month. The Court of Appeal held clause 12.6 was void under section 99(2)(b) of BCIPA, now section 200 of the Act.

Preconditions to reference dates

The position is different where the contract makes the very arising of a reference date conditional. In John Holland, Fraser JA observed that the provisions about payment claims, schedules and adjudication do not bear on the date the statutory entitlement accrues:

None of those provisions bear upon the date upon which the statutory entitlement to progress payments accrues. Bearing in mind the statutory object and the role of s 12 and the definition of “reference date” in giving effect to that object, those provisions are incapable of justifying an implication that the date upon which the statutory entitlement to a progress payment accrues may be qualified by contractual provisions other than those captured by the unambiguous terms of the definition of “reference date”.

The qualifier — “other than those captured by the unambiguous terms of the definition of ‘reference date’” — means the parties do retain some scope to condition when a reference date arises. The boundary between a permissible drafting choice and an invalid one was explored in State of Qld v T & M Buckley Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 265 (which turned on the contractual right rather than the statutory right) and again in Lean Field Developments Pty Ltd v E & I Global Solutions (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor [2014] QSC 293, where Applegarth J said:

The purpose of the Act and the role which s 99 plays in constraining the contractual freedom of parties to modify the entitlements which the Act confers means that the parties do not have an unconstrained freedom to contract about when a reference date will arise. The Act does not allow the contract to impose onerous conditions which make a reference date more of a theoretical possibility than an actuality. Likewise, the example given by Hodgson JA, of a contract which provides for an annual reference date, would seem to be inimical to the purpose of the Act and invalidated by s 99.

The extent to which a particular condition is contrary to the Act or purports to change the effect of the Act depends upon its content and practical consequences.  In assessing the validity of such a condition a useful inquiry is whether it facilitates or impedes the purpose of the Act.  A provision which has the purpose of regulating contractual rights to progress payments may not be appropriate to condition a statutory right to a progress payment.  A condition is likely to be contrary to the Act or unjustifiably change the effect of the Act’s provisions where it does not facilitate a statutory entitlement to progress payments or the resolution of payment claims made under the Act.  This is likely to be the case where the condition impedes the making of a payment claim with no corresponding benefit in achieving the Act’s purpose.

The inquiry into validity requires the identification of the condition or conditions in the absence of which there would be a statutory entitlement to a progress payment.  Even a condition which has some utility in a contractor making a payment claim and receiving a progress payment may be excessively onerous and be invalid because of its unjustified effect in denying a party what otherwise would be a statutory entitlement.  A condition which has no significant utility in terms of the scheme created by the Act may be invalid, not because it is particularly onerous, but because it impedes a statutory entitlement without any corresponding benefit.

Applegarth J revisited the issue in BRB Modular Pty Ltd v AWX Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 218, refining the test:

I had occasion to consider the operation of s 99 of the Act in Lean Field Developments Pty Ltd v E & I Global Solutions (Aust) Pty Ltd and observed that in assessing the validity of a condition, a useful inquiry is whether it facilitates or impedes the purpose of the Act. That observation was not intended to place a gloss upon s 99 or to be a substitute for the words of the statute. I accept BRB’s submission that a contractual provision could not be contrary to the Act simply because it does not further the objects of the Act. In considering whether a provision of a contract is contrary to the provisions of the Act or otherwise is ineffective by reason of s 99, it is necessary to be specific about how the Act and its operation are said to be affected by the contractual provision. As I observed in Lean Field, the extent to which a particular condition is contrary to the Act, or purports to change the effect of the Act, depends upon its content and practical consequences.  A provision which has the purpose of regulating contractual rights to progress payments may not be appropriate to condition a statutory right to a progress payment.  The condition is likely to be contrary to the Act or unjustifiably change the effect of the Act’s provisions “where it does not facilitate a statutory entitlement to progress payments or the resolution of payment claims made under the Act”. This is likely to be the case where the condition impedes the making of a payment claim with no corresponding benefit in achieving the Act’s purpose.

If, absent a contractual provision, a contractor would have a statutory entitlement to make a claim for a progress payment under the Act, then the provision will have the effect of excluding, modifying, restricting or otherwise changing the effect of the Act.  The position is otherwise where, even absent the provision, there would be no entitlement under the Act, for example, because no reference date will have arisen.

On the facts of BRB Modular, Applegarth J held the precondition (a statutory declaration on payments to subcontractors and suppliers) was void under section 99 of BCIPA because it imposed a burden with no “real utility in advancing the purpose of [BCIPA]” (at [70]).

The same line was followed in East End Projects Pty Ltd v GJ Building and Contracting Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 819, where Ball J considered a clause that required a “draft progress claim” to be served by the 25th of the month before any reference date would arise, followed by a preliminary assessment by the superintendent and then a final progress claim seven business days later. Ball J distinguished Lean Field and held the requirement was a “serious restriction” on the operation of section 8 of the NSW Act (the equivalent of section 70 BIFSOPA): if the claimant missed the 25th, it lost its entitlement to claim at all for that month, with no corresponding benefit to the statutory scheme. The clause was void.

Examples of preconditions to reference dates that have been held invalid:

  • State of Qld v T & M Buckley Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 265 — statutory declarations. Reference dates accrued monthly on the anniversary of commencement of work; the contract required a completed statutory declaration in a prescribed form to be provided before any payment claim. The Court held the reference date under BCIPA was the monthly anniversary, regardless of whether a statutory declaration had been provided.
  • BRB Modular Pty Ltd v AWX Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 218 — a precondition to a reference date in the form of a completed and signed statutory declaration on payments to subcontractors and suppliers was held not to be a valid precondition to a reference date under BCIPA.
  • J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Glavcom Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 126 — Ball J held that a clause making the occurrence of a reference date conditional on the provision of a subcontractor statutory declaration about other amounts owed was void under section 34 of the NSW Act because it added a condition to the right to obtain a progress payment without furthering the purpose of the Act.
  • Castle Constructions Pty Ltd v Ghossayn Group Pty Ltd [2017] NSWSC 1317 — “forming an opinion”. Stevenson J held that a contractual provision making the contractor’s right to payment contingent on a third party forming an opinion or certifying a state of affairs was void under section 34 of the NSW Act. His Honour drew the relevant criteria together: a precondition to a reference date will or may be invalidated where it imposes conditions on the occurrence of a reference date (Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Glavcom Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 126 at [26]); modifies or restricts the circumstances in which a contractor is entitled to a progress claim (Hutchinson at [26]); inordinately delays or effectively prevents a reference date from arising (Lean Field at [55]); unjustifiably impeaches the making of a payment claim or renders the statutory entitlement practically illusory (Lean Field at [68]); imposes onerous conditions which make a reference date more of a theoretical possibility than an actuality (Lean Field at [73]); or does not facilitate a statutory entitlement to a progress payment (Lean Field at [74]).

By contrast, a contractual condition that genuinely defines when work is “completed” for the purpose of triggering a reference date can be valid — and failing to satisfy it can leave the claimant with no reference date at all. In Wärtsilä Australia Pty Ltd v Primero Group Ltd [2020] SASC 162, Stanley J quashed an adjudication determination because the claimant had not satisfied the contractual requirements for “SW Completion” (which required, among other things, that test results be “provided” and that quality assurance documentation be “available for inspection”). The relevant clause read:

‘(2) the tests, inspections and communications required by this subcontract (including Schedule 3) to have been carried out before SW Completion have been carried out, passed and the results of the tests, inspections and commissioning provided to [Wärtsilä]

…

(8) the completed quality assurance documentation … is available for inspection by [Wärtsilä] at the Facility Land’

Primero had emailed a Dropbox link to the documents on 28 February 2020, but Wärtsilä could not download them in full. The adjudicator nonetheless treated 28 February 2020 as the reference date and awarded $15,269,674.30. Stanley J disagreed: providing a Dropbox link was not the same as “providing” the documents, and the documents could not be “inspected” at the facility land until they had been downloaded. Following Conveyor & General Engineering v Basetec Services & Anor [2015] 1 Qd R 265, his Honour held that the SW Completion preconditions had not been met, no reference date had arisen, and the $15M adjudication award was nullified.

“Under a construction contract” — work before the contract was made

Work that was carried out before the contract was made is not work “under” the contract and falls outside the Act: Walton Construction (Qld) Pty Ltd v Robert Salce [2008] QSC 235 at [22] (McMurdo J).

Example:

  • Novation as a new construction contract. Ball Construction Pty Ltd v Conart Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 124. The parties to a construction contract entered into a deed of assignment that assigned the obligations of one party to an incoming builder. The incoming builder later included amounts in a payment claim that related to work done before the deed. The Court held the deed of assignment created a new construction contract, so the incoming party had no entitlement in respect of work done before the deed — that work had not been carried out by the incoming party.

Payment claims after termination

Where a contract provides for reference dates, those dates do not continue to accrue after termination of the contract: Walton Construction (Qld) Pty Ltd v Corrosion Control Technology Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 67 at [42]–[45] (Peter Lyons J). His Honour explained:

The use of the expression “under a construction contract” found in the Queensland definition makes it somewhat more difficult to conclude that a reference date occurs after termination.  There is then no longer a contract “under” which there might be a reference date. The conclusion that a reference date does not occur after termination of a contract is, in my view, also consistent with the general nature of the payments for which provision is made by the BCIP Act, that is to say, payments which are of a provisional nature, made over the life of the contract.

The second difference which I have noted between the two definitions is also of significance.  The language used in the BCIP Act gives greater primacy to the provisions of the contract dealing with the making of a claim for a progress payment than does the language of the New South Wales Act.

For these reasons, I am not prepared to adopt the statement from the judgment of Hodgson JA in Brodyn as reflecting the effect of the definition of the expression “reference date” in the BCIP Act.

In my view, the contract provides for reference dates, by both enabling their identification, and by providing in effect that there is no right to make a progress claim after the contract is terminated under clause 44.10, with the consequence that no further reference date of this kind would then accrue.

The approach in Walton Construction (Qld) Pty Ltd v Corrosion Control Technology Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 67 was followed by the Chief Justice in McNab NQ Pty Ltd v Walkrete Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 128.

Importantly, where a reference date has accrued before termination but has not yet been used, the claimant does not lose the entitlement to make a payment claim referable to that pre-termination reference date: McNab Developments (Qld) Pty Ltd v MAK Construction Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 293 at [15]–[18] (Mullins J). Douglas J acknowledged the distinction between Walton and McNab in Kellett Street Partners Pty Ltd v Pacific Rim Trading Co Pty Ltd and Ors [2013] QSC 298. See also McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 269.

Termination for convenience

Where a contract is terminated for convenience and, as a matter of construction, the reference dates do not survive termination, the claimant cannot claim for work done between the last reference date and the date of termination. In Patrick Stevedores Operations No 2 Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2014] NSWSC 1413, Ball J said:

That leaves the question of what happens in relation to work done after the last reference date under the contract and before termination. In my opinion, the contract still provided a reference date in respect of that work at the time the work was performed because, at that time, the contract was still on foot. Consequently, there is no room for the operation of s 8(2)(b). The fact that the contract was terminated before the reference date in respect of that work arrived does not alter the position. It simply means that no reference date in respect of that work can arise. That result does not seem to me to be inconsistent with the purpose of the Security of Payment Act. As I have said, the purpose of the Act is not to ensure that a contractor is paid for work as soon as it is done. Nor do I think it is to ensure that a contractor is paid everything it is owed promptly. Rather, the purpose of the Act is to provide a practical mechanism to ensure that contractors receive progress payments for the work that they do. It seems to me that purpose is achieved even if, because of the way in which the contract and Act operate, the contractor is not entitled to use the mechanism provided for by the Act to recover a payment for work done shortly before termination.

See also Omega House Pty Ltd v Khouzame [2014] NSWSC 1837. On one view, however, a termination for convenience clause that provides for certain amounts to be payable on termination may itself create a reference date: Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373 (Ann Lyons J), rejecting a respondent’s submission based on Walton Construction (Qld) Pty Ltd v Corrosion Control Technology Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 67.

Work done up to that month

A reference date is not tightly tied to the period in which the underlying work was performed. In Spankie & Ors v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 355, the contract provided that the reference date was “the 28th day of each month for WUC done to the end of that month”. The Court of Appeal held the claimant could make a progress claim on the 28th in respect of work done up to the end of that month, whether or not the work was actually done in that particular month. Fraser JA said (at [24]):

[24] …there is no necessary relationship between the reference date upon which a claim is made and the time when the work the subject of the claim was carried out.

That principle does not rescue a claim where there is no available reference date at all. In St Hilliers Property Pty Limited v ACT Projects Pty Ltd and Simon Wilson [2017] ACTSC 177, Acting Judge Walmsley held that a new reference date can accrue where no construction work has been done in the current month, but only where a valid payment claim was not submitted in the previous month and work had been done in that previous month. Because the builder had performed no work in the current month and had already claimed for the prior month, no reference date was available and the claim was invalid. Following Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd v Lewence Construction Pty Ltd & Ors [2016] HCA 52, his Honour confirmed that a reference date is a prerequisite to a valid payment claim. The adjudicator was ordered to pay 20% of costs “because of his active role in the defence of his position”.

And in Hanson Construction Materials Pty Ltd v Brolton Group Pty Limited [2019] NSWSC 1641, Ball J observed in obiter (at [32]):

[32] “[t]here is no requirement in s 13 that the work in respect of which the payment claim is made must be performed before the claim is made. As the High Court held in Southern Han, there is a requirement that the date from which a progress claim may be made (the reference date) has occurred. Where the contract simply specifies that a claim for a progress payment may be made on a particular date each month, without tying that date to the performance of particular work, then, assuming the date has occurred, it is primarily a question for the Adjudicator what work may be made the subject of a progress claim in accordance with the contract by reference to that date.”

See also Fyntray Constructions Pty Ltd v Macind Drainage & Hydraulic Services Pty Ltd [2002] NSWCA 238 at [53] (Heydon JA).

Progress payments and insolvency

The interaction between the Act and insolvency law has produced inconsistent appellate decisions. The Victorian Court of Appeal in Façade Treatment Engineering Pty Ltd (in liq) v Brookfield Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd [2016] VSCA 247 held that section 9(1) of the Vic Act (the equivalent of section 70 of the BIF Act) does not create an entitlement to progress payments for persons in liquidation. Starting from the statutory text rather than the underlying purpose, the Court reasoned:

Section 1 states that the purpose of the Act ‘is to provide for entitlements to progress payments for persons who carry out construction work or who supply related goods and services under construction contracts’ (emphasis added).  Thus, the very first provision of the BCISP Act suggests that the protection it offers is targeted to persons who are acting pursuant to a construction contract.

…

Unlike s 1, s 9(1) refers to a person who ‘has undertaken’ to do certain things, rather than a person who is doing those things in the present.  But in its ordinary meaning, the word ‘undertake’ connotes an expectation of performance.  It is also significant that s 9(1), in creating a person’s entitlement to progress payments under the BCISP Act, emphasises that person’s undertaking to perform actions under the construction contract, rather than, say, the person’s status as a party to the contract.  The text of s 9(1) accords importance to the actions of carrying out construction work and supplying related goods and services.

The Court interpreted section 9(1) of the Vic Act in the following terms:

In our view, therefore, s 9(1) creates an entitlement to progress payments only for persons who have undertaken to, and continue to, carry out construction work or supply related goods and services.  The term ‘the claimant’ used throughout pt 3 is commensurately limited.  Consequently, the payment regime in pt 3 of the BCISP Act is not available to companies in liquidation, since such companies cannot carry out construction work or supply goods and services, and thus do not satisfy the requirements for ‘a claimant’.

At first instance, Vickery J had also identified an inconsistency between section 16 of the Vic Act (the equivalent of section 77 of the Act) and section 553C of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth):

A company to which s 553C of the Corporations Act 2001 applies, subject to s 553C(2), is precluded from entering any judgment pursuant to s 16(2)(a)(i) of the BCISP Act in respect of the debt due to it under that Act, and is further precluded from relying on s 16(4)(b) as a bar to a respondent under the BCISP Act from bringing any cross-claim against the company or raising any defence by way of set-off in relation to matters arising under the relevant construction contract.

The Court of Appeal affirmed that conclusion in obiter on the statutory inconsistency issue. Façade was followed by Reid DCJ in Tantallon Constructions Pty Ltd (in liq) v Santos GLNG & Anor [2016] QDC 324, who commented in obiter:

In my view, consideration of the provisions of BCIPA and of the decisions of Neller and of Facade make it clear that, certainly upon the plaintiff being placed into liquidation, and very probably upon its being earlier placed into administration, its ability to make and progress a claim under BCIPA was lost.

The opposite conclusion was reached in NSW. In Seymour Whyte Constructions Pty Ltd v Ostwald Bros Pty Ltd (In liquidation) [2019] NSWCA 11, the NSW Court of Appeal unanimously held that the NSW Act does operate for the benefit of claimants in liquidation, overturning Stevenson J at first instance. The Court held that section 8(1) of the NSW Act:

“read in light of in light of the analysis in Southern Han, creates an entitlement to a progress payment… on satisfaction of two conditions:

1.    A person has undertaken to carry out construction work under the contract; and

2.    A reference date under the contract has arisen.”

The Court of Appeal held that the first condition refers to a contractual undertaking, not the physical performance of work, and that the second condition (the arising of a reference date) is set by “contractual force” regardless of whether the claimant is still actually carrying out the work. There was nothing in the language of the NSW Act to support the conclusion that either condition could not be met by a company in liquidation.

Woodward J in MPA Construction Pty Ltd v Profine Construction Pty Ltd (No 2) [2020] VCC 1254 upheld a payment claim that included an element of advance payment, applying the principles in Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd (in liq) v Lewence Construction Pty Ltd [2016] HCA 52 at [67] and Seymour Whyte Constructions Pty Ltd v Ostwald Bros Pty Ltd (in liq) [2019] NSWCA 11 at [228] — the reference date can encompass work to be undertaken to be done where there is an element of advance payment in the contract.

Middleton J observed in obiter in Hastie Group Limited (in liq) v Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd (Formerly Brookfield Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd) [2020] FCA 1824 that the ratios in Façade and Seymour Whyte are inconsistent. His Honour noted that, where a respondent to a payment claim is in liquidation, failing to stay the claim or any judgment based on it would convert what is supposed to be an interim payment into a permanent one — contrary to the intention of the security of payment legislation that payments be on account.

Practice tips

  • Read the contract first. Look for the clause that fixes the time for progress claims (typically a date or a formula tied to a date) — that is your reference date under section 67(1)(a). If the clause is silent or unworkable, the statutory default in section 67(1)(b) applies (last day of the month of first work, then last day of each later month).
  • Never serve before the reference date has arrived. Deeming clauses that try to convert an early claim into a valid one will not save it (All Seasons Air, Castle Constructions v Younis Plumbing).
  • One claim per reference date. If you have already used a reference date, do not issue a fresh claim for the same date — bring forward unpaid amounts in the next claim instead (Dickson Developments; Southern Han).
  • Watch for “precondition” clauses (statutory declarations, draft claims, third-party certificates). Some are void under section 200 because they impede the statutory entitlement; others (like genuine completion criteria in Wärtsilä) can mean no reference date has actually arisen. Identify which category your clause falls into before serving.
  • On termination, check whether your contract’s reference dates survive. If not, section 67(2) gives you a single statutory reference date on the termination date — and only one post-termination payment claim is allowed (BCFK Holdings; Taylor Construction Group). A reference date that had already accrued before termination but was unused can still be relied on (McNab Developments v MAK).
  • Keep your scope clean. Work performed before the contract was made is not work “under” the contract (Walton v Salce; Ball Construction v Conart). On novation, the incoming contractor cannot claim for pre-novation work.
