Chapter 3 · Sections 94–98

Other rights and suspension of work

Sections 94 to 98 of the BIF Act deal with what happens around the edges of an adjudication: when an adjudicator runs out of time, how the adjudicator is paid, who bears the cost, how an application is brought to an end, and the claimant’s right to down tools while waiting to be paid. For a project manager, these provisions matter because they determine whether you can keep the security-of-payment process moving when something goes wrong, and they create a powerful self-help remedy — lawful suspension — that can be deployed without going to court.

Section 94 – Claimant may make a new application

If an adjudicator accepts a referral but does not deliver a decision within the time required by section 85, the claimant is not stuck. Section 94 gives the claimant a fresh choice: ask the registrar to refer the original application to another adjudicator, or start over by lodging a new application under section 79.

Section 94 BIF Act – Claimant may make new application in certain circumstances
94 Claimant may make new application in certain circumstances

(1)  Subsection (2) applies if an adjudicator, who accepts a referral to decide an adjudication application (the original application), does not decide the application within the period required under section 85.

(2)  The claimant may do either of the following within 5 business days after the period mentioned in subsection (1)—

(a)  request the registrar refer the original application to another adjudicator; or

(b)  make a new adjudication application (the new application) under section 79.

(3)  If the claimant requests the registrar refer the original application to another adjudicator—

(a)  the registrar must refer the application to another adjudicator within 4 business days after the request is made; and

(b)  no fee is payable for referring the original application to another adjudicator.

(4)  Also, if another adjudicator accepts the referral, this division applies for the original application with the following changes—

(a)  the claimant must give the adjudicator a copy of his or her submissions included in the original application within 5 business days after the adjudicator accepts the referral;

(b)  the respondent must give the adjudicator a copy of his or her submissions included in an adjudication response, if any, for the original application within 5 business days after the adjudicator accepts the referral;

(c)  the response date for the application under section 85(2) is the day—

(i)  the adjudicator receives the copy of the submission from the respondent; or

(ii)  if the respondent does not give the adjudicator the copy of the submission within the period mentioned in paragraph (b)—immediately after the end of the period;

(d)  in deciding the adjudication application, the adjudicator must not consider any submissions other than—

(i)  the submissions mentioned in paragraph (a) or (b); or

(ii)  further written submissions asked for by the adjudicator under section 84(2)(b).

(5)  This division applies to the new application in the same way it applies to any other adjudication application made under section 79.

(6)  However, the claimant may, despite section 79(2)(b), make the new application within 5 business days after the claimant becomes entitled to act under subsection (2).

An “exhaustive” definition of the right

The Queensland Court of Appeal has held that the equivalent BCIPA provision “exhaustively defines” a claimant’s right to make a new adjudication application based on an earlier payment claim: Spankie v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 355, [29] (Fraser JA, Holmes and Chesterman JJA agreeing); Spankie & Ors v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2010] QSC 336. Following the Amendment Act, regard should also be had to the effect of section 35B of BCIPA (equivalent to section 97 of the Act).

New application after a previous decision is set aside

Whether a claimant can launch a new application after the previous adjudication decision has been set aside for jurisdictional error or breach of natural justice is not entirely settled. The competing views are canvassed in Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd v Ian James Ericson t/as Flea’s Concreting & Anor [2011] QSC 327, [139]–[144] (McMurdo J); Spankie v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 355, [29] (Fraser JA, Holmes and Chesterman JJA agreeing); and Cardinal Project Services Pty Ltd v Hanave Pty Ltd [2010] NSWSC 1367.

New application where the previous decision is void

The position is much clearer where the previous decision is void for being out of time. In Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd t/a CCA Winslow & Ors [2020] QSC 51, Dalton J held that the delivery of an adjudication decision outside the time limits in sections 85 and 86 of BIFSOPA gives rise to an immediate right in the claimant to bring a second adjudication application under section 94. The Court reasoned that the purpose of the BIF Act is to provide the claimant with “a prompt extra-curial decision on its progress claim; not a guarantee that its progress claim will be paid.”

That approach was reinforced in Sun Engineering (Qld) Pty Ltd v Registrar Appointed under section 150 of the Building Industry Fairness (Security of Payment) Act 2017 [2023] QSC 168, where Kelly J held that section 94(1) applies where the adjudicator has failed to make a decision within the allocated time. Kelly J focused on the words “if an adjudicator… does not decide the application within the period required under” section 85: once that condition is met, the claimant can request a new referral or make a fresh application under section 79.

Section 95 – Adjudicator’s fees

Adjudicators are paid for their work, but only if they actually deliver a decision in time. Section 95 sets out the entitlement, the joint and several liability of the parties, and the limited carve-outs from the “no decision, no fee” rule.

Section 95 BIF Act – Adjudicator’s fees
95 Adjudicator’s fees

(1)  An adjudicator is entitled to be paid for adjudicating an adjudication application—

(a)  the amount, by way of fees and expenses, agreed between the adjudicator and the parties to the adjudication; or

(b)  if no amount is agreed, the amount, for fees and expenses, that is reasonable having regard to the work done and expenses incurred by the adjudicator.

(2)  A regulation may prescribe the maximum amount for fees and expenses (the prescribed maximum) an adjudicator may be paid for adjudicating an adjudication application.

(3)  Despite subsection (1), an adjudicator is not entitled to be paid any amount that is more than the prescribed maximum.

(4)  The claimant and respondent are jointly and severally liable to pay the adjudicator’s fees and expenses.

(5)  The claimant and respondent are each liable to contribute to the payment of the adjudicator’s fees and expenses in equal proportions unless the adjudicator decides otherwise.

(6)  An adjudicator is not entitled to be paid any fees or expenses for adjudicating an adjudication application if the adjudicator fails to make a decision on the application.

(7)  An adjudicator does not fail to make a decision only because—

(a)  the adjudication application is withdrawn; or

(b)  the adjudicator decided he or she did not have jurisdiction to adjudicate the application; or

(c)  the adjudicator decided the application was frivolous or vexatious; or

(d)  the adjudicator refuses to communicate the adjudicator’s decision on an adjudication application until the adjudicator’s fees and expenses are paid.

(8)  Also, if a court finds that the adjudicator’s decision is void and unenforceable, the adjudicator is still entitled to be paid any fees or expenses for adjudicating the application if the adjudicator acted in good faith in adjudicating the application.

(9)  In this section—

adjudicating, an adjudication application, includes accepting, considering and deciding the application.

Statutory right to payment

The adjudicator’s entitlement to fees is statutory, not contractual. In Alucity Architectural Produce Supply Pty Ltd v Australian Solutions Centre; Alucity Architectural Produce Supply Pty Ltd v Paul J Hick [2016] NSWSC 608, Hammerschlag J dismissed a claim for restitution of fees said to have been paid for a total failure of consideration, observing in respect of section 28 of the NSW Act:

The relationship between the [applicant] on the one hand, and the authority and the adjudicator on the other, is statutory. The entitlement of the authority and the adjudicator and the authority to payment arises as a consequence of the [applicant] having invoked the procedures under the Act and because ss 28 and 29 give the authority and the adjudicator a statutory right to payment.

That entitlement, however, falls away if the adjudicator misses the statutory deadline. In Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd t/a CCA Winslow & Ors [2020] QSC 51, the Court held that an adjudicator who delivered a decision outside the timeframe in sections 85 and 86 was not entitled to any fees. Dalton J found that the adjudicator failed to act in good faith for the purposes of section 95(8) where, through his agent, he represented that the decision had been made within time when that was not the case.

On appeal in Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd & Ors; Jones v Galaxy Developments Pty Ltd & Ors [2021] QCA 10, the Queensland Court of Appeal held that the primary judge had erred in finding the adjudicator had acted in bad faith and made misrepresentations, because the adjudicator had not been afforded an opportunity to be heard on those allegations. Notwithstanding that error, the Court of Appeal upheld the conclusion that the adjudicator was not entitled to fees because the decision was not made within the timeframe required by the BIF Act. The Court confirmed that even if the determination had been valid, or if the parties had been willing and able to grant an extension, section 95(6) would still apply.

Section 96 – Deciding fees payable by claimant and respondent

Section 96 lets the adjudicator decide who pays what — both the proportion of the adjudicator’s own fees and expenses, and the proportion of the application fee for which the respondent must reimburse the claimant. In doing so the adjudicator must consider the parties’ conduct and may consider relative success, improper purpose, prospects of success, the respondent’s reasons for non-payment, withdrawal, and the time the adjudicator spent on discrete issues.

Section 96 BIF Act – Deciding fees payable by claimant and respondent
96 Deciding fees payable by claimant and respondent

(1)  When making a decision about an adjudication application, an adjudicator may also decide the following—

(a)  the proportion of the adjudicator’s fees and expenses to be paid by the claimant and respondent;

(b)  the proportion of the fee, payable for the adjudication application under section 79(2)(d), for which the respondent is to reimburse the claimant.

(2)  In making the decision, the adjudicator—

(a)  must consider the conduct of the claimant and respondent; and

(b)  may consider any of the following matters—

(i)  the relative success of the claimant or respondent in the adjudication;

(ii)  whether the claimant or respondent commenced or participated in the adjudication for an improper purpose;

(iii)  whether the claimant or respondent commenced or participated in the adjudication without reasonable prospects of success;

(iv)  the reasons given by the respondent for not making the progress payment the subject of the adjudication application;

(v)  whether an adjudication application is withdrawn;

(vi)  the services provided by the adjudicator in adjudicating the adjudication application, including the amount of time taken to consider discrete aspects of the amount claimed;

(vii)  another matter the adjudicator considers relevant in making the decision.

(3)  In this section—

adjudicating, an adjudication application, includes accepting, considering and deciding the application.

conduct, of the claimant and respondent, includes—

(a)  whether the claimant or respondent has acted unreasonably leading up to, or during, the adjudication; or

(b)  whether the respondent attempted to include new reasons in an adjudication response in contravention of section 82(4). new reasons see section 82(4).

Section 97 – Withdrawing from adjudication

An adjudication application can be ended in two ways: the claimant can serve a written notice of discontinuation, or the respondent can pay the amount stated in the payment claim — in which case the application is taken to have been withdrawn. The adjudicator’s entitlement to fees for work already done survives a withdrawal (see section 95).

Section 97 BIF Act – Withdrawing from adjudication
97 Withdrawing from adjudication

(1)  An adjudication application—

(a)  is withdrawn if the claimant has given a written notice of discontinuation to the adjudicator and respondent; or

(b)  is taken to have been withdrawn if the respondent has, before an adjudicator has decided the application, paid the claimant the amount stated in the payment claim the subject of the adjudication application.

Note—

Despite the withdrawal of an adjudication application an adjudicator is still entitled to be paid fees for considering the application, see section 95.

(2)  If subsection (1)(b) applies, the claimant must as soon as practicable inform the adjudicator that the adjudication application has been withdrawn because of payment.

Refusal to communicate the adjudicator’s decision and deemed withdrawal

In Karam Group Pty Ltd as Trustee for Karam (No. 1) Family Trust v HCA Queensland Pty Ltd & Ors [2022] QSC 290, Williams J held that an adjudicator will have failed to make a determination within the statutory timeframe if they fail to attend to all aspects specified in section 88 of BIFSOPA, including notifying the parties that the decision had been made. The adjudicator in that case made the decision in time, but did not notify the parties until after the time prescribed by section 85(1) had passed. The Court held the decision was invalid because it had not been made within the time required by the Act.

The deemed-withdrawal limb of section 97 was considered in Downer EDI Rail Pty Ltd v DT Infrastructure Pty Ltd [2025] QSC 212, where Treston J declared that an adjudication application was taken to be withdrawn under section 97(1)(b) because the respondent paid the sum for which the adjudicator’s decision was sought, even though it had not paid the total payment claim. Treston J emphasised that the process under the Act is intended to be fast and efficient. The words “the subject of the adjudication application” direct attention to the genuine issues in the payment claim, and it would be an onerous construction of the legislation if the adjudicator were required to consider matters not in dispute on the face of the application (in that case, some $8.7 million worth of claims) when the submissions on both sides had been confined to $681,445.37 worth. Treston J distinguished the reasoning of Hodgson JA in Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v JM Hargreaves (NSW) Pty Ltd (2005) 63 NSWLR 385.

Section 98 – Claimant’s right to suspend work

Section 98 is the BIF Act’s self-help remedy. A claimant who has not been paid — and who has given the requisite notice of intention to suspend under section 78 (failure to pay scheduled amount) or section 92 (failure to pay an adjudicated amount) — can lawfully stop work two business days after that notice. The right runs until payment is received and continues for another three business days after payment.

Section 98 BIF Act – Claimant’s right to suspend work
98 Claimant’s right to suspend work

(1)  A claimant may suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under a construction contract if at least 2 business days have passed since the claimant gave notice of intention to do so to the respondent under section 78 or 92.

(2)  The right conferred under subsection (1) exists until the day on which the claimant receives payment from the respondent of the amount mentioned in section 78(1) or 92(1), and continues for another 3 business days immediately following that day.

(3)  If, in exercising the right to suspend carrying out construction work or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract, the claimant incurs a loss or expense because the respondent removes any part of the work or supply from the contract, the respondent is liable to pay the claimant the amount of the loss or expense.

(4)  A claimant who suspends carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services under a construction contract under subsection (1) is not liable for any loss or damage suffered by the respondent, or by any person claiming through the respondent, because of the claimant not carrying out that work or not supplying those goods and services, during the suspension.

What counts as “payment”

The right to suspend ends only when the claimant has actually received payment. In Beckhaus Civil Pty Ltd v Brewarrina Shire Council No. 2 [2004] NSWSC 1160, Master Macready defined the verb “pay” in this context as follows:

To give (a person) what is due in discharge of a debt, or as a return for services done, or goods received, or in compensation for injury done; to remunerate, recompense.” In turn, ‘payment’ is defined as “[t]he action, or an act, of paying.” From this it can readily be concluded that the touchstone of the ordinary and natural meaning of the expressions ‘to pay’ and ‘receives payment’ in the present context is the act of a debtor transferring by way of satisfaction whatever is owed by him or her to a creditor.

…

Thus an analysis of both the general and specific purposes of the Act permits the court to identify an alternative construction to the ordinary meaning of ‘pay’ and ‘payment.’ That is that a claimant will not have ‘received payment’ pursuant to s 27(2), and that a respondent will still have failed ‘to pay’ pursuant to s 15(1)(b), in circumstances where the claimant is unable to obtain the benefit of that payment due to conditions, such as the provision of a secured bank guarantee, placed on its receipt. The adoption of such a construction does not constitute a ‘re-writing’ of ss 15 and 27 as proscribed by the dictum of Dawson J above, but is rather a departure from the ordinary meaning of ‘payment’ in circumstances where the context and purpose of its use demands such a modification.

How long the right to suspend lasts

In On Forbes Developments Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 163, Crowe AJ held that, after an adjudication determination in favour of a claimant, the claimant’s right to suspend work continues until the respondent gives an unequivocal acknowledgement of the discharge of the adjudicated amount. The claimant in that case had suspended work under section 26(2)(b) of the ACT Act (equivalent to section 98(1) of BIFSOPA) following the respondent’s failure to pay the adjudicated amount. A subsequent expert determination under the construction contract concluded that the claimant in fact owed the respondent more than the adjudicated amount. The respondent argued the right to suspend ended on the date of the expert determination. Crowe AJ rejected that submission, holding that the right continued until either the outstanding amount was set aside or the claimant received an unequivocal communication acknowledging discharge of the payment claim, the adjudicated amount or, in this case, the provisional judgment debt. On the facts, constructive receipt of the adjudication amount being set aside occurred when consent orders were made on the day of the hearing, and the claimant’s right to suspend then ended three business days later.

Practice tips

  • If your adjudicator misses the section 85 deadline, diarise the 5-business-day window in section 94(2) and decide quickly whether to ask the registrar to refer the application to another adjudicator (no further fee) or to file a fresh application under section 79.
  • Before suspending under section 98, make sure a compliant notice of intention to suspend has actually gone out under section 78 or section 92, and do not down tools until at least 2 business days have passed from service of that notice.
  • Treat the right to suspend as continuing until you have actually received payment plus a further 3 business days — bank guarantees or conditional payments may not count as “payment” (see Beckhaus), and after an adjudication you are entitled to an unequivocal acknowledgement of discharge before the right ends (see On Forbes).
  • A valid suspension under section 98 protects the claimant from liability for the respondent’s loss or damage during the down-tools period, and entitles the claimant to recover loss or expense if the respondent uses the suspension as cover to remove work from scope — document any descoping in writing as it occurs.
  • If a respondent pays the amount in the payment claim that is the subject of the adjudication application before the decision issues, recognise that the application is taken to be withdrawn under section 97(1)(b) (see Downer EDI Rail) and notify the adjudicator as soon as practicable.
  • Remember that adjudicator fees are joint and several under section 95(4), and that under section 96 the adjudicator can shift the cost burden based on conduct — keep your adjudication conduct clean (no improper purpose, no late “new reasons” in a response) to avoid being loaded with the fees.
