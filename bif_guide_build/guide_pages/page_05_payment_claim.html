Chapter 3 · Sections 68 & 75(1)–(5)

Making a payment claim

Section 68 tells you what a payment claim is — the four pieces of content that turn an ordinary invoice into a statutory demand. Section 75 tells you how and when to give it: who you can serve, the six-month rule, and the “one claim per reference date” cap. Get either wrong and the adjudication that follows is exposed to being set aside. The two sections together are the gateway to the whole BIF Act payment regime.

Section 68 – The statutory definition

Section 68 BIF Act – Meaning of payment claim
68 Meaning of payment claim

(1)  A payment claim, for a progress payment, is a written document that—

(a)  identifies the construction work or related goods and services to which the progress payment relates; and

(b)  states the amount (the claimed amount) of the progress payment that the claimant claims is payable by the respondent; and

(c)  requests payment of the claimed amount; and

(d)  includes the other information prescribed by regulation.

(2)  The amount claimed in the payment claim may include an amount that—

(a)  the respondent is liable to pay the claimant under section 98(3); or

(b)  is held under the construction contract by the respondent and that the claimant claims is due for release.

(3)  A written document bearing the word ‘invoice’ is taken to satisfy subsection (1)(c).

Section 68 sets four mandatory elements: identify the work, state the amount, request payment, and include any information prescribed by regulation. Section 68(3) is the BIF Act’s practical concession to the construction industry — if the document carries the word “invoice”, the request-for-payment limb is automatically satisfied.

Two big departures from BCIPA shape this section. First, the BIF Act drops the requirement that a payment claim must state it is made under the Act. Second, it deems any “invoice” to be a request for payment. The explanatory notes confirm the policy: “A request for payment may be, for example, in the form of an invoice that contains a due date.” Together, these changes lower the bar for what counts as a payment claim — but the courts will likely continue to assess validity, in the words of Henry J in Camporeale Holdings Pty Ltd v Mortimer Construction Pty Ltd & Anor [2015] QSC 211, “not from an unduly critical viewpoint”.

How strictly must a claim comply?

Most of the case law was decided under BCIPA, which was stricter on its face. The BIF Act adopts a less demanding test — for example, by deeming “invoice” to satisfy section 68(1)(c). The cases below should be read with that in mind. In Camporeale Holdings Pty Ltd v Mortimer Construction Pty Ltd & Anor [2015] QSC 211, Henry J said:

It is well settled that because of the mandatory consequences of the BCIPA regime the content of a progress payment claim must strictly comply with the requirements of that statutory regime. However, whether a purported claim does satisfy the description of a payment claim is not to be approached from an unduly critical viewpoint and the only concern is whether the content of the purported claim satisfies the statutory description.

Henry J adopted the well-known passage from Chesterman J in Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA Pty Ltd) & Ors [2007] QSC 333 at [20]:

The Act emphasises speed and informality. Accordingly one should not approach the question whether a document satisfies the description of a payment schedule (or payment claim for that matter) from an unduly critical viewpoint. No particular form is required. One is concerned only with whether the content of the document in question satisfies the statutory description.

That approach was approved by the Queensland Court of Appeal as the correct approach to construing payment claims, consistent with equivalent jurisdictions: Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Traspunt No 5 Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 119; [2011] 2 Qd R 114, 122 [24] (Muir JA, Holmes and Chesterman JJA agreeing). The NSW Court of Appeal said much the same in Hawkins Construction (Australia) Pty Ltd v Mac’s Industrial Pipework Pty Ltd [2002] NSWCA 136, where Davies AJA held that the equivalent NSW provision:

should not be approached in an unduly technical manner… The terms used by subs (2) of s 13 are well understood words of the English language. They should be given their normal and natural meaning. As the words are used in relation to events occurring in the construction industry, they should be applied in a common sense practical manner.

See also Watpac Constructions (NSW) Pty Ltd v Charter Hall Funds Management Limited [2017] NSWSC 865, [63] (Palmer J); Mackie Pty Ltd v Counahan & Anor [2013] VSC 694, [46] (Vickery J); Protectavale Pty Ltd v K2K Pty Ltd [2008] FCA 1248, [11] (Finkelstein J); Leighton Contractors Pty Ltd v Campbelltown Catholic Club Limited [2003] NSWSC 1103, [54] (Einstein J). F.K. Gardner & Sons Pty Ltd v Dimin Pty Ltd [2006] QSC 243; [2007] 1 Qd R 10 is sometimes cited the other way for “strict” compliance.

Section 68(1)(a) – Identifying the construction work

The work has to be described well enough that the recipient can understand what is being claimed for. The test, as the Court of Appeal put it in T & M Buckley Pty Ltd v 57 Moss Rd Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 381 at [35] (Philippides J, Fraser and White JJA agreeing), is whether the claim provides “sufficient identification ‘to enable the respondent to understand the basis of the claim’”, quoting Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v Climatech (Canberra) Pty Ltd & Ors [2005] NSWCA 229, [25]. In Estate Property Holdings Pty Ltd v Barclay Mowlem Construction Ltd [2004] NSWCA 393, Hodgson JA (Mason P and Giles JA agreeing) explained why:

requires that a payment claim identify the construction work for which payment is claimed in the claim, not merely the construction work as a whole that is being carried out under the relevant construction contract. I think this is indicated by the words “construction work … to which the progress payment relates”; and strongly confirmed by the consideration that, unless a progress claim identified the particular work for which payment was claimed, it would be impossible for a respondent to provide a meaningful payment schedule supported by reasons.

The test is whether the description is reasonably comprehensible to the respondent. Finkelstein J in Protectavale Pty Ltd v K2K Pty Ltd [2008] FCA 1248 put it this way:

Nonetheless a payment claim must be sufficiently detailed to enable the principal to understand the basis of the claim.  If a reasonable principal is unable to ascertain with sufficient certainty the work to which the claim relates, he will not be able to provide a meaningful payment schedule.  That is to say, a payment claim must put the principal in a position where he is able to decide whether to accept or reject the claim and, if the principal opts for the latter, to respond appropriately in a payment schedule:  Nepean Engineering Pty Ltd v Total Process Services Pty Ltd (in liq) (2005) 64 NSWLR 462, 477; John Holland Pty Ltd v Cardno MBK (NSW) Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 258 at [18]–[21]. That is not an unreasonable price to pay to obtain the benefits of the statute.

That view was adopted by White J in Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Peet Beachton Syndicate Ltd [2011] 1 Qd R 17; [2009] QSC 376 and followed in Krongold Constructions (Aust) v SR RS Wales [2016] VSC 94, where Vickery J held a payment claim invalid for failing to identify the work with sufficient certainty — even though the principal was familiar with the work being claimed for. The principal’s background knowledge does not relieve the claimant of the need to describe the work in the claim itself.

Background knowledge can assist the analysis. The Court of Appeal in T & M Buckley said the “evaluation of the sufficiency of the identification takes into account the background knowledge of each of the parties derived from their past dealings and exchanges of documentation.” In Laimo Masonry Services Pty Ltd v TP Projects Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 127, Ball J added that the level of identification “will depend very much on the circumstances of the case, and may depend on what the terms of the contract between the parties require.”

Common-sense examples of sufficient identification in Queensland include ACP Properties (Townsville) Pty Ltd v Rodrigues Construction Group Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 45, where Bradley J held that an invoice for “labour fees and supply” sufficiently identified the work because the amount was modest and the principal was equipped to engage with the claim, and because contractual obligations to reconcile a quantity-surveying report cannot affect statutory validity. In A-Tech Australia Pty Ltd v Top Pacific Construction Aust Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 404, Parker J held that very short narratives (just “glazing and locks” and the relevant levels) were enough because the respondent’s correspondence had not suggested any difficulty understanding what was being claimed. And in Piety Constructions Pty Ltd v Megacrane Holdings Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 309, a document titled a “letter of demand” was held to be a valid payment claim because it stated it was a payment claim and sufficiently identified the work, on a fair reading of the “relatively undemanding” section 13(2) requirements.

By contrast, a description of work as “claim for completion of tilt panels” failed in Panel Concepts Pty Ltd v Tomkins Commercial & Industrial Builders Pty Ltd [2021] QDC 322. Porter DCJ found the word “completion” ambiguous — was it completion of the whole subcontract works or just a portion? — and applied KDV Sport: percentage-only descriptions are insufficient. In EnerMech Pty Ltd v Acciona Infrastructure Projects Australia Pty Ltd [2024] NSWCA 162, Basten AJA (Meagher JA and Griffiths AJA agreeing) made a useful jurisdictional point about the elements in section 13(2) (NSW equivalent of section 68(1)):

“Section 13(2) provides, in obligatory terms, three matters which a payment claim must contain. However, the respondents accepted that the use of the imperative did not necessarily indicate an essential precondition to validity, and did not do so in s 13(2). These are factors of which an adjudicator will need to be satisfied in the case of an adjudication. (In most cases, these matters will not be in issue)”.

His Honour also rejected the suggestion that a payment claim must be a claim for “construction work” (as defined) as a precondition to its validity (at [62]):

“Whether an amount is in fact payable will depend on the proper construction of the contract, identification of the work which has been carried out and a determination as to whether that work has already been paid for. These are matters to be determined by an adjudicator in the case of a dispute; they are not preconditions to the validity of a claim. If the entitlement is claimed to arise ‘under’ a construction contract, as required by s 13(1) of the Act, the soundness of a claim denied in the payment schedule is for the adjudicator. Use of the word ‘for’ to describe the required relationship between the amount and the basis of liability to pay is not a reason to substitute that word for the language of the statute, let alone to create a novel precondition to engagement”.

In Bridgeman Agencies Pty Ltd v S.E QLD Plumbing & Drainage Pty Ltd [2025] QSC 167, Kelly J (referring to section 68(1)(a)) said:

“the question whether the relevant construction work is identified sufficiently, cannot be answered by confining the inquiry to the actual document or documents comprising the payment claim.”

The same case dispels the narrow argument that any documentation referred to in a payment claim must be physically attached to it. Kelly J reiterated Brown J’s words in KDV Sport Pty Ltd v Muggeridge Constructions Pty Ltd [2019] QSC 178:

“the particular work [identified by way of percentage valuation] was being claimed from a zero base… the use of the percentage figure in this case was sufficient to identify the basis on which this part of the payment claim was being advanced.”

The KDV Sport rule on percentages

The Queensland decision in KDV Sport Pty Ltd v Muggeridge Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2019] QSC 178 is the leading authority on what happens when a claimant tries to use percentages of work completed to identify the construction work. Muggeridge served a payment claim for $2,365,432.00 supported by a Trade Breakdown Schedule with 51 categories, each with a percentage complete, plus variation claims that had not been submitted. The adjudicator awarded $802,198.59. Brown J set the determination aside, holding the claim was not reasonably comprehensible to a reasonable principal because:

  • the claim did not contain enough information for parties with background knowledge of the Trade Breakdown Schedule to identify the actual work, understand the claim, and respond;
  • a reference to a completed percentage of a category does not identify the actual work the claim relates to;
  • the principal could not reconcile various amounts of work claimed against the cost; and
  • the variation claims were unsupported and undescribed.

Brown J said (at [36]):

“While it may be accepted, as Muggeridge contends, that KDV is aware of the content of the Trade Breakdown Schedule, where there are some 51 categories of work in a sizeable contract with a number of components in the work to be undertaken, merely referring to the category of work does not identify the construction work itself to which the claim relates.”

And at [38]:

“The lack of description of the work and the inability to reconcile the figures to the amount claimed support the fact that the construction work which is subject to the claim cannot be identified with any certainty and the claim does not purport in a reasonable way to identify the work.”

Her Honour added that requiring the respondent to reconstruct prior claims to work out what the current claim was for would be unreasonable within the 10-day response window (at [49]):

“Requiring a respondent to a payment claim to undertake that kind of research which would be subject to error and within the time constraint of 10 days under the Payments Act leads me to conclude that the payment claim does not identify the construction work to which that claim relates and does not fulfil the requirements of s 17(2) of the Payments Act.”

KDV Sport has been applied repeatedly. In Denbrook Constructions Pty Ltd v CBO Developments Pty Ltd [2022] QDC 184, Porter DCJ refused summary judgment because a payment claim consisting largely of cost-spreadsheet extracts and references to previous claims did not identify the work. In Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2023] QSC 290, Williams J held a payment claim invalid under section 68(1)(a) and accepted Brown J’s “big-ticket items” gloss: if items insufficiently described add up to a substantial portion of the total claimed, the claim is invalid. The disputed items in Iris Broadbeach all fell into one of three categories: no description of the actual work; not supported by material provided at or before delivery of the payment claim; or not supported by background knowledge through past dealings.

Crucially, Williams J also confirmed that compliance with section 68(1)(a) is judged at the time of delivery — not on the basis of additional information provided afterwards. Following Basten JA in Coordinated Construction, the claim must be reasonably comprehensible to the other party at the moment of service, with supporting documents either accompanying the claim or already provided. Past practice alone is not enough to allow later information to cure a defective claim, absent something like an estoppel.

Documents in support, errors, and severance

A payment claim does not have to physically attach every supporting document, provided enough is incorporated by reference for the work to be reasonably identifiable. The Queensland Court of Appeal in T & M Buckley at [41] said:

Baxbex Pty Ltd does not stand for the proposition implicit in the judge’s reasons that a failure to provide documents referred to in a payment claim or attachment thereto per se results in there being a deficiency in identification for the purposes of s 17(2)(a). In the present case, the fact that the attachment referred to certain supplier invoices without also attaching them did not detract from the identification that was provided being sufficient.

Other useful examples:

  • Incorporation of an earlier spreadsheet: Unifor Australia Pty Ltd v Katrd Pty Ltd atf Morshan Unit Trust t/as Beyond Completion Projects [2012] QSC 252. A tax invoice referred to an earlier “Summary of Costed Variations” spreadsheet. The Court held the invoice sufficiently incorporated the spreadsheet, making the claim reasonably comprehensible.
  • Particulars sent contemporaneously: Richard Kirk Architect Pty Ltd v Australian Broadcasting Corporation & Ors [2012] QSC 177. An email attached a without-prejudice letter, a notice of dispute, and a payment-claim document. The Court held the contemporaneous notice of dispute formed part of the payment claim.

Including an obviously irrelevant or unrelated item does not necessarily invalidate the whole claim. As Douglas J noted in Matrix Projects (Qld) Pty Ltd v Luscombe [2013] QSC 4 at [24], “the inclusion of a claim for an obviously irrelevant item for what is not construction work does not deprive the adjudicator of [jurisdiction]”. A similar view appears in Walter Construction Group Ltd v CPL (Surry Hills) Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 266 at [66]–[67] (Nicholas J). Severance is also available. In Tenix Alliance Pty Ltd v Magaldi Power Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 7, Fryberg J said the inclusion of one aspect incorrectly does not invalidate the whole claim. Vickery J in Gantley Pty Ltd v Phoenix International Group Pty Ltd [2010] VSC 106 reached the same conclusion at [115]–[116], holding that severance serves the purposes of the Act and accommodates “reasonable flexibility”.

However, where the amounts in question are not for “construction work or related goods and services” at all, that can be fatal. In Grocon (Belgrave St) Developer Pty Ltd v Construction Profile Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 409, Ball J permanently restrained an adjudication on a claim that included two bank guarantees the respondent had recourse to under the contract — those amounts were not for construction work, and the small remaining valid amounts did not save the claim.

Section 68(1)(b) – Stating the claimed amount

The amount can be stated in more than one way, and in Camporeale Holdings Pty Ltd v Mortimer Construction Pty Ltd & Anor [2015] QSC 211, Henry J accepted that a claim does not need to expressly state a single “claimed amount” where a covering email and several invoices, taken together, make the total obvious:

The applicant submitted the covering email here should have cited a single total amount claimed. However, it is obvious when the documents are considered together that the amount claimed was the total of the four amounts stated in the invoices. The absence of a statement of the mathematical total of those amounts does not make the statements of the four amounts considered in combination any less a statement of the amount of the progress payment claimed. I am fortified in reaching that conclusion by s 32C of the Acts Interpretation Act 1954 (Qld) which provides that in an act words in the singular include the plural and vice versa. Thus a payment claim which states the amounts of the progress payment claimed to be payable will comply with s 17(2)(b), as long as those amounts are stated as part of one payment claim.

It is also not a requirement that the amount stated be the correct amount. Fryberg J in De Neefe Signs Pty Ltd v Build1 (Qld) Pty Ltd; Traffic Technologies Traffic Hire Pty Ltd v Build1 (Qld) Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 279 held that “[i]t is sufficient to state the amount which the claimant claims to be payable”, and his Honour was not persuaded that “payable” means “due and payable”.

Section 68(1)(c) – Requesting payment

Section 68(3) does most of the work here: a document bearing the word “invoice” is taken to satisfy section 68(1)(c). Where the document does not use the word “invoice”, the request can sometimes be inferred from the surrounding content — but the recent Court of Appeal authority warns against stretching that too far.

In Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 16, Wilson J held that a payment claim that did not expressly request payment and did not use the word “invoice” nonetheless impliedly requested payment, because it stated it was submitted under the BIF Act, was titled “progress claim”, and included a “Total Progress Claim Value for the Month”. Her Honour was also fortified by the supporting statutory declaration, which began:

“I am making this statutory declaration in connection with the payment of Progress Claim No. 7 dated 28 February 2023 pursuant to the Contract.”

That implied-request approach was disapproved by the Queensland Court of Appeal in MWB Everton Park Pty Ltd as trustee for MWB Everton Park Unit Trust v Devcon Building Co Pty Ltd [2024] QCA 94. Dalton JA (Brown J and Kelly J agreeing) gave three independent reasons why the disputed documents were not payment claims: they did not identify the construction work (applying KDV Sport); they did not state a claimed amount; and they did not request payment of the claimed amount. On the third point, the Court expressly disagreed with Iris Broadbeach:

[36] … In [Iris Broadbeach] the judge was prepared to find that the documentation impliedly included a request for payment… While I cannot see any relevant distinction between the documents in this case and the documents in Iris Broadbeach Business, I am afraid that I am not persuaded by the analysis in that case. I do not think it can be said that the documents comprising the email and attachments of 30 June 2023 made a request for payment, independently of identifying the amount the subject of the claim.

The practical lesson: do not rely on an implied request. Either bear the word “invoice” (and pick up the section 68(3) deeming) or include an explicit request for payment of the claimed amount.

No need to say the claim is made under the Act

The BIF Act has abolished the BCIPA requirement that a payment claim state on its face that it is made under the Act. The Minister’s second reading speech put it this way:

These reforms expedite the payment system, by providing that all invoices are automatically deemed to be statutory claims for payment. These provisions will restore accountability on respondents, by restoring the penalty which used to apply when there was a failure to provide a payment schedule when one was due

Although the requirement is gone in Queensland, it remains in some other jurisdictions, and the BCIPA case law remains useful background. In Leighton Contractors Pty Ltd v Campbelltown Catholic Club Ltd [2003] NSWSC 1103, Einstein J said in the NSW context: “There is no room for ambiguity of any type and it is critical that the recipient of a payment claim be made aware by the terms of that claim that the provisions of the Act have been engaged.” Substantial compliance was generally enough: see Eco Steel Homes Pty Ltd v Hippo’s Concreting Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 135 at [8] (Daubney J). Even small typographical errors in the title of the Act did not defeat a claim: in Hawkins Construction v Mac’s Industrial Pipework [2001] NSWSC 815, Windeyer J said:

This argument might have had some weight in 1800. In 2001, an argument based on the absence of the word “and” and the letters “ustry” has no merit. It should not have been put.

Section 75 – The mechanics of giving the claim

Section 75 BIF Act – Making payment claim (sub-sections (1)–(5))
75 Making payment claim

(Sub-sections (6) and following — final payment claims and post-termination claims — are addressed on a separate page.)

(1)  A person (the claimant) who is, or who claims to be, entitled to a progress payment may give a payment claim to the person (the respondent) who, under the relevant construction contract, is or may be liable to make the payment.

(2)  Unless the payment claim relates to a final payment, the claim must be given before the end of whichever of the following periods is the longest—

(a)  the period, if any, worked out under the construction contract;

(b)  the period of 6 months after the construction work to which the claim relates was last carried out or the related goods and services to which the claim relates were last supplied.

(3)  If the payment claim relates to a final payment, the claim must be given before the end of whichever of the following periods is the longest—

(a)  the period, if any, worked out under the relevant construction contract;

(b)  28 days after the end of the last defects liability period for the construction contract;

(c)  6 months after the completion of all construction work to be carried out under the construction contract;

(d)  6 months after the complete supply of related goods and services to be supplied under the construction contract.

(4)  The claimant can not make more than 1 payment claim for each reference date under the construction contract.

(5)  A payment claim may include an amount that was included in a previous payment claim.

Section 75 creates the right to make a payment claim and rolls together the procedural provisions that used to sit in sections 17 and 17A of BCIPA. The validity requirements (the four section 68(1) elements) are separate: section 75 governs how and when the claim is given.

Bona fides — not a requirement

A payment claim does not have to be made in good faith. The Court of Appeal said as much in Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Traspunt No 5 Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 119; [2011] 2 Qd R 114. Muir JA explained:

…The Act enables the respondent to a payment claim to serve a payment schedule and for the payment claim and payment schedule to go to adjudication.  The adjudicator addresses and determines the merits of the parties’ dispute as articulated in the payment claim and payment schedule.  No enquiry into the claimant’s bona fides is mentioned and it is difficult to see why a claimant’s bona fides should be any more fundamental to a valid payment claim than a plaintiff’s bona fides would be in relation to a valid writ, claim, or statement of claim.

In Bitannia, Basten JA, noted that, “… the beliefs or motivations of the plaintiff in proceedings have generally been treated as irrelevant, unless they reach the stage of an improper purpose” which would constitute an abuse of process.  An abuse of process arises where proceedings are brought, not for the purpose of prosecuting them to a conclusion, but to use them as a means of obtaining some advantage for which they are not designed, or some collateral advantage beyond that offered by the law. The “improper purpose” must be the predominant purpose.

Vickery J reached the same conclusion in 470 St Kilda Road Pty Ltd v Reed Constructions Australia Pty Ltd [2012] VSC 235 at [43]–[44]. The underlying NSW authority is Bitannia Pty Ltd & Anor v Parkline Constructions Pty Ltd [2006] NSWCA 238; (2006) 67 NSWLR 9. As Stevenson J held in The New South Wales Netball Association Ltd v Probuild Construction (Aust) Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 1339, the NSW position “does not support the proposition that the service of a payment claim involves the making of representations as to the truth of the matters contained in the payment claim.”

Section 75(1) – Who can be served

The respondent must be a person who is or may be liable under the relevant construction contract. The cases divide into two issues: who is the right legal entity, and through whom can that entity be served?

On the “is or may be liable” phrase, the Queensland Court of Appeal in Mansouri & Anor v Aquamist Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 209 at [13] held the words do not liberalise who can be a respondent — only the actual contracting counterparty qualifies:

Read in light of the definition of “construction contract”, s 12 of BCIPA gives the statutory right to a progress payment only to a person who “undertakes to carry out construction work for”, or to supply related goods and services to, another party under a contract, agreement or other arrangement. The provisions in Part 3 were designed to facilitate the enforcement of that statutory right, not to broaden it. Such a right is not established by proof only that a payment claim was served on a person who “may” be a party for whom the claimant undertook to perform construction work or to whom the claimant undertook to supply related goods and services. Rather, the effect of the expression in s 17(1) “under the construction contract concerned”, again read in light of the definition of “construction contract”, is that a payment claim may validly be served only on a person who is a party for whom the claimant undertook to perform construction work, or to whom the claimant undertook to supply related goods and services.

The NSW Court of Appeal took the same line in Grave v Blazevic Holdings Pty Ltd (2010) 79 NSWLR 132; [2010] NSWCA 324 at [26]–[28] (McDougall J, Allsop P and Macfarlan JA agreeing):

The person on whom the progress claim may be served is someone who, under the construction contract concerned, is or may be liable to make the payment. The words “is or may be liable” may be capable of referring, for example, to primary or secondary liability (the latter, for example, as guarantor). They may be capable of encompassing disputes as to quantification; and indeed, disputes as to whether, on the proper construction of the contract and the Security of Payment Act, any payment is due at all. However, whatever is the nature and amount of the liability sought to be enforced, it must be a liability “under the construction contract concerned”. If the proposed recipient of the payment claim is not a party to or liable under the construction contract, then it falls outside that statutory description.

In Collective Crane Hire Pty Ltd v ICR Steel Pty Ltd [2021] VCC 132, Woodward J softened the strictness of Grave: the gravamen is that the person served “must be someone who is not a stranger to the contract”, and any person who, under the contract, is at least arguably likely to be liable to pay can potentially be a respondent.

On agency for service, the cases turn on the contract:

  • Abigroup Contractors Pty Ltd v River Street Developments Pty Ltd [2006] VSC 425 — service on a quantity surveyor who was the principal’s agent under the contract was at least arguable.
  • The Owners Strata Plan 56597 v Consolidated Quality Projects [2009] NSWSC 1476 — a long course of dealing of serving claims on the superintendent supported a regime of valid service.
  • Metacorp Pty Ltd v Andeco Construction Group Pty Ltd [2010] VSC 199 — the contract defined the superintendent as the principal’s agent and prior claims had been served the same way; service was valid.
  • Penfold Projects Pty Ltd v Securcorp Limited [2011] QDC 77 — absent express agency, service on a construction manager was not valid under BCIPA.
  • Canadian Solar Construction Pty Ltd v Re Oakey Pty Ltd [2023] QSC 288 — Freeburn J held a payment claim was effectively served via the project manager and senior project personnel. Mode-of-service clauses are intended “to facilitate service, and not to restrict the means by which service might be effected”. Even though the respondent’s director did not personally receive the email, four of the five other recipients had authority to assess payment claims, agreeing with the reasoning in BCFK Holdings Pty Ltd v Rork Projects Pty Ltd [2022] NSWSC 1706 that “a party that actually receives a payment claim should not be entitled to assert that service did not ever happen because of a shortcoming, perhaps technical, in the manner in which the claimant purported to effect service”.
  • SJ Higgins Pty Ltd v The Bays Healthcare Group Inc [2018] VCC 805 — where the contract expressly excluded the superintendent’s authority to receive Act documents, service on the superintendent was invalid.

Section 75(1) – Who can claim

The phrase “a person…who is or who claims to be entitled” brings in those who claim entitlement, even if it later turns out the entitlement does not exist. There is some divergence between jurisdictions on how broadly “claims to be” reads. In Queensland, Lyons J in F.K. Gardner & Sons Pty Ltd v Dimin Pty Ltd [2007] 1 Qd R 10; [2006] QSC 243 held the claimant must be entitled (or claim entitlement) under section 12 of BCIPA (now section 70 of the Act) — the assertion of entitlement is not enough on its own.

In Victoria, Vickery J took a broader approach in Metacorp Pty Ltd v Andeco Construction Group Pty Ltd [2010] VSC 199:

In contrast, under the legislation as it now stands, the class of persons who may serve a payment claim has been extended to include persons ‘who claim to be entitled’ to a progress payment, in addition to those who may actually be so entitled. In my view, provided that a person makes a claim to be entitled to a progress payment, and that claim is made bona fide, the claimant is permitted to serve its payment claim pursuant to s. 14(1) of the Victorian Act, and this is so, whether or not there existed an actual entitlement to payment at the time when the payment claim was served.

The NSW Court of Appeal in Lewence Construction Pty Ltd v Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd [2015] NSWCA 288 read “or claims to be” broadly, with Sackville AJA concluding:

It is a strained interpretation of the introductory words to s 13(1) of the BCI Act (“[a] person referred to in section 8(1) who is or who claims to be entitled to a progress payment”) to read them as referring to a claimant who not only satisfies either sub-par (a) or sub-par (b) of s 8(1), but who is also able to show that the relevant reference date under the construction contract has in fact arrived. The very point of the procedure created by Pt 3 of the BCI Act is to establish a mechanism, in the event of a dispute, for an adjudicator to determine precisely that question.

In Canberra Drilling Rigs Pty Ltd v Haides Pty Ltd [2019] ACTCA 15, the ACT Court of Appeal held that “liability under a construction contract is something which may be claimed rather than established as a jurisdictional fact” — though the existence of the construction contract itself is a jurisdictional fact.

Two related points on early claims and improper claimants. In Cool Logic Pty Ltd v Citi-Con (Vic) Pty Ltd [2020] VCC 1261, Woodward J held that a payment claim served shortly before a reference date cannot be saved by a good-faith argument: the majority of the NSWCA in All Seasons applied Southern Han and held that “[a] statutory payment claim served before the relevant reference date is not served ‘on or from’ that reference date for the purpose of the NSW SOP Act, the effect being that the phrase ‘on or from’ when properly construed, is to be understood as meaning ‘on or after’ the relevant reference date.” Misdescriptions of the claimant’s name will not necessarily defeat a claim where the parties knew who was claiming: G W Enterprises Pty Ltd v Xentex Industries Pty Ltd & Ors [2006] QSC 399. And on assignment, the NSW Court of Appeal in Quickway Constructions Pty Ltd v Electrical Energy Pty Ltd [2017] NSWCA 337 held that a claimant who has assigned its payment rights to a third party (a financier) before service may lose standing to pursue the claim under the Act.

Section 75(2) – The six-month rule

For non-final claims, section 75(2) requires the claim to be given before the end of the longest of two periods: any contractual period, or six months after the relevant work was last carried out (or related goods and services last supplied). The wording is “must be given before the end of whichever of the following periods is the longest”.

The leading authority on what “construction work to which the claim relates” means in this context is Estate Property Holdings Pty Ltd v Barclay Mowlem Construction Ltd [2004] NSWCA 393. Hodgson JA rejected a narrow item-by-item interpretation in favour of a workable middle ground: the claim is in time if some of the work for which payment is claimed was done within the six-month window:

Consistently with this, in my opinion “construction work … to which the claim relates” in s.13(4)(b) is also the construction work for which payment is claimed in the claim; and accordingly, the requirement of s.13(4)(b) is that some of that construction work be carried out in the relevant twelve month period. Accordingly, in my opinion, the primary judge’s acceptance of the first of the three interpretations of s.13(4)(b) which he identified, both in this case and in Leighton, was incorrect.

However, in my opinion ss.13–15 of the Act do not provide any basis for dividing up the construction work to which the claim relates into items which may be considered discrete, and asking in respect of each such item whether some work was carried out in the twelve month period… In my opinion, there is no good reason to introduce an unclear distinction of this kind into the operation of the Act.

That approach has been followed in the ACT (Canberra Drilling Rigs Pty Ltd v Haides Pty Ltd [2018] ACTSC 282) and was foreshadowed in Pacific General Securities Ltd & Anor v Soliman & Sons Pty Ltd & Ors [2006] NSWSC 13; (2006) 62 NSWLR 421 at [37] (Brereton J).

An adjudicator cannot simply look the other way if the claim is out of time. In South East Civil and Drainage Contractors Pty Ltd v AMGW Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 45; [2013] 2 Qd R 189, Jackson J held that “a respondent’s failure to take the point of non-compliance with s 17(4) in a payment schedule does not authorise an adjudicator to ignore the point, where it is apparent on the face of the material which the adjudicator is obliged to consider under s 26(2)”.

Whether non-compliance with the time-limit destroys the claim entirely or is something that goes to jurisdiction has split authority. Fryberg J in De Neefe Signs [2010] QSC 279 held that “compliance is not a condition precedent to the existence of, nor an essential element of a valid payment claim”. The opposite was reached in Winyu Pty Ltd v King & Anor [2015] ACTSC 387, where Mossop AsJ declared an adjudication invalid because the claim was not given within the statutory window. In Iridium Developments Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd [2021] NSWSC 1601, Williams J held that an adjudicator’s opinion that the claim was within time is sufficient to enliven jurisdiction, even if the opinion is wrong — provided the opinion is lawfully formed. In Eq Constructions Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd [2021] NSWSC 1604, the same judge declared a determination void where the adjudicator answered the wrong question (whether some work had been performed in the period, rather than whether the claim related to such work). In Commercial Fitouts Australia Pty Ltd v Miracle Ceilings (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2020] SASC 11, the South Australian Supreme Court quashed a determination because the work claimed within the six-month window had already been paid for, leaving only out-of-time work.

Section 75(4) – One claim per reference date

Section 75(4) caps the claimant at one payment claim per reference date. The principle was established in Tailored Projects Pty Ltd v Jedfire Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 32; [2009] 2 Qd R 171, where Douglas J accepted that “where more than one payment claim issues for a reference date, the first must be considered to be the payment claim for the reference period so that any subsequent claims for that reference period are invalid”. See also Kellett Street Partners Pty Ltd v Pacific Rim Trading Co Pty Ltd and Ors [2013] QSC 298, where Payment Claim 10 was held to be the claim for the relevant reference date with the consequence that no other payment claims could validly be served.

Identical claims are not permitted. In Doolan v Rubikcon (QLD) Pty Ltd [2007] QSC 168; [2008] 2 Qd R 117, Fryberg J held that section 17 does not allow a claimant to serve identical payment claims:

“However, it is in my judgment reasonably clear that his Honour in saying that successive payment claims do not necessarily have to be in respect of additional work was not saying that successive payment claims may be identical.  That is because payment claims may include not only work but claims for goods and services and also claims for liability under section 33(3).”

The Court of Appeal in Spankie & Ors v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 355 confirmed, however, that re-claiming an unpaid amount in a later payment claim does not breach section 75(4): Fraser JA held the predecessor of section 75(5) was “wholly inapt” to restrict that practice. Re-agitation may still be impermissible on other grounds (issue estoppel, abuse of process). Importantly, multiple servings of the same claim are not multiple claims: Eco Steel Homes Pty Ltd v Hippo’s Concreting Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 135 at [10] (Daubney J). And reasonably supplementing a claim with extra material is permitted: in Amasya Enterprises Pty Ltd & Anor v Asta Developments (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor (No 2) [2015] VSC 500 at [86], Vickery J said “the re-sending of the same payment claim, even if reasonably supplemented with additional material and information, does not offend these objectives of the Act”.

A package of multiple invoices delivered together can constitute a single payment claim. In Tailored Projects Pty Ltd v Jedfire Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 32; [2009] 2 Qd R 171, the claimant served a covering document attaching 18 invoices. Douglas J held that, taken together, the documents were a single payment claim (at [18]):

To conclude otherwise would require the triumph of form over substance, even in an area where adherence to form and strict compliance with the Act is important.

The same view was taken in Camporeale Holdings, where Henry J held (at [29]):

The four invoices here were received as one set of documents in the one act of service as attachments to a single covering email. To interpret s 17 as requiring that the covering email here had to explicitly state the attachments formed part of one payment claim would be to ignore the collective state in which they were received and referenced by the covering email. Similarly, to interpret s 17 as requiring the covering email to state that it was made under the Act would be to ignore that each of the invoices stated they were made under the Act. Considered collectively, those invoices and the covering email constituted one payment claim which was stated to be made under the Act.

In Sought After Investments Pty Ltd v Unicus Homes Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 600, Ball J held that four separate invoices attached to a single delivery were a single claim where it was objectively clear the recipient understood one consolidated claim was being made. As his Honour put it, there is “no difference between receiving a single letter enclosing four invoices and a single document consolidating the four invoices or claims.” The same reasoning was applied in J.R. & L.M. Trackson Pty Ltd v NCP Contracting Pty Ltd & Ors [2019] QSC 201 (Ryan J) and in Modog Pty Ltd v ZS Constructions (Queenscliff) Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 1743.

The opposite happened in Spirito Development Pty Ltd v Sinjen Group Pty Ltd [2020] VCC 1368, where Woodward J held two invoices sent separately could not constitute one payment claim: “where emails attaching discrete invoices are separated in time even by only a few minutes, and there is nothing on their face to confirm that they constitute a single payment claim, I consider that they must be viewed as separate payment claims…”

In Taringa Property Group Pty Ltd v Kenik Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 298, Hindman J held a final invoice issued on 8 September 2023 was the only valid payment claim, despite a series of earlier emails containing eleven variation claims. The earlier emails lacked the features of a payment claim — a clear demand for payment and a supporting statement — and were properly construed as supporting documents. A payment claim must be objectively recognisable as a single demand for payment.

One contract, one claim

A payment claim must relate to one construction contract only: Matrix Projects (Qld) Pty Ltd v Luscombe [2013] QSC 4 at [17]–[18] (Douglas J); Rail Corporation of NSW v Nebax Constructions [2012] NSWSC 6 at [44] (McDougall J). In Acciona Infrastructure Australia Pty Ltd v Holcim (Australia) Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 1330, Hammerschlag J declared a determination void where each purchase order created a separate contract.

In Ausipile Pty Ltd v Bothar Boring and Tunnelling (Australia) Pty Ltd [2021] QSC 39, Wilson J initially declared a payment claim invalid for relating to two contracts, observing: “In my view, the [claimant’s] approach does not give effect to the purpose of the Act. It was the legislatures intention that a payment claim must only relate to one contract. If a payment claim in substances concerns more than one contract, that fact is fatal to its validity under the Act, whether or not the claimant makes this patent on the face of the payment claim.”

That decision was overturned by the Queensland Court of Appeal in Ausipile Pty Ltd v Bothar Boring and Tunnelling (Australia) Pty Ltd [2021] QCA 223. The Court of Appeal held the wet-hire arrangement was a variation, not a separate contract, and stated the broader principle:

“A payment claim should not be treated as a nullity for failure to comply with s 75(1) of the Act, unless that failure is patent on its face. Where a payment claim purports to be made under one contract, it is not rendered invalid simply because at a later time (either during the adjudication or otherwise) it is determined that part of the claim was, in fact, a claim under a different contract. Provided a payment claim is made in good faith and purports to comply with s 75(1) of the Act, the merits of that claim, including questions as to whether it complies with s 75(1), is a matter for adjudication after having been raised in a payment schedule. A recipient of a payment claim cannot simply sit by and raise that point later, if it is not put in a payment schedule in response.”

The NSW Court of Appeal took a similar line in BSA Advanced Property Solutions (Fire) Pty Ltd v Ventia Australia Pty Ltd [2022] NSWCA 82, observing in obiter that the “one contract” rule is not a strict precondition to validity under the NSW Act.

Section 75(5) – Re-claiming previously claimed amounts

Section 75(5) expressly permits a payment claim to include an amount that was included in a previous payment claim. The provision was previously covered by section 17(4) of BCIPA, and before that, the former subsections 17(5) and (6). In VK Property Group Pty Ltd & Ors v A A D Design Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 54, Boddice J said:

In any event, s 17(6) of the Act does not impose any restriction upon the generally expressed entitlement in s 17(1), namely an entitlement to claim an unpaid amount of work done earlier before an earlier reference date, whether or not it was claimed in an earlier payment claim. The effect of s 17(6) of the Act is merely to ensure that no implication may be drawn that s 17(5) precludes a claimant from making a payment claim for an unpaid amount claimed in a previous claim. There is no general implication in the Act against “re-agitation” of a payment claim in a subsequent payment claim where there has been no adjudication determination.

In Civil & Civic Corporation Pty Ltd v Nova Builders Pty Ltd [2023] ACTCA 30, the ACT Court of Appeal confirmed that the equivalent of section 75(5) allows a payment claim to include construction work that was the subject of a previous claim, provided the new payment claim is made from a new and valid reference date. The protection for the claimant is real: a decision not to seek adjudication of an earlier claim does not constitute an election to abandon it (Denham Constructions Pty Ltd v Islamic Republic of Pakistan (No 2) [2016] ACTSC 215 (Mossop AsJ); Harlech Enterprises Pty Ltd v Beno Excavations Pty Ltd [2022] ACTCA 41).

Withdrawing a payment claim

A payment claim can be withdrawn and replaced by mutual consent, which can be implied from conduct: NC Refractories Pty Ltd v Consultant Bricklaying Pty Ltd [2013] NSWSC 842 at [39] (Hammerschlag J); Veer Build Pty Ltd v TCA Electrical and Communication Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 864 at [32] (Darke J); Reitsma Constructions Pty Ltd v Davies Engineering Pty Ltd t/as In City Steel [2015] NSWSC 343 at [21] (Ball J). Whether a claim can be withdrawn unilaterally is doubtful. McDougall J in Kitchen Xchange v Formacon Building Services [2014] NSWSC 1602 at [17], [26] said:

Whether or not implied unilateral withdrawal is sufficient is a difficult question. It seems to me, in particular given the serious consequences that follow if a respondent does not reply to a payment claim by providing a payment schedule, that the circumstances must make it very clear to the respondent that a payment claim is to be withdrawn, if it is intended that withdrawal should occur.

Practice tips

  • Use the word “invoice” on the face of every claim. Section 68(3) deems the request-for-payment limb satisfied automatically — do not rely on a court reading in an implied request after MWB Everton Park v Devcon [2024] QCA 94.
  • Describe the work, do not just point to a percentage. After KDV Sport and Iris Broadbeach, percentages of categories on a trade-breakdown schedule are not enough on their own. Spell out what was done; where you must use a percentage, anchor it to a clearly described scope so the principal can reconcile it.
  • Get the recipient right. Serve the contracting counterparty, not a stranger to the contract (Mansouri; Grave). If you are serving an agent (superintendent, project manager, QS), check the contract expressly authorises that agent to receive Act documents — otherwise rely on a clear, documented course of dealing (Canadian Solar v Re Oakey).
  • Watch the six-month clock. Some of the work for which payment is claimed must have been carried out within six months of giving the claim (Estate Property Holdings v Barclay Mowlem). Diary the cut-off — out-of-time claims have been quashed for jurisdictional error.
  • One claim per reference date. The first claim for a reference date locks in your shot at that date; later claims for the same date are invalid (Tailored Projects; Kellett Street Partners). Roll unpaid amounts forward into the next reference-date claim under section 75(5) instead.
  • If you bundle invoices into one claim, make the bundling obvious. A covering email or letter that ties the invoices together as a single claim works (Tailored Projects; Camporeale Holdings; Sought After Investments). Sending invoices in separate emails minutes apart risks them being read as multiple claims (Spirito Development).
  • Stick to one contract per claim. Even after Ausipile (CA) softened the consequence of crossing contracts, a claim that patently mixes contracts is still vulnerable. Keep variations clearly framed as variations under the existing contract.
  • Do not serve early. A claim served before the reference date arises is not on or from that reference date and cannot be saved by a deeming clause or a good-faith argument (All Seasons; Cool Logic).
