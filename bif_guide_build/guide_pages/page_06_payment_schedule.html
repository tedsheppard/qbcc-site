Chapter 3 · Sections 69 & 76

Payment schedule

Section 69 sets out what a payment schedule is — the four content elements that turn a written response into the document the Act recognises. Section 76 sets out when and how to give it: the 15-business-day longstop, the contractual shorter period, and the consequences of saying nothing or saying too little. Get either wrong and the respondent is exposed to summary judgment for the full claimed amount, with no opportunity to litigate the merits in the adjudication that follows.

Section 69 – What a payment schedule is

Section 69 BIF Act – Meaning of payment schedule
69 Meaning of payment schedule

A payment schedule, responding to a payment claim, is a written document that—

(a)  identifies the payment claim to which it responds; and

(b)  states the amount of the payment, if any, that the respondent proposes to make; and

(c)  if the amount proposed to be paid is less than the amount stated in the payment claim—states why the amount proposed to be paid is less, including the respondent’s reasons for withholding any payment; and

(d)  includes the other information prescribed by regulation.

Section 69 carries forward the substance of section 18 of BCIPA, with section 76 now housing the procedural mechanics that previously sat in section 18 and section 18A. The four elements operate as a checklist — identify the claim, state the proposed amount, give reasons if you are paying less than the claimed amount, and include any prescribed information. There is no required form. There is no required title. There is no requirement that the document call itself a “payment schedule”.

Form and substance — the Minimax approach

The leading statement on form is from Chesterman J in Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA Pty Ltd) & Ors [2007] QSC 33. His Honour’s formulation has been applied repeatedly under BCIPA and continues to apply under section 69:

The Act emphasises speed and informality. Accordingly one should not approach the question whether a document satisfies the description of a payment schedule (or payment claim for that matter) from an unduly critical viewpoint. No particular form is required. One is concerned only with whether the content of the document in question satisfies the statutory description. To constitute a payment schedule the applicant’s email of 14 December had to:

(i)  identify the payment claim to which it related, and

(ii)  state any amount which the recipient of the payment claim proposed to make in response to it; and

(iii)  importantly, if that amount is less than the amount claimed the payment schedule it must state why it is less.

If these three criteria are satisfied the document will be a payment schedule. How they are expressed, with what formality or lack of it, and with what felicity or awkwardness, will not matter.

That passage was approved by the Queensland Court of Appeal in Neumann Contractors Pty Ltd v Traspunt No 5 Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 119 at [24] (Muir JA, Holmes and Chesterman JJA agreeing). The same approach was applied in Linke Developments Pty Ltd v 21st Century Developments Pty Ltd [2014] SASC 203 at [45] (Nicholson J): a document is sufficient if it “achieves the basic objective of putting the claiming party on notice as to how much the party making payment intends to pay with reference to the payment claim and the reasons why that amount is lower than that claimed, if that is the case”.

The label is irrelevant. In Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386 at [11] (Muir JA, Gotterson and Morrison JJA agreeing), the Court of Appeal confirmed that a document does not need to be described internally as a “payment schedule” to be one.

That said, informality is not a licence for sloppy drafting. The role of the schedule is to define the dispute. In Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Luikens & Anor [2003] NSWSC 1140, Palmer J said:

A payment claim and a payment schedule are, in many cases, given and received by parties who are experienced in the building industry and are familiar with the particular building contract, the history of construction of the project and the broad issues which have produced the dispute as to the claimant’s payment claim. a payment claim and a payment schedule must be produced quickly; much that is contained therein in an abbreviated form which would be meaningless to the uninformed reader will be understood readily by the parties themselves. A payment claim and a payment schedule should not, therefore, be required to be as precise and as particularised as a pleading in the Supreme Court. Nevertheless, precision and particularity must be required to a degree reasonably sufficient to apprise the parties of the real issues in the dispute.

The NSW Court of Appeal echoed this in Style Timber Floor Pty Ltd v Krivosudsky [2019] NSWCA 171, holding that the equivalent NSW provision “is not a licence for informality or an excuse for vague, generalised objections to payment.” A schedule asserting reliance on “many emails, photos, videos, back charges from builders and other trades, complains [sic] from my clients” failed because incorporated documents must be “identified with sufficient particularity so that the recipient of the schedule knew what was being incorporated.”

And meeting the section 69 elements does not always settle the question. In Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2013] QCA 386 at [24] (Muir JA, Gotterson and Morrison JJA agreeing) and in McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 223 at [48] (Boddice J), it was confirmed that a document meeting the requirements is not necessarily “the” payment schedule — the surrounding circumstances, including the parties’ course of dealing, can shape that question. The Court of Appeal at [23] said:

If, as the parties accepted, only one payment schedule may be served in response to a payment claim, as a general proposition, whether a document is a payment schedule is to be determined by reference to the contents of the document and, in some circumstances, any accompanying or supplementary document. The fact that, as is the case here, a later document is described as a payment schedule and the earlier one is not would not normally bear on whether the earlier document is a payment schedule. The above proposition is qualified because it may be possible that the later document has some evidentiary value. Take, for example, circumstances in which there has been a course of dealing between the parties in which documents such as payment claims and payment schedules have been clearly identified in a particular way and the document alleged by the recipient to be a payment schedule lacks such identification. It may well be that the later document provided and described as a payment schedule would constitute some evidence of adherence to and continuance of the previous course of conduct.

The Court of Appeal in Heavy Plant Leasing also said:

It follows from the foregoing discussion that I do not accept as correct the unqualified proposition that a document which meets the requirements of s 18 of the Act is necessarily a payment schedule. Section 18 does not so provide. It merely stipulates the matters which must be incorporated in a payment schedule…A document such as a payment schedule does not necessarily take its character only from its own form and content.

Boddice J in McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 223 took the same view of Minimax:

is not authority for a proposition that the first correspondence sent by a party following receipt of a payment claim is its payment schedule. Whether that response is the payment schedule will depend upon all of the circumstances of the case. Minimax is also not authority for the proposition that a document which does not purport to be a payment schedule must be considered to be a payment schedule if it was delivered within the statutory timeframe.

One consequence flagged in Heavy Plant Leasing at [37]: language that is “provisional in any way” or that suggests “a further and more detailed response to the payment claim would be forthcoming” can prevent an early document from qualifying as the payment schedule.

Useful examples of what works and what does not:

  • A “relatively informal” email. Gisley Investments Pty Ltd v Williams & Anor [2010] QSC 178. An email sent the same day a payment claim was received, referring to “paperwork, stating I owe you this outrageous amount of money” and saying that “whilst the job continues to remain unfinished, final payment is not yet owed”, was held to be a payment schedule.
  • An email about one component only. Denham Constructions Pty Ltd v Islamic Republic of Pakistan (No 2) [2016] ACTSC 215. Mossop AsJ held the email was not a payment schedule because it was directed at one variation forming only part of the claim and was “not sufficient to make it clear that it is a response to the payment claim as opposed to being a communication in relation to another document generated for the purposes of the underlying contract which happened to relate to one component of the payment claim.”
  • A letter that does not call itself a payment schedule. Springs Golf Club Pty Ltd v Profile Golf Club Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 344. The letter summarised an earlier meeting and confirmed the amount the claimant had been offered. It was held to be a payment schedule.
  • A letter enclosing a cheque with reasons for the shortfall. Linke Developments Pty Ltd v 21st Century Developments Pty Ltd [2014] SASC 203. The letter referred to the claim, enclosed a part-payment cheque and explained the lower amount — a valid payment schedule.
  • An email rejecting the claim but partially paying it. T & T Building Pty Ltd v GMW Group Pty Ltd & Ors [2010] QSC 211. Of five payment claims, claim 22 was met by an email saying “We do not agree with the above claim as the variations schedule is incorrect” and a part-payment of about half the claim. The Court held the part-payment showed the respondent did not object to the entire amount, and the email failed to state why the scheduled amount was less.
  • A second document served in time. Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2013] QCA 386. The respondent served documents on 6 March 2013 and 8 March 2013. The Court of Appeal held the 8 March document was “the” payment schedule — the first document satisfying section 18 is not necessarily the payment schedule.

Payment certificates

A payment certificate issued under the contract is not automatically the payment schedule for the purposes of the Act. In McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 223 at [49], Boddice J said:

The first respondent contended significance was to be attached to the reference in the 6 March document to the “payment certificate”, as Part B of the schedule to the subcontract provides that a payment schedule for the purposes of the Payments Act is a payment certificate. However, that provision allows a party to contend that the payment schedule delivered by it is a payment certificate under the contract. The provision does not mean every payment certificate must be the party’s payment schedule in respect of a payment claim.

The Queensland Court of Appeal returned to deeming provisions of this kind in RHG Construction Fitout and Maintenance Pty Ltd v Kangaroo Point Developments MP Property Pty Ltd & Ors [2021] QCA 117. At first instance, Dalton J had held a contractual clause deeming the superintendent’s assessment to be a payment schedule was void because the assessment was the superintendent’s opinion, not a statement of what the respondent proposed to pay. The Court of Appeal overturned that decision, holding that interpreting the contract otherwise would fail to give the document a business-like reading and would ignore the parties’ use of long-standing statutory terms; and that the superintendent’s schedule, when read with the deeming provision, meant the amount stated was the amount the respondent proposed to pay, satisfying section 69(b).

Section 69(a) – Identifying the payment claim

The schedule must identify the payment claim to which it responds. The bar is not high — in Gisley Investments Pty Ltd v Williams & Anor [2010] QSC 178, an email sent on the same day a payment claim was received, referring to “paperwork, stating I owe you this outrageous amount of money”, was a sufficient reference because there was “no other document identified with which it could have been confused”. In BCS Infrastructure Support Pty Ltd v Jones Lang Lasalle (NSW) Pty Ltd [2020] VSC 739, Stynes J held that a schedule referring to a letter of demand which contained the payment claim, with a figure clearly relating to the disputed amount, was enough.

However, identification cannot be skipped. In Melaleuca View Pty Ltd v Sutton Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2019] QSC 226, Brown J held a payment schedule failed because it did not identify the initial payment claim, observing that the section 69 obligation to “state” the amount carries a higher demand than the NSW Act’s “indicate”, and that the onus rests on the respondent to comply — even where the result looks harsh.

And on a more cautionary note, in Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA) Pty Ltd [2007] QSC 333, Chesterman J held a payment schedule invalid where the payment claim contained three items but the schedule provided reasons in respect of only two. His Honour said:

The machinery for prompt payment and enforcement of payment would break down if a document, said to be a payment schedule, took issue with part only of a claim but was silent as to what it proposed to pay in respect of the balance.

Section 69(b) – Stating the scheduled amount

Parliament has not formally defined “the amount of the payment, if any, that the respondent proposes to make”, but the cases continue to use the shorthand “scheduled amount”. As McDougall J explained in Cornerstone Danks Street v Parkview Constructions [2014] NSWSC 866 at [25], the scheduled amount is the overall amount the respondent proposes to pay (if any) — not a discrete figure for each item.

An amount of “nothing”, “nil” or “zero” is enough to satisfy section 69(b): see Barclay Mowlem Construction Ltd v Tesrol Walsh Bay Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 1232 at [15] (McDougall J) and Façade Treatment Engineering Ltd v Brookfield Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd [2015] VSC 41 at [37] (Vickery J), where an email asserting the payment claim was invalid was held to clearly state that nil was proposed.

The amount can also be inferred where the document, taken as a whole, makes clear no amount is to be paid: Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA Pty Ltd) [2007] QSC 333. But the inferred nil must address the whole of the claim. In Minimax, Chesterman J said:

There are two possible constructions of the 14 December email. The first is that the applicant did, in fact, object to paying anything for items 2 and 3, and proposed to pay nothing in respect of them. In that case for the document to be a payment schedule the applicant had to give reasons for its objection. The email is silent on this point. The second, more likely, construction is that the email does not address that part of the payment claim at all. It did not state the amount of the payment which the applicant proposed to make. If this be the true construction the email did not satisfy the second criterion in s 18 of the Act. On either view the 14 December email did not comply with the definition. The email is incomplete if it is intended to be a payment schedule. It had to address the claim made and not only a part of it. I think this is clear.

A payment schedule which complies with the Act will set out the amount it proposes to pay in response to a claim. By s 20 a respondent who does not pay the amount its payment schedule proposes to pay can suffer summary judgment in a court of competent jurisdiction and enforce the amount as a judgment debt. The machinery for prompt payment and enforcement of payment would break down if a document, said to be a payment schedule, took issue with part only of a claim but was silent as to what it proposed to pay in respect of the balance. The contractor could not enter judgment. The respondent’s reticence could frustrate the operation of the Act.

However, the respondent need not address every individual invoice in a multi-invoice claim. In Brimar Electrical Services Pty Ltd v Zi-Argus Australia Pty Ltd [2014] QDC 78, the Court held that although the respondent had to state why the overall scheduled amount was less than the claimed amount, there was no need to address each constituent invoice individually.

Two contemporary examples confirm the breadth of what counts as “stating” a nil. In ACP Properties (Townsville) Pty Ltd v Rodrigues Construction Group Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 45, Bradley J held that two emails saying the respondent considered no amount was payable in respect of the claimant’s invoices, and proposing to pay $nil under a contractual cap, were sufficient under section 69(b) and (c). In BCS Infrastructure Support Pty Ltd v Jones Lang Lasalle (NSW) Pty Ltd [2020] VSC 739, Stynes J held a nil amount can be inferred where the respondent does not dispute the claim’s validity and expresses an intention not to pay anything.

Two contrasting examples on conditional or settlement-style language:

  • Without-prejudice letters. National Vegetation Management Solutions Pty Ltd v Shekar Plant Hire Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 3. A “without prejudice” letter did not constitute a payment schedule because it stated no scheduled amount — it was merely an offer to settle.
  • Conditional payment. Tenix Alliance Pty Ltd v Magaldi Power Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 7. A document making payment conditional on two future events did not state that nothing would be paid, or that the stated amount would be paid; it failed to comply with section 18(2)(b).

Section 69(c) – Reasons for withholding payment

The schedule must state why any shortfall is being proposed, including reasons for withholding any payment. The leading statement on the function of those reasons is from Palmer J in Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Luikens & Anor [2003] NSWSC 1140:

The evident purpose of s.13(1) and (2), s.14(1), (2) and (3), and s.20(2B) is to require the parties to define clearly, expressly and as early as possible what are the issues in dispute between them; the issues so defined are the only issues which the parties are entitled to agitate in their dispute and they are the only issues which the adjudicator is entitled to determine under s.22. It would be entirely inimical to the quick and efficient adjudication of disputes which the scheme of the Act envisages if a respondent were able to reject a payment claim, serve a payment schedule which said nothing except that the claim was rejected, and then “ambush” the claimant by disclosing for the first time in its adjudication response that the reasons for the rejection were founded upon a certain construction of the contractual terms or upon a variety of calculations, valuations and assessments said to be made in accordance with the contractual terms but which the claimant has had no prior opportunity of checking or disputing. In my opinion, the express words of s.14(3) and s.20(2B) are designed to prevent this from happening.

Section 14(3) requires that if the respondent to a payment claim has “any reason” for “withholding payment”, it must indicate that reason in the payment schedule. To construe the phrase “withholding payment” as meaning “withholding payment only by reason of a set-off or cross claim” is to put a gloss on the words which their plain meaning cannot justify. The phrase, in the context of the subsection as a whole, simply means “withholding payment of all or any part of the claimed amount in the payment claim”. If the respondent has any reason whatsoever for withholding payment of all or any part of the payment claim, s.14(3) requires that that reason be indicated in the payment schedule and s.20(2B) prevents the respondent from relying in its adjudication response upon any reason not indicated in the payment schedule. Correspondingly,s.22(d) requires the adjudicator to have regard only to those submissions which have been “duly made” by the respondent in support of the payment schedule, that is, made in support of a reason for withholding payment which has been indicated in the payment schedule in accordance with s.14(3).

…

…For a respondent merely to state in its payment schedule that a claim is rejected is no more informative than to say merely that payment of the claim is “withheld”: the result is stated but not the reason for arriving at the result. Section 14(3) requires that reasons for withholding payment of a claim be indicated in the payment schedule with sufficient particularity to enable the claimant to understand, at least in broad outline, what is the issue between it and the respondent. This understanding is necessary so that the claimant may decide whether to pursue the claim and may know what is the nature of the respondent’s case which it will have to meet if it decides to pursue the claim by referring it to adjudication.

(emphasis original)

Provided the reasons are part of the schedule, BCIPA placed no further bar on their adequacy or sufficiency: Barclay Mowlem v Tesrol Walsh Bay [2004] NSWSC 1232, [26] (McDougall J). And failing to give a payment schedule at all is fatal to disputing a claim. In TFM Epping Land Pty Ltd v Decon Australia Pty Ltd [2020] NSWCA 93, the Court of Appeal dismissed each of the respondent’s grounds: variation claims arose under the contract and should have been disputed via a section 14 schedule (the NSW equivalent of section 69 BIFSOPA); a valid reference date existed under section 13(5) (equivalent to section 75(4) BIFSOPA), and the addition of post-reference-date interest was permissible; and the absence of a supporting statement under section 13(7) (a requirement not present in BIFSOPA) did not invalidate the claim.

Where reasons are given, the bar is whether they define the dispute. In BCS Infrastructure Support Pty Ltd v Jones Lang Lasalle (NSW) Pty Ltd [2020] VSC 739, Stynes J accepted that “the substantive dispute between parties with regards to pricing” was a valid withholding reason because the schedule clearly responded to the claim, the dispute scope was described with some particularity, and the claimant’s subjective view of whether a dispute existed was irrelevant.

Reasons that defer payment, rather than reject it, will not work. In Joye Group Pty Ltd v Cemco Projects Pty Ltd [2021] NSWCA 211, the NSW Court of Appeal overturned a District Court finding that a refusal to pay until work was complete was a sufficient withholding reason. The Court of Appeal held that “we will not pay your claim until…” says when payment may be made, not that any part of the claim is being rejected; that even where only one item is the subject of the claim, a refusal pending completion does not identify which items have been done by the reference date; and that a payment schedule is not to be reconstructed by reference to external materials, where there is no ambiguity, to give it a degree of particularity it did not have.

Two further issues on reasons — the requirement under section 69(c) to “state” reasons, and the incorporation of documents into a schedule — are addressed in the next two sections.

In Turnkey Innovative Engineering Pty Ltd v Witron Australia Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 981, Stevenson J considered whether a payment schedule was invalid if it failed to “indicate” reasons for withholding payment, for a specific part of the Payment Claim.

Relevantly, part of the Payment Claim was for amounts which were the subject of an increase to the Contract Sum – which the Principal had not confirmed. Stevenson J held that the words “please adjust your claim accordingly and resubmit for approval” was sufficient to indicate to the Contractor that it’s reasons for non-payment was that it disagreed with the repricing of the contract.

However, the Payment Claim also included a claim for variations. Stevenson J found that the Payment Schedule did not indicate at all why there was to be no payment for those aspects of the Payment Claim, nor any indication of any reasons for withholding payment. Stevenson J held that a payment schedule must address the entire Payment Claim, as the purpose of a Payment Schedule is to identify each of the amounts in dispute and why. On that basis, the Court held that the Payment Schedule was invalid.

In Roberts Construction Group Pty Ltd v Drummond Carpentry Services Pty Ltd [2024] VSC 246, Niall JA found that an email was not a payment schedule within the meaning of section 15 of the Vic Act (equivalent to section 69 of the QLD Act) on the basis that it did not provide a substantive reason for withholding payment as required by s 15(3) of the Vic Act (equivalent to section 69(c) of the BIF Act). Rather, it was held that ‘[t]he emails amount to little more than a bare assertion that further substantiation is required’, and Niall JA also observed at [98] that:

[98] When a respondent to a payment claim disputes the quantum of a payment claim and seeks that the claimant submit another ‘accurate realistic figure’, that does not state what it proposes to pay but invites a further, lesser claim to be made. The purpose of ensuring the timely payment of progress payments and narrowing the area of dispute would not be served by treating such a communication as sufficient to constitute the giving of reasons as required by s 15(3).

In Actif Concrete v Design Builders [2025] VCC 275, Lauritsen J held that an email requesting clarification about a payment claim did not constitute a payment schedule under sections 15(2)(b) and (3) of the Victorian Act (equivalent to sections 69(b) and (c) BIFSOPA). His Honour emphasised that for a document to qualify as a payment schedule, it must indicate the amount the respondent proposes to pay, if any, and provide reasons for withholding payment with sufficient particularity.

Lauritsen J held that the email, which stated “Could you expand on the difference between these 2 quotes as the first item has gone from 332,130 to 388,550 against the same wording: “the boys in Melbourne have asked me to check it & need a few more words against the new value”, did not indicate an intention to dispute the claim or specify a proposed payment amount. In coming to this decision, Lauritsen J applied Façade Treatment Engineering Pty Ltd v Brookfield Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd [2016] VSCA 247, where the Victorian Court of Appeal reaffirmed for a document to qualify as a Payment Schedule, it must do more than merely question a claim or seek additional information.

In CPB Contractors Pty Ltd & Ors v MSS Projects (NSW) Pty Ltd t/as MSS Steel & Ors [2025] QSC 239, Johnstone J held that a payment schedule satisfied the requirements of s 69(c) of the BIF Act where the respondent’s reasons for paying less than the sum claimed were stated with sufficient clarity to define the scope of the dispute. In this case, both parties were aware that a component of the payment claim had been descoped by direction, and the respondent expressly stated that it proposed to pay $0 in relation to that descoping claim, expressly referring to the direction. Furthermore, the respondent adequately explained its calculations of liability for variation claims by reference to the figures in the “Certified this Month” section of Schedule 2 of the payment schedule. Johnstone J agreed with the reasoning of Chesterman J in Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA) Pty Ltd & Ors [2007] QSC 333 that there is no particular way the respondent must respond and the respondent is not required to provide a detailed spreadsheet in response to a payment claim.

“States why” versus “indicates”

Section 69(c) requires the respondent to “state” reasons. The NSW Act uses the softer word “indicate”. The Queensland verb sets a higher bar. Useful guidance on the NSW formulation comes from Palmer J in Multiplex Constructions Pty Ltd v Luikens & Anor [2003] NSWSC 1140:

A respondent to a payment claim cannot always content itself with cryptic or vague statements in its payment schedule as to its reasons for withholding payment on the assumption that the claimant will know what issue is sought to be raised. Sometimes the issue is so straightforward or has been so expansively agitated in prior correspondence that the briefest reference in the payment schedule will suffice to identify it clearly.

His Honour also said the following about the word “indicate”:

Section 14(3) of the Act, in requiring a respondent to “indicate” its reasons for withholding payment, does not require that a payment schedule give full particulars of those reasons. The use of the word “indicate” rather than “state”, “specify” or “set out”, conveys an impression that some want of precision and particularity is permissible as long as the essence of “the reason” for withholding payment is made known sufficiently to enable the claimant to make a decision whether or not to pursue the claim and to understand the nature of the case it will have to meet in an adjudication.

The NSW Court of Appeal in Perform (NSW) Pty Ltd v MEV-AUS Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 157 at [49] said the word “indicate” is to be applied “in a common sense practical manner and often where the provider and the recipient of the payment schedule will have debated a claim to payment in prior correspondence”, and held the NSW Act does not exclude incorporation by reference to extrinsic material, particularly the contract or prior correspondence between the parties. See also Pacific General Securities Ltd v Soliman and Sons Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 13 at [71] (Brereton J).

Multiple documents and incorporation by reference

A payment schedule may consist of more than one document. In Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2013] QCA 386, the Queensland Court of Appeal said:

It is not implicit in s 18 that a payment schedule must consist of only one document. It would be surprising if the parliament had so intended. In substantial construction disputes, it is common place for documents disputing the validity of payment claims to contain schedules or appendices. Where more than one document is alleged to constitute a payment schedule, the validity or otherwise of the allegation must be tested by an analysis of the documents in question. It may be that one such document, although not part of a payment schedule, will be relevant to the assessment of whether the other documents constitute a payment schedule.

An accompanying document delivered at the same time can be part of the schedule. In Baulderstone Hornibrook Pty Ltd v Queensland Investment Corporation [2006] NSWSC 522, a box of eight folders served on the claimant, with the top document in the top folder identified as a payment schedule and a covering letter that did not call itself a payment schedule, was a valid payment schedule.

On using earlier documents as part of the schedule, the Court of Appeal in Heavy Plant Leasing accepted under BCIPA “that there can be no objection in principle to an obviously incomplete purported payment schedule being completed, within time, by the provision of the omitted material”, with this important qualification:

A claimant cannot be expected on its own initiative to compose a payment schedule for the respondent by assembling miscellaneous documents received from the respondent until the assembled materials satisfy the requirements of s 18 of the Act.

And in Perform (NSW) Pty Ltd v MEV-AUS Pty Ltd & Anor [2009] NSWCA 157, Giles JA (McColl and Young JJA agreeing) held that “indication within s 14(3) does not exclude what the adjudicator described as incorporation by reference of material extrinsic to the payment schedule”.

Section 76 – Responding to the payment claim

Section 76 BIF Act – Responding to payment claim
76 Responding to payment claim

(1)  If given a payment claim, a respondent must respond to the payment claim by giving the claimant a payment schedule within whichever of the following periods ends first—

(a)  the period, if any, within which the respondent must give the payment schedule under the relevant construction contract;

(b)  15 business days after the payment claim is given to the respondent.

Maximum penalty—100 penalty units.

Note—A failure to give a payment schedule as required under this section is also grounds for taking disciplinary action under the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991.

(2)  However, the respondent is not required to give the claimant the payment schedule if the amount claimed in the payment claim is paid in full on or before the due date for the progress payment to which the payment claim relates.

Section 76 combines the procedural mechanics that previously sat in sections 18 and 18A of BCIPA with the new section 69 definition. Three things stand out: a 15-business-day cap; a contractual period that overrides only if it is shorter; and an exemption only where the entire claim is paid in full on or before the due date.

Purpose of a payment schedule

Once exchanged, the payment claim and payment schedule define the dispute. In Minimax Fire Fighting Systems Pty Ltd v Bremore Engineering (WA Pty Ltd) & Ors [2007] QSC 33, Chesterman J said:

The whole purpose of such a document is to identify what amounts are in dispute and why. The delivery of a payment claim and a payment schedule is meant to identify, at an early stage, the parameters of a dispute about payment for the quick and informal adjudication process for which the Act provides. If a builder wishes to take advantage of the Act to dispute the claim it must comply with its provisions and must, relevantly, take the trouble to respond to a payment claim in the manner required by the Act. The process is not difficult. The applicant was required to identify those parts of the claim which it objected to paying and to say what the grounds of its objection were.

The function of the schedule is to “identify the scope of the dispute”: Clarence Street Pty Ltd v Isis Projects Pty Ltd [2005] NSWCA 391 at [31] (Mason P, Giles and Santow JJA agreeing). That case law now needs to be read alongside the new BIF Act regime that allows “new reasons” in an adjudication response on a complex payment claim, with a corresponding right of reply for the claimant.

Two BIF-era worked examples of what counts (and what does not). In NewGrow Pty Ltd v Buxton Constructions (Vic) Pty Ltd [2019] VCC 464, Cosgrave J held that a schedule riddled with errors so significant that a reasonable person would be unsure whether it actually related to the corresponding claim failed the equivalent of section 76, and a later schedule reissued out of time could neither cure the first nor stand in its own right. In Vannella Pty Limited atf Capitalist Family Trust v TFM Epping Land Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 1379, Henry J held that an email titled “Your Client’s Claims”, sent in response to a covering letter referring to “our client’s Progress Claim dated 3 June 2019”, was too broad to be a valid schedule because it did not directly respond to the payment claim and could be construed as referring to more than one of the past claims.

Giving the schedule — service and by whom

The previous BCIPA language of “serve” and the NSW language of “provide” have been replaced under the BIF Act with “give”. The change is more cosmetic than substantive — in Falgat Constructions Pty Ltd v Equity Australia Corporation Pty Ltd [2006] NSWCA 259, Hodgson JA said:

The use of the word “provide” rather than the word “serve” does carry a suggestion that a different meaning is intended, and that accordingly s.31 does not apply in the case of the word “provide”. Against this, however, I do not think the legislature would have (1) used a problematic word like “provide” with the intention that it have a different meaning from “serve”, (2) given useful instructions as to how service may be effected, yet (3) given no instructions whatsoever as to how provision may be effected. When this consideration is combined with the consideration raised in the previous paragraph, in my opinion this justifies the conclusion, reached by the primary judge in this case, that “provide” does not mean anything different from “serve”, and that s.31 applies to “provision” as well as to “service”.

On service by a solicitor, the early NSW position was strict. In Emag Constructions Pty Ltd v Highrise Concrete Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 903, Einstein J said:

In my view the character of the subject legislation is such that general principles of actual or ostensible authority in solicitors to receive service of copies of relevant notices must yield to the strictures of the strict requirement to prove service. The service provisions of the Act require to be complied with in terms. Prudence dictates that those responsible for complying with the service provisions take steps to be in a position to strictly prove service in the usual way. One only example of the difficulties which may arise is where a solicitor who may have been instructed to act in relation to an adjudication application has his/her instructions withdrawn. There are no provisions similar to those to be found in the Supreme Court Rules 1970 for notices of ceasing to act and the like. The Act here under consideration simply proceeds by requiring particular steps to be taken by the parties and by the adjudicator and proof of strict compliance with the Act is necessary for the achievement of the quick and efficient recovery of progress payments and resolution of disputes in that regard.

That approach was applied in Taylor Projects Group Pty Ltd v Brick Dept Pty Ltd [2005] NSWSC 439 to invalidate service by a solicitor. But the NSW Court of Appeal took a more pragmatic line in Baulderstone Hornibrook Pty Ltd v Queensland Investment Corporation [2007] NSWCA 9, accepting that a solicitor had authority to serve a payment schedule after a detailed review of the retainer, prior conduct and the commercial sense of the conclusion.

On out-of-hours email service, in CKP Constructions Pty Ltd v Gabba Holdings Pty Ltd [2016] QDC 356, McGill DCJ held that a payment schedule emailed at 11:45 pm on the fifth business day after a section 20A notice was validly given. The contract permitted email service and, under section 24(1)(a) of the Electronic Transactions Act 2001 (Qld), the schedule was given when it became capable of being retrieved at the addressee’s email address — 11:45 pm.

Timing — the 15-business-day longstop and any shorter contractual period

Section 76(1) requires the respondent to give the schedule within whichever of two periods ends first: any period required under the contract, or 15 business days after the claim is given. The contract can shorten the statutory period; it cannot lengthen it.

For the contract to displace the statutory period, the case law requires “clear contextual support for a necessary implication” of such displacement: Theiss Pty Ltd v Lane Cove Tunnell Nominee Company Pty Ltd [2008] NSWSC 729 at [21] (Hammerschlag J); Tailored Projects Pty Ltd v Jedfire Pty Ltd [2009] 2 Qd R 171; [2009] QSC 32 at [21]–[22] (Douglas J). Hammerschlag J said:

The test is thus whether there is clear contextual support for a necessary implication that the Contract has supplanted the ten-day period in s 14(4)(b)(ii) with the four business day period in cl 14.3A.

Undoubtedly for a Contract to require a particular time as contemplated by s 14(4)(b)(i) it need not make express reference to that section.

That decision was upheld on appeal in Theiss Pty Ltd v Lane Cove Tunnel Nominee Company Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 53. The same approach was accepted in Tailored Projects Pty Ltd v Jedfire Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 32; [2009] 2 Qd R 171, 179 [23] by Douglas J.

The Queensland Court of Appeal applied the principle in Allencon Pty Ltd v Palmgrove Holdings Pty Ltd trading as Carruthers Contracting [2023] QCA 6. The contract said:

Within 21 days after receipt of a claim for payment, the Main Contractor’s Representative shall issue to the Main Contractor and to the Subcontractor a payment certificate stating the payment which, in the opinion of the Main Contractor’s Representative, is to be made by the Main Contractor to the Subcontractor or by the Subcontractor to the Main Contractor. The Main Contractor’s Representative shall set out in the certificate the calculations employed to arrive at the amount and, if the amount is more or less than the amount claimed by the Subcontractor, the reasons for the difference.

The respondent argued this was a contractual mechanism for a payment certificate, not a payment schedule, so the 15-business-day BIF Act period applied. The Court of Appeal disagreed: a 21-calendar-day period was stipulated and that was the date by which the schedule had to be given. Failing to meet it left the respondent liable for the full claimed amount. The Court of Appeal distinguished Theiss on the basis that the contract there required an Independent Verifier’s Certificate to accompany progress payments, indicating the contractual clause related to a payment certificate under the contract and not a payment schedule:

The Court of Appeal distinguished the contract in this case from the contract in Thiess Pty Ltd v Lane Cove Tunnel Nominee Co Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 53. The Court explained that the contract in Theiss provided a requirement for an Independent Verifier’s Certificate to be accompanied with any progress payment, which indicated that the contract clause related to a payment certificate under the contract and not a payment schedule “reply to the contractual progress claim and not a reply to a statutory payment claim”.

In Roberts Co (NSW) Pty Ltd v Sharvain Facades Pty Ltd (Administrators Appointed) [2025] NSWCA 161, Hammerschlag CJ held that the 10-business day period in section 14(4)(b)(ii) of the NSW SOP Act (equivalent to section 76(1)(b) of the QLD Act) commences from the date a payment claim is served, and that parties may only agree to shorten, not lengthen, that period. The Court found that Sharvain’s payment claim was served on 28 February 2025 and the payment schedule issued by Roberts on 17 March 2025 was therefore, certainly out of time. In coming to this conclusion, his Honour rejected Roberts’ reliance on a deeming clause that postponed service to 3 March 2025, holding that such a clause could not delay the operation of the statutory timeframe. McHugh JA agreed, confirming that the contractual deeming clause had no effect on the operation of section 14(4), and that Roberts became liable for the full amount claimed.

Practice tips

  • Diary 15 business days from the date the payment claim is given — and check the contract for any shorter period that overrides the statutory longstop. Under Allencon v Palmgrove, a contractual 21-day clause was treated as the operative deadline; if your contract has a shorter period (or one that pulls forward in any way), that is the date that matters.
  • Identify the claim by reference. State the claim number, date and total. After Melaleuca View, a schedule that fails to identify the claim can be struck down even where the parties plainly knew which claim was in play.
  • State a single overall scheduled amount. If you propose to pay nothing, say so — “nil”, “zero” or “$0” works (Barclay Mowlem; Façade Treatment Engineering). Do not leave the recipient to infer it.
  • Address the whole claim. Minimax and Turnkey Innovative Engineering are clear: a schedule that takes issue with part of the claim and is silent on the balance is invalid. You do not need to address every invoice individually (Brimar Electrical), but every line of dispute must be answered with a proposed amount and a reason.
  • Give substantive reasons, not deferrals. “We will pay when work is complete” was held insufficient in Joye Group v Cemco, and asking the claimant to “resubmit” or to provide more information is not a reason (Roberts Construction Group; Actif Concrete). State why you are paying less — tie reasons to specific items in the claim and avoid generalised objections (Style Timber Floor).
  • Do not rely on implied withholding reasons or extrinsic material to plug gaps. The schedule must stand on its own (Joye Group). If you intend to rely on background documents, identify them in the schedule with enough particularity that the claimant knows what is being incorporated.
  • Avoid “without prejudice” or conditional language. Settlement-style letters (National Vegetation Management Solutions) and conditional payment offers (Tenix Alliance v Magaldi Power) have failed to qualify as payment schedules. State unconditionally what you propose to pay.
  • Do not let a deeming or part-payment cure a missing schedule. If you do not give a schedule within time, and the claim is not paid in full by the due date, you are exposed to summary judgment for the full claimed amount under the Act and to disciplinary action under the QBCC Act. Get the schedule out, even briefly, rather than relying on later correspondence.
