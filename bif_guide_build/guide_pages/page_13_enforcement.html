Chapter 3 · Sections 90–93

Enforcement

Sections 90 to 93 turn an adjudicator’s decision into something with real teeth. Section 90 fixes the date by which the respondent must pay. Section 91 produces the adjudication certificate — the document the registrar issues that records the debt. Section 92 unlocks the right to suspend work for non-payment under s98. Section 93 is the conversion mechanism: filed in court with the right affidavit, the certificate becomes a judgment debt that can be enforced like any other, and the respondent’s ability to fight back is sharply curtailed. For a PM, this page is the playbook for moving from a paper win to money in the bank, and for understanding what an unhappy respondent can — and cannot — do about it.

Section 90 — respondent required to pay the adjudicated amount

Section 90 sets the statutory due date for payment of the adjudicated amount. Missing the date is not just a contractual default — it carries a penalty and is also a disciplinary matter under the QBCC Act.

Section 90 BIF Act – Respondent required to pay adjudicated amount
90 Respondent required to pay adjudicated amount

If an adjudicator decides that a respondent is required to pay an adjudicated amount, the respondent must pay the amount to the claimant on or before—

the day that is 5 business days after the day on which a copy of the adjudicator’s decision is given to the respondent by the adjudicator; or

if the adjudicator decides a later date for payment under section 88(1)(b)—the later date.

Maximum penalty—200 penalty units.

Note—

A failure to pay an adjudicated amount on or before the due date is also a ground for taking disciplinary action under the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991.

Section 91 — the adjudication certificate

If the respondent has been ordered to pay, the registrar must issue an adjudication certificate as soon as practicable, and no later than five business days after the decision. The certificate is the document the claimant needs to convert the decision into a judgment under s93. The registrar will not issue a certificate where there is nothing to enforce — for example where the respondent owes nothing under the decision, or where the adjudicator declined jurisdiction or found the application frivolous or vexatious.

Section 91 BIF Act – Adjudication certificate
91 Adjudication certificate

As soon as practicable after being given a copy of a decision by an adjudicator, but no later than 5 business days after being given the decision, the registrar must give the claimant a certificate (an adjudication certificate) of the decision stating the following matters—

the name of the claimant;

the name of the respondent who is liable to pay the adjudicated amount;

the adjudicated amount;

the date on which payment of the adjudicated amount was required to be paid to the claimant;

the rate of interest payable on the adjudicated amount;

the fees, identified in the decision, that the respondent is to pay;

that the certificate is made under this Act.

However, the registrar does not have to give the claimant an adjudication certificate if—

the respondent is not required to pay an amount under the decision; or

the adjudicator decided he or she does not have jurisdiction to decide the application; or

the adjudicator decided the adjudication application is frivolous or vexatious.

Section 92 — consequence of not paying: the right to suspend

If the adjudicated amount is not paid in full by the s90 due date, the claimant can put the respondent on notice that it intends to suspend work or supply under s98. The notice must say it is given under this Act. Suspension under s98 is the most powerful self-help tool the claimant has short of court enforcement — a properly served s92 notice is the trigger.

Section 92 BIF Act – Consequence of not paying adjudicated amount
92 Consequence of not paying adjudicated amount

If the respondent fails to pay the whole or any part of the adjudicated amount to the claimant as required under section 90, the claimant may give the respondent written notice of the claimant’s intention to suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under the construction contract under section 98.

The notice about suspending work must state that it is made under this Act.

Section 93 — filing the adjudication certificate as a judgment debt

Section 93 is the mechanism that gives the regime its commercial bite. Once filed in a court of competent jurisdiction with the right affidavit, the certificate is treated as a judgment for a debt and can be enforced as such. Critically, if the respondent tries to set the judgment aside, s93(4) shuts the door on counterclaims, contractual defences and challenges to the adjudicator’s decision — and forces the unpaid portion into court as security in the meantime.

Section 93 BIF Act – Filing of adjudication certificate as judgment debt
93 Filing of adjudication certificate as judgment debt

An adjudication certificate may be filed as a judgment for a debt, and may be enforced, in a court of competent jurisdiction.

An adjudication certificate can not be filed under this section unless it is accompanied by an affidavit by the claimant stating that the whole or a part of the adjudicated amount has not been paid to the claimant at the time the certificate is filed.

If the affidavit states that part of the adjudicated amount has been paid, the judgment is for the unpaid part of the amount only.

If the respondent commences proceedings to have the judgment set aside—

the respondent is not, in those proceedings, entitled—

to bring any counterclaim against the claimant; or

to raise any defence in relation to matters arising under the construction contract to which the adjudication certificate relates; or

to challenge the adjudicator’s decision; and

the respondent is required to pay into the court, as security, the unpaid portion of the adjudicated amount pending the final decision in those proceedings.

93.1 — injunctions to restrain filing or enforcement

Where a respondent wants to head off enforcement, the usual route is to apply for an interlocutory injunction restraining the claimant from filing the certificate (or enforcing the resulting judgment). The framework comes from Australian Broadcasting Corporation v O’Neill [2006] HCA 46; (2006) 227 CLR 57, which requires the applicant to establish:

  • a prima facie case; and
  • that the balance of convenience favours the granting of an injunction.

“Prima facie case” here does not mean “more probable than not”:

it is sufficient that the plaintiff show a sufficient likelihood of success to justify in the circumstances the preservation of the status quo pending the trial.

On balance of convenience, the legislative policy of the Act — preserving cash flow to builders — is itself a relevant factor: see R J Neller Building Pty Ltd v Ainsworth [2008] QCA 397; [2009] 1 Qd R 390; Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Sun Engineering (Qld) Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 170; BRB Modular Pty Ltd v AWX Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 222.

The leading Queensland authority is R J Neller Building Pty Ltd v Ainsworth [2008] QCA 397; [2009] 1 Qd R 390. The Court of Appeal held that the legislature has assigned the risk that a builder may not be able to refund money later found to be due to the owner. Per Keane JA (Fraser JA and Fryberg J agreeing):

It is evidently the intention of the BCIP Act, and, in particular, s 31 and s 100 to which reference has been made, that the process of adjudication established under that Act should provide a speedy and effective means of ensuring cash flow to builders from the parties with whom they contract, where those parties operate in a commercial, as opposed to a domestic, context. This intention reflects an appreciation on the part of the legislature that an assured cash flow is essential to the commercial survival of builders, and that if a payment the subject of an adjudication is withheld pending the final resolution of the builder’s entitlement to the payment, the builder may be ruined.

The BCIP Act proceeds on the assumption that the interruption of a builder’s cash flow may cause the financial failure of the builder before the rights and wrongs of claim and counterclaim between builder and owner can be finally determined by the courts. On that assumption, the BCIP Act seeks to preserve the cash flow to a builder notwithstanding the risk that the builder might ultimately be required to refund the cash in circumstances where the builder’s financial failure, and inability to repay, could be expected to eventuate. Accordingly, the risk that a builder might not be able to refund moneys ultimately found to be due to a non-residential owner after a successful action by the owner must, I think, be regarded as a risk which, as a matter of policy in the commercial context in which the BCIP Act applies, the legislature has, prima facie at least, assigned to the owner.

Neller has been applied beyond Queensland. In Parkview Constructions Pty Limited v Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 1911, Slattery J cited R J Neller in considering an injunction restraining the claimant from applying for or filing an adjudication certificate. The underlying issue was whether the adjudicator had erred in finding the response was out of time. On the threshold question, his Honour held:

In this case, it does seem to me that there is a serious factual issue to be tried. It is a simple issue, but one which will have decisive consequences for each side depending on how it is decided. If Parkview does succeed in showing that delivery of this application to it occurred on the 10th November, then it has a strong prospects of success. If on the other hand the adjudicator’s determination is upheld as a matter of fact, then Windows has sound chances of success, notwithstanding the alternative legal arguments that have been deployed.

On balance of convenience, Slattery J held:

In my view the balance of convenience favours the payment of these funds into Court rather than their payment to Windows in accordance with the filed adjudication certificate. This is principally because the factual issue in the case is so simple that it can very quickly be readied for hearing on both sides and be heard very early in the new term…

His Honour granted the interlocutory injunction restraining the claimant from filing the certificate.

In Low v MCC Pty Ltd & Ors; MCC Pty Ltd v Low [2018] QSC 6, Jackson J restrained MCC from enforcing two adjudication certificates and the warrants issued under them, on the basis that:

  • there was a serious question to be tried as to whether the payment claim, the subject of each adjudication, was served in respect of a valid reference date; and
  • the balance of convenience favoured granting the injunction, particularly because Low had given an undertaking to lodge a bank guarantee with the registrar of the court as security for the unpaid portion of the adjudicated amount pending the final decision of the Court regarding the validity of the adjudicator’s decision.

The doubt about whether a valid reference date had accrued was enough to amount to a serious question; the bank guarantee undertaking, combined with concerns about MCC’s ability to repay, swung the balance of convenience.

The relationship between R J Neller and post-Kirk jurisdictional error case law has been litigated. In Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Sun Engineering (Qld) Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 170, Daubney J considered R J Neller in light of Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531, and concluded that nothing in Kirk displaced the legislative policy on apportionment of risk identified in Neller. That approach was followed in Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 271, [15], where Peter Lyons J characterised the relevant level of repayment risk as “substantial risk”: at [23].

However, Wiggins Island was doubted and not followed in BRB Modular Pty Ltd v AWX Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 222. Bond J drew a distinction between valid adjudication decisions and decisions affected by jurisdictional error, holding that a prima facie case of jurisdictional error is a relevant matter on the injunction analysis:

The intention of the Act – and in particular the intention revealed in ss 31 and 100 of the Act, to which Keane JA adverted in R J Neller at [39] – must be taken to be an intention revealed in relation to decisions of an adjudicator, made within jurisdiction. The policy of the Act described by Keane JA in R J Neller only applies in its full force to valid adjudication decisions. To my mind, it could not possibly be intended to be the policy of the Act that the risk is so passed in relation to decisions which are void and liable to be quashed by the Courts.

It follows that I respectfully disagree with the approach taken by Daubney J in the WICET decision. It seems to me that the appropriate analytical significance of the Act’s policy in cases such as the present is that the policy characterises the nature of the right that is in question in the interlocutory injunction application. So if, on an interlocutory injunction application of this nature, the applicant has established a prima facie case that the adjudication decision is void for jurisdictional error, in considering the question which arises on the balance of convenience, in considering where the lower risk lies, it is appropriate to consider the fact that granting an injunction would deny to someone a right, the nature of which is that described by Keane JA in R J Neller. The way in which this plays out in the exercise of the discretion is in the balancing process described by Hansen J in Kellogg Brown & Root Pty Ltd.

NSW and the ACT have taken a similar position: where a prima facie case (or serious issue to be tried) is shown, courts will grant an interlocutory injunction but typically only on the condition that the disputed amount be paid into court (or an agreed managed fund) pending final determination — see Filadelfia Projects Pty Limited v EntirlTy Business Services [2010] NSWSC 473; Nazero Group Pty Limited v Top Quality Construction Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 232; Milburn Lake Pty Ltd v Andritz Pty Ltd [2016] VSC 3. Where a party cannot presently make payment in, an undertaking to do so may be sufficient: Filadelfia Projects Pty Limited v EntirlTy Business Services [2010] NSWSC 473.

In Hakea Holdings Pty Limited v Denham Constructions Pty Ltd; BaptistCare NSW & ACT v Denham Constructions Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 1120, Ball J continued an injunction preventing the respondent from enforcing an adjudication determination. The applicants had established prima facie cases that they did not owe the amounts under their respective contracts, and there was little prospect of recovering any amount paid to the respondent given its current and future solvency. Those factors strongly favoured a stay and continuation of the injunction.

In Superb Build Pty Ltd v Petrosyan [2023] NSWDC 2, Andronos J extended an interim stay on a judgment giving effect to an adjudication determination, applying the Hakea factors. The matters that drove the result were:

  • the strength of the Respondent’s case;
  • significant risk of irreparable financial harm to the Respondent if the stay was not extended; and
  • no evidence that the Claimant would become insolvent if the interim stay was extended.

In Richard Crookes Constructions Pty Ltd v CES Projects (Aust) Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 1119, McDougall J held the adjudicator had arguably failed to perform his statutory function of assessing the construction work performed and valuing it. There was a prima facie case for relief and the balance of convenience favoured an injunction restraining enforcement until final hearing.

The contrary line is also strong. In Bellerive Homes Pty Ltd v FW Projects Pty Ltd [2018] NSWSC 1435, N Adams J refused a stay of judgment because granting it would have cut across the policy of prompt payment. Her Honour treated stays as discretionary and fact-specific, and gave the following examples of when one might be granted:

  • Failure to order a stay would “convert an amount which ought to be an interim payment into a final payment” (i.e. where there is a certainty that the party against who the stay is sought will suffer irreparable prejudice).
  • A respondent can demonstrate that the result produced by the NSW Act is unjust and there is a substantial risk the money paid over would be irrecoverable and that proceedings for a final resolution are being pursued.
  • The builder may have engaged in tactics calculated to delay the ultimate determination of the rights and liabilities of the parties so as to unfairly increase the owner’s exposure to the risk the builder’s insolvency.
  • The builder may have restructured its finances after the making of the contract to increase the risk to the owner of the possible inability of the builder to meet its liabilities to the owner when they are ultimately declared by the courts.

Concerns about the claimant’s future income stream were not enough on their own to justify a stay.

That same reluctance to interfere appears in Paul Kennedy McCullough trading as The Green Side Up v New Start Australia Pty Limited [2023] NSWDC 82, where Dicker SC DCJ held that a court should not stay enforcement of an adjudication certificate, where “the whole purpose of the (Security of Payment) Act would… be circumvented if the adjudication certificate was not given proper force consistently with its registration in accordance with s 25” of the NSW SOP Act (equivalent to s93 BIFSOPA), unless “failure to do so would [make] permanent that which the Act intended to be only interim” and “there is a likelihood of ‘irreparable prejudice’…”, referring to Veolia Water Solutions & Technologies (Australia) Pty Ltd v Kruger Engineering Australia Pty Ltd (No 3) [2007] NSWSC 459.

In Kuatro Build Pty Ltd v Elite Formwork Group Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 372, Hmelnitsky J held that, although interlocutory orders had initially restrained enforcement under s24(1)(a), the stay should not be continued (relevant to s92(1) BIFSOPA). His Honour applied Martinus Rail Pty Ltd v Qube RE Services (No 2) [2024] NSWSC 1223 and Martinus [2025] NSWCA 49, reiterating that “considerable caution should attend the grant of such an injunction or a stay,” as it risks undermining the SOP Act’s policy of immediate cash flow. Elite’s financial evidence, including evidence of trading viability and tax repayment capacity, was unchallenged, and Kuatro’s insolvency concerns were insufficient to maintain the stay.

On enforcement procedure, in Fitz Jersey Pty Ltd v Atlas Construction Group Pty Ltd [2017] NSWCA 53, Beazley ACJ, Basten JA and Leeming JA considered s25(4) of the BCIPA (NSW), the equivalent of s19 of the Act, and addressed whether an affected party must be given notice of a judgment debt before enforcement steps are taken. The Court held a judgment can be enforced without notice; nothing in the Act required the builder to notify the developer of garnishee proceedings. Basten J reasoned at [70]:

Although s25(4) of the Act is said not to confer any right to have a judgment set aside, the developer’s claim was that it recognized an entitlement which otherwise existed to have the judgment set aside. It would be incorrect to suggest that s25 (4) put the respondent to a claim in any better position than an unsuccessful respondent against whom a judgment debt has been obtained in civil proceedings in a court.

On the question of what counts as the “unpaid portion” of the adjudicated amount, in Kennedy Civil Contracting Pty Ltd (subject to Deed of Company Arrangement) v Linx Constructions Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 366, Wright J held that a payment to a third party could be taken into account in determining the unpaid portion under s15(2)(a)(i) of the SOP Act (equivalent to s93(3) BIFSOPA). The Magistrate had treated this as “simply a factual determination” that the third-party payment reduced the amount outstanding under the payment claim. Wright J also rejected the argument that such payment was not made “to the claimant” within s14(4) of the SOP Act (equivalent to s77(2) BIFSOPA), and refused leave to appeal on the basis that the unpaid portion was a question of fact or mixed fact and law.

That outcome was upheld in Kennedy Civil Contracting Pty Ltd v Linx Constructions Pty Ltd [2024] NSWCA 243, where the Court of Appeal held that a respondent’s payment to a third-party supplier did not constitute a cross-claim under s15(4)(b)(ii) of the NSW Act (equivalent to s93 BIFSOPA). The respondent had failed to issue a payment schedule in response to a $147,000 payment claim that included approximately $78,000 for goods and services supplied by a third party, and had subsequently paid $102,800 in total. The Magistrate found the $30,000 direct payment to the third party had partially satisfied the debt, and the Court of Appeal affirmed that conclusion: while the Act strictly limits defences where no payment schedule is given, reductions based on accepted third-party payments may be considered in limited, fact-specific circumstances.

93.2 — statutory demands

Adjudicated amounts are also routinely used as the basis for statutory demands under s459H of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth). The current authority in this context is Re Douglas Aerospace Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 167, which held that:

  • a genuine dispute is not founded on an application to set aside the debt; but
  • an offsetting claim can exist on the presence of a counterclaim, set-off or cross demand that does not deny the debt, but asserts a countervailing liability.

That approach has not been universally embraced. In Re J Group Constructions Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 1607, Robb J reviewed the line of authority running through Diploma Construction (WA) Pty Ltd v KPA Architects Pty Ltd [2014] WASCA 91 and Re Douglas Aerospace Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 167, ultimately following Re Douglas Aerospace, but flagging “reasons for doubting” the application of the principles in Deputy Commissioner of Taxation v Broadbeach Properties Pty Ltd [2008] HCA 41; (2008) 237 CLR 473 to security-of-payment debts — given the different nature of those debts compared with the tax debts in Broadbeach.

In In the matter of Rockwall Homes Pty Limited [2017] NSWSC 223, Black J considered whether ss15(2) and (4) of the NSW Act prevented a company served with a creditors statutory demand from raising a genuine dispute under s459G of the Corporations Act. His Honour referred to Brereton J’s reasoning in Re Douglas Aerospace at [76]:

some debts are not capable of genuine dispute — because they are judgment debts or conclusive debts — but may still be amenable to an offsetting claim. It is difficult to see how a debt arising under s 14(4) (“SOPA”), which creates a statutory liability upon the failure to provide a payment schedule, could be the subject of a genuine dispute, if the conditions in s 15(1) are satisfied, regardless of any underlying dispute. As it seems to me, the only way in which a “genuine dispute” could be raised in respect of such a debt would be by disputing whether the circumstances referred to in s 15(1) existed.

Black J found that reasoning did not apply to the claim before him: the SOP Act only operates where a payment claim has been served on a party to a construction contract, and a dispute about whether that requirement is satisfied is not excluded. His Honour also referred to CGI Information Systems & Management Consultants Pty Ltd v APRA Consulting Pty Ltd [2003] NSWSC 728, where Barrett J observed:

“[T]he task faced by the company challenging a statutory demand on the genuine dispute grounds is by no means at all a difficult or demanding one. The company will fail in that task only if it is found, upon the hearing of its s 459G application, that the contentions upon which it seeks to rely in mounting its challenge are so devoid of substance that no further investigation is warranted. Once the company shows that even one issue has a sufficient degree of cogency to be arguable, a finding of genuine dispute must follow. The court does not engage in any form of balancing exercise between the strengths of competing contentions. If it sees any factor that on rational grounds indicates an arguable case on the part of the company, it must find that a genuine dispute exists, even where any case apparently available to be advanced against the company seems stronger.”

Form matters as well as substance. In Re Roberts Construction Group Pty Ltd [2024] VSC 679, Steffensen AsJ set aside a statutory demand issued in respect of an unpaid scheduled amount under the Victorian SOP Act because the demand did not adequately describe the legal basis of the debt — it did not refer to the “scheduled amount”, the document under which it arose, or the SOP Act. Her Honour relied on Re Three Pillars Lynbrook Pty Ltd [2022] VSC 540 (a statutory demand must be unambiguous) and CM Luxury Pty Ltd v Menzies Civil Australia Pty Ltd [2023] WASC 340 (a statutory demand must accurately describe the legal basis of the debt or it can cause substantial injustice). The takeaway is that statutory demands relying on SOP debts must expressly state the basis of the demand.

93.3 — challenging an adjudication determination

A respondent who wants to set aside the s93 judgment must usually do so by attacking the underlying adjudication for jurisdictional error. In Amasya Enterprises Pty Ltd & Anor v Asta Developments (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor [2015] VSC 233, Vickery J considered s28R(5)(a)(iii) of the VIC Act (equivalent to s31(4)(a)(iii) of the Act) where the plaintiffs sought to challenge the adjudicator’s determination, and held:

… if the Plaintiffs are successful in a challenge to the Adjudicator’s Determination founded upon jurisdictional error, the Supreme Court may make an order in the nature of certiorari or a declaration to remove the legal consequences or purported legal consequences of the Adjudication Determination under the Act, with the result that the judgment founded upon it, must also be set aside.

In arriving at that conclusion, his Honour stated:

…the operation of the privative clause in s 28R(5)(a)(iii) is confined to denying relief being granted by a court in Victoria, including the Supreme Court, in the course of a proceeding to set aside a judgment entered pursuant to s 28R, where the error relied upon is an error on the face of the record in an adjudication determination which is the foundation of the judgment. In other words, pursuant to s 28R(5)(a)(iii) of the Act, it is not open to challenge an adjudication determination (or a review determination) in a proceeding to have a s 28R judgment set aside, on the basis or an error on the face of the record in the relevant determination.

It follows in this case that, if the Plaintiffs are able to establish a jurisdictional error in the Adjudication Determination, they are not precluded by the operation of s 28R(5)(a)(iii) of the Act from challenging the Adjudication Determination on that basis.

93.4 — section 93(4)(a): no counterclaim, no contract defence, no challenge

Section 93(4)(a) is the lock on the door. In set-aside proceedings, the respondent cannot bring a counterclaim, raise contract defences, or challenge the adjudicator’s decision. The cases below show how the courts have policed those limits.

In Infinity Formwork Group Pty Ltd v Adobe Construction Group Pty Ltd [2023] NSWDC 220, the Court considered whether equitable set-off fell within s15(4)(b)(ii) of the NSW SOP Act (equivalent to s93(4)(a)(ii) BIFSOPA) and was therefore excluded as a defence to a summary judgment application. The respondent had failed to provide a payment schedule. Adronos SC DCJ held that equitable set-off was caught by the exclusion and was not available, stating that it was “incumbent on the defendant, if it wished to rely on the defence, to raise it in a payment schedule”. Summary judgment for the full amount followed.

The line is not absolute. In Marques Group Pty Ltd v Parkview Constructions Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 625, Rees J considered a respondent’s defence to summary judgment under s16(2)(a)(i) of the NSW SOP Act for failure to pay a scheduled amount. Sections 15(4)(b) and 16(4)(b) (equivalent to s93(4)(a) BIFSOPA) preclude crossclaims and defences in relation to matters arising under the construction contract. Applying Bitannia Pty Ltd v Parkline Constructions Pty Ltd (2006) 67 NSWLR 9, Rees J held that a misleading and deceptive conduct argument under the ACL is not a defence in relation to matters arising under the contract, so the respondent was not precluded from running it. The defence was “arguable”, and summary judgment was refused.

In Kennedy Civil Contracting Pty Ltd (subject to Deed of Company Arrangement) v Linx Constructions Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 366, Wright J held that the Respondent’s reliance on a payment to a third party to reduce the “unpaid portion of the claimed amount” did not amount to an impermissible cross-claim within s15(4)(b)(i) of the SOP Act (equivalent to s93(4)(a)(i) BIFSOPA). At first instance, the Magistrate had rejected the cross-claim characterisation and reasoned that the Claimant should not be entitled to be paid an amount it never intended to retain. The Court emphasised that the Respondent had not advanced a claim for independent or additional relief that could be given in separate proceedings — which is inherent in a cross-claim. The payment was a factual contention going to the level of debt outstanding under the payment claim.

Where the underlying determination is void for jurisdictional error, the s93(4) restrictions do not save the resulting judgment. In Harlech Enterprises Pty Ltd v Beno Excavations Pty Ltd t/as Benex Pipeline [2024] NSWDC 151, Montgomery DCJ considered whether to set aside a 2021 judgment and garnishee order based on an adjudication determination later quashed by the ACT Supreme Court for jurisdictional error in Beno Excavations Pty Ltd v Harlech Enterprises (No 2) [2021] ACTSC 269 (Mossop J). His Honour applied Brodyn Pty Ltd t/as Time Cost and Quality v Davenport & Anor [2004] NSWCA 394, citing [61]:

…until it [adjudication determination] is filed, proceedings can appropriately be brought in a court with jurisdiction to grant declarations and injunctions to establish that it is void and to prevent it being filed. However, once it has been filed, the resulting judgement is not void. An application can be made to set aside the judgement… it is not contrary to s 25(4)(a)(iii) to do so on the basis that there is in truth no adjudicator’s determination.

Montgomery DCJ found it was not contrary to s25(4)(a)(iii) of the SOP Act (NSW) (equivalent to s93(4)(iii) BIFSOPA) to set aside the judgment on that basis: the statutory scheme did not contemplate enforcement of a judgment debt where the determination was infected with jurisdictional error. The judgment and garnishee order were set aside.

The set-off line was tested again in BRP Industries Pty Ltd v Hynash Constructions Pty Ltd [2024] NSWDC 392. The Claimant applied for summary judgment for $234,286.80 where the Respondent had failed to issue a payment schedule. The Respondent argued it had paid a $110,756.80 upfront amount and that the debt should be reduced by that figure. The issue was whether that argument was a defence “in relation to matters arising under the construction contract” for the purposes of s15(4)(b)(ii) of the NSW Act (equivalent to s93(4)(ii) BIFSOPA). Russell SC DCJ held the set-off could not be raised: the contract required a 10% upfront establishment and mobilisation fee, and the $110,756.80 was that fee. Because the obligation to make the upfront payment arose under the contract, the set-off was a defence in relation to a matter arising under the contract and was barred.

That decision was upheld in Hynash Constructions Pty Ltd v BRP Industries Pty Ltd [2025] NSWCA 14, where the NSW Court of Appeal held that the “unpaid portion” in s15(2)(a)(i) of the NSW Act refers to amounts unpaid in response to the payment claim, not unrelated prior payments. If a respondent wants a prior payment credited, it must be raised in the payment schedule.

Finally, in Martinus Rail Pty Ltd v Qube RE Services (No.2) Pty Ltd [2025] NSWCA 49, Payne JA confirmed that entry of judgment under s25 (equivalent to s93(1) BIFSOPA) is not precluded by a pending arbitration or by the mere possibility of repayment risk. The Court upheld the primacy of the SOP Act’s enforcement mechanism, rejecting Qube’s argument that enforcement should be stayed pending a separate arbitration. Adjudicated amounts became payable by the statutory due date and “the grant of a stay until the end of arbitration would be contrary to the statutory purpose of the SOP Act.” The Court applied Kennedy Civil Contracting Pty Ltd v Richard Crookes Construction Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 99 and Probuild in affirming the swift enforcement intended under s25.

Practice tips

  • Diary the s90 due date the moment the decision lands — five business days from delivery, unless the adjudicator has set a later date under s88(1)(b). Missing the date triggers a 200 penalty unit exposure and is a QBCC disciplinary trigger.
  • If you are the claimant and you have not been paid by the due date, get the adjudication certificate from the registrar (s91) and prepare the s93 affidavit confirming non-payment (or partial payment) before filing — the certificate cannot be filed without it.
  • If you intend to suspend under s98, serve a written s92 notice that expressly says it is given under the BIF Act — that statement is mandatory and is the gateway to the s98 right.
  • If you are the respondent and want to restrain filing or enforcement, expect the court to require payment of the unpaid portion into court (or an undertaking to do so). Plan the cash — R J Neller, Low v MCC and the NSW line all point the same way.
  • In set-aside proceedings, do not waste time on contractual defences, counterclaims or merits challenges to the decision — s93(4)(a) shuts those out. Focus on jurisdictional error or, in narrow cases, ACL/statutory arguments that are not “defences in relation to matters arising under the contract”.
  • If you are running a statutory demand off an adjudicated debt, draft it carefully: identify the scheduled or adjudicated amount, the underlying document and the SOP Act basis. Vague demands have been set aside (Re Roberts Construction Group).
