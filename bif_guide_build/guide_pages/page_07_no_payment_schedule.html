Chapter 3 · Sections 77 & 78

Failing to give a payment schedule

Sections 77 and 78 of the BIF Act set out what happens when a respondent does not engage with a payment claim properly. Section 77 catches the worst case for a respondent — no payment schedule at all within the time required by section 76. Section 78 deals with the more common scenario where a payment schedule was given on time but the scheduled amount has not been paid by the due date. The two pathways are closely related but trigger different rights and a different shortlist of recovery options for the claimant.

Section 77 BIF Act – Consequence of failing to give payment schedule
77 Consequence of failing to give payment schedule

(1)  This section applies if a respondent given a payment claim does not respond to the claim by giving the claimant a payment schedule as required under section 76.

(2)  The respondent is liable to pay the amount claimed under the payment claim to the claimant on the due date for the progress payment to which the payment claim relates.

What triggers s77

Section 77 bites the moment the section 76 window closes without a payment schedule in the claimant’s hands. Once that happens, the respondent is on the hook for the full claimed amount — not the amount the respondent thinks is owing, and not the amount that might be assessed by an adjudicator. The claim becomes the debt.

“Is liable” — the timing of liability

The wording of s77(2) has been read broadly. In Fyntray Constructions Pty Ltd v Macind Drainage & Hydraulic Services Pty Ltd [2002] NSWCA 238, the New South Wales Court of Appeal looked at the equivalent phrase “becomes liable” and refused to confine it to a future-only meaning. At [48], Heydon JA (Hodgson JA and Ipp AJA agreeing) said:

[48] … even if a “claimant”, as defined by s 13(1), delays in serving a payment claim beyond a time which is ten business days before the contractual due date in which the “respondent” becomes liable to pay the claimed amount, as a matter of language there is no strain in concluding that, if no payment schedule is provided by the respondent in time, the respondent “becomes” liable to pay at a passed date. If strictness of construction, which the defendant’s approach calls for, is to be applied, that approach is not strict enough. It fastens on the contrast between the present tense of “becomes” and the occurrence of liability to pay in the past. But if the defendant’s construction were correct, the word “becomes” should have been “will become” or, to cover the possibility of a payment claim served precisely ten business days before the due date for the progress payment, “will become or becomes”. Since that language was not used, there is no strain in construing “becomes” to mean “became, becomes or will become”.

The practical takeaway: even if the claim arrives late in the cycle relative to the contractual due date, the respondent’s liability under s77 still attaches once the schedule deadline is missed.

Statutory demands and the s77 debt

Because s77 creates a statutory debt, claimants sometimes use it as the springboard for a creditor’s statutory demand. In K2M Investments Pty Ltd v Unita (Qld) Pty Ltd [2022] QSC 274, the Court dismissed an application to set aside a statutory demand that had been issued after the respondent failed to pay a payment claim or respond with a payment schedule.

Cooper J referred to Re Douglas Aerospace Pty Ltd [2015] NSWSC 167, where a debt arising under the NSW equivalent of s77 was treated as a statutory debt. On that approach, the only way a genuine dispute can be raised about the debt is by disputing whether there was in fact a failure to provide a payment schedule, or a failure to pay the claimed amount. In K2M, neither of those things was in dispute, so a genuine dispute about the existence of the debt could not be made out.

In In the matter of Nicolas Criniti Pty Ltd (in Liquidation) [2022] NSWSC 1149, the NSW Supreme Court considered when a statutory debt arises and whether a claimant who succeeded at adjudication held a valid “proof of debt” against a respondent in liquidation. The Court held:

  • a valid statutory debt must arise against the company before the appointment of an administrator;
  • where the amount is claimed and pursued at adjudication, the debt does not arise as a statutory debt until there is an adjudication determination; and
  • because that right arose only after the respondent had entered voluntary administration, there was no valid “statutory debt” against the company.

That sequencing matters: a claimant who is too slow to crystallise the debt before insolvency intervenes may find there is nothing to prove up.

Section 78 BIF Act – Consequences of failing to pay claimant
78 Consequences of failing to pay claimant

(1)  This section applies if a respondent given a payment claim for a progress payment does not pay the amount owed to the claimant in full on or before the due date for the progress payment.

(2)  The claimant may either—

(a)  recover the unpaid portion of the amount owed from the respondent, as a debt owing to the claimant, in a court of competent jurisdiction; or

(b)  apply for adjudication of the payment claim under part 4.

(3)  In addition to the action mentioned in subsection (2), the claimant may give the respondent written notice of the claimant’s intention to suspend carrying out construction work, or supplying related goods and services, under the relevant construction contract under section 98.

(4)  The notice to suspend work must state that it is made under this Act.

(5)  In this section—

amount owed, to a claimant for a payment claim, means—

(a)  if the respondent did not respond to the payment claim with a payment schedule as required under section 76— the amount claimed under the payment claim; or

(b)  if the respondent did respond to the payment claim with a payment schedule as required to do so under section 76—the amount proposed to be paid under the payment schedule.

When s78 applies vs s77

Section 78 is the broader provision — it covers any failure to pay the “amount owed” by the due date. The definition of “amount owed” in s78(5) ties the two sections together:

  • if no payment schedule was given (the s77 scenario), the amount owed is the full amount claimed; or
  • if a payment schedule was given, the amount owed is the scheduled amount.

So a respondent who scheduled a lower figure but then doesn’t pay even that figure by the due date is squarely within s78. The claimant’s pathways under s78(2) — court recovery or adjudication — are then available.

Recovery options for the claimant — s78(2)

Section 78(2) gives the claimant a binary choice: court or adjudication. The election can be a powerful one. In Kyle Bay Removals Pty Ltd v Dynabuild Project Services Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 334, Meagher JA held that a party has not “elected” to take court proceedings instead of adjudication where there was no valid payment claim in the first place. Without a valid payment claim, no liability to pay attaches to the recipient, and so there can be no binding election to pursue a course of action under the equivalent of s78(2).

“Court of competent jurisdiction” — s78(2)(a)

In Northside Roofing Pty Ltd v Pires Constructions Pty Ltd [2007] QDC 172, Durward SC DCJ held that the only courts with competent jurisdiction to enforce a BCIPA debt for the purposes of s19 BCIPA (the equivalent of s78 of the Act) are the Magistrates Court, District Court and Supreme Court.

Court recovery and parallel proceedings can co-exist. In Canterbury-Bankstown Council v Payce Communities Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 1419, Henry J held it was not an abuse of process to commence proceedings under the NSW Act while the dispute was also being heard before the Supreme Court. Payce had sued the Council for variation works. During the proceedings the final reference date under the contract occurred, and Payce served its final payment claim under the contract. The Council scheduled nil, arguing the claim was invalid and an abuse of process, then sought to restrain Payce from making an adjudication application under the equivalent of s79 of the Act. The Court treated abuse of process as confined to “exceptional circumstances” with a “heavy” onus, which the Council did not discharge.

Defences to summary judgment under s78(2)(a) face an uphill fight, but they are not impossible. In Marques Group Pty Ltd v Parkview Constructions Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 625, Rees J considered the respondent’s defence to a summary judgment application brought under the NSW equivalent of s78(2)(a) for failure to pay a scheduled amount. The respondent argued the claimant had engaged in misleading and deceptive conduct by stating in the statutory declaration accompanying the payment claim that its subcontractors had been paid (which was untrue), and that but for that conduct the respondent would have scheduled $nil. Rees J described the defence as “inherently unattractive” because it appeared to “strike at the heart” of the security of payment scheme, but found it did not meet the threshold of being “so clearly untenable that it cannot possibly succeed”, and the summary judgment application was struck out [20].

Invalid adjudication and falling back on debt recovery

What happens to the debt-recovery option in s78(2)(a) if a claimant first tries adjudication and the application turns out to be invalid? In Seymour Whyte Constructions Pty Ltd v Ostwald Bros Pty Ltd (In liquidation) [2019] NSWCA 11, the NSW Court of Appeal held that a failure to recover via an invalid adjudication application under the NSW equivalent of s78(2)(b) does not preclude a claimant from pursuing the unpaid portion of a scheduled amount as a debt due under the equivalent of s78(2)(a).

Ostwald had made an invalid adjudication application and then sought to recover the scheduled amount as a debt due from Seymour Whyte. Seymour Whyte argued the two pathways in the NSW equivalent of s78(2)(a) were mutually exclusive — pick one, lose the other. The Court of Appeal accepted that a claimant who has “made” an adjudication application is precluded from also recovering the unpaid portion of the scheduled amount as a debt due. But because Ostwald’s adjudication application was invalid, it had not actually been “made”, so Ostwald remained entitled to recover under the equivalent of s78(2)(a).

Suspension of work — s78(3) and (4)

On top of the recovery options in s78(2), the claimant may give written notice of an intention to suspend construction work, or the supply of related goods and services, under section 98. The notice must state that it is made under the Act. Suspension is a significant lever for an unpaid contractor and a real cost pressure for a respondent, but the s78(4) requirement to identify the Act in the notice is a formal trap that needs to be honoured.

Practice tips

  • For respondents, missing the section 76 schedule deadline is the worst result on the board — it locks in liability for the full claimed amount under s77, regardless of what you actually owe. Diary the deadline and treat it as immovable.
  • If you are a respondent who has scheduled less than the claimed amount, do not assume the schedule itself is the end of the story; if you don’t pay the scheduled amount by the due date, s78(2) gives the claimant immediate court access for that figure.
  • For claimants, court recovery under s78(2)(a) can be quicker and cheaper than adjudication where the debt is the scheduled amount or a s77 debt, because the “genuine dispute” runway is narrow — K2M and Re Douglas Aerospace show how hard it is to dispute the existence of the debt itself.
  • If you are issuing a statutory demand off the back of s77, be ready to prove that no schedule was given and no payment was made — those are the only realistic grounds on which a respondent can mount a genuine dispute.
  • Choose your s78(2) pathway carefully. Seymour Whyte v Ostwald shows that “making” a valid adjudication application can shut the door on debt recovery; an invalid application may leave the door open, but that is a precarious position to plan around.
  • If using the suspension lever in s78(3), make sure the written notice expressly states that it is made under the Act — section 78(4) is mandatory, and a defective notice will not protect a contractor that walks off site.
