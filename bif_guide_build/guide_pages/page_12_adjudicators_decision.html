Chapter 3 · Sections 87–89

Adjudicator’s decision

Section 88 is the engine room of the security-of-payment regime. It tells the adjudicator what to decide, what to consider, what to ignore, and what the decision must look like. It is also the section the courts have used to draw the line between an adjudication that stands and one that is set aside for jurisdictional error or denial of natural justice. Section 87 governs how an earlier valuation feeds into a later adjudication. Section 89 is the housekeeping provision that lets an adjudicator fix slips after the decision has issued. For a PM, this page is the map for understanding when an adverse decision can realistically be challenged — and when you simply need to pay and fight later.

Section 88 BIF Act – Adjudicator’s decision
88 Adjudicator’s decision

(1)  An adjudicator is to decide—

(a)  the amount of the progress payment, if any, to be paid by the respondent to the claimant (the adjudicated amount); and

(b)  the date on which any amount became or becomes payable; and

(c)  the rate of interest payable on any amount.

(2)  In deciding an adjudication application, the adjudicator is to consider the following matters only—

(a)  the provisions of this chapter and, to the extent they are relevant, the provisions of the Queensland Building and Construction Commission Act 1991, part 4A;

(b)  the provisions of the relevant construction contract;

(c)  the payment claim to which the application relates, together with all submissions, including relevant documents, that have been properly made by the claimant in support of the claim;

(d)  the payment schedule, if any, to which the application relates, together with all submissions, including relevant documents, that have been properly made by the respondent in support of the schedule;

(e)  the results of any inspection carried out by the adjudicator of any matter to which the claim relates.

(3)  However, the adjudicator must not consider any of the following—

(a)  an adjudication response, to which the adjudication application relates, that was not given to the adjudicator within the time required under section 83;

(b)  a reason included in an adjudication response to the adjudication application, if the reason is prohibited from being included in the response under section 82.

(4)  Also, the adjudicator may disregard an adjudication application or adjudication response to the extent that the submissions or accompanying documents contravene any limitations relating to submissions or accompanying documents prescribed by regulation.

(5)  The adjudicator’s decision must—

(a)  be in writing; and

(b)  include the reasons for the decision, unless the claimant and the respondent have both asked the adjudicator not to include the reasons in the decision.

(6)  The adjudicator must give a copy of the decision, and notice of the fees and expenses to be paid to the adjudicator for the decision, to the registrar at the same time the adjudicator gives a copy of the decision to the claimant and respondent.

Maximum penalty for subsection (6)—40 penalty units.

What the adjudicator must decide — the adjudicated amount

The adjudicator’s core task under s88(1)(a) is to fix the adjudicated amount. In State of Queensland v Epoca Constructions Pty Ltd & Anor [2006] QSC 324, Philippides J held that, where the respondent had no valid reason for withholding payment, the adjudicator was entitled to accept the uncontested valuation put forward by the claimant. In J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Cada Formwork Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 63, Peter Lyons J doubted whether a decision would be invalid simply because it did not include an express finding as to the legal source of the claimant’s entitlement to payment.

An adjudicator is not bound by the Superintendent’s progress certificate: see Abacus v Davenport & Ors [2003] NSWSC 1027, [35] (McDougall J); Hervey Bay (JV) Pty Ltd v Civil Mining and Construction Pty Ltd & Ors [2008] QSC 58, [24] (McMurdo J).

A determination that no money is payable is still a determination. In Alucity Architectural Produce Supply Pty Ltd v Australian Solutions Centre; Alucity Architectural Produce Supply Pty Ltd v Paul J Hick [2016] NSWSC 608, Hammerschlag J held:

A determination that no amount is to be paid because the claim is invalid is no less a determination than one which determines that no money is payable for some other reason.

By contrast, in Parrwood Pty Ltd v Trinity Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd [2020] NSWCA 172, the Court of Appeal upheld a finding that the determination was void for jurisdictional error because the adjudicator “decline[d] [from] determining” entitlement on the basis that a finding would be “premature”. Recording “No amount” rather than “nil” or “$0” was a failure to do the “very thing” required by s22(1)(a) of the SOP Act (NSW) (equivalent to s88(1)(a) BIFSOPA). The Court also confirmed that a party can treat a void determination as void and seek a fresh adjudication rather than wait for a court declaration.

Other recent applications:

  • Whitehorse Box Hill Pty Ltd v Alliance CG Pty Ltd and Anor [2022] VSC 22 – Stynes J upheld a determination of a final payment claim where the adjudicator’s reasoning, although “formulaic”, showed he had considered each alleged defect. A reviewing court does not undertake an unduly critical analysis.
  • Binah Constructions Pty Ltd v PTMG Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 872 – Ball J upheld a determination that included an amount certified by default in respect of an earlier unscheduled claim. The claimant was not awarded more than it had claimed when previously certified amounts were treated as not in dispute.
  • Chevron Park Pty Ltd v Groupline Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors [2024] QSC 202 – Treston J held that the adjudicator could not consider payments made after the payment claim because the adjudication response could not include new reasons for withholding payment.

The floor and ceiling on the adjudicated amount

The amount claimed in the payment claim and the amount certified in the schedule create a “floor and ceiling” on what the adjudicator can award. In Creative Building Services Pty Ltd v TIO Air Conditioning Pty Ltd [2016] ACTSC 367, Mossop AsJ held that an adjudicator who awards more than the claimed amount commits jurisdictional error:

…in my view, having regard to the statutory provisions earlier referred to, the capacity to determine the amount of a progress payment must be confined, at the upper end, by the claimed amount. That is because the statutory process of a payment claim, payment schedule, adjudication application and adjudication response are all based upon the identification by a claimant of the claimed amount and a response by the respondent to that claim. The statutory process is clearly one which permits the parties by an adversarial process to define the scope of their dispute. That is consistent with the contractual processes which the Act mirrors and partially adopts. It is also consistent with the autonomy that parties have to determine the claims which they wish to make and pursue under the Act. It would be inconsistent with those statutory processes if, notwithstanding that a claimant had only sought a particular amount, the adjudicator awarded a greater amount.

The reverse applies at the lower end. In Maxcon Constructions Pty Ltd v Vadasz (No 2) [2017] SASCFC 2 at [228] Blue J held:

In the present case, the adjudicator was required by section 22 of the Act to determine Mr Vadasz’s application for adjudication of his payment claim. Mr Vadasz in his payment claim and his adjudication application claimed payment of $204,864.55. Maxcon in its payment schedule and in its submissions to the adjudicator accepted liability to pay $141,163.55. The adjudicator had no jurisdiction in the circumstances to determine the amount of the progress payment at less than $141,163.55 or more than $204,864.55.

Section 88(2) — the closed list of what the adjudicator considers

Section 88(2) does double duty: it limits what the adjudicator may consider but mandates the consideration of those things that are listed: see Ware Building Pty Ltd v Centre Projects Pty Ltd & Anor (No 1) [2011] QSC 424, 6 (Fryberg J).

In Livingstone Shire Council v EarthTEC Pty Ltd and Ors [2018] QSC 271, Lyons SJA declared a decision void where the adjudicator refused to consider submissions about amounts paid into court under the Subcontractors’ Charges Act 1974 (Qld). Section 26(2) BCIPA (equivalent to s88(2)) did not preclude the adjudicator from considering a statutory provision raised in a payment schedule or properly made submission.

If a respondent fails to provide a payment schedule, the adjudicator can infer the respondent was not in a position to contest the claim and proceed to determine the application on that basis: 1155 Nepean Highway Pty Ltd v Promax Buildings Pty Ltd [2020] VSCA 253. In Insite Construction Services Pty Ltd v Daniels Civil Pty Ltd [2023] QSC 33, Crowley J set out a useful summary of what an adjudicator must do when the schedule says “Nil” without reasons:

  • If a payment schedule schedules “Nil”, reasons for withholding payment must be included under s69 BIFSOPA.
  • A schedule that fails to include reasons for withholding or proposing to pay less is invalid under s69.
  • Failure to contest the claimant’s valuation can be treated as implicit acceptance of it.
  • Where the valuation is uncontested, the adjudicator is entitled to adopt the unchallenged (and implicitly accepted) valuation.
  • Section 88 obliges the adjudicator to “decide” and “consider” — not to “inquire” or undertake an independent valuation exercise.

Conversely, in Kangaroo Point Developments MP Property Pty Ltd v RHG Construction Fitout and Maintenance Pty Ltd [2021] QSC 30, Dalton J declared a determination void because the adjudicator failed to determine whether the document the claimant had nominated as the payment schedule was a payment schedule under s69, and based the determination on a document that was not the actual schedule.

“To consider” — an active process of intellectual engagement

The duty to “consider” the s88(2) matters is not a ritual incantation. In Timwin Construction v Façade Innovations [2005] NSWSC 548 at [39], McDougall J said:

…A requirement to consider, or take into consideration, is equivalent to a requirement to have regard to something: see Zhang v Canterbury City Council (2001) 51 NSWLR 589 at 602 (Spigelman CJ, with whom Meagher and Beazley JJA agreed).

That theme was developed in Laing O’Rourke Australia Construction v H & M Engineering & Construction [2010] NSWSC 818:

As a matter of plain English, the obligation to “consider” something requires that it be given attention, or looked at on its merits… In Tickner v Chapman (1995) 57 FCR 451, Black CJ, speaking of a statutory obligation on a minister of the Crown to consider representations made to him, said at 464 that “the consideration of a representation involves an active intellectual process directed at that representation”.

In my view, the obligation to consider various matters imposed by s 22(2) of the Act should be read in the same way: namely, as requiring an active process of intellectual engagement… Having regard both to the limited ability for adjudicators’ determinations to be reviewed and to the nature of the estoppels that they create, the parties to an adjudication are entitled to have the adjudicator’s consideration, in the sense that I have explained, of the case that each of them brings.

See also QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd v Consolidated Contracting Company Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 292, [40] (Peter Lyons J).

Failure to consider — when does it become jurisdictional error?

Failing to consider a significant submission can invalidate a determination. In QCLNG Pipeline, Peter Lyons J held:

…Notwithstanding the limited time specified in the BCIP Act for an adjudicator to determine an adjudication application, it seems to me that the failure to consider submissions which are of such significance constitutes a failure by the second respondent to comply with an essential requirement of the BCIP Act for a valid decision. On that basis, I consider that the adjudication decision fails to comply with one of the “basic and essential requirements” of the BCIP Act, and for that reason is not a valid adjudication decision.

In TWT Property Group Pty Ltd v Cenric Group Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 72, Stevenson J said in obiter that an adjudicator who reaches a conclusion that a fact was “common ground” only because of a refusal to consider submissions properly made would commit a material breach of natural justice.

In Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 16, Wilson J held a decision void because procedural fairness was denied where the adjudicator decided the case on clause 5.2 (which neither party addressed) and failed to consider Iris’s submission about clause 5.7(a). The Court held at [63]:

even if the applicant was not denied natural justice, in my view the adjudicator did not “consider” the applicant’s submissions as to why clause 5.7 (a) of the contract entitled it to withhold the retention, as mandated by section 88 (2) (d) of the Payment Act.

The high-water mark on the other side is the recent NSW Court of Appeal authority. In Martinus Rail Pty Ltd v Qube RE Services (No.2) Pty Ltd [2025] NSWCA 49, Payne JA held:

the duty to consider a submission is different from a duty to deal with it correctly, whether in law or in fact.

even assuming that there was no reference to the matters identified by Qube… it was not established that the adjudicator failed to consider any submission he regarded as ‘duly made’.

The Court emphasised the “pay now, fight later” design of the legislation and warned against excessive judicial interference. The same restraint runs through Ceerose Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd [2023] NSWCA 215 (Payne JA), Niclin Constructions Pty Ltd v Robotic Steel Fab Pty Ltd & Anor [2023] QSC 218 (Applegarth J applying Ceerose), and Taringa Property Group Pty Ltd v Kenik Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 298 (Hindman J). In Kuatro Build Pty Ltd v Elite Formwork Group Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 372, Hmelnitsky J accepted that “perfunctory” reasons can still demonstrate a bona fide attempt, and in BuiltCom Construction Pty Ltd v VSD Investments Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 250, Peden J confirmed an adjudicator’s incorrect conclusion that submissions were not “duly made” was not jurisdictional.

The contract, “properly made” submissions and new reasons

Under s88(2)(c) and (d) the adjudicator considers submissions “properly made”. Under the NSW Act the equivalent phrase is “duly made”. In J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Cada Formwork Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 63, Peter Lyons J said:

It seems to me that, for the purposes of s 26(2) of the BCIP Act, a properly made submission is one made in a manner provided for in that Act.

Whether a submission is “properly made” is for the adjudicator to decide. In John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads & Traffic Authority of New South Wales & Ors [2007] NSWCA 19, Hodgson JA (Beazley JA agreeing) held:

The legislature plainly entrusts to the adjudicator the role of determining whether submissions are or are not duly made, and thus of determining whether a submission contained in an adjudication response is one that should not be there because of the effect of s.20(2B).

That position has been reinforced repeatedly: see State Water Corporation v Civil Team Engineering Pty Ltd [2013] NSWSC 1879 (Sackar J); Civmec Electrical & Instrumental Pty Ltd v Southern Cross Electrical Engineering Limited & Ors [2019] QSC 300 at [38] (Mullins J: “even if the adjudicator made an error…in concluding that parts of the adjudication response should be rejected on the basis of not being a properly made submission, that would be an error within jurisdiction and not reviewable”); Canterbury Bankstown Council v General Works and Construction Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 310 (Nixon J); and Lipman Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 865 (Nixon J).

In Theiss Pty Ltd & John Holland Pty Ltd v Civil Works Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 187; [2011] 2 Qd R 276, Daubney J held that an adjudicator does not commit jurisdictional error by failing to consider clauses raised only in the adjudication response and not in the payment schedule. Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 92 confirms the same principle for new reasons relying on contract provisions: the adjudicator must not consider a withholding reason that depends on a contract provision unless that reason was already in the schedule.

An adjudicator who omits to refer to a crucial document may, on inference, be found not to have considered it. In Total Lifestyle Windows, Martin J held that where a document directly contradicts the respondent’s case, the adjudicator cannot discharge the s88(2)(c) obligation by stating in general terms that “all relevant documentation” was considered.

“Real relevance” — matters not raised in submissions

There is a long line of NSW Court of Appeal authority that an adjudicator may consider, under s88(2)(a) and (b), matters going to the construction of the Act or the contract that the adjudicator becomes aware of even if not raised in submissions. In The Minister for Commerce v Contrax Plumbing (NSW) Pty Ltd & Ors [2005] NSWCA 142, Hodgson JA held:

…paragraphs (a) and (b) of s.22(2) require the adjudicator to consider the provisions of the Act and the provisions of the construction contract; and in my opinion, that entitles and indeed requires the adjudicator to take into account any considerations (other than considerations arising from facts and circumstances of the particular case not otherwise before him or her) that he or she thinks relevant to the construction of the Act, the construction of the contract, and the validity of terms of the contract having regard to provisions of the Act.

Hodgson JA refined that view in John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads & Traffic Authority of New South Wales & Ors [2007] NSWCA 19:

…the adjudicator should not ignore something which he or she is aware of and also believes is of real relevance to issues arising under pars.(a) and (b), simply because the matter was not raised in submissions duly made by a respondent. Of course, if the matter has not been so raised, there may be questions of natural justice to the claimant that need to be addressed…

Thus, in my opinion, any requirement to consider such matters which were not raised in submissions duly made arises only after a threshold is crossed, involving both awareness of the matters in question and a belief that they are of real relevance.

In Queensland, in Theiss Pty Ltd & John Holland Pty Ltd v Civil Works Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 187; [2011] 2 Qd R 276, Daubney J distinguished Contrax: the “real relevance” doctrine does not allow an adjudicator to take into account brand-new clauses or time-bar arguments first raised in an adjudication response. In Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2011] QCA 22; [2012] 1 Qd R 525, White JA confirmed that an adjudicator who departs from the s88(2) list — for example, deciding what is a “fair thing” — or who truly disregards a claimant’s submissions, makes a decision outside the Act.

Brodyn and the “basic and essential” requirements

The starting point for any review of an adjudication is Brodyn Pty Ltd t/as Time Coast and Quality v Davenport & Ors [2004] NSWCA 394. Hodgson JA (Mason P and Giles JA agreeing) identified five “basic and essential requirements”:

What then are the conditions laid down for the existence of an adjudicator’s determination? The basic and essential requirements appear to include the following:

1. The existence of a construction contract between the claimant and the respondent, to which the Act applies (ss.7 and 8).

2. The service by the claimant on the respondent of a payment claim (s.13).

3. The making of an adjudication application by the claimant to an authorised nominating authority (s.17).

4. The reference of the application to an eligible adjudicator, who accepts the application (ss.18 and 19).

5. The determination by the adjudicator of this application (ss.19(2) and 21(5)), by determining the amount of the progress payment, the date on which it becomes or became due and the rate of interest payable (ss.22(1)) and the issue of a determination in writing (ss.22(3)(a)).

Hodgson JA then identified what counts as “essential” for validity:

What was intended to be essential was compliance with the basic requirements (and those set out above may not be exhaustive), a bona fide attempt by the adjudicator to exercise the relevant power relating to the subject matter of the legislation and reasonably capable of reference to this power (cf R v Hickman; Ex Parte Fox and Clinton (1945) 70 CLR 598), and no substantial denial of the measure of natural justice that the Act requires to be given. If the basic requirements are not complied with, or if a purported determination is not such a bona fide attempt, or if there is a substantial denial of this measure of natural justice, then in my opinion a purported determination will be void and not merely voidable…

The Brodyn framework was adopted in Queensland in Cant Contracting Pty Ltd v Casella [2006] QCA 538; [2007] 2 Qd R 13, 29 [52]–[53] (McMurdo J) and Mansouri & Anor v Aquamist Pty Ltd [2010] QCA 209, [12] (Fraser JA, de Jersey CJ and White JA agreeing). It must now be read against:

  • Chase Oyster Bar Pty Ltd v Hamo Industries Pty Ltd (2010) 78 NSWLR 393; [2010] NSWCA 190;
  • Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531; and
  • Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2011] QCA 22; [2012] 1 Qd R 525.

As McDougall J explained in Cardinal Project Services Pty Ltd v Hanave Pty Ltd [2010] NSWSC 1367, Chase Oyster Bar opened the door to s69 review but did not displace the Brodyn grounds — they go to jurisdiction. In Northbuild, Chesterman JA held that Brodyn remains authority for the proposition that adjudications which do not comply with the essential statutory requirements are void.

The adjudicator’s reasons (s88(5))

Section 88(5)(b) requires reasons unless both parties have asked for none. The reasons need not be lengthy or elaborate. In SSC Plenty Road Pty Ltd v Construction Engineering (Aust) Pty Ltd [2015] VSC 631, Vickery J said:

These circumstances do not call for an exacting standard to be applied to an adjudicator’s reasons. Indeed, the legislative scheme is such that the standard of reasons delivered by an adjudicator within the time constraints required by the Act, and given the nature of the decision to be delivered in this demanding context, and by persons who are often not legally qualified, of necessity means that it falls towards the lower end of the scale.

In Avopiling (NSW) Pty Ltd v Menard Bachy Pty Ltd [2012] NSWSC 1466, Sackar J set out the working approach (cited with approval by Bond J in JAG Projects Qld Pty Ltd v Total Cool Pty Ltd & Anor [2015] QSC 229):

No mechanical formula can be given in determining the precise form the reasons are to take. There is clearly no need to refer to all of the evidence, in the reasons. It has to be clear enough that the relevant evidence or point has been considered. Any issue which is critical should be adverted to, but again the extent to which it needs to be dealt with will be a matter of degree and depends to what extent the issue is canvassed by the parties themselves… It is often said that subjecting every statement of reasons with a “fine tooth comb” will inevitably lead to the exposure of inadequacies often of no relevance.

…the mere fact an adjudicator blandly says he or she has read “all of the submissions and accompanying documents” or simply that he or she is “satisfied” without more in relation to a particular issue under consideration may not, subject to viewing the determination as a whole survive as adequate reasons.

In Sierra Property Qld Pty Ltd v National Construction Management Pty Ltd [2016] QSC 108, Jackson J held that the reasons required by s26(3)(b) BCIPA are an essential part of the decision and a failure to provide them is jurisdictional error. Acciona Agua Australia Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd [2020] QSC 133 (Bond J) found jurisdictional error where the reasons were “nothing more than a vote one way” and revealed no active intellectual engagement.

Importantly, in Cockram Construction Ltd v Fulton Hogan Construction Pty Ltd [2018] NSWCA 107, the NSW Court of Appeal held that asking whether reasons are “adequate” or “sufficient” raises a false issue: the statutory question is whether the adjudicator gave their reasons. Iskra v MMIR Pty Limited [2019] NSWCA 126 confirms that a “fine-tooth comb” approach to reasons is to be avoided. 1155 Nepean Highway Pty Ltd v Promax Buildings Pty Ltd [2020] VSCA 253 makes the same point: brevity is not the same as failure to assess.

Pressure-cooker reality

Adjudicators work under intense time pressure. In Shell Refining (Australia) Pty Ltd v A J Mayr Engineering Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 94, Bergin J observed:

Adjudicators are under pressure to produce their determinations within tight timeframes and in large and/or complex cases that pressure can be intense… A consideration of whether they have so complied is made from the content of their determinations rather than from a statement or claim by the adjudicator in the determination that he/she has so complied.

The Queensland courts have echoed the same point: see Altys Multi-Services Pty Ltd v Grandview Modular Building Systems Pty Ltd [2008] QSC 26 (Skoien AJ).

Judicial review — the post-Kirk landscape

Statutory judicial review under the Judicial Review Act 1991 (Qld) is unavailable for chapter 3, part 4 of the Act (s18(2) JRA). The grounds on which an adjudicator’s decision can be reviewed were summarised by Applegarth J in John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Pty Ltd [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302:

The Courts have recognised that an adjudication decision is void if:

1. it fails to comply with the basic requirements of the Act; or

2. it is not a bona fide attempt by the adjudicator to exercise the relevant power; or

3. there has been a substantial denial of natural justice to a party;

and only a declaration regarding its invalidity (and perhaps injunctive relief, if necessary) is needed to give it its quietus.

Following Kirk v Industrial Court of New South Wales [2010] HCA 1; (2010) 239 CLR 531, the Queensland Court of Appeal in Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2011] QCA 22; [2012] 1 Qd R 525 confirmed that a State legislature cannot exclude the supervisory jurisdiction of the Supreme Court for jurisdictional error.

Non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record

In Queensland, a decision is not amenable to review for non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record. The High Court settled the position in Probuild Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd v Shade Systems Pty Ltd [2018] HCA 4 (Kiefel CJ, Bell, Keane, Nettle and Gordon JJ):

The Security of Payment Act evinces a clear legislative intention to exclude the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court to make an order in the nature of certiorari to quash an adjudicator’s determination for non-jurisdictional error of law on the face of the record…

To permit potentially costly and time-consuming judicial review proceedings to be brought on the basis of error of law on the face of the record, regardless of whether an adjudicator had exceeded the limits of their statutory functions and powers, would frustrate the operation and evident purposes of the statutory scheme.

That confirms the line taken in Shade Systems Pty Ltd v Probuild Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd (No 2) [2016] NSWCA 379, Maxcon Constructions Pty Ltd v Vadasz & Ors (No 2) [2016] SASC 156 and [2017] SASCFC 2, and Samsung C&T Corporation v Loots [2016] WASC 330.

Jurisdictional error — the categories

The High Court in Kirk held that “[i]t is neither necessary, nor possible, to attempt to mark the metes and bounds of jurisdictional error”. It identified three illustrations from Craig v South Australia [1995] HCA 58; (1995) 184 CLR 163 — absence of a jurisdictional fact; disregard of a matter required to be taken into account (or considering one required to be ignored); and misconstruction of the relevant statute. McDougall J translated those for adjudication purposes in Chase Oyster Bar Pty Ltd v Hamo Industries Pty Ltd (2010) 78 NSWLR 393:

(1) The mistaken denial or assertion of jurisdiction, or (in a case where jurisdiction does exist), misapprehension or disregard of the nature of or limits on functions and powers;

(2) Entertaining a matter or making a decision of a kind that lies, wholly or partly, outside the limits on functions and powers, as identified from the relevant statutory context;

(3) Proceeding in the absence of a jurisdictional fact; disregarding something that the relevant statute requires to be considered as a condition of jurisdiction, or considering something required to be ignored; and misconstruction of the statute leading to misconception of functions.

In Demex Pty Ltd v McNab Building Services Pty Ltd [2023] NSWCA 261, the NSW Court of Appeal held that procedural unfairness will only set aside a determination if it causes substantial injustice. The adjudicator had used a reverse-engineered conversion rate to convert tonnes to cubic metres — a rate not put forward by either party. The Court found that, because some rate was implicit in the payment claim and the respondent could have made submissions on it, there was no substantial injustice.

Absence of jurisdiction — common Queensland examples

In Heavy Plant Leasing Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QCA 386, the Court of Appeal held jurisdictional error was committed where the adjudicator decided the application by reference to a document that was not the payment schedule, ignoring the actual schedule. ACP Properties (Townsville) Pty Ltd v Rodrigues Construction Group Pty Ltd & Anor [2021] QSC 45 (Bradley J) applied that principle to invalidate a decision where the adjudicator wrongly held the schedule was invalid.

Other recurring grounds:

  • Contract terminated. McNab NQ Pty Ltd v Walkrete Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 128 — a payment claim made after termination, with no surviving reference date, falls outside jurisdiction.
  • Unlicensed work under the QBCC Act. Ooralea Developments Pty Ltd v Civil Contractors (Aust) Pty Ltd & Anor [2013] QSC 254 — an unlicensed claimant’s award of $969,836.23 was set aside.
  • Unlicensed engineering / electrical work. Agripower Australia Ltd v Queensland Engineering & Electrical Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 268, following Cant Contracting Pty Ltd v Casella [2007] 2 Qd R 13.
  • Awarding more than was claimed. Samsung C&T Corporation v Loots [2016] WASC 330 — an adjudicator awarded $32.4 million that had not been claimed in the payment claim.

Misapprehending functions; misinterpreting the contract

An error in identifying or interpreting a contract term is not, by itself, jurisdictional error: Caltex Refineries (Qld) Pty Ltd & Anor v Allstate Access (Australia) Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 223, [36] (Philip McMurdo J). In Laing O’Rourke Australia Construction Pty Ltd v Samsung C&T Corporation [2016] WASCA 130, Martin CJ said:

the Adjudicator made error with respect to the identification of the particular term of the contract which governed the dispute which he was required to determine, such an error cannot be properly characterised as a failure to determine the dispute by reference to the terms of the contract; rather, such an error can only be characterised as an error with respect to the proper construction or application of the contract… an error of this kind is precisely the kind of departure from contractual and legal precision which the legislature has accepted as part of the ‘trade-off’ for speed and efficiency.

The line shifts where the adjudicator’s interpretation is “so unreasonable that no reasonable person could have arrived at it”: Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 168, [65] (Byrne SJA). Or where the adjudicator is no longer applying the contract as interpreted but allowing the claim on some other basis: Northbuild Construction Sunshine Coast Pty Ltd v Beyfield Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 80, [30] (Philip McMurdo J).

Worked Queensland examples include:

  • Caltex Refineries [2014] QSC 223 — misinterpretation of a hire contract clause was an error within jurisdiction.
  • Northbuild v Beyfield [2014] QSC 80 — treating an EOT precondition as not required was an error within jurisdiction; but allowing variation claims (VO28, VO29) without identifying any contractual basis was jurisdictional error.
  • Ball Construction Pty Ltd v Conart Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 124 — awarding a delay-related adjustment without reference to the defined EOT grounds was jurisdictional error.
  • BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 346 (Applegarth J) and [2013] QCA 394 — awarding “termination costs” arising after the reference date, with no other contractual basis, was jurisdictional error.

Disregarding or considering matters — s88(2) and s88(3)

This ground operates through s88(2) and (3). Examples:

  • Unifor Australia Pty Ltd v Katrd Pty Ltd atf Morshan Unit Trust t/as Beyond Completion Projects [2012] QSC 252 — the adjudicator awarded a “fair and reasonable value” not referable to the contract, taking into account a matter outside s26(2) BCIPA.
  • QCLNG Pipeline Pty Ltd v McConnell Dowell Constructions (Aust) Pty Ltd [2011] QSC 292 — failure to notice a missing variation proposal was not jurisdictional error on the facts.
  • Syntech Resources Pty Ltd v Peter Campbell Earthmoving (Aust) Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 293 — refusing to consider spreadsheets supporting set-offs was a breach of an essential requirement.
  • Samsung C&T Corporation v Loots [2016] WASC 330 — considering merits of competing claims not contained in the payment claim was jurisdictional error.
  • Cooper & Oxley Builders Pty Ltd v Steensma [2016] WASC 386 (Le Miere J) — failing to take a counterclaim/set-off defence into account was jurisdictional error.

Failure to consider a submission properly made

In John Holland Pty Ltd v Roads and Traffic Authority of New South Wales & Ors [2007] NSWCA 19, Hodgson JA cautioned at [55] that an accidental or erroneous omission is not invalidating, provided the listed classes of considerations are addressed:

If an adjudicator does consider the provisions of the Act and the contract which he or she believes to be relevant, and considers those of the submissions that he or she believes to have been duly made, I do not think an accidental or erroneous omission to consider a particular provision of the Act or a particular provision of the contract, or a particular submission, could either wholly invalidate a determination, or invalidate it as regards any part affected by the omission… In a case where there were 1,000 submissions duly made, an accidental failure to consider one of them could not reasonably be considered as invalidating a whole determination…

The key Queensland example of the opposite outcome is Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373. The adjudicator wrote to the Court conceding that he was “fully aware” of the relevant submissions but had simply “forgot” them in final drafting. Ann Lyons J held that the adjudicator had failed to “consider” submissions properly made and had not made a genuine attempt to exercise his power.

Jurisdictional facts — what is and what is not

A jurisdictional fact is “a criterion the satisfaction of which enlivens the exercise of the statutory power or discretion in question”: Gedeon v Commissioner of the New South Wales Crime Commission (2008) 236 CLR 120 at 139 [43], cited by McDougall J in Chase Oyster Bar. The Court of Appeal in Saville v Hallmarc Construction Pty Ltd [2015] VSCA 318 confirmed that jurisdictional facts are not confined to mechanically ascertainable matters and may require evaluation. Categories under the Act:

  • Existence of a construction contract. Jurisdictional fact: Fifty Property Investments Pty Ltd v O’Mara [2006] NSWSC 428; Walton Construction (Qld) Pty Ltd v Salce & Ors [2008] QSC 235; Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Anor [2012] QCA 276; Agripower Australia Ltd v J & D Rigging Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 164.
  • Existence of a valid payment claim. A valid payment claim is necessary: Thiess Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 373.
  • Scope of a payment claim. Within jurisdiction: Richard Kirk Architect Pty Ltd v ABC [2012] QSC 177.
  • Reference date. Originally held not to be a jurisdictional fact (Kembla Coal & Coke v Select Civil [2004] NSWSC 628), but the High Court in Southern Han Breakfast Point Pty Ltd (in Liquidation) v Lewence Construction Pty Ltd [2016] HCA 52 held that the existence of a reference date is a precondition to a valid payment claim.
  • Extent and value of construction work. Not a jurisdictional fact: Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Anor [2012] QCA 276 at [103].
  • Termination of contract. Effective termination with no surviving reference dates is a jurisdictional fact: McNab NQ Pty Ltd v Walkrete Pty Ltd & Ors [2013] QSC 128.
  • Whether services were rendered under a contract. Not a jurisdictional fact: Roberts Construction Group Pty Ltd v Drummond Carpentry Services Pty Ltd [2024] VSC 246.

In Brolton Group Pty Ltd v Hanson Construction Materials Pty Ltd [2020] NSWCA 63, Gleeson JA (Meagher and Payne JJA agreeing) confirmed that calculating an entitlement on an unavailable reference date, while ignoring the available reference date, was jurisdictional error and a denial of natural justice.

Effect of jurisdictional error — remittal and severance

A decision affected by jurisdictional error “is regarded, in law, as no decision at all”: BM Alliance Coal Operations Pty Ltd v BGC Contracting Pty Ltd [2013] QCA 394. Section 101(4) of the Act allows the Court to identify and sever an affected part. Byrne SJA explained the purpose in Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 168 at [73]:

The evident purpose of s.100(4) is not difficult to discern. It had been judicially determined that “jurisdictional error which affects one part of a decision will render the whole of [the decision] void”. That rule produced inconvenient consequences. More than one judge urged that the Act be amended to permit so much of an adjudicator’s decision as was not affected by jurisdictional error to stand.

The Queensland Court of Appeal in Heavy Plant Leasing [2013] QCA 386 noted “obvious difficulties” with remittal: changed circumstances, an outdated payment schedule, the speed-and-finality policy of the Act, removal of adjudication decisions from the JR Act, and that remittal would force a decision outside the statutory time. Total Lifestyle Windows Pty Ltd v Aniko Constructions Pty Ltd (No. 2) [2021] QSC 231 (Freeburn J) refused a third remittal because the adjudication is only a provisional finding; (No 3) [2021] QSC 238 declined to remit to a different adjudicator due to the “grave risk of expanding the fronts of the battle”.

Severance examples: Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro (re-agitation of an EOT issue severed); Karam Group Pty Ltd ATF The Karam (No. 1) Family Trust v HCA Queensland Pty Ltd & Ors [2024] QSC 34 (Bradley J: discretion to save the unaffected part to avoid further adjudication costs); YTO Construction Pty Ltd v Innovative Civil Pty Ltd [2019] NSWCA 110 (NSW: severance and remittal of fraud-affected part).

“Good faith” and bona fide attempt

There is divergence in the authorities on whether good faith requires a “broad” or “narrow” test. The broad view, taken by Peter Lyons J in Queensland Bulk Water Supply Authority v McDonald Keen Group Pty Ltd & Anor [2009] QSC 165 (and adopted by Applegarth J in John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Pty Ltd & Ors [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302) requires “a genuine attempt to exercise the power in accordance with the provisions in the Payments Act”. The Queensland Court of Appeal in Queensland Bulk Water Supply Authority t/as Seqwater v McDonald Keen Group Pty Ltd (in liq) & Anor [2010] QCA 7; [2010] 2 Qd R 322 (Holmes JA, Fraser JA and Fryberg J agreeing) preferred a narrower approach in this statutory context.

White JA in Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd [2011] QCA 22; [2012] 1 Qd R 525 reframed the inquiry away from descriptors:

…the enquiry should focus more on whether the adjudicator has performed the function demanded by the Payments Act and less on pursuing elusive synonyms, keeping always in mind that the legislative intent dictates a person with recognised expertise in the area be selected for the task by an informed body and this, necessarily, facilitates the rapid decision making required.

That view has since been adopted by Ann Lyons J in Theiss v Warren Brothers and Atkinson J in James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd v ASAP Plasterers Pty Ltd & Ors [2011] QSC 145. The shorthand from Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 307 is:

In summary, what is required of an adjudicator is that he or she make a genuine attempt to understand and apply the relevant contract and to exercise the power in accordance with the Act.

Adoption of an “abbreviated” assessment method is not, by itself, an absence of bona fides: Northbuild Construction Pty Ltd v Central Interior Linings Pty Ltd & Ors [2010] QSC 95 (Martin J). The Court’s analysis must look at the determination as a whole — not cherry-picked passages.

Wednesbury unreasonableness

Whether Wednesbury unreasonableness has any role under the Act is contested. Peter Lyons J in Queensland Bulk Water Supply Authority accepted that an “extreme degree of unreasonableness” could deprive a decision of validity. Gotterson JA in McNab Developments (Qld) Pty Ltd v MAK Construction Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QCA 232 expressed hesitation, noting that Wednesbury attaches to discretionary power and was not identified as a species of jurisdictional error in Kirk. Byrne SJA in Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro took the view that a “perverse, capricious or irrational” interpretation can establish jurisdictional error.

Natural justice — the substantial threshold

A denial of natural justice is jurisdictional error if it is “substantial”. In John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Ltd [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302, Applegarth J held:

The adjective “substantial” has been used in the relevant authorities to capture the principle that the opportunity denied was material… Reference to the High Court’s decisions in Stead v State Government Insurance Commission and Ex parte Aala supports the proposition that even if the Court is satisfied that there has been a denial of natural justice, relief may be denied if it can be shown that compliance with the requirements of natural justice could have made no difference to the outcome. It is probably sufficient in this regard for the applicant for relief to show that there were substantial submissions that, as a matter of reality and not mere speculation, might have persuaded the adjudicator to change his or her mind.

The threshold is something that “might well persuade the adjudicator to change his or her mind”: Northbuild Construction Sunshine Coast Pty Ltd v Beyfield Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 80, [38] (Philip McMurdo J), quoting Trysams Pty Ltd v Club Constructions (NSW) Pty Ltd [2008] NSWSC 399, [45].

The natural justice obligation is shaped by the statutory scheme: VK Property Group Pty Ltd & Ors v A A D Design Pty Ltd & Anor [2011] QSC 54, [29] (Boddice J); Coordinated Construction Co Pty Ltd v Climatech (Canberra) Pty Ltd & Ors [2005] NSWCA 229. In Brodyn, Hodgson JA confirmed that the legislative scheme’s requirement for notice to the respondent and an opportunity to make submissions reflects what natural justice the Act requires.

Where natural justice typically arises

Considering material known only to one party:

  • J Hutchinson Pty Ltd v Cada Formwork Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 63 — the “10 June email” sent directly to the ANA, not seen by the respondent, was relied on by the adjudicator and the decision was held void.
  • TQM Design & Construct Pty Ltd v Dasein Constructions Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 1216 — ex parte correspondence with the adjudicator about service date.
  • Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd v Ian James Ericson t/as Flea’s Concreting & Anor [2011] QSC 327 — missing letter, but the response would not have differed and relief was refused.

Failure to engage with the evidence: in Suprima Bakeries Pty Ltd v Australian Weighing Equipment Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 998, McDougall J held the adjudicator had “fail[ed] to deal in a reasoned way with a fundamental part of [the respondent’s] case”.

Decision on a basis not contended for: in John Holland Pty Ltd v TAC Pacific Pty Ltd & Ors [2009] QSC 205; [2010] 1 Qd R 302, 321 [57], Applegarth J said:

The statutory scheme may permit an adjudicator to make unreviewable errors of law in quickly deciding complex legal issues in adjudications of the present kind after considering the parties’ submissions. The statutory scheme does not permit an adjudicator to determine an adjudication on the basis of a view of the law for which neither party has contended. An adjudicator may be free, as it were, to make an unreviewable error of law based on the submissions of one of the parties. He should not be so free where the error is all his own work and might have been avoided by affording natural justice.

Worked Queensland and interstate examples of decisions on a basis neither party contended:

  • Vis Constructions Ltd v Anor v Cockburn & Anor [2006] QSC 416 — existence of an “arrangement” not contended for.
  • David & Gai Spankie & Northern Investments Holdings Pty Ltd v James Trowse Constructions Pty Ltd & Ors (no 2) [2010] QSC 166 — novel interpretation of liquidated damages clause 34.7.
  • Caltex Refineries (Qld) Pty Ltd [2014] QSC 223 — misconstruction of contract term in a way the parties could not reasonably anticipate.
  • Ostwald Bros Pty Ltd v Jaylon Pacific Pty Ltd & Ors [2016] QSC 240 — LDs decided on a basis neither party advanced.
  • Hanson Construction Materials Pty Ltd v Brolton Group Pty Limited [2019] NSWSC 1641 — adjudicator chose a reference date neither party advanced.
  • Acciona Infrastructure Australia Pty Ltd v Holcim (Australia) Pty Ltd [2020] NSWSC 1330 — valuation by way of an implied contract not the subject of either party’s submissions.
  • Ceerose Pty Ltd v A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 239 (Darke J) and Ceerose (No 2) [2023] NSWSC 345 (Richmond J) — new points and a retention-monies finding decided without notice; A-Civil Aust Pty Ltd v Ceerose Pty Ltd [2024] NSWCA 7 confirmed the material denial of procedural fairness.
  • York Property Holdings Pty Ltd v Tomkins Commercial & Industrial Builders Pty Ltd — failure to consider the claimant’s concession that part of the works had reduced from $11.8m to $8.1m, and the misstatement that there were “no submissions” on an increased deduction for defective work, were jurisdictional error.

By contrast, where the adjudicator chooses between competing positions actually advanced, there is no breach: Watkins Contracting Pty Ltd v Hyatt Ground Engineering Pty Ltd [2018] QSC 65 (Brown J); Rogers Construction Group Pty Ltd v Mirage Interiors & Construction Pty Ltd [2024] NSWSC 1344 (Stevenson J).

Refusal to call for further submissions. An adjudicator is not obliged to seek further submissions: Abel Point Marina (Whitsundays) Pty Ltd v Uher & Anor [2006] QSC 295 (Wilson J); Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous Engineering Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 307 (Philip McMurdo J); Ku-Ring-Gai Council v Ichor Constructions Pty Ltd [2014] NSWSC 1534 (Stevenson J). Where further submissions are invited on a particular issue, the adjudicator is not required to invite a second round on every nuance: Modscape Pty Ltd v Francis [2017] TASSC 55 (Blow CJ).

Consequences of denial of natural justice

A “substantial” or “material” denial sets the decision aside: Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd v Ian James Ericson t/as Flea’s Concreting & Anor [2011] QSC 327, [122] (McMurdo J); Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro Pty Ltd & Ors [2015] QSC 168, [20] (Byrne SJA). In Fifty Property Investments Pty Ltd v O’Mara [2006] NSWSC 428, Brereton J held:

The result of a denial of natural justice is that the decision is void, even if the decision would not have been affected by any submissions which might have been made had an opportunity to make them been afforded. While, as a matter of discretion, relief might be declined if it can be shown that the denial of natural justice could not possibly have made a difference to the outcome, all that a plaintiff need establish is that the denial of natural justice deprived it of the possibility of a better outcome, and in order to negate that possibility it is necessary to conclude that a properly conducted adjudication could not possibly have produced a different result.

In Livingstone Shire Council v EarthTEC Pty Ltd and Ors [2018] QSC 271, Lyons SJA followed Szbel v Minister for Immigration and Multicultural and Indigenous Affairs (2006) 228 CLR 152: an adjudicator must alert parties to any adverse conclusion not based on the material before them and allow further submissions.

Fraud

Hodgson JA in Brodyn recognised fraud as a separate ground:

If there is fraud of the claimant in which the adjudicator is also involved, the determination will be void because the adjudicator has not bona fide attempted to exercise the power. If the determination is induced by fraud of the claimant in which the adjudicator is not involved, then I am inclined to think that the determination is not void but voidable; and it is liable to be set aside by proceedings of the kind appropriate to judgments obtained by fraud.

In Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd v Tan James Ericson t/as Flea’s Concreting [2011] QSC 327, McMurdo J discussed fraud post-Kirk, drawing on the Privy Council in Colonial Bank of Australasia v Willan — certiorari may issue for “manifest fraud in the party procuring [the decision]”. In QC Communications NSW Pty Ltd v CivComm Pty Ltd [2016] NSWSC 1095, Ball J set a determination aside where the claimant had relied on invoices it knew to be false, holding the fraud was “sufficiently significant and widespread that it had a substantial effect on the Adjudicator’s determination”.

Abuse of process and re-agitation

The Act “as a whole generally manifests an intention to prevent repetitious re-agitation of the same issues”: John Holland Pty Ltd v Schneider Electric Buildings Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 159 (Applegarth J), adopting the New South Wales Court of Appeal in Dualcorp Pty Ltd v Remo Constructions Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 69; (2009) 74 NSWLR 190. In Dualcorp, Macfarlan JA held:

Thus the primary judge here was correct in considering that “principles akin to res judicata” or “abuse of process” were applicable. Consistent with that broad description, I conclude that the principles of issue estoppel were applicable. Primarily because temporal considerations are of particular significance in relation to progress claims, the analogy between an adjudicator’s determination and a completed cause of action which the principles of res judicata would require is an incomplete one. It is best that the applicable principles be recognised to be those of issue estoppel.

That principle was qualified by McDougall J in Urban Traders v Paul Michael [2009] NSWSC 1072 and Watpac Constructions v Austin Corp [2010] NSWSC 168: not every repetition is an abuse of process; the relevant concept is abuse of the processes of the Act, taking into account s13(6) and s22(4) (NSW) (the “binding valuation” equivalent of s87 BIFSOPA).

The Queensland adoption of the qualified Dualcorp position runs through AE & E Australia Pty Ltd v Stowe Australia Pty Ltd [2010] QSC 135 (Applegarth J), John Holland Pty Ltd v Schneider Electric [2010] QSC 159 (Applegarth J), and Wiggins Island Coal Export Terminal Pty Ltd v Monadelphous & Ors [2016] QSC 96 (Jackson J).

In Caltex Refineries (Qld) Pty Ltd & Anor v Allstate Access (Australia) Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 223, Philip McMurdo J doubted the breadth of Dualcorp issue estoppel:

Although Macfarlan JA described the outcome as a result of an issue estoppel, it is clear that his Honour had in mind something less than the operation of the common law doctrine of issue estoppel as it is usually understood… Where the doctrine does apply, then subject to any qualification by legislation or agreement, it precludes the reagitation in any forum of the same issue. Yet, the estoppel for which Allstate contends, in attempted reliance upon Dualcorp, at its highest is one in which the issue could be reagitated in some forums but not others.

Most recently, in Ingeteam Australia Pty Ltd v Susan River Solar Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 30, Applegarth J held that Dualcorp issue estoppel was engaged where an earlier finding on a licensing issue led the adjudicator to decline jurisdiction, and that the estoppel did not cease to apply simply because an alternative jurisdictional basis existed. The Anshun principle is also recognised as applicable, with appropriate caution: see Watpac Constructions v Austin Corp; HM Hire Pty Ltd v National Plant and Equipment Pty Ltd [2013] QSC 274; [2014] 2 Qd R 44 (Applegarth J).

Mere concurrent pursuit of a payment claim alongside contractual arbitration is not abuse of process: Civil Mining & Construction Pty Ltd v Isaac Regional Council [2014] QSC 231 (Philip McMurdo J).

Section 87 BIF Act – Valuation of work etc. in later adjudication application
87 Valuation of work etc. in later adjudication application

(1)  This section applies if, in deciding an adjudication application, an adjudicator has decided the value of—

(a)  any construction work carried out under a construction contract; or

(b)  any related goods and services supplied under a construction contract.

Note—See section 72 for the valuation of construction work and related goods and services.

(2)  Any adjudicator must, in any later adjudication application that involves the working out of the value of the construction work or of the related goods and services, give the work, or the goods and services, the same value as that previously decided by the adjudicator unless the claimant or respondent satisfies the adjudicator concerned that the value of the work, or the goods and services, has changed since the previous decision.

(3)  However, if a decision or order of a court changes the value of the construction work or of the related goods and services, the adjudicator must give the work, or the goods and services, the same value as changed by the decision or order unless the claimant or respondent satisfies the adjudicator concerned that the value of the work, or the goods and services, has changed since the decision or order.

Section 87 — the binding valuation rule

Section 87 binds a later adjudicator to a previously adjudicated value of construction work or related goods and services unless the parties show the value has changed. The provision rests on a critical distinction between valuation (or quantification) and entitlement. In Rothnere Pty Ltd v Quasar Constructions NSW Pty Ltd [2004] NSWSC 1151, McDougall J said an adjudicator’s decision may involve both, or one or the other. In John Goss Projects Pty Ltd v Leighton Contractors Pty Ltd [2006] NSWSC 789; (2006) 66 NSWLR 707, his Honour clarified:

Sections 9 and 10 make it clear that there is a distinction between the calculation of the amount of a progress payment (which is, ultimately, what the adjudicator is required to do) and the valuation of construction work. That is the distinction that I sought to point out (on reflection, in a way that was perhaps unduly brief and somewhat delphic) in para [43] of my decision in Rothnere.

The approach has been approved by the NSW Court of Appeal in Dualcorp Pty Ltd v Remo Constructions Pty Ltd [2009] NSWCA 69; (2009) 74 NSWLR 190 and by the Queensland Supreme Court in ACN 060 559 971 Pty Ltd v O’Brien [2007] QSC 91; [2008] 2 Qd R 396. The practical consequence is that amounts not referable to a particular item of construction work — such as liquidated damages — do not attract s87. O’Brien set the precedent: a subsequent adjudicator erred in relying on s27 BCIPA to adopt the previous adjudicator’s LD decision.

Where an adjudicator carries out a partial valuation of a large number of variation claims, the binding effect of s87 is limited to those claims actually valued: Hitachi Ltd v O’Donnell Griffin Pty Ltd [2008] QSC 135 (Skoien AJ — only six of 113 variation claims had been valued in the first adjudication).

The Queensland Court of Appeal in Bezzina Developers Pty Ltd v Deemah Stone (Qld) Pty Ltd & Ors [2008] QCA 213; [2008] 2 Qd R 495 held that a later adjudicator who is unaware of an earlier decision is not under an implied obligation to make inquiries:

does not know that there is, or that there probably is, a prior adjudication decision, no occasion arises for the exercise of the power to call for further submissions concerning any prior adjudication decision.

an obligation to seek further submissions from the parties on that topic should not be implied in s 27 when an express power to seek further submissions exists in s 25(4)(a). Implying such an obligation would also create practical difficulties in view of the very limited time available to adjudicators to make decisions and the absence of compulsive powers to obtain any prior adjudication.

In Iris Broadbeach Business Pty Ltd v Descon Group Australia Pty Ltd [2024] QSC 16, Wilson J found jurisdictional error where the adjudicator applied her own previous valuation of architectural work at $200,000 without engaging with Iris’s post-decision invoice suggesting only $183,780 was payable — a clear failure to consider whether the value had changed under s87(2). In Goyder Wind Farm 1 Pty Ltd v GE Renewable Energy Australia Pty Ltd & Ors [2024] SASC 108, Stein J declined to quash a determination on s87 grounds where successive payment claims for EOTs and delay costs were not properly characterised as a single claim for the same construction work.

In Harlech Enterprises Pty Ltd v Beno Excavations Pty Ltd [2022] ACTCA 42, the ACT Court of Appeal held that the equivalent of s87(2) limits the protection of an earlier valuation to the narrow situation the section describes — the Act’s purpose is not to apply issue estoppel to broader findings of adjudicators.

Section 89 BIF Act – Adjudicator may correct clerical mistakes etc.
89 Adjudicator may correct clerical mistakes etc.

(1)  This section applies if the adjudicator’s decision includes—

(a)  a clerical mistake; or

(b)  an error arising from an accidental slip or omission; or

(c)  a material miscalculation of figures or a material mistake in the description of a person, thing or matter mentioned in the decision; or

(d)  a defect of form.

(2)  The adjudicator may, on the adjudicator’s own initiative or on the application of the claimant or respondent, correct the decision.

(3)  The adjudicator may, if requested by the registrar, correct the decision.

Section 89 — the slip rule

Section 89 lets an adjudicator fix four discrete kinds of error: clerical mistake, accidental slip or omission, material miscalculation/material mistake in description, or defect of form. As McDougall J observed in Musico v Davenport [2003] NSWSC 977, the existence of the slip-rule power confirms that ordinary mistakes do not vitiate a determination — the legislature recognised that mistakes can be corrected at a final hearing, and the slip-rule power is permissive only.

In McNab Developments (Qld) Pty Ltd v MAK Construction Services Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QCA 232, Jackson J explained that an accidental slip or omission in considering the schedule, submissions or documentation under s26(2) BCIPA does not invalidate the decision — precisely because s89 (and its predecessors) preserve the operation of the decision so it can be corrected. Whether the error is significant enough to warrant correction is a matter for the parties and the adjudicator.

The slip-rule does not allow the adjudicator to redo their reasoning. In Uniting Church in Australia Property Trust (Qld) v Davenport & Anor [2009] QSC 134 and Theiss Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Ors [2012] QSC 373, the Court held that a “correction” cannot involve adopting a completely different method of calculation or chain of reasoning that produces materially different results.

If the adjudicator declines to correct, that is itself within jurisdiction. McDougall J in Musico v Davenport:

It might be thought that, in the ordinary case, a proper exercise of the discretion conferred by s22 (5) (s28 BCIPA) would favour the correction of mistakes of the kind referred to therein. However, it is a matter for the adjudicator whether or not any such correction is to be made. If an adjudicator declines to make any correction — for example, because he or she thinks there is no mistake — then the error (if any) will be an error within jurisdiction.

The touchstone is inadvertence: Cawood v Infraworth Pty Ltd [1990] 2 Qd R 114 at 122 (Macrossan CJ): “Inadvertence, as distinguished from an error or mistake resulting from deliberate decision, is the basis of the jurisdiction to correct under the slip rule”. Hansen Yuncken Pty Ltd & Anor v Yuanda Australia Pty Ltd & Anor [2018] SASC 158 (Lovell J) confirms that a refusal to exercise the slip rule is within jurisdiction and not reviewable.

Practice tips

  • Treat the adjudication as “pay now, fight later”. Even where you have strong grounds, plan the cash response (security, bond, draw on retention) before you plan the legal response — the courts repeatedly say they will not readily set aside an adjudication.
  • Build the payment schedule like a pleading. Every reason you might want the adjudicator to consider must already be there. New reasons in the response are excluded by s88(3)(b) and the cases applying s82 (Total Lifestyle Windows [2021] QSC 92; Chevron Park [2024] QSC 202; Lipman [2025] NSWSC 865). If the schedule says “Nil” without reasons, expect the claim to be paid in full (Insite [2023] QSC 33).
  • Tie every value to a specific item of construction work or related goods and services if you ever want to rely on s87 to bind a later adjudicator. Liquidated damages, set-offs and global delay costs typically will not attract s87 (O’Brien; Goyder Wind Farm; Harlech).
  • Do not rely on a court to fix an adjudicator’s contract-interpretation error. Misconstruing the contract is almost always within jurisdiction (Caltex Refineries; Laing O’Rourke v Samsung). The decision is set aside only if the adjudicator stops applying the contract entirely or interprets it so unreasonably no reasonable person could have done so.
  • If the adjudicator decides on a basis neither side argued — a clause not raised, a reference date not contended for, an interpretation no one canvassed — flag a denial of natural justice immediately and identify what you would have said. The threshold is something that “might well persuade the adjudicator to change his or her mind”: Trysams; Northbuild v Beyfield.
  • Do not pin a challenge on inadequate or sparse reasons. Brevity is not the same as failure to engage (Avopiling; 1155 Nepean Highway; Cockram Construction; Iskra; Kuatro Build). The recent NSW Court of Appeal authority — Ceerose and Martinus Rail — has narrowed the runway considerably.
  • Consider severance under s101(4) before declaring a whole determination dead. The court can save the parts not affected by jurisdictional error (Sunshine Coast Regional Council v Earthpro; Karam Group) and remittal is increasingly unlikely (Heavy Plant Leasing; Total Lifestyle Windows (No. 2)).
  • Use s89 properly: if there is a genuine clerical slip or material miscalculation in the decision, write to the adjudicator quickly and identify the four-corner basis. Do not dress up a substantive disagreement as a slip — the adjudicator cannot redo their reasoning, and refusal to correct is itself within jurisdiction (Uniting Church; Theiss; Hansen Yuncken v Yuanda).
