Chapter 3 · Section 64

Construction contract

The BIF Act’s payment regime only switches on if there is a “construction contract” between the parties. Section 64 of the BIF Act defines that term broadly — it is not limited to a signed, formal agreement. Getting this threshold question right matters: if there is no construction contract (or no “other arrangement”), an adjudicator has no jurisdiction and any decision will be set aside.

Section 64 BIF Act – “construction contract” (definition)
64 Definitions for chapter

In this chapter—

construction contract means a contract, agreement or other arrangement under which 1 party undertakes to carry out construction work for, or to supply related goods and services to, another party.

“Contract”, “agreement” and “other arrangement”

The definition deliberately separates a “contract”, an “agreement” and an “other arrangement”. That structure suggests “other arrangement” reaches further than a common law contract. That was the view of Nicholas J in Okaroo Pty Ltd v Vos Constructions and Joinery Pty Ltd [2005] NSWSC 45:

With regard to the authorities, and to its context in the Act, in my opinion the term “arrangement” in the definition is a wide one, and encompasses transactions or relationships which are not legally enforceable agreements. The distinction in the definition between “a contract” and “other arrangement” is intended by the legislature to be one of substance so that under the Act construction contracts include agreements which are legally enforceable and transactions which are not. Thus in distinguishing between these relationships I understand the legislature intends that “contract” is to be given its common law meaning and that “arrangement” means a transaction or relationship which is not enforceable at law as a contract would be. Accordingly I reject the submission for Okaroo that the term “arrangement” should be understood to mean an agreement which is tantamount to a contract enforceable at law.

Douglas J approved this approach in Queensland in Bezzina Developers Pty Ltd v Deemah Stone (Qld) Pty Ltd [2007] QSC 286.

McDougall J explored the same idea in Machkevitch v Andrew Building Commissions [2012] NSWSC 546:

As a matter of language, it seems to be clear that the legislature intended that a "construction contract" could include both a "contract" (as that concept is known to and understood in the law) and some "other arrangements" that would not in law be regarded as contracts.

It seems to me, as a simple matter of reading the legislative words, that the concept of "other arrangement" is something which goes beyond the concept of "contract".

No doubt, the legislature had in mind that, from time to time, work would be done pursuant to arrangements which might not be susceptible to classification as contracts, formal or informal. Clearly, it did not intend that the entitlement to payment should depend on the degree of formality in the arrangements pursuant to which work should be done. In this respect, the legislative intention could be contrasted with the intention underlying s 10 of the Home Building Act 1989 (NSW), under which a builder is not entitled to enforce a contract unless it is licensed, and carries out work pursuant to a written contract.

The word "arrangement" may be thought to be a somewhat strange one in the context of the Act. It its primary meaning, it denotes the ordering or disposition of things (see, for example, the online editions of the Macquarie Dictionary and the Oxford English Dictionary). But the same reference sources suggest that a secondary meaning of "arrangement" denotes measures or preparations, or plans for the accomplishment of some purpose.

How much is enough to amount to an “other arrangement”?

Even though “other arrangement” is broader than a contract, the courts have set a floor: there must still be a settled, two-sided dealing — not just one party turning up and doing work.

In Crown Green Square Pty Ltd v Transport for NSW [2021] NSWSC 1557, Henry J refused progress payment relief because the claimant was not engaged by the respondent under a construction contract or “other arrangement”. The respondent had contracted with a related entity of the claimant, not the claimant itself. After the respondent failed to provide a payment schedule, the claimant sued, arguing the entitlement arose under an “other arrangement”. Henry J disagreed: the work obligation sat under a different construction contract to which the claimant was not a party. In obiter, her Honour said an “other arrangement” need not be legally binding but requires more than one party simply undertaking to do construction work for another — there “must be a concluded state of affairs between two or more parties involving some element of reciprocity or acceptance of mutual rights and obligations relating to payment or price for the works (which may or may not be legally binding obligations) under which one party undertakes to carry out construction work for another party to the arrangement.”

In Ventia Australia Pty Ltd v BSA Advanced Property Solutions (Fire) Pty Ltd [2021] NSWSC 1534, Rees J observed in obiter that “arrangement” is the right label where the parties’ dealings about the work lack the precision of express contractual terms. On the facts, the contract expressly provided that each work order created a new construction contract, and her Honour held the court could not look past that express regime to find an “arrangement”.

In Bettar Holdings Pty Ltd v RWC Brookvale Investments Pty Ltd, Cole DCJ held there was no construction contract or “other arrangement” where negotiations remained “subject to a comprehensive agreement being arrived at and recorded in a written and executed contract” that never materialised. Citing BSA Advanced Property Solutions (Fire) Pty Ltd v Ventia Australia Pty Ltd (2022) 108 NSWLR 350, his Honour confirmed that an “other arrangement” must involve an undertaking to perform work for consideration.

In Justar Property Group Pty Ltd v Chase Building Group (Canberra) Pty Ltd [2020] ACTSC 231, Mossop J had to decide whether the adjudicator had jurisdiction. The respondent argued the contract was actually with another company in its group. Mossop J held the adjudicator was entitled to find some “other arrangement” existed between the named claimant and respondent, and there was no error of law on the face of the determination. His Honour applied Machkevitch and looked for “a concluded state of affairs, which is bilateral at least, which can amount to an arrangement under which one party to it undertakes to perform construction work for another party to it”.

His Honour also adopted Nicholas J’s reasoning from Okaroo, that an “arrangement encompasses transactions or relationships which are not legally enforceable”, including the following passage:

the only matter for consideration is whether it is one under which one party undertakes to carry out construction work, or to supply related goods and services, for another party. There is no other requirement or qualification which is expressly or by implication included in the definition which must be satisfied. It may be safely assumed that had the legislature intended any additional requirement or qualification it would have included it in the definition.

And on what “arrangement” itself requires, McDougall J in Machkevitch said:

In my view, what is required is that there be something more than a mere undertaking; or something which can be said to give rise to an engagement, although not a legally enforceable engagement, between two parties; or a state of affairs under which one party undertakes to the other to do something; or an arrangement between parties to like effect.

In those circumstances, the court must look for a concluded state of affairs, which is bilateral at least, which can amount to an arrangement under which one party to it undertakes to perform construction work for another party to it. It is not necessary that the arrangement be legally enforceable; but an "arrangement" which is legally enforceable may be, a priori, a construction contract.

Identifying the right parties

Naming the wrong respondent is fatal. In Ratcliffe v Horizon Glass & Aluminium Pty Ltd [2023] NSWSC 196, Rees J set aside an adjudication determination because the claimant had named the principal’s individual director as the respondent, when the contract was actually with the principal company. Her Honour applied Brereton JA’s summary in Mills v Walsh [2022] NSWCA 255 at [73] on identifying parties:

“The parties to a contract are identified according to the objective theory of contract, which involves ascertaining the intention of the parties from their communications and the circumstances in their mutual knowledge, including their evident commercial aims and expectations; their subjective beliefs and intentions are irrelevant, save insofar as they are manifest and shared. However, the post-contractual conduct of the parties may more readily be resorted to for this purpose than for the purpose of construing contractual terms.”

On the balance of probabilities the parties had intended the principal company — not its director — to enter the contract, so the determination was set aside.

Queensland application: GCB Constructions

In GCB Constructions Pty Ltd v SEQ Formwork Pty Ltd & Ors [2023] QSC 71, the Queensland Supreme Court applied the “other arrangement” concept directly under s 64 BIF Act. The parties had had an on-site discussion about additional works. The adjudicator treated that conversation as an “other arrangement”.

Burns J held that to be an “other arrangement” under s 64 there must be at least a bilateral concluded state of affairs, and a sufficient degree of mutuality — including enough settled detail for the work or materials to be claimed with precision. The site discussion fell short for these reasons:

  • The payment claim was for the supply of material, but nothing in the discussion addressed supply of, or payment for, material.
  • There was no specification of the rates to be charged for labour.
  • Nothing was agreed as to the time for performance for any of the work, the making of the claim, the time for payment or retention money.

Because there was no construction contract or other arrangement, the adjudicator’s decision was void.

Pleading the arrangement and the “undertaking”

In EnerMech Pty Ltd v Acciona Infrastructure Projects Australia Pty Ltd [2024] NSWCA 162, Basten AJA (Meagher JA and Griffiths AJA agreeing), considering the equivalent NSW definitions, said at [16]:

“Two points are immediately apparent from these provisions: first, the Security of Payment Act does not purport to limit the amount or nature of a payment to which a party is entitled under a construction contract; secondly, there is a risk in compartmentalising payments according to their character, regardless of the terms of the contract, so as to contend that some fall within the concept of a progress payment for which a claim may be made, and some do not”.

In Class Electrical Services v Go Electrical [2013] NSWSC 363, McDougall J clarified his earlier remarks in Machkevitch on the word “undertaking”:

The first use of the word "undertaking" seems to me now to be somewhat unfortunate, having regard to the definition of "construction contract". It was intended to pick up undertakings of the kind said to have been given by Mr Machkevitch to the builder, as I explained above. It was not intended to be "an undertaking" in a cognate sense to the verb "undertakes" as it is used in the definition of "construction contract".

Thus, properly understood, I do not think that anything that I said in Machkevitch focused on what is required to satisfy or demonstrate the concept of undertaking to do construction work or supply related goods and services. It was concerned with the existence of a contract or arrangement.

Most recently, in Bettar Holdings Pty Ltd trading as Hunt Collaborative v RWC Brookvale Investment Pty Ltd as trustee for Brookvale Development Trust [2025] NSWCA 242, McHugh JA (Bell CJ and Kirk JA agreeing) confirmed that an “other arrangement” under the equivalent NSW definition must be properly pleaded with the material facts constituting the arrangement and the basis on which the party had “undertaken” to carry out work [135]–[136]. It also requires either a legally binding obligation or a “concluded state of affairs” involving “some element of reciprocity or acceptance of mutual rights and obligations relating to payment or price for the works” [139]. His Honour noted the conflict between Lendlease Engineering Pty Ltd v Timecon Pty Ltd and Crown Green Square Pty Ltd v Transport for NSW [140] but declined to resolve it — on the facts there was no “other arrangement” under either test, because the pleading only alleged a common law contract or an estoppel preventing denial of one, and there was no allegation that Hunt had “undertaken” to perform work on any other basis. Any quantum meruit case was foreclosed by the pleaded contract date of 27 October 2023, with no request for work or performance before then [141]–[142].

By contrast, in Bloc Constructions (NSW) Pty Limited v Alorra Piling (NSW) Pty Ltd [2025] NSWSC 1324, Peden J confirmed that no formal, signed contract is needed. At [51], her Honour held that even without a provable formal contract, the parties’ objective conduct — the principal requesting piling work, the contractor performing it, and the principal making payments — was enough to establish an “arrangement”. The decision underscores that the Act’s remedial purpose lets it operate on the commercial reality of work performed, even where strict legal enforceability might be open to question (the claimant would in any event likely have a restitutionary entitlement).

Both Okaroo and Machkevitch on “arrangement” were summarised by Stevenson J in IWN No 2 Pty Ltd v Level Orange Pty Ltd [2012] NSWSC 1439, at [25]. See also Vis Constructions Ltd & Anor v Cockburn & Anor [2006] QSC 416, where Jones J considered that to establish a contract, agreement or other arrangement within the meaning of Schedule 2 BCIPA, “[i]t must be established that there was an undertaking by one party…to carry out construction work or supply related goods to another party”.

Worked examples

  • Reference to an earlier contract is just background. Kaycee Trucking Pty Ltd v M and C Rogers Transport Pty Ltd & Ors [2014] QSC 185. There were two contracts between the parties — a 2012 contract and a 2013 contract. The payment claim mentioned both. The respondent argued the claim was therefore made under two contracts and invalid. Because no work claimed was actually done under the 2012 contract, the Court held the references to 2012 were background only and the claim was made under the 2013 agreement.
  • Email exchange as a “concluded state of affairs”. SMLXL Projects Pty Ltd v RIIS Retail A/S [2017] NSWDC 131. Dicker SC DCJ found that an email from the claimant setting out services performed, followed by an email from the respondent accepting the works, was enough to constitute a construction contract under the NSW Act — the works were agreed and amounted to “a concluded state of affairs”.
  • Whether an “other arrangement” must be legally binding. Lendlease Engineering Pty Ltd v Timecon Pty Ltd [2019] NSWSC 685. Ball J held that for an “other arrangement” to qualify under the NSW equivalent provision, it must give rise to a legally binding obligation, though not necessarily contractual in nature. Lendlease had argued there was no “contract or other arrangement” at all, so Timecon could not claim a progress payment. The judgment expressly distinguished earlier authority that an “other arrangement” need not be legally enforceable.

The Queensland approach to construction: Capricorn Quarries

In Capricorn Quarries Pty Ltd v Inline Communication Construction Pty Ltd [2012] QSC 388; [2013] 2 Qd R 1, Jackson J rejected the argument that BCIPA should be given a “remedial” construction. Instead, his Honour said the definitions of “construction contract”, “construction work” and “related goods and services” should be read “naturally”:

Having regard to the context as discussed, in my view, a “natural” construction of the relevant definitions of BCIPA in this case is to be preferred to an approach which seeks to extend the operation of BCIPA by a “liberal interpretation” to be engaged in with the purpose of increasing the width of the class of persons who are entitled to the benefit of a payment claim and correspondingly increasing the width of the class of persons who are subject to BCIPA’s restriction and obligations.

The language chosen by Parliament to define “construction contract”, “construction work” and “related goods and services” has no purpose other than to draw the line between who is in and who is out of those classes. There seems to be little logic in seeking to stretch that language either way. In saying this, I take a “natural” construction to be that arrived at by the usual process of the application of the common law of statutory interpretation, as affected by statute, but without a presumptive approach.

“Mixed” contracts

A construction contract can include both work that is “construction work” and work that is not. McDougall J in Brian Leigh Smith & Anor v Coastivity Pty Ltd [2008] NSWSC 313 held (at [35]):

The definition of “construction contract” does not require that all the work undertaken to be performed, or all the goods and services undertaken to be provided, pursuant to the contract should be construction work or related goods and services as defined. It is sufficient if some of them fall within the definition. Thus, it is not to the point that some of the obligations undertaken by Coastivity (for example, in relation to raising finance) do not amount to related services. The question must be whether any of the other obligations undertaken fall within the statutory definition.

The Queensland Court of Appeal took the same view in Thiess Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Anor [2012] QCA 276, with Philippides J stating (at [56]):

In my view, his Honour was clearly correct in stating at [77] that “a contract or arrangement is a construction contract if it contains an undertaking of the type specified in the definition of construction contract, notwithstanding that it contains other undertakings or imposes other obligations not within the definition”. It also accords with the approach taken in New South Wales in respect of equivalent legislation: see Brian Leigh Smith v Coastivity Pty Ltd [2008] NSWSC 313 at [35]; HM Australia Holdings Pty Ltd v Edelbrand Pty Ltd [2011] NSWSC 604 at [30]. What is required for the purposes of the definition of “construction contract” is that it is one under which a party undertakes to carry out some “construction work”.

The same case confirmed that “the adjudicator’s determination as to the extent and value of the ‘construction work’ or ‘related goods and services’ the subject of the payment claim does not…concern a matter of jurisdictional fact”: Thiess Pty Ltd v Warren Brothers Earthmoving Pty Ltd & Anor [2012] QCA 276, [103] (Philippides J, Holmes and White JJA agreeing).

One claim, one contract

A payment claim must relate to one construction contract only, and “there can only be one adjudication application for any particular payment claim for any particular contract”: Matrix Projects (Qld) Pty Ltd v Luscombe [2013] QSC 4, [17]–[18] (Douglas J); Rail Corporation of NSW v Nebax Constructions [2012] NSWSC 6, [44] (McDougall J).

  • Claim spanning two contracts is invalid. Matrix Projects (Qld) Pty Ltd v Luscombe [2013] QSC 4. Work was performed under a period subcontract and separately as “do and charge” rectification. The Court rejected the argument that “arrangement” was wide enough to bundle both, holding the claim was not made under a single construction contract — fatal to its validity.
  • Adjudication void if the claim spans two contracts. Trinco (NSW) Pty Ltd v Alpha A Group Pty Ltd [2018] NSWSC 239. McDougall J held the adjudication determination void on reference-date grounds, but added that, if his contract construction was wrong, the determination would also be void because the payment claim related to works under both the first and second contract.

Side deeds

A side deed that varies the head contract is itself a construction contract. In APN DF2 Project 2 Pty Ltd v Grocon Constructors (Victoria) Pty Ltd [2014] VSC 596, a side deed was entered after the design and construct contract was signed, varying that contract. Vickery J held the side deed to be a “construction contract” under the Victorian Act:

It is plainly obvious that the concept of “construction contract” as defined in s 4 of the Act, encompasses both an original contract entered into between the parties (in this case the D&C Contract) and any instrument which validly effects a variation to the original contract (in this case the Side Deed). The Act could not properly operate in a commercial context if this was not the case.

In this case the Side Deed in law varied the D & C Contract, including the payment regime in critical respects. Further, the D & C Contract remained in place following entry into the Side Deed, subject to the alterations reflected in the variations. This resulted in the parties making two contracts which co-existed: the D & C Contract as amended by the Side Deed; and the Side Deed itself.

Practice tips

  • Before issuing or responding to a payment claim, identify the exact contract or arrangement it relies on — and the exact legal entities on each side. Naming the wrong respondent (a director instead of the company, or a related entity instead of the actual contracting party) will sink the claim: see Ratcliffe and Crown Green Square.
  • If you are working under an informal arrangement (verbal scope, site-meeting agreement, work-on-handshake), make sure the dealings cover the basics — what is being supplied, the rates, timing, payment terms. GCB Constructions shows that a vague on-site discussion is not enough to be an “other arrangement” under s 64.
  • Always issue one payment claim per construction contract. If work has been done under both a head subcontract and a separate “do and charge” arrangement, claim them separately (Matrix Projects; Trinco).
  • Treat a side deed or variation deed as a construction contract in its own right. If the side deed reorganises the payment regime, your claim regime needs to follow the deed (APN DF2).
  • Don’t panic if the contract bundles construction and non-construction obligations (e.g. finance, advisory work). The contract is still a construction contract so long as some of the obligations fall within the definition (Coastivity; Thiess).
  • Where there is no signed contract but the principal has been requesting and paying for the work, the conduct alone may amount to a construction contract (Bloc Constructions) — document that conduct contemporaneously (emails accepting works, scope confirmations, payment history) so the “concluded state of affairs” is provable.
